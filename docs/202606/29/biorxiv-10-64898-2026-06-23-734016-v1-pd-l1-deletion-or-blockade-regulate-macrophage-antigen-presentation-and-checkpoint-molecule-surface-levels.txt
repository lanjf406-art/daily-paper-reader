# Abstract 17 

Macrophages in the tumor microenvironment are known to upregulate PD-L1 expression, 18 

thereby suppressing T cells through PD-1 ligation. However, the manner in which PD-L1 19 

expression intrinsically impacts macrophages and their immunomodulatory phenotype is less 20 

clear. Clarifying this knowledge gap would yield insight into the mechanisms of 21 

immunosuppression within the tumor microenvironment. To characterize the macrophage 22 

intrinsic role of PD-L1, we used complementary genetic and pharmacological approaches by 23 

analyzing primary murine bone marrow-derived macrophages (BMDMs) with complete genetic 24 

PD-L1 deletion and wildtype BMDMs treated with anti-PD-L1 blocking antibodies. 25 

Macrophages were evaluated across naïve, pro-inflammatory (M1), and tumor conditioned 26 

(TCM) polarization states in vitro. Unlike prior reports, neither genetic deletion nor antibody 27 

blockade dramatically altered the expression of macrophage polarization markers or in vitro 28 

phagocytic capacity. Both conditions consistently reduced surface levels of the M1-associated 29 

costimulatory molecule CD80, prompting further analysis of T cell interacting and antigen 30 

presenting proteins, in which we revealed disparate effects of genetic deletion and antibody 31 

blockade on the surface levels of MHCI, MHCII, PD-1, and PD-L2. These findings suggest that 32

# Introduction 38 

Strategies to successfully target tumor macrophages are needed to improve patient 39 

therapeutic responses. Macrophages are highly plastic innate immune cells that play an essential 40 

role in shaping the tumor microenvironment. They display a spectrum of polarization states, 41 

classically defined by a framework of pro- versus anti-inflammatory states, by which they 42 

facilitate myriad roles in infection, cancer, and tissue homeostasis (1–3). Cell-extrinsic and -43 

intrinsic cues shape macrophage polarization states, which, in turn, enforce the context-specific 44 

activation states of T cells. Because of their immunomodulatory functions and their ability to 45 

take on unique polarization or functional profiles in response to local microenvironmental cues, 46 

macrophages play an important role in the efficacy of immunotherapy (4,5). 47 

The canonical function of programmed cell death-ligand 1 (PD-L1), a membrane-bound 48 

immune checkpoint ligand protein, is to bind the T cell inhibitory receptor programmed cell 49 

death protein 1 (PD-1), thereby suppressing T cell activation and adaptive immune responses (6– 50 

8). PD-L1 is frequently overexpressed on cancer cells as a mechanism of immune evasion, 51 

leading to suppression of T cell activation in the tumor microenvironment (9,10). Immune 52 

checkpoint blockade therapies have been developed to interfere with the PD-1/PD-L1 axis with 53 

blocking antibodies to restore cytotoxic T cell function (11,12). This therapeutic has had 54 

significant impact on patient outcomes in many types of cancers, underscoring the clinical 55 

relevance of PD-L1 (13). 56 

While PD-L1 overexpression in cancer cells has been a primary target in PD-1/PD-L1 57 

immune checkpoint blockade, PD-L1 expression by tumor-associated macrophages also plays a 58 

significant role in therapeutic outcomes (14–16). Prior work has indicated potential differential 59 

efficacies of PD-1 and PD-L1 blocking antibodies (17–20), suggesting that the cellular 60 

mechanisms driving outcomes of each monotherapy may differ. Despite its limited cytoplasmic 61 

domain, PD-L1 has been shown to have cell-intrinsic functions independent of PD-1 ligation, 62 

including intracellular and nuclear activities in a variety of cancer cell types (21,22) and in 63 

dendritic cells (23). Connections between PD-L1 expression and macrophage activation status 64 

within tumors have also been noted (24). Subsequent studies in our lab (25) and others (17,26– 65 

28) suggested that treatment of macrophages in monoculture with PD-L1 blocking antibodies 66 

promoted an immunostimulatory phenotype and function. These findings suggest that PD-L1 67 

exerts function independent of the canonical PD-1/PD-L1 axis. However, the literature remains 68 

inconsistent as to whether PD-L1 expression intrinsically promotes or stabilizes macrophage 69 

states. 70 

There currently remains a lack of understanding as to whether PD-L1 actively regulates 71 

macrophage activation or merely serves as a marker of macrophage activation state. PD-L1 72 

expression is highest in M1 inflammatory macrophages and lowest in naïve macrophages (29). 73 

This is likely due to its upregulation induced by interferon-gamma (IFN γ), (30–32) 74

We established an in vitro monoculture system in which we differentiated primary 94

(TCM) state using interleukin-4 (IL-4), interleukin-10 (IL-10), and E0771 murine triple negative 98 

breast cancer cell line supernatant ( Figure 1A ). For the PD-L1 blockade group WT macrophages 99 

were treated with either anti-PD-L1 antibodies (clone 10F.9G2) or isotype controls during the 100 

48-hour polarization step of our experimental workflow ( Figure 1A ). Macrophages were 101

were confirmed by detection of anti-PD-L1 surface staining with flow cytometry, and deficiency 103 

was further confirmed by Western blot detection of whole PD-L1 protein from lysates of M1-104

# Discussion 253 

We investigated the cell-intrinsic role of PD-L1 in macrophages by comparing genetic 254 

deletion and antibody-mediated blockade with PD-L1-sufficient controls across multiple 255 

polarization states. Our findings suggest a macrophage intrinsic role of PD-L1 that influences the 256 

surface levels of immunomodulatory molecules on the cell surface. They also demonstrate that 257 

PD-L1 deletion and PD-L1 blockade differentially regulate macrophage immune phenotype, 258 

particularly surface levels of CD80, MHC molecules, and other T-cell interaction proteins. The 259 

distinct effects of the two tested methods of PD-L1 interference are consistent with PD-L1 260 

having macrophage intrinsic functions beyond its known surface interactions. 261 

Contrary to reports that PD-L1 blockade promotes inflammatory macrophage polarization, 262 

we observed minimal effects on classical polarization markers. Previous models indicated that 263 

PD-L1 blockade increased pro-inflammatory features MHCI, MHCII, and CD86 and decreased 264 

Arg1 by comparing fold-change of marker expression levels within macrophages treated with 265 

PD-L1 blockade and isotype controls(27,36). While these studies reported statistically significant 266 

changes in marker expression, the biological relevance of these results was less clear. In our 267 

analysis we compared the changes in marker expression with WT M1- or TCM-polarized 268 

macrophages as controls to provide context for the relative changes in polarization profiles. We 269 

also analyzed naïve, M1- and TCM-polarized macrophages, in which we demonstrated varied 270 

responses to PD-L1 interference. To ensure that the lack of changes in polarization was not due 271 

to preferential survival of less polarized macrophages upon polarization, we compared cell 272 

viability between WT, KO, isotype and antibody treated groups in each polarization condition 273 

and observed no decrease in viability ( Supplementary Figure 2 ). Taken together, our findings 274 

suggest that PD-L1 perturbation is insufficient to broadly regulate macrophage polarization in 275 

isolated BMDMs. While perturbations to PD-L1 may induce statistically significant fold changes 276 

in polarization marker expression levels, they may not be so relevant when compared to that of 277 

bona fide M1 or TCM-polarized macrophages. However, this conclusion should be interpreted 278 

within the context of our in vitro system, as PD-L1 may exert more pronounced effects within 279 

tissue microenvironments where macrophages are exposed to complex cellular and inflammatory 280 

signals. 281 

Although macrophage activation is frequently accompanied by changes in phagocytic 282 

function, neither genetic deletion nor antibody blockade of PD-L1 altered zymosan bead uptake 283 

in our system. Phagocytosis is a canonical function of macrophages that is frequently associated 284 

with macrophage activation. Prior studies have reported increased phagocytic function of 285 

macrophages following interference with the PD-1/PD-L1 axis in several tumor models, whereas 286

observation that PD-L1 perturbation did not broadly alter macrophage polarization and suggests 290

inflammatory cues in the tumor microenvironment rather than PD-L1 perturbation alone. 292 

A consistent change to macrophage phenotype upon PD-L1 deletion or blockade was a 293 

reduction in CD80 surface levels, which we had also previously observed in our melanoma 294 

model (25). CD80 and PD-L1 have been shown to bind in cis on the plasma membrane of 295 

dendritic cells(38,39). Consistent with our findings, Zhao et al.(39) reported reduced CD80 296 

surface levels following PD-L1 blockade and attributed this effect to CTLA4-mediated trans-297 

endocytosis of CD80. However, because our experiments were performed in BMDM 298 

monocultures lacking CTLA4-expressing T cells, the consistent reduction in CD80 observed 299 

following both PD-L1 blockade and genetic deletion cannot readily be explained by this 300 

mechanism. Instead, our findings suggest that PD-L1 contributes to the maintenance of CD80 301 

surface levels through a macrophage-intrinsic mechanism, potentially involving regulation of 302 

CD80 stability, trafficking, or surface retention. 303 

The observed reduction in PD-L2 was particularly unexpected. Although direct interactions 304 

between PD-L1 and PD-L2 have not been described, both ligands bind PD-1 and share 305 

overlapping immunoregulatory functions. PD-L2 expression is typically lower than PD-L1, but 306 

exhibits approximately three-fold greater affinity for PD-1(40). In our model, PD-L1 was highly 307 

expressed in M1 macrophages and moderately expressed in TCMs, whereas PD-L2 surface 308 

expression was largely restricted to the TCM state ( Supplementary Figure 4 ). Given these 309 

overlapping functions, we anticipated that loss of PD-L1 signaling might be accompanied by 310 

compensatory upregulation of PD-L2. Instead, PD-L2 surface levels decreased significantly 311 

following PD-L1 blockade and were nearly abolished in PD-L1-deficient macrophages ( Figure 312 

4). These findings suggest that PD-L1 and PD-L2 may be coordinately regulated rather than 313 

functionally compensatory, raising the possibility that PD-L1 contributes to the maintenance of 314 

PD-L2 surface levels. Further studies are needed to define the mechanisms underlying this 315 

relationship. 316 

When comparing changes in MHCII surface levels, we observed opposing effects of PD-317 

L1 genetic deletion and antibody-mediated blockade, with MHCII surface levels increasing 318 

following PD-L1 knockout but decreasing following PD-L1 blockade ( Figure 4 ). This 319 

divergence is notable given the concurrent alterations in other molecules involved in T-cell 320 

communication, including CD80 and PD-L2. Together, our data indicated that genetic deletion 321 

and antibody-mediated blockade of PD-L1 do not produce equivalent effects on macrophage 322 

phenotype. These findings suggest that PD-L1 interference remodels the macrophage: T cell 323 

interaction landscape through coordinated changes in antigen presentation, co-stimulation, and 324 

checkpoint signaling pathways. 325 

Although the specific effects of PD-L1 deletion and blockade differed across molecules, 326 

the most substantial changes were consistently observed in antigen presentation, co-stimulation, 327 

and checkpoint signaling. This is a surprising result when considering the prior literature 328 

suggested the largest changes would be seen in canonical polarization markers. These findings 329

Conclusions 340 

The importance of macrophage PD-L1 in the tumor microenvironment continues to expand 341 

as the functions of both macrophages and PD-L1 are increasingly appreciated. Here, we 342 

demonstrate that genetic deletion and antibody-mediated blockade of PD-L1 produce distinct 343 

effects on macrophage phenotype. These findings support a cell-intrinsic role for PD-L1 in 344 

macrophages that extends beyond its canonical function as a ligand for PD-1 on T cells. Further 345 

investigation of the mechanisms by which PD-L1 regulates macrophage function may provide 346 

new opportunities to modulate the tumor microenvironment and improve the efficacy of 347 

immunotherapeutic strategies. 348 

Limitations 349 

Our model was refined to one Tumor Conditioned Media E0771, while overlapping in 350 

some methodology with previous work done in the lab (Kwok et al., 2025), using the B16 351 

melanoma model, some results differed. This suggests a highly tumor type dependent regulation 352 

of macrophage function. To further investigate PD-L1 functions in macrophages the ability to 353 

reintroduce PD-L1 expression in PD-L1 knockout backgrounds would add strength to these 354 

analyses. Because the PD-L1 KO model used is missing PD-L1 expression throughout 355 

maturation of monocytes to macrophages, there could be differences in macrophage maturation 356 

that would not be caused by blockade after differentiation. Reintroducing PD-L1 consructs could 357 

help parse out any differences due to PD-L1 loss before differentiation. Macrophage 358 

phagocytosis is regulated by diverse pathways which require multiple methods of analysis to 359 

clarify. Here we analyzed the effects of PD-L1 interference on a single phagocytic pathway. Our 360 

analysis does not include the effects of PD-L1 on phagocytosis of opsonized targets or apoptotic 361 

cells, but points to a general fitness in a core macrophage function. 362

The evidence we have gathered suggests that direct contact between PD-L1 and CD80 is 364 

important for maintaining CD80 expression levels on the cell surface. Previous studies have 365 

demonstrated the importance of PD-L1 and CD80 cis-interactions for dendritic cell migration 366 

and that the intracellular tail of PD-L1 is important for this interaction (39). However, it has not 367 

been clarified whether PD-L1 simply stabilizes CD80 on the cell surface or plays a role in its 368 

stabilization and trafficking to the cell surface. Further studies are needed to clarify this 369 

interaction. The loss of PD-L2 surface expression suggests a positive regulation of PD-L2 by 370

Mice/Cell Culture 377 

Wildtype C57Bl/6J mice were purchased from The Jackson Laboratories and PD-L1 -/- C57Bl/6 378 

mice were generated and bred in-house(30). Mice were bred and maintained under specific-379

Mayo Clinic animal facility. Female mice 6-10 weeks of age were used in experiments in 381 

accordance with protocols approved by the Mayo Clinic Institutional Animal Care and Use 382 

Committee (AUP #A00005857-21-R24). 383 

E0771 murine triple negative breast cancer cells (ATCC #CRL-3461) were cultured under 384 

normoxic conditions at 37°C, 5% CO 2 in Dulbecco's Modified Eagle's Medium (DMEM; 385 

Thermofisher), 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS; Thermofisher). 386 

Naïve, M1, and TCM macrophages were treated with anti-PD-L1 (Clone 10F.9G2 BioXcell 387 

BE0101) or isotype matched (IgG2b LTF-2 BioXcell BE0090) at 20ug/mL during polarization 388 

(48hrs or 96hrs) and after polarization (48hrs) depending on experimental conditions. 389 

Generation of bone-marrow derived macrophages (BMDM) 390 

BMDMs were generated using a modified version of the protocol described by (25,30). Bone 391 

marrow from the femur and tibiae of C57Bl/6J or PDL1 -/- C57Bl/6J mice were flushed out with 392 

serum free DMEM and cultured at densities between 2x10^6 and 4x10^6 per mL on 10cm, 6-393 

well, or 12-well plates in DMEM supplemented with FBS (10%), Penicillin-Streptomycin (1%), 394 

and 20 ng/ml of M-CSF (Peprotech) for 6 days. Fresh media was replaced every 3 days. M0 395 

macrophages were cultured in DMEM supplemented with M-CSF (20 ng/ml). Tumor-396 

conditioned macrophages (TCMs) were generated by culturing macrophages in DMEM 397 

supplemented with M-CSF (20 ng/ml) and 20 ng/ml of IL-4 and IL-10 (Peprotech), and E0771 398 

tumor conditioned media. M1 macrophages were generated by culturing macrophages in DMEM 399 

supplemented with M-CSF (20 ng/ml, Peprotech), 10 ng/ml of LPS (Sigma Aldrich), and 20 400 

ng/ml of IFNγ (Peprotech). Macrophages were polarized for 48 hours then detached with 401 

Versene (Thermofisher), washed with PBS, then used for downstream applications such as flow 402 

cytometry, western blots, and imaging. 403

hours at 10x magnification. After the 3 hour analysis with the incucyte, cells were washed and 421 

detached for flow cytometry analysis. 422

2.5 x 10 6 Bone marrow cells were plated and differentiated in 6-well plates. Once differentiated, 424 

they were polarized to M1 and TCM states and WT BMDMs treated with anti-PD-L1 antibody 425 

or Isotype controls. Cells were detached using trypsin for 5-10 minutes and stained for viability. 426 

They were then fixed using the FOXP3 Fixation/Permeabilization kit and stained for Ki67 for 427 

20min at 4°C. They were then washed and stained with DAPI for 3-5 minutes at room 428 

temperature. They were then analyzed using a BD Symphony cytometer. 429 

Western Blot 430 

Whole cell lysates were prepared in RIPA buffer containing Halt protease inhibitors. Equal 431 

amounts of protein (30ug) were separated by SDS-PAGE, transferred to low fluorescence PVDF 432 

membranes using a turbotransfer system, and probed with antibodies against PD-L1 (MIH5 433 

eBioscience 14-5982-82) and GAPDH(14C10 Cell Signaling 2118). Blots were visualized using 434 

IRDye conjugated secondary antibodies using a LI-COR Odyssey Clx imaging system. 435
