# Spatial co-expression and cell-cell communication inference from spatially resolved transcriptomics with CONCISE

## Abstract 

Cell-cell communication is fundamental to tissue organization, homeostasis, and disease pro-1

gression. Recent advances in spatial transcriptomics provide unprecedented opportunities to 2

systematically characterize ligand-receptor interactions directly within intact tissues. However, 3

robust inference of spatial ligand-receptor interactions remains challenging because intrinsic 4

features of spatial transcriptomics data, including spatial autocorrelation, variation in total 5

molecular counts, and measurement errors, can induce spurious spatial co-expression and lead 6

to inflated false-positive results. Most existing methods do not adequately account for these 7

confounding factors, limiting the reliability of inferred cellular communication. Here, we present 8

CONCISE, a statistical method for spatially constrained co-expression and ligand-receptor 9

interaction inference that jointly models spatial autocorrelation, variation in total molecular 10 

counts, measurement errors, and spatial proximity constraints. CONCISE combines efficient 11 

moment-based parameter estimation with analytical hypothesis testing, enabling fast and sta-12 

tistically rigorous inference without restrictive distributional assumptions. Through extensive 13 

simulations, real-data permutation experiments, and biologically motivated negative-control 14

## Introduction 23 

Cell-cell interactions are fundamental to the organization of multicellular systems and play 24 

essential roles in diverse biological processes [ 1, 2], including tissue development [ 3], homeostatic 25 

regulation [ 4], and responses to stress and disease [ 5, 6]. A major form of these interactions 26 

is cell-cell communication (CCC), mainly mediated by ligand-receptor interactions (LRIs). 27 

In this process, ligands released by one cell bind to receptors on another cell to activate 28 

signaling pathways that coordinate gene expression and cellular functions [ 1, 2]. Identifying 29 

context-dependent LRIs holds great potential to elucidate disease mechanisms and inform 30 

therapeutic target discovery. 31 

Direct measurement of proteins mediating CCC remains technically challenging [ 1]. As a 32 

more accessible alternative, transcriptomic measurements of ligands and receptors from bulk 33 

and single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) have been widely used to study CCC across 34 

biological contexts [ 7, 8, 9, 10 , 11 ]. Many computational tools have been developed, including 35 

CellPhoneDB [ 12 ], SingleCellSignalR [ 13 ], CellChat [ 14 ], ICELLNET [ 15 ] and CytoTalk [ 16 ]. 36 

These methods infer LRIs by employing distinct scoring strategies to evaluate the co-expression 37 

of candidate ligands and receptors in transcriptomic datasets, leveraging curated LRI databases. 38 

Although these approaches have generated important biological insights, their non-spatial 39 

nature can lead to inflated false-positive rates, as CCC typically occurs between cells in close 40 

physical proximity [17]. 41 

Recent advances in spatial transcriptomics (ST) enable high-throughput, spatially resolved 42 

transcriptomic measurements in intact tissues, providing new opportunities for more accurate 43 

LRI inference. Many computational methods have been developed, aiming to identify LRIs from 44

COMMOT [ 23 ] further refine interaction characterization among L-R pairs by estimating 50 

directional spatial signal flow from ligands to receptors with optimal transport. Despite 51 

these advances, accurate identification of LRIs from ST datasets remains limited by intrinsic 52 

characteristics of ST data that, if not properly modeled, can generate spurious spatial co-53 

expression signals and consequently lead to false-positive discoveries. 54 

First, spatial autocorrelation is an intrinsic feature of ST data, whereby nearby spatial 55 

locations tend to display similar expression levels [ 24 , 25 ]. This property can substantially 56 

confound spatial co-expression analyses. As widely recognized in spatial statistics, assessing 57 

bivariate correlation in the presence of spatial autocorrelation can markedly underestimate 58 

uncertainty, often to varying degrees, thereby leading to spurious associations and inflated false-59 

positive rates if spatial autocorrelation is not properly accounted for [ 26 , 27 , 28 , 29 ]. Nevertheless, 60 

existing ST-based LRI inference methods incorporate spatial information primarily to restrict 61 

candidate LRIs to spatial nearby locations, but do not account for spatial autocorrelation 62 

in ligand and receptor expression themselves. In particular, SpatialDM assumes that gene 63 

expression is independent of spatial location in order to derive an analytical null distribution for 64 

LRI inference, whereas other methods rely on permutation-based tests for L-R co-expression, 65 

which implicitly depend on the same assumption. Consequently, existing LRI methods can 66 

be seriously confounded by varying levels of spatial autocorrelation in ligand and receptor 67 

expression. 68 

Second, additional properties of ST count data further challenge LRI inference. ST data 69 

exhibit varying total molecular counts across spatial locations, and expression counts are sparse 70 

and noisy [ 30 , 31 , 32 ]. If the count-based nature of the data, variation in total molecular 71 

counts, and measurement errors are not properly modeled, LRI inference can be distorted. 72 

Similar challenges have been recognized in scRNA-seq analysis, where ignoring these factors 73 

can substantially confound downstream analyses including gene-gene co-expression inference 74 

[33 , 34 , 35 ]. However, most existing ST-based LRI methods still rely on CPM normalization 75

Here, we introduce CONCISE, a principled statistical method for co -expressio n and cell-cell 84 

communication inference from s patially-r esolved transcriptomics. Through innovations in model 85 

and algorithm design, CONCISE addresses the aforementioned challenges within a unified 86 

statistical framework. Specifically, CONCISE introduces the following key methodological 87 

advances for spatial CCC analysis. First, CONCISE models the unobserved true spatial 88 

expression levels as latent variables and links them to the observed count data through a 89 

statistical measurement model. This explicitly accounts for the count-based nature of ST 90 

data, variation in total molecular counts, and measurement errors. Second, by treating the 91 

latent expression levels as spatial processes, CONCISE jointly models ligand and receptor 92 

expression through a spatial process model. This enables CONCISE to infer L-R co-expression 93 

under spatial proximity constraints while adaptively accounting for different levels of spatial 94 

autocorrelation in ligand and receptor expression, mitigating false-positive discoveries arising 95 

from spatial autocorrelation. Third, CONCISE proposes an efficient moment-based approach 96 

for inferring spatial LRIs. This approach avoids imposing a restrictive distributional family for 97 

the underlying expression levels and can flexibly accommodate the data-generating process. 98 

Importantly, with this approach, CONCISE explicitly derives the analytical null distribution, 99 

enabling fast and principled statistical testing of spatial LRIs under the proposed model, thereby 100 

facilitating efficient and reliable CCC analysis from ST datasets. 101 

To evaluate the performance of CONCISE, we conducted comprehensive simulation and 102

calibrated p-values for spatial co-expression inference, thereby effectively controlling type I error 104

utility of CONCISE by applying it to multiple ST datasets, including human breast cancer [ 36 ], 106

Method overview. 121 

CONCISE infers CCC from context-dependent ST data by identifying L-R pairs whose ex-122

inference, CONCISE introduces a unified measurement-expression modeling framework that 124 

accounts for key confounding factors in ST data, including variation in total molecular counts 125 

across spots or cells, measurement errors in count observations, and spatial autocorrelation 126 

in gene expression (Fig. 1). Explicitly modeling these factors is critical to avoid spurious 127 

co-expression signals that may lead to false-positive LRI discoveries. 128

yp1 and yp2 using a measurement model (Fig. 1 b, panel b1 ) [ 34 , 40 ]. In this model, latent 132 

variables xp1 and xp2 represent the underlying true expression levels of the ligand and receptor, 133 

and the observed counts depend on these latent levels as well as the total molecular counts 134 

{si}i. This formulation captures the count-based nature of ST data, separates the underlying 135

We next considered spatially proximal co-expression analysis, the setting essential for spatial 197 

LRI inference. Many existing methods detect LRIs using statistics derived from bivariate 198 

Moran’s I [19 , 21 ]. To examine the implications of spatial autocorrelation in this context, we 199 

compared a bivariate Moran’s I-based approach with CONCISE. In the Moran’s I approach, 200 

spatially proximal co-expression is quantified using bivariate Moran’s I and significance is 201 

assessed using permutation-based p-values. When at least one of the variables is free from 202 

spatial autocorrelation, both approaches produced consistent co-expression estimates and 203 

well-calibrated statistical inference. With 1,000 permutations for Moran’s I, the two methods 204 

also yielded comparable uncertainty quantification (Fig. 2d, e; Supplementary Fig. 2a,205 

b). However, when both variables exhibit spatial autocorrelation, the Moran’s I approach 206 

substantially underestimated the uncertainty (Fig. 2 f). This underestimation again results 207 

in spurious signals and inflated false-positive discoveries. In contrast, by explicitly modeling 208 

spatial dependencies in the data, CONCISE provided reliable uncertainty quantification and 209 

reduced false-positive discoveries (Supplementary Fig. 2 c). 210

To assess false positive rate control in spatial LRI inference, we conducted permutation-based 225 

analyses on the breast cancer Visium dataset [ 36 ] to generate null datasets in which spatially 226 

proximal co-expression is absent. Using these data, we evaluated type I error control of 227 

CONCISE and benchmarked its performance against representative state-of-the-art methods, 228 

including MERINGUE [18], SpatialDM [19], Copulacci [20], and LIANA+ [21]. 229 

To systematically characterize the confounding effects of varying total molecular counts, 230 

measurement errors, and spatial autocorrelation on spatial LRI inference, we focused on genes 231 

showing spatial autocorrelation (Fig. 2 g) and designed two experimental scenarios to disentangle 232 

these effects (Fig. 3 a). In Scenario 1, variation in total molecular counts and measurement 233 

errors inherent to the data were preserved, while confounding effect from spatial autocorrelation 234 

was removed. In Scenario 2, spatial autocorrelation was reintroduced to quantify its additional 235 

confounding impact. 236 

In Scenario 1, for each randomly sampled gene pair, we retained the raw counts of one 237 

gene and permuted the expression of the other across spatial locations to remove spatial 238 

co-expression. Specifically, counts were first normalized by total molecular counts at each 239 

location and then permuted across locations, after which count data were regenerated using the 240 

original total molecular counts. This procedure preserves variation in total molecular counts 241 

and measurement errors while eliminating spatial autocorrelation in one gene from each pair. 242 

Indicated by the simulation study (Fig. 2; Supplementary Fig. 2), spatial LRI inference under 243 

this setting is not confounded by spatial autocorrelation. Under this null setting, CONCISE 244 

uniquely achieved well-controlled type I error rates (Fig. 3c) and well-calibrated p-values 245

To further demonstrate the ability of CONCISE to uncover disease-associated CCC, we applied 482 

it to a MERFISH section from a dextran-sodium-sulfate (DSS)-induced mouse colitis model at 483 

peak inflammation [ 38 ]. This mouse model recapitulates key features of inflammatory bowel 484

remains an unmet medical challenge [ 72 , 73 ]. By capturing the cellular composition and spatial 486 

organization of the inflamed gut, it provides an opportunity to investigate how CCC shapes 487 

inflammatory microenvironment and tissue remodeling. Understanding these communication 488 

programs may provide insights into IBD pathogenesis and identify potential therapeutic targets. 489

## Discussion 602 

In this paper, we presented CONCISE, a statistically principled framework for spatially 603 

constrained co-expression and LRI inference from spatial transcriptomics data. By jointly 604 

accounting for spatial autocorrelation, heterogeneity in total molecular counts, measurement 605 

errors, and spatial proximity constraints within a unified modeling framework, while avoiding 606 

restrictive assumptions on the underlying expression distributions, CONCISE enables robust and 607 

rigorous inference of spatial CCC. Through an analytically derived null distribution, CONCISE 608 

further supports computationally efficient statistical testing without relying on computationally 609

Importantly, our study highlights several fundamental challenges underlying spatial CCC 614

fundamental concept in spatial statistics. Research in spatial statistics has established that 630

and better identification of biologically meaningful spatial interactions. 637
