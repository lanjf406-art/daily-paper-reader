Abstract 1

Resistance to PD-1–based immunotherapy remains a major challenge in cancer treatment, especially 2

in immunologically “cold” tumors characterized by T-cell exclusion and impaired effector function. 3

Tumor-associated myeloid cells are important mediators of this resistance, but whether they directly 4

suppress T-cell immunity through mechanical regulation remains unclear. Here, we identify DC-SIGN 5

(CD209), a myeloid-expressed C-type lectin receptor, as a mechanosuppressive regulator of antitumor 6

T-cell immunity. DC-SIGN is selectively enriched in tumor-infiltrating M2-like macrophages and 7

monocytes, and its expression is associated with poor clinical response to PD-1 blockade and elevated 8

TIDE scores across multiple cancer types. Mechanistically, DC-SIGN directly engages ICAM-2 on T 9

cells and activates ROCK1–ERM signaling. This interaction increases T-cell cortical stiffness, 10 

destabilizes T cell–antigen-presenting cell interactions, and attenuates T-cell receptor signaling. 11 

Blocking the DC-SIGN–ICAM-2–ERM pathway restores T-cell activation and promotes CD8+ T-cell 12 

infiltration and effector function in mouse, humanized, and ex vivo human tumor systems. Importantly, 13 

DC-SIGN blockade overcomes resistance to PD-1–based immunotherapy in refractory and 14 

immunologically “cold” tumor models, and further enhances the response to PD-1 inhibition. Together, 15 

our findings define a myeloid-derived mechanical immune checkpoint and reveal T-cell 16 

mechanosuppression as a targetable mechanism of tumor immune evasion and immunotherapy 17 

resistance. 18

Introduction 1

Immune checkpoint blockade targeting the PD-1/PD-L1 axis has transformed cancer therapy. However, 2

most patients with solid tumors still fail to achieve durable responses, particularly those with 3

immunologically “cold” tumors marked by limited T-cell infiltration and impaired effector function(1-4

3). These limitations suggest that suppressive mechanisms beyond canonical PD-1/PD-L1 signaling 5

continue to constrain productive antitumor T-cell immunity. Tumor-associated myeloid cells are major 6

contributors to this resistance. They restrict T-cell infiltration, activation, and function through 7

inhibitory ligands, cytokines, metabolic competition, and stromal remodeling(4-7). However, how 8

myeloid cells directly impose suppressive constraints on T cells, especially in PD-1–refractory and T-9

cell-excluded tumors, remains incompletely understood. 10 

11 

Most established immune checkpoints are viewed as biochemical receptor–ligand pathways. These 12 

pathways attenuate intracellular signaling in T cells and thereby limit TCR signaling, cytokine 13 

production, proliferation, and effector differentiation(8-15). By contrast, it remains largely unclear 14 

whether tumor-associated myeloid cells can suppress T-cell activation through contact-dependent 15 

mechanical regulation. Productive T-cell activation requires not only antigen recognition and 16 

costimulatory signaling, but also stable engagement with antigen-presenting cells, sustained 17 

immunological synapse formation, and dynamic remodeling of the actin cortex(16-19). These 18 

mechanical processes control T-cell spreading, receptor organization, signaling-zone stability, and the 19 

duration of T cell–APC interactions. Thus, perturbations in T-cell cortical mechanics may represent a 20 

distinct mode of immune suppression. This mode may operate in parallel with, or independently of, 21 

canonical inhibitory checkpoint signaling. Defining such mechanisms may reveal previously 22 

unrecognized myeloid-derived checkpoints and provide therapeutic opportunities for tumors that 23 

remain resistant to PD-1–based immunotherapy. 24 

25 

DC-SIGN, also known as CD209, is a C-type lectin receptor expressed by dendritic cells, monocytes, 26 

and macrophages(20,21). It regulates cell–cell adhesion and immune-cell trafficking through 27 

interactions with ICAM family members, including ICAM-3 and ICAM-2, and serves as an attachment 28 

receptor for diverse pathogens(22-28). In cancer, DC-SIGN ⁺ myeloid cells are associated with 29

immunosuppressive tumor microenvironments, impaired antitumor immunity, poor clinical outcomes, 1

and reduced responsiveness to immunotherapy(29-34). Previous studies have linked DC-SIGN ⁺ tumor-2

associated macrophages to CD8⁺ T-cell dysfunction. Tumor-derived ligands, including CEA, 3

CEACAM1, and Mac-2BP, can also engage DC-SIGN to suppress dendritic-cell function(30-32). 4

However, whether DC-SIGN directly acts on T cells, and whether its adhesive interactions with ICAM 5

family members impose mechanical constraints on T-cell activation, remains unknown. 6

7

Here, we identify DC-SIGN as a myeloid-derived mechanosuppressive regulator that directly restrains 8

antitumor T-cell immunity. DC-SIGN engages ICAM-2 on T cells and activates ROCK1–ERM 9

signaling. This pathway increases T-cell cortical stiffness, destabilizes T cell–antigen-presenting cell 10 

interactions, and attenuates T-cell receptor signaling. Disruption of the DC-SIGN–ICAM-2–ERM axis 11 

restores T-cell activation, enhances CD8⁺ T -cell infiltration and effector function, and promotes 12 

antitumor immunity across mouse, humanized, and ex vivo human tumor systems. DC-SIGN blockade 13 

also overcomes resistance to PD-1–based immunotherapy in refractory and immunologically “cold” 14 

tumors, including ovarian cancer and glioblastoma. Analyses of human tumors show that elevated DC-15 

SIGN expression is associated with CD8⁺ T -cell exclusion, poor clinical outcome, and resistance to 16 

PD-1 blockade. Together, these findings define a myeloid-driven mechanical immune checkpoint and 17 

establish T-cell cortical mechanics as a targetable mechanism of tumor immune evasion. 18

Results 1

Myeloid CD209/DC-SIGN is associated with immunosuppressive tumor microenvironments and 2

resistance to PD-1 blockade 3

To identify myeloid-derived mechanisms associated with resistance to PD-1 blockade, we analyzed 4

single-cell transcriptomes of surgically resected NSCLC tumors from 25 patients treated with 5

neoadjuvant chemotherapy plus anti–PD-1 therapy (GSE243013) (35) (Fig. 1A). Among these patients, 6

9 achieved a major pathological response (MPR), whereas 16 did not. Because tumor-associated 7

myeloid cells are key regulators of immune evasion, we focused on the myeloid compartment and 8

compared macrophages from non-MPR and MPR tumors. This analysis identified a set of genes 9

preferentially upregulated in macrophages from non-MPR tumors (Fig. 1B). Among the genes 10 

upregulated in macrophages from non-MPR tumors, several macrophage-enriched molecules, 11 

including MERTK, FOLR2, CCL8, ENPP2, OLFML3, and PDGFB, have previously been reported to 12 

participate in TAM-mediated immunosuppressive tumor microenvironments through promoting 13 

suppressive cytokine production, T-cell dysfunction, stromal remodeling, angiogenesis, immune 14 

evasion, and resistance to immune checkpoint blockade(11,36-41). Intersecting the genes upregulated 15 

in macrophages with a curated human membrane-associated gene set yielded six candidate surface 16 

molecules (Fig. 1C). Further prioritization using TIDE-based T-cell dysfunction risk scoring identified 17 

CD209 as the top-ranked candidate, nominating it as a potential myeloid-associated checkpoint linked 18 

to anti–PD-1 resistance (Fig. 1D). 19 

20 

We next examined the cellular distribution of CD209 within the same NSCLC cohort. CD209 21 

expression was largely restricted to myeloid cells and was highest in M2-like macrophages (Fig. 1E– 22 

H). Notably, CD209 expression in M2-like macrophages and monocytes was significantly elevated in 23 

non-MPR tumors compared with MPR tumors, whereas no significant difference was observed in 24 

dendritic cells or M1-like macrophages (Fig. 1H). Receiver operating characteristic analysis further 25 

showed that CD209 expression in M2-like macrophages and monocytes discriminated nonresponders 26 

from responders to PD-1–based therapy (Fig. 1I–J), supporting its potential association with 27 

therapeutic resistance. Consistently, CD209⁺ cell abundance was significantly higher in an independent 28 

cohort of PD-1–refractory lung cancer patients than in responders (Fig. S1A–C). 29

To further define the cellular distribution of CD209 within the tumor microenvironment, we analyzed 2

single-cell RNA-sequencing data generated from paired tumor and adjacent normal tissues in our 3

laboratory. Unsupervised clustering identified 18 major immune cell populations based on established 4

lineage markers. CD209 expression was predominantly enriched in immunosuppressive M2-like 5

macrophages and monocytes, but was largely absent from M1-like macrophages (Fig. S1D–F). 6

Compared with adjacent tissues, tumors showed significantly increased CD209 expression in M2-like 7

macrophages and monocytes, whereas CD209 expression in dendritic cells was reduced (Fig. S1F). 8

This pattern is consistent with previous studies linking CD209⁺ myeloid cells to tumor immune evasion 9

and poor clinical outcomes (21,29,31,32). Analysis of an independent LUAD single-cell RNA-10 

sequencing dataset (GSE194070) further confirmed the preferential enrichment of CD209 in tumor-11 

infiltrating M2-like macrophages and monocytes (42) (Fig. S1G–I). Together, these data indicate that 12 

CD209 is selectively upregulated in immunosuppressive myeloid populations within the tumor 13 

microenvironment, supporting its potential involvement in myeloid-mediated immune suppression. 14 

15 

To evaluate the broader clinical relevance of CD209, we performed pan-cancer analyses and found 16 

that elevated CD209 expression correlated with increased TIDE scores across multiple tumor types, 17 

including lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD), ovarian cancer, glioma, melanoma, prostate adenocarcinoma 18 

(PRAD), breast invasive carcinoma (BRCA), head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSC) and 19 

uterine corpus endometrial carcinoma (UCEC) (Fig. 1K–N and Fig. S1J–N). Together, these findings 20 

nominate CD209 as a myeloid-associated marker of dysfunctional antitumor immunity and resistance 21 

to PD-1–based immunotherapy, raising the possibility that CD209 may functionally contribute to 22 

therapeutic resistance and serve as a candidate biomarker for patient stratification. 23

assessed its direct effects on T-cell activation. Recombinant human CD209-Fc bound efficiently to 27 

Jurkat T cells as well as primary murine CD4⁺ and CD8⁺ T cells ( Fig. 2A–B), suggesting the presence 28 

of conserved receptors across species. Functionally, plate-bound CD209-Fc markedly inhibited T-cell 29

To determine whether targeting CD209 has therapeutic potential, we generated a neutralizing 7

monoclonal antibody against human CD209 that effectively reversed CD209-mediated suppression of 8

T-cell activation in both human and mouse systems (Fig. S2A–D). We next established a human CD209 9

knock-in mouse model (hCD209-KI), in which human CD209 is expressed under the endogenous 10 

mouse cd209 promoter (Fig. 2D). Therapeutically, CD209 blockade significantly inhibited tumor 11 

growth across multiple models. In MC38 tumor–bearing hCD209-KI mice, anti-CD209 treatment 12 

markedly suppressed tumor progression compared with control (Fig. 2E). Notably, in the PD-1– 13 

refractory “cold” LLC model, CD209 blockade remained highly effective, resulting in pronounced 14 

tumor growth inhibition (Fig. 2F). In addition, CD209 blockade significantly reduced metastatic 15 

burden in B16 hepatic and pulmonary metastasis models (Fig. S2E). Collectively, these results 16 

demonstrate that targeting CD209 elicits potent antitumor activity in vivo , including in 17 

immunotherapy-resistant tumor settings. 18 

19 

A major limitation of immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) is its poor efficacy in immunologically “cold” 20 

tumors such as ovarian cancer and glioblastoma(44,45). We therefore evaluated whether CD209 21 

blockade is effective in these settings. In a murine ovarian cancer model, anti-CD209 treatment 22 

markedly reduced ascites accumulation and abdominal distension compared with isotype controls (Fig. 23 

2G). We next assessed the therapeutic potential of CD209 blockade in an orthotopic glioblastoma 24 

model. Because antibodies do not efficiently cross the blood–brain barrier (BBB), we employed 25 

focused ultrasound to transiently open the BBB and facilitate antibody delivery(46,47). Fluorescence 26 

imaging confirmed efficient intracerebral accumulation of FITC-labeled anti-CD209 antibodies 27 

following ultrasound treatment (Fig. 2H–I). In this model, CD209 blockade significantly prolonged 28 

survival and reduced weight loss compared with control treatment (Fig. 2J–L). Notably, CD209 29

CD209 blockade restores CD8⁺ T cell –mediated antitumor immunity by relieving myeloid-9

mediated suppression 10 

To investigate the cellular mechanisms underlying the therapeutic effects of CD209 blockade, we 11 

performed single-cell RNA sequencing of MC38 tumors from hCD209-KI mice. Unsupervised 12 

clustering identified 12 major cell populations, with a significant increase in the relative abundance of 13 

T cells following CD209 blockade (Fig. 3A–C, Fig. S3A, S3D). Re-clustering of the T-cell 14 

compartment revealed eight transcriptionally distinct subsets, among which CD8⁺ effector T cells were 15 

markedly expanded (Fig. 3D–F, Fig. S3B, S3E), suggesting a central role for this population in 16 

mediating the antitumor response. Analysis of the myeloid compartment identified seven macrophage 17 

subsets, and CD209 blockade selectively reduced the abundance of M2-like macrophages without 18 

affecting other populations (Fig. 3G–I, Fig. S3C, S3F). Collectively, these findings show that CD209 19 

blockade relieves myeloid-mediated suppression and promotes CD8⁺ T-cell–driven antitumor 20 

immunity. 21 

22

by a marked increase in IFN-γ–producing T-cell subsets and elevated systemic IFN-γ levels in serum 25 

and tumor culture supernatants (Fig. 3M–N, Fig. S3J–N), indicating enhanced effector function. 26 

Consistent with our single-cell RNA-seq data and human LUAD datasets, CD209⁺ monocytes and 27 

macrophages were enriched in tumors compared with spleens, whereas dendritic cells displayed 28 

reduced expression (Fig. S4A–B, Fig. S1F, Fig. S1I), supporting a conserved tumor-specific myeloid 29

ICAM-2 is the functional receptor mediating CD209-dependent T-cell suppression 14 

To identify candidate receptors mediating CD209-dependent T-cell inhibition, we performed 15 

immunoprecipitation–mass spectrometry using recombinant CD209-Fc as bait in Jurkat T-cell 16 

membrane lysates. This analysis identified multiple transmembrane proteins interacting with CD209, 17 

among which ICAM-3—previously reported to bind CD209 and regulate T cell–dendritic cell 18 

interactions—emerged as a candidate (Fig. 4A) (48). However, genetic deletion of ICAM-3 in Jurkat 19 

cells did not impair the ability of CD209 to suppress TCR-mediated activation (Fig. S5A–B), 20 

demonstrating that ICAM-3 is not required for CD209-mediated inhibition. 21 

22 

To identify the functional receptor responsible for CD209-mediated inhibition, we examined ICAM-23 

2, a previously reported CD209 ligand(49) , which was also recovered in our IP–MS screen (Fig. 4A). 24 

Direct interaction between hCD209 and hICAM-2 was confirmed by reciprocal co-25 

immunoprecipitation (Fig. 4B). Strikingly, CRISPR-mediated deletion of ICAM-2 in Jurkat cells 26 

completely abrogated CD209-mediated suppression, as evidenced by full restoration of TCR-induced 27 

phosphorylation of LCK, ERK, and I κBα, as well as IL-2 production (Fig. 4C–E). These findings 28 

establish ICAM-2 as the essential receptor through which CD209 inhibits T-cell activation. 29

Structurally, CD209 forms tetramers via its neck domain(50), whereas ICAM-2 exists predominantly 7

interface involving five CD209 residues (Fig. 4H). Alanine substitution of these residues (CD209-5A) 9

activation, confirming that this interface is functionally required (Fig. 4I–K). Collectively, these 11 

findings establish ICAM-2 as the obligate receptor mediating CD209-dependent suppression of T-cell 12 

activation. 13

Anti-CD209 antibodies inhibit CD209 function by competitively disrupting ICAM-2 engagement 15

incompletely defined role in T-cell biology. To assess its relevance in the tumor microenvironment, we 17 

examined ICAM-2 expression on T-cell subsets. Flow-cytometric analysis revealed that ICAM-2 is 18 

expressed at significantly higher levels on CD8⁺ than on CD4⁺ T cells in both murine tumor models 19 

and human lung adenocarcinoma specimens (Fig. S5D–E ), suggesting a preferential role in CD8⁺ T -20 

cell regulation. 21

CD209–ICAM-2 signaling impairs T cell–APC interactions via ROCK1–ERM–mediated 4

mechano-regulation 5

ERM (ezrin/radixin/moesin) proteins link the plasma membrane to the actin cytoskeleton by binding 6

the cytoplasmic tail of ICAM-2 via their N-terminal FERM domains and anchoring F-actin through 7

their C-terminal domains, thereby regulating cortical rigidity and leukocyte deformability(51-53). 8

Previous studies have shown that ICAM-2 cross-linking induces ERM activation in T cells. Consistent 9

with this, engagement of Jurkat cells with surface-bound hCD209-Fc induced robust ERM 10 

phosphorylation at the plasma membrane, an effect that was largely abolished in ICAM-2–deficient 11 

cells (Fig. 5A–C), indicating that CD209 activates ERM signaling in an ICAM-2–dependent manner. 12 

Given the role of ERM proteins in regulating cortical mechanics, we next assessed T-cell deformability. 13 

High-speed centrifugation followed by 3D reconstruction revealed that CD209 engagement 14 

significantly increased cellular stiffness, as reflected by a reduced deformability index (DI). In contrast, 15 

ICAM-2–deficient cells exhibited comparable deformability under control and CD209-Fc conditions 16 

(Fig. 5D–G). Together, these findings demonstrate that CD209–ICAM-2 ligation enhances T-cell 17 

cortical rigidity through ERM activation, providing a mechanistic basis for CD209-mediated 18 

suppression of T-cell function. 19 

20 

Cortical rigidity is a key determinant of stable T cell–antigen-presenting cell (APC) interactions 21 

required for efficient TCR activation(53). We therefore examined whether CD209-mediated increases 22 

in T-cell stiffness affect T cell–APC conjugation. Jurkat cells exposed to plate-bound CD209-Fc 23 

exhibited a marked reduction in superantigen-dependent APC coupling, whereas ICAM-2–deficient 24 

cells maintained normal APC contact following CD209 engagement (Fig. 5H–I, Fig. S5I), indicating 25 

that CD209–ICAM-2 ligation destabilizes T cell–APC interactions. This effect was dependent on ERM 26 

activation. CD209 significantly impaired APC contact in cells expressing wild-type ezrin, but not in 27 

cells expressing either the phospho-deficient ezrin-T567A or the constitutively active ezrin-T567D 28 

mutant (Fig. 5J–K), demonstrating a requirement for regulated ezrin phosphorylation. 29

CD209 blockade mediates antitumor immunity independently of Fc effector functions 8

To determine whether the therapeutic effects of CD209 blockade depend on Fc-mediated cytotoxicity, 9

we generated a humanized anti-CD209 IgG4 antibody ( αhCD209-hIgG4; KD = 2.7 × 10⁻⁹ M) lacking 10 

Fc effector activity (Fig. S6A–B). Notably, despite elimination of ADCC, αhCD209-hIgG4 fully 11 

recapitulated the antitumor efficacy of the parental IgG2a antibody across multiple tumor models. In 12 

MC38 tumors, treatment significantly suppressed tumor growth and reduced tumor weight while 13 

increasing intratumoral IFN-γ⁺ CD8⁺ T cells ( Fig. S6C–E). Similar effects were observed in LLC 14 

tumors, with reduced tumor burden and increased IFN-γ⁺ CD4⁺ and CD8⁺ T -cell populations (Fig. 15 

S6F–H). Importantly, in immunologically “cold” models, including ID8 ovarian carcinoma and 16 

orthotopic GL261 glioblastoma, αhCD209-hIgG4 remained highly effective, reducing ascites 17 

accumulation and prolonging survival while maintaining body weight (Fig. S6I–O). Collectively, these 18 

findings demonstrate that the antitumor activity of CD209 blockade is independent of Fc-mediated 19 

effector functions and is driven primarily by direct inhibition of CD209 signaling. 20 

21 

CD209 blockade elicits antitumor immunity in human tumor systems and overcomes resistance 22 

to PD-1–based immunotherapy 23 

We next assessed the translational potential of CD209 blockade in human tumor systems. Ex vivo 24

Discussion 2

This study identifies a myeloid-derived mechanosuppressive checkpoint that restrains antitumor T-cell 3

immunity through physical modulation of T-cell cortical mechanics. We show that CD209 engages 4

ICAM-2 on T cells to activate a ROCK1–ERM–dependent mechanotransduction pathway, leading to 5

increased cortical rigidity, impaired T cell–antigen-presenting cell interactions, and attenuated TCR 6

signaling. Functionally, disruption of CD209 signaling restores T-cell infiltration and effector function 7

and elicits antitumor immunity across multiple tumor models, including immunologically “cold” and 8

PD-1–refractory tumors. These findings establish CD209 as a mechanistic link between suppressive 9

myeloid cells, T-cell exclusion and resistance to PD-1–based immunotherapy, a major barrier to 10 

effective cancer treatment (Fig. 7G )(5,8,56).  Consistent with this model, CD209⁺ myeloid -cell 11 

infiltration has been associated with poor immunotherapy response and adverse clinical 12 

outcomes (29,34) . Collectively, our work defines CD209 as a myeloid-driven mechanical immune 13 

checkpoint and provides a rationale for targeting the CD209–ICAM-2–ERM axis to overcome T-cell 14 

dysfunction and immunotherapy resistance in cancer. 15 

16 

ICAM-2, a member of the immunoglobulin superfamily, is primarily expressed on endothelial cells 17 

and platelets and has been implicated in leukocyte adhesion and migration, including LFA-1– 18 

dependent transendothelial trafficking(57,58) . It has also been reported to promote T-cell survival via 19 

AKT signaling (51).  In contrast to these established roles, our study identifies ICAM-2 as a previously 20 

unrecognized negative regulator of T-cell activation, functioning through a mechanical mechanism that 21 

increases cortical stiffness. Although ICAM-2 has not been widely explored as an immunotherapeutic 22 

target, prior studies demonstrated that an anti–ICAM-2 monoclonal antibody (EOL4G8) exerts 23 

antitumor effects in murine colon carcinoma models in a CD8⁺ T cell –dependent and LFA-1– 24 

independent manner (59,60).  Moreover, combination therapy with EOL4G8 and IL-12 further 25 

enhances tumor regression and is associated with increased ICAM-2 expression on activated CD8⁺ T 26 

cells (60).  These observations are consistent with our findings and raise the possibility that therapeutic 27 

targeting of ICAM-2 may disrupt CD209–ICAM-2–mediated mechanosuppression, thereby restoring 28 

effective T cell–APC interactions. This mechanistic link warrants further investigation. Notably, we 29

observed that human CD209 can engage both human and murine ICAM-2, and that neutralizing anti-1

CD209 antibodies effectively disrupt these interactions across species. These findings support the 2

conservation of the CD209–ICAM-2 axis and validate the use of the hCD209 knock-in mouse model 3

as a physiologically relevant platform for studying CD209-mediated immunoregulation in vivo. 4

5

Cellular mechanics has emerged as an important regulator of antitumor immunity, although prior 6

studies have primarily focused on tumor-intrinsic stiffness or extracellular matrix–driven mechanical 7

cues. For example, soft cancer cells enriched in membrane cholesterol can resist T-cell–mediated 8

killing, whereas extracellular matrix stiffening activates ROCK–myosin II–F-actin signaling in tumor 9

cells to suppress dendritic cell maturation and CD8⁺ T -cell priming (61-63).  Our study extends this 10 

paradigm by showing that mechanical immune regulation can also be imposed directly by myeloid 11 

cells onto T cells. Specifically, CD209 expressed by tumor-associated myeloid cells engages ICAM-2 12 

on T cells to activate ERM-dependent cortical remodeling, thereby increasing T-cell stiffness, 13 

destabilizing T cell–APC interactions and suppressing T-cell activation. These findings reveal 14 

myeloid-driven mechanosuppression as a previously underappreciated mode of immune checkpoint 15 

function, distinct from but potentially cooperating with canonical biochemical inhibitory pathways. 16 

17 

Together, these findings reveal a previously unrecognized mode of immune regulation in which 18 

myeloid cells impose mechanical constraints on T cells. By linking CD209–ICAM-2 engagement to 19 

ERM-dependent cortical stiffening, our study establishes cellular mechanics as an integral component 20 

of immune checkpoint function. Targeting this axis may enhance antitumor immunity in PD-1– 21 

refractory or T cell–excluded tumors, and T-cell mechanical properties may provide a functional 22 

biomarker for patient stratification. 23
