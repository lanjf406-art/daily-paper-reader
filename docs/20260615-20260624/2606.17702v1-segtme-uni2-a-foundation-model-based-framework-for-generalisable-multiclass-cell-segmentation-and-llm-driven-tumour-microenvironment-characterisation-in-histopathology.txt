A B S T R A C T 

Characterising the tumour microenvironment (TME) from routine haematoxylin and eosin (H&E)-stained histology images requires simultaneous cell segmentation, biological feature extraction, and interpretable clinical reporting. We present S EG TME-UNI2, a unified framework addressing all three requirements. At its core is UNI2-U PER HOVER , a dual-head segmentation model that pairs the UNI2-H pathology foundation model (ViT-Giant, pretrained on over 100 million tiles from 100,000 slides) with two parallel UperNet decoders: one for six-class semantic segmentation and one for horizontal-vertical (HV) gradient regression enabling watershed-based nuclear instance separation. To address the absence of pixel-level annotations on large real-world repositories, UNI2-U PER HOVER is trained via a three-stage progressive pseudo-label curriculum. Each stage trains a fresh model: Stage 1 uses human-annotated PanNuke (7,901 images, 189,744 nuclei at 0.25 𝜇 m/pixel); Stage 2 uses entropy-filtered pseudo-labels generated by the Stage 1 model on 271,711 TCGA-UT scale-0 patches (0.5 𝜇 m/pixel); Stage 3 uses pseudo-labels from the Stage 2 model on all 1,608,060 TCGA-UT patches across six resolution scales (0.5 to 1.0 𝜇 m/pixel). No weights are transferred between stages; improvement is driven entirely by increasing pseudo-label quality. Segmentation outputs feed a structured TME feature extraction pipeline computing 20+ per-patch compositional, morphological, spatial entropy, and intercellular distance metrics, encoded as JSON and passed to a fine-tuned NVIDIA BioNeMo GPT model for clinically interpretable TME narrative generation. Preliminary validation on held-out PanNuke and TCGA-UT partitions demonstrates framework feasibility and internal consistency. The pseudo-labelled TCGA-UT dataset and UNI2-U PER HOVER checkpoint are publicly released to support large-scale TME profiling and spatial biology research. 

## 1. Introduction 

Histopathological examination of haematoxylin and eosin (H&E)-stained tissue sections is the clinical gold standard for cancer diagnosis, staging, and treatment planning. Meaningful computational analysis of H&E images requires resolving two complementary and deeply coupled tasks: (i) semantic segmentation , assigning each pixel to a biologically meaningful cell-type category, and (ii) nuclear instance separation , delineating individual nuclei as discrete quantifiable objects. Together these outputs enable quantitative tumour microenvironment (TME) profiling, like tumour cellularity, immune infiltration fraction, nuclear morphology, and immune-to-tumour spatial proximity, which carry independent prognostic value across multiple cancer types [14, 9]. Existing computational tools address these tasks separately and each faces a fundamental bottleneck. Instance segmentation models such as HoVer-Net [6] require costly nucleus-level polygon annotations available only for small curated benchmarks such as PanNuke [5] (7,901 patches, 189,744 annotated nuclei, across 19 tissue types) and CoNSeP [6]. They do not encode broader tissue semantics and have narrow training distributions that do not generalise to the full diversity of TCGA. Semantic segmentation methods ( e.g. UperNet [20]) provide tissue-level context but

The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) repository contains digitised whole-slide images (WSIs) from more than 30,000 patients spanning 33 cancer types, yet remains entirely unannotated at the pixel level. The TCGA-UT Restructured subset used in this work covers 32 solid cancer types from 7,175 patients across 8,736 diagnostic slides, comprising 1,608,060 patches at six resolution scales. Bridging the gap between this breadth and the narrow scope of annotated benchmarks is the central practical challenge this work addresses. Large-scale pathology foundation models have recently made this bridge tractable. UNI2 [3], pretrained on over 100 million tiles from 100,000 slides across 40+ cancer types, provides representations with demonstrated cross-domain transfer across diverse downstream tasks. Crucially, UNI2’s ViT-Giant architecture is architecturally compatible with the attachment of dense-prediction decoder heads, enabling pixel-level segmentation without retraining the encoder. For the resolution shift from PanNuke (0.25 𝜇 m/pixel, high-resolution nuclei) to TCGA-UT (0.5 to 1.0 𝜇 m/pixel, richer tissue context per patch), UperNet’s pyramid pooling is the preferred decoder: its Feature Pyramid Network (FPN) structure aggregates features across multiple spatial scales simultaneously [20], a property critical when training and inference data span from 0.25 to 1.0 𝜇 m/pixel. At coarser resolutions where individual cells subtend fewer pixels, point-wise decoders (UNet, SegFormer, DeepLabV3+) lack this native multi-scale hierarchy. Interpretability is the remaining gap. Quantitative TME features such as cell counts, spatial entropy, immune-to-tumour distances, are difficult for clinicians to assimilate without narrative framing. The NVIDIA BioNeMo GPT framework [12], a domain-adapted large language model (LLM) for biomedical applications, provides a supervised fine-tuning (SFT) pathway for translating structured numerical TME features into clinically grounded patch descriptions. We present S EG TME-UNI2 ( Seg mentation-based TME characterisation with UNI2 ), a unified end-to-end framework with three primary contributions: 1. UNI2-U PER HOVER : a novel dual-head segmentation model coupling UNI2-H with parallel UperNet semantic (6-class) and HV regression decoder heads. Dynamic HV target synthesis enables training from semantic-only annotations, removing the requirement for instance-level ground truth across all stages. 2. Three-stage progressive pseudo-label curriculum : a scalable self-training strategy that bootstraps from PanNuke and expands progressively to 1,608,060 TCGA-UT patches via entropy-filtered pseudo-labelling. 3. Structured TME extraction and BioNeMo narrative generation : a complete post-segmentation pipeline com-puting 20+ per-patch histomic features and generating clinically interpretable TME narratives via BioNeMo SFT. 

## 2. Related Work 

2.1. Nuclear instance segmentation 

HoVer-Net [6] simultaneously segments and classifies nuclei by learning horizontal-vertical distance gradient maps, whose energy surface drives watershed-based instance separation. It remains the most directly comparable baseline to UNI2-U PER HOVER but requires instance annotations and uses a ResNet-50 encoder with a narrower training distribution. CellPose [16] learns a flow field for cell boundaries; StarDist [15] predicts star-convex polygons. Both are effective for compact, homogeneous nuclei but handle multi-class tissue contexts less well. 

2.2. Semantic tissue segmentation and decoder selection 

UperNet [20] combines a Pyramid Pooling Module (PPM) with an FPN for multi-scale context aggregation, achieving strong results on ADE20K and diverse dense prediction benchmarks. UNet and SegFormer have been applied to histopathology segmentation [13, 21] but lack UperNet’s ability to fuse features across large spatial scale differences, which is a critical property when training data spans 0.25 to 1.0 𝜇 m/pixel. 

2.3. Pathology foundation models 

UNI [2] and UNI2 [3] demonstrated that self-supervised pretraining on large-scale pathology slide corpora produces representations that transfer across cancer type, staining protocol, and scanner vendor. CONCH [11] adds vision-language pretraining; ProvGigaPath [22] scales to gigapixel-level WSI representations. Among these, UNI2-h (ViT-Giant) provides the richest per-patch feature space and is most directly extensible to dense prediction.

2.4. Semi-supervised learning and pseudo-labelling 

Semi-supervised self-training with pseudo-labels has been applied to medical image segmentation [1, 17]. Entropy-based confidence thresholding [7] filters noisy pseudo-labels by retaining only high-confidence predictions. Our curriculum extends this paradigm by staging the domain expansion across resolution scales, giving the model consolidation opportunities before each expansion, which differs from single-step self-training approaches. 

2.5. LLM integration in pathology 

Recent work has explored vision-language models for pathology report generation [8, 11]. BioNeMo [12] provides an SFT framework for domain-specific GPT models, enabling controlled and verifiable narrative generation from structured numerical inputs. Grounding LLM outputs in quantitative histomic features substantially reduces hallucination risk compared to purely image-conditioned generation. 

## 3. Datasets 

3.1. PanNuke: supervised seed (Stage 1) 

PanNuke [5] comprises 7,901 H&E image patches of size 256 × 256 pixels, captured at ×40 magnification (0.25 𝜇 m/pixel), sourced from 19 tissue types. The dataset provides pixel-level instance segmentation masks for 189,744 labeled nuclei across five foreground cell categories plus Background: Neoplastic (class 1), Inflammatory (class 2), Connective (class 3), Dead (class 4), and Non-neoplastic Epithelial (class 5). PanNuke is the only source of human-annotated pixel-level labels used in S EG TME-UNI2 and serves exclusively for Stage 1 supervised training of 

1.

3.2. TCGA-UT Restructured: large-scale unlabelled domain (Stages 2 & 3) 

The TCGA-UT dataset [10] is a large-scale collection of H&E-stained histopathological image patches derived from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA). It was constructed from 32 solid cancer types, sourcing 9,662 diagnostic slides from 7,951 patients; after quality control (removal of 926 slides with poor staining, low resolution, out-of-focus regions, or absent cancerous tissue), the final corpus comprises 8,736 slides from 7,175 patients. For each slide, trained pathologists selected representative tumour regions as polygon annotations, from which 10 patches per region were randomly cropped at 0.5 𝜇 m/pixel resolution, yielding 256 × 256 -pixel patches covering 128 × 128 𝜇 m physical area. The dataset provides patches at six resolution levels (scales 0–5), with each scale representing a progressively coarser sampling of the same underlying tissue: Scale 0 at 0.5 𝜇 m/pixel through Scale 5 at 1.0 𝜇 m/pixel (Table 1). Figure 1 illustrates the same tissue region at all six resolutions, showing how fine-grained nuclear details visible at Scale 0 transition to broader tissue-architecture views at coarser scales. We restructured the dataset from the original per-image subdirectory layout (each image directory containing multiple individual patch files) to a flattened per-patch layout (each patch stored directly under its cancer-type directory), which substantially reduces filesystem overhead during large-scale training. The restructured dataset [18] is publicly released on HuggingFace. Table 1 summarises the patch counts per scale.

## 4. UNI2-U PER HOVER : Dual-Head Segmentation Architecture 

4.1. Overview 

Figure 2 presents the full S EG TME-UNI2 system as a two-panel diagram. The left panel (a) shows the UNI2-UPER HOVER model architecture: a shared UNI2-H ViT-Giant backbone feeds four-scale FPN features into two independent UperNet decoder heads, one producing six-class semantic logits and one producing two-channel HV gradient maps. The right panel (b) illustrates the end-to-end inference and reporting pipeline, from MPP normalisation and tiling through watershed-based instance separation, TME feature extraction, and BioNeMo narrative generation. The dual-head design is novel in that it enables simultaneous semantic classification and nuclear instance separation from a single shared foundation model encoder, removing the need for separate specialist models and enabling unified training on the same patch without any additional instance-level ground truth beyond what can be synthesised dynamically from semantic labels. 

4.2. UNI2-H multi-scale feature extractor 

UNI2-H is a ViT-Giant model pretrained on >100M histopathology tiles from 100,000 slides across 40+ cancer types [3]. Configuration: patch size 14, embedding dimension 𝑑 = 1536 , depth 𝐿 = 24 transformer blocks, 𝐻 = 24 

attention heads, SwiGLU-packed MLP (ratio 5.33), 8 register tokens, no classification head. Because ViT architectures produce a flat token sequence at a single stride, a four-scale feature pyramid is constructed by extracting patch token sequences at blocks 𝑏 ∈ {5 , 11 , 17 , 23} (≈ {25 , 50 , 75 , 100}% of network depth). These four taps are chosen to correspond to roughly equal quartile depths of the 24-block network, providing a balanced hierarchy of low-level, mid-level, high-level, and semantic features respectively, which mirrors the design rationale of conventional CNN-based FPNs. 4.3. Dual UperNet decoder heads 

Both heads are instantiated as UperNetForSemanticSegmentation decoders (Hugging Face Transformers [19]), with the internal backbone replaced by a null reference so only the decode_head (PPM + FPN fusion + segmentation convolution) is active. Parameters are not shared between the two heads: the semantic and HV regression tasks have fundamentally different objectives (discrete class probability estimation versus continuous gradient field regression), and sharing parameters would force a single decoder to simultaneously optimise for softmax-normalised six-class output and unbounded two-channel regression, degrading performance on both. Each head processes the four-scale FPN features in two stages. First, a Pyramid Pooling Module (PPM) applies pooling at multiple spatial scales on the coarsest feature map and concatenates the results, capturing global context that would otherwise be lost at small spatial resolutions. Second, an FPN fusion step progressively combines feature maps from coarser to finer scales via lateral connections, producing a single fused representation that encodes both local detail and global context simultaneously. The fused representation is then passed through a segmentation convolution and bilinearly upsampled to the full input resolution for loss computation.

4.5. Dynamic HV target synthesis 

Instance-level polygon annotations (one mask polygon per nucleus) are expensive to produce at scale: PanNuke required trained pathologists to delineate 189,744 individual nuclei across 7,901 images, yet this effort covers only a single institution’s staining protocol at a single resolution. ## 6. Training Configuration 

6.1. Pre-processing and augmentation 

Images are padded to the nearest multiple of 14 (right/bottom edges, zero-fill), resized to 224 × 224 (bilinear for images; nearest-neighbour for masks), and normalised with ImageNet statistics ( 𝜇 = (0 .485 , 0.456 , 0.406) , 𝜎 =(0 .229 , 0.224 , 0.225) ). Training augmentations (each applied with probability 0.5): colour jitter (brightness, contrast, saturation ∈ [0 .8, 1.2] ; hue ∈ [−0 .05 , 0.05] ); HLS-space multiplicative perturbation ∈ [0 .9, 1.1] ; horizontal flip; vertical flip. Spatial augmentations are applied identically to image and mask. 

6.2. Hyperparameters 

Table 3 lists hyperparameters shared across all three stages. Stages 1 and 2 ran for 250 epochs each. Stage 3 was trained for 100 epochs; because each epoch processes all 1,608,060 patches, Stage 3 accumulates substantially more total gradient steps than the earlier stages despite the lower epoch count (335,100 steps for Stage 3 vs. 24,651 for Stage 1 and 212,500 for Stage 2).

7.1. Phase 1 ( analyze_tme_from_maps , grey region) 

The pipeline first validates input array dimensionality, raising a fatal error if the instance mask is incorrectly passed as a 3-channel RGB array rather than a 2D integer map. This is a common preprocessing error that would silently corrupt all downstream measurements. Validated masks are then parsed via skimage.measure.regionprops to extract per-nucleus centroid coordinates and pixel-area values, which are mapped to their semantic class identities using CLASS_MAP (linking instance IDs to classes 0–5: Background, Neoplastic, Inflammatory, Connective, Dead, Epithelial). Composition metrics are subsequently computed for each class: absolute nucleus count 𝑁 𝑐 , population fraction 𝑟 𝑐 = 𝑁 𝑐 ∕𝑁 total , and mean nuclear area ̄𝑎 𝑐 , along with two bounded clinical ratios, namely the Tumour-Stroma fraction 𝑓 TS = 𝑁 neo ∕( 𝑁 neo +𝑁 conn )

and the Immune Infiltration fraction 𝑓 II = 𝑁 inf ∕( 𝑁 neo + 𝑁 inf ). The Advanced Spatial Analysis step constructs two 

scipy.spatial.cKDTree structures over cell centroids. The first tree, built over neoplastic centroids, is queried once per inflammatory cell to yield three immune-to-tumour spatial metrics: min_dist_Inflammatory_to_Neoplastic (closest tumour cell distance), mean_dist_Inflammatory_to_N 

(mean over all inflammatory cells), and count_engaged_immune (number of inflammatory cells within a 50 px proximity threshold, operationalising direct immune-tumour contact). The second tree, built over all cell centroids, retrieves the 𝑘 = 6 nearest neighbours per cell; the Shannon entropy 

𝐻 𝑖 = − ∑𝑐 𝑝 𝑖,𝑐 log 2 𝑝 𝑖,𝑐 of each cell’s neighbour class distribution is computed and averaged across the patch to yield 

mean_spatial_entropy that is a scalar index of cellular mixing, where high values indicate heterogeneous immune-tumour co-localisation and low values indicate class-segregated spatial compartmentalisation. ## 8. Biological Token Translation and BioNeMo Narrative Generation 

With UNI2-U PER HOVER providing six-class semantic segmentation maps and watershed-derived nuclear instance masks (Section 4), and a three-stage curriculum ensuring that these outputs are available across the full resolution range of the TCGA-UT corpus (Section 5), the next step is to translate the raw segmentation output into clinically actionable information. Section 7 described how structured per-patch TME features are computed from these segmentation outputs. This section describes how those quantitative features are first encoded as categorical biological tokens and then used to drive a language model that generates clinically interpretable patch descriptions. 

8.1. Rule-based biological token translation 

Raw numeric TME features are mapped to categorical phenotype labels and a flat biological token list (Table 4) via the translate_tme_to_bio_tokens function. This translation (i) provides an immediately interpretable categorical patch summary, and (ii) anchors the BioNeMo prompt to quantitatively grounded descriptors, substantially reducing LLM hallucination risk. Rule-based thresholds are used in preference to learned classifiers for three reasons: interpretability (each threshold maps directly to a published or clinically used cut-off value, making the token assignment auditable), reproducibility (the same feature values always produce the same tokens without stochastic elements), and the absence of any additional training data requirement (no labelled feature-to-token examples are needed). The six phenotype axes (Composition, Immune, Stromal, Spatial, Architecture, Morphology) cover the primary TME descriptors used in clinical pathology reporting and correspond to the dimensions routinely assessed in manual TME scoring. The token list is deliberately flat and unordered to remain compatible with any text encoder downstream, including bag-of-words retrievers, transformer sequence encoders, and the BioNeMo GPT model used here. A key design advantage of this approach is that the biological tokens and the resulting narrative description are derived from aggregate patch-level statistics, specifically total cell counts, population fractions, mean nuclear areas, and

8.2. BioNeMo GPT supervised fine-tuning 

The structured JSON object, containing raw_data (numeric features), interpretation (categorical labels from Table 4), and generated_tokens (flat token list), is used to fine-tune the NVIDIA BioNeMo GPT model via the SFT framework. The fine-tuned model generates clinically interpretable per-patch TME narratives, as shown in Figure 5. Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) in this context means that the GPT model is first pre-trained on a large corpus of general biomedical text to acquire biomedical language priors, and then fine-tuned on paired examples consisting of a structured JSON input (the feature set computed by the TME pipeline) and a human-written narrative output (a clinical-style description of the TME phenotype). During inference, the fine-tuned model takes a new JSON feature set as its prompt and autoregressively generates the corresponding narrative, conditioned on the numeric values it receives. 

Prompt format. The structured JSON is serialised into a text prompt using a fixed template: 

TME features: {raw_data} Phenotype labels: {interpretation} Biological tokens: {generated_tokens} Narrative: 

where raw_data , interpretation , and generated_tokens are the three JSON fields computed by the TME pipeline (Section 7). The model is trained to complete the Narrative: field. This explicit field separation ensures that all three information levels (raw measurements, categorical labels, and biological vocabulary) are available to the model during generation.

9.3. Training dynamics 

Figure 7 shows the validation mIoU and training loss trajectories for all three curriculum stages. All three models initialise from the same UNI2-H pretrained backbone with randomly initialised decoder heads; the progressive improvement observed across stages reflects increasing pseudo-label quality rather than weight inheritance between stages. 

Stage 1 ( 1, PanNuke). 1 begins at a validation mIoU of 0.41 at the first checkpoint and improves rapidly during the first 25% of training, reaching 0.83 mIoU as the model learns the six-class taxonomy from clean human annotations. Convergence continues steadily through 249 epochs, reaching a best validation mIoU of 0.931 at the final checkpoint (step 24,651). The final training loss of 0.025 reflects the well-constrained nature of the PanNuke training set. 

Stage 2 ( 2, TCGA-UT Scale 0). 2 initialises fresh from UNI2-H and begins training on Scale-0 TCGA-UT pseudo-labels generated by 1. The initial mIoU of 0.47 is higher than 1’s starting point (0.41), reflecting the higher quality of pseudo-label initialisation compared to the random decoder alone. However, the model converges smoothly over 250 epochs to a best mIoU of 0.820 on the TCGA-UT Scale-0 pseudo-label validation split. The final training loss of 0.061 is higher than Stage 1, consistent with the reduced label quality of pseudo-annotated data. 

Stage 3 ( 3, TCGA-UT Scales 0–5). 3 trains on the full 1,608,060-patch multi-resolution corpus using pseudo-labels generated by 2. Training runs for 100 epochs; because each epoch processes all 1,608,060 patches, Stage 3 accumulates substantially more total gradient steps (335,100) than Stage 1 (24,651) or Stage 2 (212,500) despite the lower epoch count. The model reaches a best mIoU of 0.772 on the TCGA-UT all-scales pseudo-label validation split. The higher final training loss of 0.120 reflects the greater label noise and domain diversity introduced by five additional resolution scales not seen in Stages 1 or 2. Notably, the mIoU curve for 3 continues to improve steadily at the 100-epoch cutoff, suggesting that further training would yield additional gains. 

Cross-stage comparison. A key observation from Figure 7 is that each stage’s initial mIoU is higher than the initial mIoU of the preceding stage (0.41 for 1, 0.47 for 2, 0.53 for 3). This demonstrates the increasing quality of pseudo-labels generated at each stage: 2’s pseudo-labels, produced by a model trained on TCGA-UT data, are more informative starting points than the random decoder initialisation alone. This is the core mechanism by which the progressive curriculum improves TME characterisation without any additional human annotation.

## 10. Discussion 

SEG TME-UNI2 addresses the core bottleneck in computational TME analysis: the disconnect between the scale of available TCGA H&E image data (1,608,060 patches) and the scale of human-annotated pixel-level labels (7,901 PanNuke images, 189,744 annotated nuclei). The three-stage progressive pseudo-label curriculum closes this gap without additional manual annotation by staging domain expansion across resolution scales, giving the model repeated consolidation opportunities and avoiding the error accumulation that typically limits single-step self-training. The UperNet decoder was selected over UNet, SegFormer, and DeepLabV3+ specifically because the training-to-inference resolution shift (0.25 𝜇 m/pixel PanNuke to 0.5–1.0 𝜇 m/pixel TCGA-UT) requires a decoder that aggregates features across a wide spatial scale range. UperNet’s PPM + FPN fusion outperforms point-wise decoders at coarser resolutions where individual cells subtend fewer pixels but TME-level tissue architecture is richer. The BioNeMo integration transforms quantitative TME outputs into a form directly consumable by clinical pathologists. Grounding generated narratives in verifiable numerical measurements, rather than image pixels alone, which substantially reduces hallucination risk and enables spot-checking by comparing narrative claims against the feature values in the JSON output. The 11-token biological token list further provides a compact, standardised vocabulary for downstream retrieval-augmented generation. An important and practically valuable property of the S EG TME-UNI2 reporting pipeline is its robustness to imperfect segmentation. Because the biological tokens and TME narrative are derived from aggregate statistical features computed over all detected cells in a patch, rather than from the precise accuracy of any individual cell boundary, the generated description maintains clinical relevance even when segmentation quality is reduced by challenging imaging conditions. The HNSCC qualitative validation illustrates this directly: Case D, characterised by pale pink staining that reduces nuclear-cytoplasm contrast and complicates individual cell detection, nonetheless yields a coherent Immune-Excluded, Stroma-Dominant phenotype classification. This classification emerges from the overall counts and spatial organisation of the detected cell population, which remains informative even when boundary precision is lower. This decoupling of narrative quality from single-cell segmentation accuracy is a distinct advantage over approaches that generate descriptions directly from raw image pixels, where local imaging artefacts can propagate unchecked into the output text. 

Limitations and future work. The current paper presents an architectural framework with preliminary internal validation. Several important experiments are planned as follow-up work (to be reported in a companion paper targeting Nature Methods): 1. External benchmark evaluation: UNI2-U PER HOVER will be evaluated on MoNuSeg, CryoNuSeg, and CoNSeP against HoVer-Net, CellPose, and StarDist, with Dice Score Coefficient (DSC), Aggregated Jaccard Index (AJI), and Panoptic Quality (PQ). 2. Ablation studies: the contribution of the HV auxiliary head, FPN tap depth, entropy threshold 𝜏 , and curriculum stage count will each be quantified in isolation. 3. Statistical rigour: all metrics will be reported with 95% confidence intervals and 𝑝 -values from three independent training seeds. 4. Pseudo-label quality against human ground truth: a pathologist-annotated sample of 200 TCGA-UT patches will provide human reference labels for direct accuracy assessment. 5. Biological downstream application: TME features from 3 will be linked to clinical outcomes (Cox regression, log-rank test) in a TCGA cohort to demonstrate clinical utility. 6. BioNeMo narrative quality: BLEU-4, ROUGE-L, Cohen’s 𝜅 (two independent pathologists), and hallucination rate will quantify narrative fidelity. Dynamic HV target synthesis introduces approximation errors for abutting same-class nuclei; morphological pre-separation priors and learned refinement modules are planned improvements. Last-writer HV map stitching at tile boundaries may introduce gradient discontinuities; Gaussian-feathered blending will be evaluated. Rule-based biological token thresholds are currently literature-derived; outcome-supervised classifiers will replace them in subsequent work.

## 11. Conclusion 

We presented S EG TME-UNI2, a unified framework for H&E-based TME characterisation combining UNI2-UPER HOVER (a dual-head UNI2-UperNet segmentation model), a three-stage progressive pseudo-label curriculum scaling PanNuke supervision to 1,608,060 real-world TCGA-UT patches, and a BioNeMo GPT-driven narrative generation pipeline. Preliminary results confirm the framework’s feasibility and demonstrate consistent mIoU improve-ment across curriculum stages. The public release of the pseudo-labelled TCGA-UT dataset and UNI2-U PER HOVER 

checkpoint establishes the largest nuclei-level annotated TCGA corpus to date and provides an open, reproducible platform for AI-assisted TME profiling. Comprehensive external validation and biological application are reported in a companion paper.
