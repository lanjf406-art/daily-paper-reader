A B S T R A C T 

Characterising the tumour microenvironment (TME) from routine haematoxylin and eosin (H&E)-stained histology images requires simultaneous cell segmentation, biological feature extraction, and interpretable clinical reporting. We present SegTME-UNI2, a unified framework addressing all three requirements. At its core is UNI2-UperHoVer, a dual-head segmentation model that pairs the UNI2-h pathology foundation model (ViT-Giant, pretrained on over 100 million tiles from 100,000 slides) with two parallel UperNet decoders: one for six-class semantic segmentation and one for horizontal-vertical (HV) gradient regression enabling watershed-based nuclear instance separation. To address the absence of pixel-level annotations on large real-world repositories, UNI2-UperHoVer is trained via a three-stage progressive pseudo-label curriculum. Each stage trains a fresh model: Stage 1 uses human-annotated PanNuke (7,901 images, 189,744 nuclei at 0.25 𝜇 m/pixel); Stage 2 uses entropy-filtered pseudo-labels generated by the Stage 1 model on 271,711 TCGA-UT scale-0 patches (0.5 𝜇 m/pixel); Stage 3 uses pseudo-labels from the Stage 2 model on all 1,608,061 TCGA-UT patches across six resolution scales (0.5 to 1.0 𝜇 m/pixel). No weights are transferred between stages; improvement is driven entirely by increasing pseudo-label quality — each stage’s fresh decoder achieves a higher initial validation mIoU (0.41 → 0.47 → 0.53; Figure 6) because it trains from the first step on higher-quality pseudo-labels generated by the preceding model. Segmentation outputs feed a structured TME feature extraction pipeline computing 20+ per-patch compositional, morphological, spatial entropy, and intercellular distance metrics, encoded as JSON and passed to a LLaMA-3.2-1B model fine-tuned via NVIDIA BioNeMo ( llama3_native_te ) for clinically plausible TME narrative generation. Preliminary validation on held-out PanNuke and TCGA-UT partitions demonstrates framework feasibility and internal consistency. The pseudo-labelled TCGA-UT dataset and UNI2-UperHoVer checkpoint are publicly released to support large-scale TME profiling and spatial biology research. 

## 1. Introduction 

Histopathological examination of haematoxylin and eosin (H&E)-stained tissue sections is the clinical gold standard for cancer diagnosis, staging, and treatment planning. Meaningful computational analysis of H&E images requires resolving two complementary and deeply coupled tasks: (i) semantic segmentation , assigning each pixel to a biologically meaningful cell-type category, and (ii) nuclear instance separation , delineating individual nuclei as discrete quantifiable objects. Together these outputs enable quantitative tumour microenvironment (TME) profiling, like tumour cellularity, immune infiltration fraction, nuclear morphology, and immune-to-tumour spatial proximity, which carry independent prognostic value across multiple cancer types [22, 12]. Existing computational tools address these tasks separately and each faces a fundamental bottleneck. Instance segmentation models such as HoVer-Net [8] require costly nucleus-level polygon annotations available only for small curated benchmarks such as PanNuke [7] (7,901 patches, 189,744 annotated nuclei, across 19 tissue types) and

They do not encode broader tissue semantics and have narrow training distributions that do not generalise to the full diversity of TCGA. Semantic segmentation methods ( e.g. UperNet [28]) provide tissue-level context but cannot separate touching nuclei. Neither class of method produces outputs in a form directly accessible to clinical reporting workflows. The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) repository contains digitised whole-slide images (WSIs) from more than 30,000 patients spanning 33 cancer types, yet remains entirely unannotated at the pixel level. The TCGA-UT Restructured subset used in this work covers 32 solid cancer types from 7,175 patients across 8,736 diagnostic slides, comprising 1,608,061 patches at six resolution scales—yet not a single patch carries a human pixel-level annotation. PanNuke, the largest publicly available multi-class nucleus dataset, provides just 7,901 annotated patches. Bridging a 200-fold annotation gap without additional human labelling effort is the central challenge SegTME-UNI2 addresses. Large-scale pathology foundation models have recently made this bridge tractable. UNI2 [4], pretrained on over 100 million tiles from 100,000 slides across 40+ cancer types, provides representations with demonstrated cross-domain transfer across diverse downstream tasks. Crucially, UNI2’s ViT-Giant architecture is architecturally compatible with the attachment of dense-prediction decoder heads, enabling pixel-level segmentation without retraining the encoder. For the resolution shift from PanNuke (0.25 𝜇 m/pixel, high-resolution nuclei) to TCGA-UT (0.5 to 1.0 𝜇 m/pixel, richer tissue context per patch), UperNet’s pyramid pooling is the preferred decoder: its Feature Pyramid Network (FPN) structure aggregates features across multiple spatial scales simultaneously [28], a property critical when training and inference data span from 0.25 to 1.0 𝜇 m/pixel. At coarser resolutions where individual cells subtend fewer pixels, point-wise decoders (UNet, SegFormer, DeepLabV3+) lack this native multi-scale hierarchy. Interpretability is the remaining gap. Quantitative TME features such as cell counts, spatial entropy, immune-to-tumour distances, are difficult for clinicians to assimilate without narrative framing. NVIDIA BioNeMo [20] provides a TransformerEngine-native supervised fine-tuning (SFT) recipe ( llama3_native_te ) that is used here to adapt 

meta-llama/Llama-3.2-1B on structured TME features, translating quantitative segmentation outputs into clinically grounded patch descriptions. We present SegTME-UNI2 ( Seg mentation-based TME characterisation with UNI2 ), a unified end-to-end frame-work with three primary contributions: 1. UNI2-UperHoVer : a novel dual-head segmentation model coupling UNI2-h with parallel UperNet semantic (6-class) and HV regression decoder heads. Dynamic HV target synthesis enables training from semantic-only annotations, removing the requirement for instance-level ground truth across all stages. 2. Three-stage progressive pseudo-label curriculum : a scalable self-training strategy that bootstraps from PanNuke and expands progressively to 1,608,061 TCGA-UT patches via entropy-filtered pseudo-labelling. 3. Structured TME extraction and BioNeMo narrative generation : a complete post-segmentation pipeline com-puting 20+ per-patch histomic features and generating clinically plausible TME narratives via BioNeMo SFT (quantitative narrative quality assessment planned for the companion paper; see Limitations and future work, Section 5). 

## 2. Related Work 

2.1. Nuclear instance segmentation 

HoVer-Net [8] simultaneously segments and classifies nuclei by learning horizontal-vertical distance gradient maps, whose energy surface drives watershed-based instance separation. It remains the most directly comparable baseline to UNI2-UperHoVer but requires instance annotations and uses a ResNet-50 encoder with a narrower training distribution. CellPose [24] learns a flow field for cell boundaries; StarDist [23] predicts star-convex polygons. Both are effective for compact, homogeneous nuclei but handle multi-class tissue contexts less well. CellViT [10] applies a pre-trained ViT encoder to cell segmentation via a U-Net-style decoder, demonstrating that ViT-based encoders outperform CNN-based encoders for nucleus delineation on PanNuke and MoNuSeg. UNI2-UperHoVer extends this direction by pairing a larger-scale pathology-pretrained ViT-Giant encoder (UNI2-h) with a dual-head UperNet decoder that jointly optimises semantic and instance objectives, and by removing the dependency on instance-level training annotations through dynamic HV target synthesis from semantic labels alone. 

2.2. Semantic tissue segmentation and decoder selection 

UperNet [28] combines a Pyramid Pooling Module (PPM) with an FPN for multi-scale context aggregation, achieving strong results on ADE20K and diverse dense prediction benchmarks. UNet and SegFormer have been applied 2 of 28 to histopathology segmentation [21, 29] but lack UperNet’s ability to fuse features across large spatial scale differences, which is a critical property when training data spans 0.25 to 1.0 𝜇 m/pixel. Panoptic segmentation methods [14] unify semantic and instance prediction in a single output head but require panoptic-quality annotations (both semantic classes and instance IDs for every pixel). UNI2-UperHoVer adopts a dual-head design instead: the semantic head and HV regression head have fundamentally different output types (discrete class probabilities vs. continuous gradient fields), and the dynamic HV synthesis approach explicitly requires separate semantic labels as input, making the dual-head factorisation a design necessity rather than an architectural choice. Furthermore, the pseudo-label curriculum operates exclusively on semantic labels, which are substantially cheaper to generate at scale than full panoptic annotations. 

2.3. Pathology foundation models 

UNI [3] and UNI2 [4] demonstrated that self-supervised pretraining on large-scale pathology slide corpora produces representations that transfer across cancer type, staining protocol, and scanner vendor. CONCH [17] adds vision-language pretraining; ProvGigaPath [30] scales to gigapixel-level WSI representations. Among these, UNI2-h (ViT-Giant) provides the richest per-patch feature space and is most directly extensible to dense prediction. 

2.4. Semi-supervised learning and pseudo-labelling 

Semi-supervised self-training with pseudo-labels has been applied to medical image segmentation [1, 25]. Entropy-based confidence thresholding [9] filters noisy pseudo-labels by retaining only high-confidence predictions. Our curriculum extends this paradigm by staging the domain expansion across resolution scales, giving the model consolidation opportunities before each expansion, which differs from single-step self-training approaches. 

2.5. LLM integration in pathology 

Recent work has explored vision-language models for pathology report generation [11, 17]. PathChat [18] demonstrates a multimodal generative AI copilot for pathology that combines a vision encoder with a large language model for diagnostic question answering and report generation from whole-slide images. While PathChat and similar image-conditioned systems generate narratives directly from pixel features, SegTME-UNI2 takes a complementary approach: narratives are grounded in verifiable quantitative TME features (cell counts, spatial entropy, intercellular distances) rather than pixel values, which reduces hallucination risk and enables spot-checking. BioNeMo [20] provides an SFT framework for domain-specific GPT models, enabling this controlled and verifiable narrative generation from structured numerical inputs.

3.1. Datasets 

3.1.1. PanNuke: supervised seed (Stage 1) 

PanNuke [7] comprises 7,901 H&E image patches of size 256 × 256 pixels, captured at ×40 magnification (0.25 𝜇 m/pixel), sourced from 19 tissue types. The dataset provides pixel-level instance segmentation masks for 189,744 labeled nuclei across five foreground cell categories plus Background: Neoplastic (class 1), Inflammatory (class 2), Connective (class 3), Dead (class 4), and Non-neoplastic Epithelial (class 5). 3.1.2. TCGA-UT Restructured: large-scale unlabelled domain (Stages 2 & 3) 

The TCGA-UT dataset [15] is a large-scale collection of H&E-stained histopathological image patches derived from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA). It was constructed from 32 solid cancer types, sourcing 9,662 diagnostic slides from 7,951 patients; after quality control (removal of 926 slides with poor staining, low resolution, out-of-focus regions, or absent cancerous tissue), the final corpus comprises 8,736 slides from 7,175 patients. For each slide, trained pathologists selected representative tumour regions as polygon annotations, from which 10 patches per region were randomly cropped at 0.5 𝜇 m/pixel resolution, yielding 256 × 256 -pixel patches covering 128 × 128 𝜇 m physical area. 3.2. UNI2-UperHoVer: Dual-Head Segmentation Architecture 

3.2.1. Overview 

Figure 2 presents the full SegTME-UNI2 system as a two-panel diagram. The left panel (a) shows the UNI2-UperHoVer model architecture: a shared UNI2-h ViT-Giant backbone feeds four-scale FPN features into two independent UperNet decoder heads, one producing six-class semantic logits and one producing two-channel HV gradient maps. The right panel (b) illustrates the end-to-end inference and reporting pipeline, from MPP normalisation and tiling through watershed-based instance separation, TME feature extraction, and BioNeMo narrative generation. The dual-head design is novel in that it enables simultaneous semantic classification and nuclear instance separation from a single shared foundation model encoder, removing the need for separate specialist models and enabling unified training on the same patch without any additional instance-level ground truth beyond what can be synthesised dynamically from semantic labels.

5 of 28 3.2.3. Dual UperNet decoder heads 

Both heads are instantiated as UperNetForSemanticSegmentation decoders (Hugging Face Transformers [27]), with the internal backbone replaced by a null reference so only the decode_head (PPM + FPN fusion + segmentation convolution) is active. Parameters are not shared between the two heads: the semantic and HV regression tasks have fundamentally different objectives (discrete class probability estimation versus continuous gradient field regression), and sharing parameters would force a single decoder to simultaneously optimise for softmax-normalised six-class output and unbounded two-channel regression, degrading performance on both. Each head processes the four-scale FPN features in two stages. First, a Pyramid Pooling Module (PPM) applies pooling at multiple spatial scales on the coarsest feature map and concatenates the results, capturing global context that would otherwise be lost at small spatial resolutions. Second, an FPN fusion step progressively combines feature maps from coarser to finer scales via lateral connections, producing a single fused representation that encodes both local detail and global context simultaneously. The fused representation is then passed through a segmentation convolution and bilinearly upsampled to the full input resolution for loss computation.

11 of 28 3.6. Biological Token Translation and BioNeMo Narrative Generation 

With UNI2-UperHoVer providing six-class semantic segmentation maps and watershed-derived nuclear instance masks (Section 3.2), and a three-stage curriculum ensuring that these outputs are available across the full resolution range of the TCGA-UT corpus (Section 3.3), the next step is to translate the raw segmentation output into clinically actionable information. Section 3.5 described how structured per-patch TME features are computed from these segmentation outputs. This section describes how those quantitative features are first encoded as categorical biological tokens and then used to drive a language model that generates clinically plausible patch descriptions (pending formal quantitative evaluation; see Section 5). 

3.6.1. Rule-based biological token translation 

Raw numeric TME features are mapped to categorical phenotype labels and a flat biological token list (Table 4) via the translate_tme_to_bio_tokens function. This translation (i) provides an immediately interpretable categorical patch summary, and (ii) anchors the BioNeMo prompt to quantitatively grounded descriptors, substantially reducing LLM hallucination risk. Rule-based thresholds are used in preference to learned classifiers for three reasons: interpretability (each threshold follows established tumour microenvironment spatial biology conventions where available, making the token assignment auditable), reproducibility (the same feature values always produce the same tokens without stochastic elements), and the absence of any additional training data requirement (no labelled feature-to-token examples are needed). Phenotype thresholds follow established conventions from tumour microenvironment spatial biology: immune status follows the hot/cold tumour framework [6, 5], architecture follows the cold/mixed/compartmentalized categorisation of Keren et al. [13], stromal status follows percentage-based tumour stroma quantification [2], spatial interaction follows established distance-based infiltration/exclusion analysis [22], and morphology follows area-based nuclear grading [16]. Composition thresholds and the specific numeric cut-off values throughout were calibrated to this study’s pixel-level resolution and six-class ontology rather than directly transferred from prior work, which typically uses different units, magnifications, or scoring systems. The six phenotype axes (Composition, Immune, Stromal, Spatial, Architecture, Morphology) cover the primary TME descriptors used in clinical pathology reporting and correspond to the dimensions routinely assessed in manual TME scoring. The token list is deliberately flat and unordered to remain compatible with any text encoder downstream, including bag-of-words retrievers, transformer sequence encoders, and the LLaMA-3.2-1B model fine-tuned here via BioNeMo. A key design advantage of this approach is that the biological tokens and the resulting narrative description are derived from aggregate patch-level statistics, specifically total cell counts, population fractions, mean nuclear areas, and spatial distances, rather than from the precise delineation of individual cell boundaries. This property makes TME characterisation robust to imperfect segmentation. Even in challenging cases where staining or scanner conditions hinder accurate cell segmentation, the overall composition ratios and spatial interaction metrics computed from the ensemble of detected cells remain statistically representative of the true TME phenotype, as demonstrated across the inter-scanner variability of the IGNITE tiles (Section 4.3). The generated token set can therefore retain clinical accuracy at the patch level even when individual cell boundaries are imperfectly resolved. 

3.6.2. LLaMA-3.2-1B supervised fine-tuning via BioNeMo 

The structured text prompt — serialised from raw_data (numeric features), interpretation (categorical labels from Table 4), and generated_tokens (flat token list) — is used to fine-tune meta-llama/Llama-3.2-1B 

(NVLlamaForCausalLM ) via NVIDIA BioNeMo’s TransformerEngine-native SFT recipe. The fine-tuned model generates clinically interpretable per-patch TME narratives, as shown in Figure 5. Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) in this context means that LLaMA-3.2-1B, pre-trained by Meta on general-domain text, is further fine-tuned on paired examples consisting of a structured text prompt (the feature set computed by the TME pipeline) and a target narrative output (a clinical-style description of the TME phenotype). Pretrained weights are loaded and converted from HuggingFace format to the TransformerEngine fused-kernel tensor layout via

During inference, the fine-tuned model takes a new structured prompt as input and autoregressively generates the corresponding narrative, conditioned on the numeric values it receives.

15 of 28 3.7. Inference and Post-Processing 

Inference on full-resolution images uses MPP-normalised tiled processing. The input image is downscaled by 

𝑠 = MPP input ∕0 .314 to a canonical resolution of ≈0 .35 𝜇 m/pixel, then partitioned into 224×224 -pixel tiles with a stride of 112 px (50% overlap). Each tile is zero-padded to the nearest multiple of 14 pixels before inference, satisfying the ViT-Giant patch-size constraint. Semantic maps from overlapping tiles are stitched using foreground-priority blending: each pixel adopts the foreground class prediction from any overlapping tile that assigns it a non-background class; HV maps use last-writer overwrite. Instance segmentation applies a marker-controlled watershed to the HV energy surface: seed markers are identified as local maxima of the Euclidean distance transform (minimum separation 7 px); watershed is run with compactness 0.01 on the combined Sobel–HV energy map; objects smaller than 30 px 2 are removed as spurious detections. Instance class labels are assigned by majority vote of the semantic segmentation map within each instance region, preserving consistency between the semantic and instance outputs without requiring any additional inference pass.

4.1. Semantic segmentation: internal validation 

Important note on evaluation design. Part (A) of Table 7 evaluates all three curriculum-stage models on PanNuke human-annotated ground truth. This is a cross-domain evaluation for 2 and 3, which were trained on TCGA-UT pseudo-labels rather than PanNuke. Lower Part (A) scores for 2 and 3 therefore reflect domain shift away from PanNuke , not model degradation. The curriculum’s success criterion is (i) increasing pseudo-label self-consistency in Part (B), and (ii) the training dynamics in Figure 6, which show each stage’s initial mIoU (0.41 → 0.47 → 0.53) progressively higher than the preceding stage—the direct evidence that each stage generates better pseudo-labels than the last. 3’s Part (A) collapse to 0.1587 is additionally attributable to a systematic magnification mismatch detailed in Section 5; a corrected Stage 3 retraining is planned. Table 7 reports evaluation results across two protocols. Part (A) evaluates all models on the same PanNuke 20% held-out test set, which contains human-annotated ground truth, enabling fair cross-stage comparison on validated labels. Since Stage 2 and Stage 3 models are trained on entropy-filtered pseudo-labels rather than human annotations, evaluating them on PanNuke ground truth assesses cross-domain generalisation: whether pseudo-label training preserves the cell-type taxonomy learned from PanNuke. Part (B) reports self-consistency on each model’s own TCGA-UT held-out pseudo-label partition; these values indicate training convergence but cannot be interpreted as accuracy against human ground truth. The baseline is a single-head UperNet (semantic only, no HV head) with the same UNI2-h backbone and training configuration as UNI2-UperHoVer, trained on PanNuke. The Dead class consistently produces the lowest IoU across all models ( 1: 0.1345), reflecting its inherent difficulty: dead and apoptotic nuclei are morphologically heterogeneous (pyknotic, karyorrhectic, and ghost nuclei all map to the same class label), numerically rare in PanNuke (representing < 3% of all annotated nuclei), and frequently confused with small Inflammatory cells due to nuclear size overlap. This class-specific difficulty is consistent with the original PanNuke benchmark report [7] and is not specific to UNI2-UperHoVer.

Cross-stage comparison. This is the central empirical finding of the paper: the progressive curriculum measurably improves TME characterisation without any additional human annotation at each stage. 

4.3. Qualitative case study: TME characterisation in non-small cell lung cancer 

To demonstrate the generalisability of SegTME-UNI2 beyond the TCGA-UT training corpus, we applied the full inference and TME characterisation pipeline to five H&E-stained whole-slide image tiles from the publicly available IGNITE data toolkit [19], a multi-centric, multi-scanner non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) histopathology dataset (887 annotated regions of interest from 155 patients). NSCLC presents particular challenges for automated TME analysis: staining intensity and protocol vary substantially across the toolkit’s multiple contributing centres and scanners, tissue architecture spans densely cellular tumour nests to fibrous desmoplastic stroma, and immune infiltration patterns are a known determinant of immunotherapy response in NSCLC [19]. These five tiles were selected to represent five distinct and clinically meaningful TME phenotypes spanning high and low tumour purity, immune-hot and immune-excluded patterns, and histological diversity (adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and adenosquamous), testing the robustness of UNI2-UperHoVer to the inter-institutional and inter-scanner stain variability inherent to the IGNITE toolkit’s multi-centric design. All five tiles were processed using 2 (Stage 2 checkpoint, trained exclusively on TCGA-UT Scale 0 at 0.5 𝜇 m/pixel). 3, despite training on a broader six-scale TCGA-UT distribution, currently yields lower segmentation quality owing to a systematic magnification mismatch in its Stage 3 pseudo-label targets—an internal training artefact unrelated to the resolution of the input patches—as characterised in Section 5. 23 of 28 5. Discussion 

SegTME-UNI2 addresses the core bottleneck in computational TME analysis: the disconnect between the scale of available TCGA H&E image data (1,608,061 patches) and the scale of human-annotated pixel-level labels (7,901 PanNuke images, 189,744 annotated nuclei). The three-stage progressive pseudo-label curriculum closes this gap without additional manual annotation by staging domain expansion across resolution scales, giving the model repeated consolidation opportunities and avoiding the error accumulation that typically limits single-step self-training. The UperNet decoder was selected over UNet, SegFormer, and DeepLabV3+ specifically because the training-to-inference resolution shift (0.25 𝜇 m/pixel PanNuke to 0.5–1.0 𝜇 m/pixel TCGA-UT) requires a decoder that aggregates features across a wide spatial scale range. UperNet’s PPM + FPN fusion outperforms point-wise decoders at coarser resolutions where individual cells subtend fewer pixels but TME-level tissue architecture is richer. The BioNeMo integration transforms quantitative TME outputs into a form directly consumable by clinical pathologists. Grounding generated narratives in verifiable numerical measurements, rather than image pixels alone, substantially reduces hallucination risk and enables spot-checking by comparing narrative claims against the feature values in the JSON output, as illustrated for the five IGNITE NSCLC tiles in Table 8. The 11-token biological token list further provides a compact, standardised vocabulary for downstream retrieval-augmented generation. Table 9 contextualises SegTME-UNI2’s TME quantification output relative to two widely-used existing tools. An important and practically valuable property of the SegTME-UNI2 reporting pipeline is its robustness to imperfect segmentation. Because the biological tokens and TME narrative are derived from aggregate statistical features computed over all detected cells in a patch, rather than from the precise accuracy of any individual cell boundary, the generated description maintains clinical relevance even when segmentation quality is reduced by challenging imaging conditions. The IGNITE qualitative validation illustrates this directly across five tiles spanning three NSCLC histological subtypes and three contributing scanners: despite inter-institutional and inter-scanner stain variability, each tile yields a biologically coherent TME phenotype classification and narrative that correctly reflects the dominant cell composition and spatial organisation observed in the H&E image. This robustness arises because the biological tokens and TME narrative are derived from aggregate statistical features computed over all detected cells in a patch, rather than from the precise accuracy of any individual cell boundary. This decoupling of narrative quality from single-cell segmentation accuracy is a distinct advantage over approaches that generate descriptions directly from raw image pixels, where local imaging artefacts can propagate unchecked into the output text. 

3 magnification mismatch and current limitations. Although 3 achieves higher pseudo-label self-consistency than 2 on its own teacher’s distribution (0.443 vs. 0.367 PL-val mIoU, five foreground classes, across TCGA-UT scales 0–5), its overall segmentation quality is currently lower than 2’s, as demonstrated by the independent IGNITE NSCLC tiles in Figure 7. The primary cause is a systematic scale mismatch in 2’s pseudo-label generation pipeline: all six TCGA-UT scales were processed with a fixed MPP normalisation factor calibrated for Scale 0 ( 0.5 𝜇 m/px ÷0.314 ≈ 1 .59× ). For Scales 1–5 (0.6–1.0 𝜇 m/pixel), this factor under-downsamples the images: nuclei appear smaller than in 2’s training domain, generating pseudo-labels with systematic boundary errors and elevated class confusion for morphologically subtle classes. 3 then trains simultaneously on gradients from six resolution regimes without any explicit scale conditioning, requiring the single model to reconcile low-magnification tissue-context cues from Scale 5 (where individual nuclei subtend only a few pixels) with fine-grained nuclear morphology cues from Scale 0 (where nucleus boundaries and chromatin texture are clearly resolved). Quantitatively, 3’s PanNuke ground-truth IoU for the Dead and Epithelial classes—which require fine nuclear morphology to distinguish—collapses to near zero (0.008 and 0.005 respectively), compared with 2’s 0.066 and 0.255, consistent with the loss of fine-grained morphological signal introduced by the coarser-scale pseudo-labels. The qualitative IGNITE figures (Figure 7) therefore use 2,which produces reliable segmentation on high-resolution patches at 0.5 𝜇 m/pixel. Improving 3 is a primary goal of the planned Stage 3 retraining described in the next paragraph.

Rule-based biological token thresholds follow established TME spatial biology conventions (Section 3.6) but their exact numeric cut-offs were calibrated to this dataset rather than learned from outcome data; outcome-supervised classifiers trained on the survival analysis described above (Limitations item 5) will replace them in subsequent work. 

## 6. Conclusion 

We presented SegTME-UNI2, a unified framework for H&E-based TME characterisation combining UNI2-UperHoVer (a dual-head UNI2-UperNet segmentation model), a three-stage progressive pseudo-label curriculum scaling PanNuke supervision to 1,608,061 real-world TCGA-UT patches, and a LLaMA-3.2-1B narrative generation pipeline fine-tuned via NVIDIA BioNeMo. Preliminary results confirm the framework’s feasibility and demonstrate consistent mIoU improvement across curriculum stages. The public release of the pseudo-labelled TCGA-UT dataset and UNI2-UperHoVer checkpoint establishes the largest nuclei-level pseudo-labelled TCGA corpus to date and provides an open, reproducible platform for AI-assisted TME profiling. Comprehensive external validation and biological application are reported in a companion paper. 25 of 28 Ethics Statement 

All TCGA whole-slide images used in this work are publicly available through the NCI Genomic Data Commons (https://portal.gdc.cancer.gov/ ) under controlled access. The PanNuke dataset is publicly available and uses only de-identified tissue sections without individual patient identifiers. The five non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) tissue tiles used in the qualitative validation (Section 4.3) are sourced from the publicly available IGNITE data toolkit [19], which received its own institutional ethical approval for public release as documented in the original publication; no additional institutional approval was required for the secondary use of this de-identified, publicly released dataset in the present study. No direct patient contact or prospective clinical intervention was involved.
