Abstract 19 

Astrocytoma recurrence is shaped by genetic, epigenetic and transcriptional evolution, but how DNA 20 

remodeling is propagated into full -length RNA isoforms and coding consequences remains poorly 21 

resolved. Here we applied paired PacBio HiFi whole -genome sequencing and Kinnex full -length 22 

RNA sequencing to matched primary and recurrent astrocytoma from six patients, with matched 23 

blood controls. Recurrent tumors preserved core glioma driver identity while acquiring patient -24 

specific remodeling across somatic variants, copy number, structural variation, loss of 25 

heterozygosity, haplotype imbalance and DNA methylation. Long -read transcriptomics revealed 26 

extensive recurrence -associated gene -expression, isoform -usage, differential transcript -usage and 27 

predicted ORF/protein -fate remodeling beyond gene -level expression. A denominator -aware 28 

integration framework showed that copy -number changes provide broad RNA -dosage links, whereas 29 

expressed somatic variants, allele/haplotype -specific transcript usage, methylation -linked isoform 30 

remodeling and structural variants generate more focused DNA –RNA –ORF chains. These findings 31 

establish longitudinal long -read multi -omics as a framework for prioritizing patient -specific 32 

remodeling trajectories and identifying targets for precision medicine in recurrent astrocytoma .33

Introduction 38 

Gliomas are the most common malignant primary brain tumors in adults and remain a major 39 

cause of cancer -related morbidity and mortality in neuro -oncology 1. Although molecular classification 40 

and standard treatment have improved the diagnosis and management of diffuse gliomas, 41 

recurrence remains a central clinical problem 2,3 . Recurrent gliomas are not simple regrowth of the 42 

original tumor. Instead, they emerge through longitudinal remodeling across genetic, epigenetic, 43 

transcriptional, microenvironmental, and post -transcriptional layers 4-8. Large -scale and longitudinal 44 

studies have shown that glioma progression is shaped by somatic evolution, treatment -associated 45 

selection, DNA methylation remodeling, transcriptional state transitions, and interactions with the 46 

tumor microenvironment 4-7. Nonetheless , connecting these regulatory layers within the same patient 47 

remains difficult, especially when the molecular consequences of DNA remodeling extend beyond 48 

gene -level expression. Likewise, Recent longitudinal studies have begun to define disease -specific 49 

routes of IDH -mutant astrocytoma progression. Several studies have shown progressive genomic, 50 

epigenomic, transcriptomic, and proteomic remodeling changes that drive IDH mutant astrocytoma 51 

recurrence , cell state change, and immune evasiveness 8-11 . However, the se studies do not resolve 52 

how these alterations are expressed through full -length transcript structures, allele - or haplotype -53 

specific isoform usage, and isoform -specific coding consequences within the same patient. 54 

Despite these advances, an important technical gap remains. Short -read DNA and RNA 55 

sequencing have been indispensable for defining glioma /astrocytoma driver alterations, copy -56 

number states, mutational processes, gene -expression programs, and DNA methylation classes. 57 

However, short reads provide only fragmented views of many molecular features that are central to 58 

tumor evolution, including complex structural variants, allele -specific events, long -range haplotypes, 59 

phased methylation states, full -length transcript isoforms, fusion transcripts, and isoform -specific 60 

coding consequences. As a result, most studies still analyze genome, epigenome, transcriptome, 61 

and predicted protein -output changes as partially separate layers. This limits the ability to ask a 62 

mechanistic question that is central to recurrence biology: which genomic and epigenomic 63 

alterations are reflected at patient -matched RNA, isoform, allele -specific, and predicted open reading 64 

frames ( ORF )/protein -fate levels? 65 

Long -read sequencing provides an opportunity to address this gap. Long -read RNA 66 

sequencing can capture full -length transcripts, enabling direct characterization of isoform diversity, 67 

alternative transcript usage, fusion transcripts, and coding -potential changes that are difficult to infer 68 

from short -read junction counts alone 12,13 . Recent cancer studies have shown that long -read 69 

transcriptomics can uncover extensive previously unannotated isoform diversity, identify cancer -70 

associated transcript structures, resolve isoform -level expression programs, and reveal genomic 71 

alterations or fusions that may be misinterpreted by short -read RNA -seq 12,13 . Long -read DNA 72 

sequencing, although adopted more slowly in cancer because of cost, input requirements, and 73 

analytical complexity, can resolve structural variants, haplotypes, methylation landscapes, repetitive -74 

element insertions, and complex rearrangements that are often inaccessible or ambiguous with 75 

short -read data 14,15 . These properties make long -read sequencing particularly well suited for 76 

studying recurrence, where DNA remodeling, epigenetic state changes, and transcriptome 77 

reprogramming may converge on patient -specific molecular outcomes. 78 

Despite these advances, the full potential of paired long -read DNA and RNA sequencing 79 

remains underused in cancer. Many long -read RNA studies annotate novel isoforms and predict 80 

open reading frames but rarely connect these isoforms to patient -matched DNA alterations, allele or 81

and methylation states, but their transcriptomic consequences are often inferred indirectly , combined 84

Here, we apply paired long -read DNA and RNA sequencing to matched primary and 89 

recurrent astrocytomas from six patients, with matched blood samples used as germline controls for 90 

DNA analysis. We first define the recurrent astrocytoma DNA landscape using long -read genome 91 

sequencing, including somatic variants, structural rearrangements, copy -number alterations, loss of 92 

heterozygosity, and DNA methylation remodeling. We then use long -read RNA sequencing to 93 

characterize recurrence -associated changes in gene expression, isoform diversity, transcript usage, 94 

junction architecture, and predicted ORF/protein fate. Finally, we integrate the DNA and RNA layers 95 

to determine how genomic, and epigenomic remodeling is reflected at patient -matched RNA, 96 

isoform, allele/haplotype -specific, and predicted protein -output levels. This analysis reveals that 97 

recurrent astrocytoma remodeling is frequently expressed through isoform and ORF -level 98 

consequences that are not captured by gene -level expression alone and provides a denominator -99 

aware framework for prioritizing high -confidence DNA -RNA -ORF remodeling chains in individual 100 

patients , providing basis for precision oncology and patient -specific therapeutics .101

Results: 102 

➢ Cohort, study design, and sequencing quality control 103 

We selected six patients with matched primary and recurrent astrocytoma specimens collected 104 

during routine surgical resection, together with matched blood samples for germline DNA control. 105 

Samples were selected after RNA and DNA quality screening to ensure suitability for long -read 106 

genome and transcriptome sequencing (Figure 1a). The cohort included four males and two 107 

females, with age at primary tumor presentation ranging from 24 to 45 years. All patients underwent 108 

debulking surgery. Except one patient , none had received either radi otherapy or chemotherapy 109 

before recurrence. One patient had undergone postoperative concomitant radio therapy with 110 

temozolomide . Four patients showed increased tumor grade at recurrence, and all tumors carried 111 

IDH1 mutation. Additional clinical and molecular features are summarized in Supplementary Table 1. 112 

Integrated diagnoses were made in accordance with the 5th edition of the WHO Classification of 113 

Tumors of the Central Nervous System 3.114 

We performed PacBio HiFi whole -genome sequencing on tumor and matched blood DNA, and 115 

Kinnex full -length RNA sequencing on matched tumor RNA (Figure 1a). Tumor sequencing depth 116 

was sufficient for DNA -dependent analyses in five of six recurrent -primary pairs, with most tumor 117 

samples reaching approximately 60× or higher mean depth (Figure 1b; Supplementary Table 1). The 118 

recurrent sample from patient 2 had substantially lower tumor depth because of a technical 119 

sequencing issue and therefore that patient was excluded from DNA -dependent analyses, while 120 

retained for RNA -based analyses (Supplementary table 1) . Tumor purity and effective tumor 121 

coverage were acceptable for most samples, although lower purity in patient 4 primary and patient 6 122 

recurrent tumors was noted and considered during downstream interpretation (Fig ure 1c,d). 123 

Kinnex full -length RNA sequencing was performed at 10 million reads per sample and produced 124 

4-8 million mapped and processed full -length (FL) reads per sample, providing sufficient depth for 125 

isoform -level analyses across the cohort (Fig ure 1e). Pigeon transcript classification showed broadly 126 

comparable structural -category composition across primary and recurrent samples, without evidence 127 

of a major sample -level technical shift in transcript annotation classes (Fig ure 1f). Across genes, the 128 

number of detected isoforms was highly variable: most genes were represented by a small number 129 

of isoforms, whereas a subset showed extensive isoform diversity (Fig ure 1g). Gene -level full -length 130 

read support was strongly associated with the number of detected isoforms, indicating that read 131 

depth contributes to isoform discovery and must be considered when interpreting isoform richness 132 

(Fig ure 1h). Genes with higher isoform counts also showed a larger fraction of non -canonical 133 

transcript categories, consistent with increased transcript -structure diversity among highly complex 134 

genes (Fig ure 1i). 135 

Together, these quality -control analyses established a longitudinal long -read DNA/RNA cohort 136 

suitable for downstream analysis of recurrent astrocytoma genome remodeling, transcriptome 137 

remodeling, and patient -matched DNA -RNA integration. Because patient 2 recurrent tumor lacked 138 

sufficient DNA coverage, DNA -dependent recurrence analyses were restricted to the five DNA -139 

evaluable patient pairs, whereas RNA analyses included all six patients. 140

We next used matched HiFi whole -genome sequencing to define the somatic DNA landscape of 143  primary and recurrent astrocytoma s. Across the paired cases, somatic SNV/indel analysis showed 144 

shared, primary -only, and recurrent -only variants, indicating clonal relatedness between primary and 145  recurrent tumors together with ongoing patient -specific evolution (Fig. 2a). A driver -focused 146 

SNV/indel heatmap confirmed the expected glioma genetic backbone, including recurrent 147 

involvement of canonical glioma genes such as IDH1, TP53, ATRX, CIC, and other cancer -148  associated loci, while also showing patient -specific driver -context events (Fig. 2b). However, paired 149 

SNV/indel burden, median variant allele fraction, and read depth did not show a uniform recurrence -150 

associated increase across patients (Fig. 2c). Variant -level VAF and depth distributions supported 151  the reliability of the small -variant calls, while SBS96 context shifts showed patient -specific 152 

mutational -context changes rather than one shared recurrence -wide mutational pattern 153 

(Supplementary Fig. 1a,b). Thus, although small variants provide evidence of tumor lineage and 154 

patient -specific evolution, they do not by themselves explain the recurrent DNA phenotype. 155 

We therefore examined larger -scale copy -number and allelic remodeling. Gene copy -number 156 

profiling revealed patient -specific gains, losses, and homozygous deletion -like states across 157 

canonical glioma -associated genes, including receptor tyrosine kinase, PI3K pathway, cell -cycle, 158  and tumor suppressor loci (Fig. 2d). Loss of heterozygosity ( LOH ) analysis further showed 159 

substantial allelic remodeling across samples, with total LOH exceeding copy -neutral LOH in most 160  tumors and marked variation between primary and recurrent disease (Fig. 2e). Chromosome -level 161 

haplotype imbalance analysis identified recurrent -primary shifts affecting selected chromosomes 162 

rather than a uniform genome -wide direction of change (Fig. 2f). Additional haplotype -aware 163  analyses showed chromosome -level dominant haplotype switching, ranked chromosome -level 164 

haplotype imbalance shifts, and patient -specific changes in CN -LOH, genome -wide haplotype 165  imbalance, ploidy, purity, subclonal burden, and total LOH (Supplementary Fig. 1c –e). These results 166 

indicate that astrocytoma recurrence is accompanied by allele -aware copy -number remodeling, 167 

including changes in LOH and haplotype imbalance that are not fully captured by gene -level CNV 168 

calls alone. 169 

Methylation analysis revealed a third major layer of DNA remodeling. Differentially methylated 170  region burden varied strongly across patients, with patient 1 showing a prominent recurrent increase 171 

in DMR number and median absolute methylation change, whereas other patients showed more 172  modest or opposite shifts (Fig. 2g). Cancer -gene -focused promoter DMR analysis revealed broad 173 

promoter methylation differences across primary and recurrent tumors, including both 174 

hypermethylated and hypomethylated states across genes involved in signaling, transcriptional 175  regulation, and tumor biology (Fig. 2h). A focused analysis of shared promoter genes with the 176 

strongest primary -recurrent methylation shifts further highlighted patient -specific promoter 177 

methylation remodeling (Supplementary Fig. 2a). DMRs were detected across genomic contexts, 178  with the largest absolute burden in intronic regions, while promoter and exon -associated DMRs 179 

provided more directly interpretable regulatory contexts (Supplementary Fig. 2b). Signal -level 180  methylation summaries supported the DMR calls: promoter bigWig signal shifts differed by haplotype 181 

context, DMR -derived methylation differences showed high agreement with bigWig signal, and most 182 

promoter regions with bigWig support overlapped haplotype -resolved regions rather than no -overlap 183  regions (Supplementary Fig. 2c –e). Across samples, promoter methylation signal shifted between 184 

primary and recurrent tumors, absolute promoter methylation change varied by patient, DMR -derived 185  methylation estimates correlated strongly with bigWig signal, and genome -wide DMR signal showed 186 

patient -specific primary -recurrent remodeling (Supplementary Fig. 2f –i). Together, these data show 187 

that recurrent astrocytoma s undergo broad, patient -specific DNA methylation remodeling that can be 188 

linked to promoter and haplotype contexts. 189

We then focused on structural genome remodeling, which represented one of the richest long -192  read DNA signals. SV burden varied markedly across patients, with some tumors showing reduced 193 

SV burden at recurrence and others showing persistent or increased structural alteration (Fig. 3a). 194  Representative circos plots for patient 1 illustrated extensive primary structural rearrangement and a 195 

remodeled recurrent structural landscape, supporting the idea that recurrence is not simply a linear 196 

accumulation of identical structural events (Fig. 3b). At the chromosome level, structural complexity 197  was concentrated in selected patient -chromosome combinations rather than distributed evenly 198 

across the cohort (Fig. 3c). SV type composition showed that deletions, insertions, and duplications 199 

formed the major classes of detected SVs, with lower relative contributions from inversions and 200  breakend -like events in most samples (Fig. 3d). These findings point to patient -specific structural 201 

genome remodeling as a major substrate of recurrent astrocytoma evolution. 202 

To determine whether recurrent disease preserved the same structural loci or instead remodeled 203 

related genomic neighborhoods, we examined breakpoint -cluster sharing. Breakpoint -cluster 204  neighborhoods showed recurrently involved chromosomal regions across patient pairs, suggesting 205 

that some structurally unstable regions persist or re -emerge even when exact breakpoints are not 206 

retained (Fig. 3e). Exact private breakpoint events were highly patient - and timepoint -specific, with 207  large private SV burdens in selected primary or recurrent samples (Supplementary Fig. 3a). 208 

Complex rearrangement candidate classes, including clustered rearrangement, dense breakpoint 209  cluster, rearranged amplicon candidate, and chromothripsis -like candidate classes, further showed 210 

that structural architecture varied across samples (Supplementary Fig. 3b). These labels should be 211 

interpreted as structural proxy classes rather than formally validated mechanisms, but they show 212  that recurrent astrocytoma’s structural remodeling often occurs at the level of regional genome 213 

architecture rather than isolated SV events alone .214 

We next evaluated focal amplicon and rearranged copy -number architecture. Copy -number 215 

amplitude plotted against local SV breakpoint support identified amplified regions with variable 216  degrees of structural support, including regions with high copy number and local breakpoint 217 

enrichment (Fig. 3f). Refined driver/onco -amplicon gene copy -number calls highlighted patient -218 

specific focal copy -number gains involving cancer -relevant loci, providing a direct link between 219  structural genome remodeling and oncogenic dosage architecture (Supplementary Fig. 3c). CCG -220 

focused SV heatmaps further showed that SVs intersected selected cancer -associated genes in a 221  patient -specific manner (Supplementary Fig. 3d). These results indicate that copy -number 222 

remodeling in recurrent astrocytoma includes not only broad CNV states but also structurally 223 

complex focal amplicon -like architectures. 224 

We also examined repeat, mobile -element, and telomere -associated contexts as supporting 225 

annotations of the structural landscape. RepeatMasker annotation showed that SV breakpoints 226 

frequently intersected repeat contexts, including Alu, LINE/L1, LTR/ERV, DNA transposon, simple -227 

repeat, low -complexity, and related repeat classes (Fig. 3g). RepeatMasker annotation of somatic 228  indels showed related but not identical repeat -class distributions, and primary -to -recurrent mobile -229 

element/repeat -context shifts were patient -specific rather than uniform (Supplementary Fig. 3e,f). 230 

Telomere -proximal breakage and telomere -window LOH only partially overlapped, suggesting that 231  local telomere -proximal structural breakage and broader telomeric allelic imbalance are related but 232 

non -identical features of recurrence -associated genome instability (Supplementary Fig. 3g). These 233 

repeat and telomere analyses should be interpreted as genomic -context associations rather than 234 

definitive evidence of active mobile -element insertion or telomere -maintenance mechanism 235 

switching .236

➢ Long -read transcriptome sequencing reveals global changes in gene programs during 248 

astrocytoma recurrence 249 

After defining the DNA remodeling landscape, we next asked whether recurrent tumors showed 250 

corresponding transcriptomic state changes using long -read RNA sequencing. We first analyzed 251 

gene -level expression by summing full -length (FL) Pigeon read counts per gene and fitting a paired 252 

primary -versus -recurrent model. This conventional gene -level analysis identified both upregulated 253 

and downregulated genes in recurrence (Fig. 4a). Over -representation analysis of recurrently 254 

upregulated genes highlighted mitochondrial and respiratory programs, including proton 255 

transmembrane transport, oxidative phosphorylation, electron transport chain, aerobic electron 256 

transport, ATP synthesis -coupled electron transport, and mitochondrial ATP synthesis -coupled 257 

electron transport (Fig. 4b). To complement single -gene differential expression, we performed 258 

sample -level program scoring using ssGSEA across Hallmark, Reactome, Oncogenic, and 259 

Immunologic gene -set collections. This analysis revealed patient -paired shifts in multiple tumor -state 260 

and microenvironment -related programs, including hypoxia, reactive oxygen species, epithelial –261 

mesenchymal transition, UV -response -associated programs, interferon and inflammatory signatures, 262 

PDGF/ERK -related programs, IL15 response, and Rho GTPase signaling (Supplementary Fig. 4a –263 

k). Because many program -level changes were nominal and did not uniformly survive global 264 

multiple -testing correction, we interpret these results as a paired tumor -state remodeling landscape 265 

rather than as a set of independently validated pathway claims. Together, this data show s that 266 

recurrent astrocytoma s exhibit broad expression and pathway -state changes, but this analysis alone 267 

cannot resolve whether recurrence also remodels the structure and coding output of transcripts. 268 

➢ Long -read transcriptome sequencing reveals extensive isoform remodeling 269 

independent of apparent gene expression changes 270 

We therefore next examined isoform -level remodeling directly. Differential isoform expression 271 

analysis using paired primary -vs -recurrent DEseq2 -based analysis revealed sets of up and 272 

downregulated isoforms in recurrent tumors (Fig. 4c). However, these changes cannot be completely 273 

separated from global changes in gene expression, and they fail in capturing the complexity of gene 274 

isoform remodeling beyond simple changes in expression. Therefore, next we examined isoform 275 

gain/loss in recurrent tumors, restricting our criteria to isoforms with at least 5 FL reads that are 276 

present in at least 4 patient samples in either primary or recurrent tumors, and that represent at least 277 

10% of the total gene FL reads. The paired isoform gain/loss analysis showed that each patient 278 

acquired and lost thousands of detected isoforms between primary and recurrent tumors, indicating 279 

extensive turnover of the expressed isoform repertoire across recurrence (Fig. 4d). Structural -280 

category enrichment analysis of high -confidence gained and lost isoforms showed that recurrently 281 

gained isoforms were enriched in full -splice match and incomplete -splice match categories, whereas 282 

lost isoforms enriched in novel isoforms either in catalog or not in catalog (Supplementary Fig. 5a). 283

To capture gene -level isoform remodeling more comprehensively, we used to independent 286 

statistical approaches. In the first approach, we classified each gene using a model based on 287 

isoform fractions rather than absolute abundance alone. First, we defined the dominant isoform per 288 

gene across primary and recurrent group (see methods ). This framework allowed us to separate 289 

genes into six remodeling classes: Class A, dominant isoform switching, where a dominant isoform 290 

is present in both groups but changes identity; Class B, same -dominant remodeling, where the 291 

dominant isoform is preserved but secondary isoforms remodel; Class C, primary -dominant lost, 292 

where a dominant primary isoform is no longer dominant in recurrence; Class D, recurrent -dominant 293 

gained, where a dominant isoform emerges in recurrence; Class E, no -dominant remodeling, where 294 

no clear dominant isoform exists in either group but the isoform distribution changes strongly; and 295 

Class F, stable or not high -confidence remodeled genes. Most genes were classified as Class F 296 

(i.e., stable with no major isoform changes) and Class A had the least number of genes 297 

(Supplementary Fig. 5b). To identify genes within each class that showed statistical divergence from 298 

primary to recurrent tumors, we calculated, for each gene, the isoform fraction of each transcript 299 

within the gene, compared the primary and recurrent isoform -fraction distributions using Jensen –300 

Shannon divergence (JSD), and quantified the largest individual isoform usage shift using maximum 301 

absolute delta isoform fraction, max |dIF| (see methods). High -confidence remodeling required JSD 302 

≥ 0.1, max |dIF| ≥ 0.2, and support in at least four patients. This model distinguished a small subset 303 

of high -confidence remodeled genes from a large background of stable genes (Fig. 4e; 304 

Supplementary Fig. 5c –d). 305 

We then extended this model by adding Shannon entropy, which measures how concentrated or 306 

diffuse isoform usage is within each gene, providing an additional layer of complexity that allows the 307 

capture of complex isoform remodeling events. A positive recurrent -primary entropy shift indicates 308 

that recurrent isoform usage becomes more diffuse across multiple isoforms, whereas a negative 309 

shift indicates that recurrent isoform usage becomes more focused toward fewer isoforms. The 310 

JSD –entropy framework separated genes with high distributional divergence but stable entropy from 311 

genes in which recurrence produced either a more diffuse or more focused isoform landscape (Fig. 312 

4f; Supplementary Fig. 5e,f). The JSD –entropy quadrant analysis showed that remodeling classes 313 

differ not only in whether their isoform distributions diverge, but also in how recurrence changes 314 

isoform complexity (Fig. 4f). For example, many Class A and Class B genes remained in low -JSD 315 

bins, indicating that dominant isoform identity or secondary isoform structure can change without 316 

producing the largest global distributional divergence. In contrast, Classes C, D, and E contained 317 

larger fractions of high -JSD genes, consistent with stronger remodeling when dominant isoforms are 318 

lost, gained, or absent. Entropy further separated high -JSD genes into cases where recurrence 319 

produced a more diffuse isoform landscape, a more focused isoform landscape, or a large usage 320 

shift with relatively stable complexity. Thus, the JSD –dIF –entropy model captures three related but 321 

distinct dimensions of isoform remodeling: distributional divergence, largest isoform -fraction shift, 322 

and change in isoform -usage complexity. 323

random but concentrates in genes linked to structural and motility -associated biology 327

The second approach used Dirichlet -multinomial modeling with DRIMSeq 16 to identify genes with 332 

statistically significant differential transcript usage while accounting for patient pairing. The 333 

distribution of gene -level adjusted P values indicated a subset of genes with significant or near -334 

significant transcript -usage remodeling (Fig. 4g). Enrichment analysis of DTU -significant genes (271 335

DTU gene structural -category composition was broadly similar between primary and recurrent 339 

tumors, indicating that the most important signal was not a total replacement of transcript annotation 340 

classes, but selective redistribution within and across transcript structures (Fig. 4h). At the isoform 341 

level, DTU events involved both increased and decreased usage of full -splice match, incomplete -342 

splice match, and novel -in -catalog isoforms, showing that recurrent transcript usage changes were 343 

distributed across annotated and partially novel transcript structures (Fig. 4i, Supplementary Fig. 7b). 344 

When DTU -significant genes were overlaid onto the Class A –F isoform -remodeling framework, many 345 

DTU genes still fell into the stable Class F category, while smaller subsets mapped to Class A, Class 346 

C, and Class D remodeling classes, indicating that many apparently stable genes show significant 347 

remodeling of their transcript usage (Supplementary Fig. 7c). This distinction is important: Dirichlet -348 

multinomial modeling detects statistically significant transcript -usage shifts, whereas the 349 

JSD/dIF/entropy model prioritizes larger effect -size remodeling of gene -level isoform architecture. 350 

The two approaches are therefore complementary rather than redundant. Paired isoform trajectory 351 

examples, including DELE1 and SLC25A6, showed reciprocal gain and loss of specific isoforms 352 

across patients, illustrating how transcript usage remodeling can change dominant isoform 353 

architecture even when the same gene remains globally expressed in both primary and recurrent 354 

tumors (Supplementary Fig. 7d,e). 355 

Overall, the RNA analysis shows that recurrent astrocytoma s undergo transcriptome remodeling 356 

at multiple resolutions. Conventional gene -level analysis captures recurrence -associated expression 357 

and program shifts, but long -read RNA sequencing reveals a deeper layer of isoform repertoire 358 

turnover, dominant and secondary isoform remodeling, entropy -defined changes in transcript usage 359 

complexity, and statistically significant differential transcript usage. These data establish that 360 

recurrence -associated RNA remodeling is not simply a change in gene abundance. Instead, 361 

recurrent tumors reorganize the structure and usage of expressed transcripts, providing the 362 

substrate for the ORF/protein -fate analysis and DNA -RNA integration analyses that follow. 363 

➢ Isoform remodeling is linked to distinct ORF/protein consequences not captured by 364 

traditional sequencing approaches 365 

Having established that recurrent astrocytoma s undergo extensive isoform remodeling, we next 366 

asked whether these transcript changes were predicted to alter coding potential or protein -output 367

Discussion 824 

In this study, we used paired long -read DNA and RNA sequencing to show that recurrent 825 

astrocytoma remodeling is not only a genetic or transcriptional process, but a multi -layer DNA -to -826 

RNA -to -isoform -to -ORF remodeling trajectory. The central finding is that genetic and epigenetic 827 

alterations acquired or retained during recurrence can be traced to distinct RNA consequences, 828 

including gene -expression shifts, isoform redistribution, allele - or haplotype -specific transcript usage, 829 

expressed mutant RNA, SV -linked transcript architecture, and predicted ORF/protein -fate changes. 830 

Importantly, these effects were not uniform across patients or DNA layers. CNV provided the 831 

broadest dosage -associated RNA signal, SVs provided the clearest architectural DNA -to -RNA links, 832 

expressed SNVs/indels provided high -specificity mutant RNA and isoform -specific mutant -833 

expression events, and DMRs showed context -dependent relationships with expression, isoform 834 

usage, and predicted coding output. Thus, the main contribution of this study is not simply that long -835 

read sequencing detects more events, but that matched long -read DNA and full -length RNA can 836 

connect recurrence -associated genome and epigenome remodeling to patient -specific transcript and 837 

predicted coding consequences. 838 

Glioma recurrence has been extensively studied using short -read genomic, transcriptomic, 839 

and epigenomic profiling, but most prior studies have described recurrence through molecular layers 840 

that remain partly disconnected. Paired glioblastoma studies showed that primary and recurrent 841 

tumors are clonally related yet often diverge through patient -specific genetic evolution, therapy -842 

associated selection, expression -state switching, and altered copy -number structure 4,18 . Large -scale 843 

diffuse glioma studies extended this framework by showing that many founding driver alterations are 844 

retained at recurrence, whereas recurrent tumors acquire heterogeneous changes in aneuploidy, 845 

subclonal architecture, hypermutation, cell -cycle alterations, transcriptional state, and 846 

microenvironmental composition 5,6,19 . More recent longitudinal single -cell and multi -omic studies 847 

further emphasized that glioma progression cannot be understood from DNA alone, because genetic 848 

evolution is accompanied by changes in malignant -cell state, DNA methylation, immune and stromal 849 

composition, proteomic state, and treatment -associated ecosystem remodeling 7,20,21 . Several studies 850 

have also interrogated IDH -mutant astrocytoma recurrence using multi -omics approaches. 851 

Vallentgoed et al. 8 identified cell cycling, altered differentiation, extracellular -matrix remodeling, and 852 

DNA methylation loss as convergent features of malignant progression. Rodriguez Almaraz et al. 9

853 

showed that recurrent high -grade tumors can acquire diverse RAS –MAPK pathway alterations 854 

associated with inferior survival, emphasizing that progression can converge at the pathway level 855 

despite heterogeneity in the individual genomic event. Rautajoki et al. 10 reported increased 856 

rearrangements and deletions during progression to grade 4, together with recurrent CDKN2A/RB1 857 

inactivation, activating PDGFRA/MET alterations, DNA -repair changes, and NRG3 loss. Tang et al. 11 858 

showed that recurrent IDH -mutant astrocytomas increasingly adopt proliferative/progenitor and 859 

immune/mesenchymal -enriched proteomic states, with the latter characterized by gemistocytic 860 

differentiation and immune infiltration. Our findings fit an emerging disease -specific model in which 861 

IDH -mutant astrocytomas retain their founding molecular identity while acquiring heterogeneous 862 

progression -associated changes. Further, despite smaller cohort, our findings complement and 863 

support these previous studies. However, t he previous approaches using in the glioma or 864 

astrocytoma cohorts remain constrained by the read structure of short -read technologies. Short -read 865 

DNA sequencing is powerful for SNVs, indels, copy -number alterations, and mutational signatures, 866 

but has limited ability to resolve complex rearrangements, long -range haplotypes, repeat -associated 867 

structures, phased methylation states, and allele -specific structural context. Short -read RNA 868

The current approach differs also from classical long -read sequencing fundamentally. Many 875 

cancer cohorts long -read studies have used long -read DNA or RNA as powerful single -layer 876 

discovery tools 14,22 . Long -read DNA studies have resolved complex rearrangements, haplotypes, 877 

methylation landscapes, viral integrations, ecDNA, and repeat -associated structural events that are 878

An important advantage of this study is the introduction of new analytical modalities to 886 

resolve complex isoform remodeling and to connect the DNA and RNA layers. To date, s tandard 887 

long -read RNA analysis workflows often stops at transcript discovery, structural -category annotation, 888 

presence/absence analysis, or differential isoform abundance 12,13,23 . Those approaches are useful, 889 

but they cannot resolve whether there is remodel ing in isoform architecture without large gene -890 

expression change . We therefore introduce complementary approaches to quantify isoform 891 

remodeling that can detect significant changes even within apparently stable genes . The class A–F892 

framework separated dominant isoform switching, same -dominant secondary remodeling, primary -893 

dominant isoform loss, recurrent -dominant isoform gain, no -dominant remodeling, and stable genes. 894 

Jensen –Shannon divergence and maximum absolute delta isoform fraction quantified the overall 895 

distributional shift and the largest isoform -level usage change, while Shannon entropy measured 896 

whether recurrence made isoform usage more diffuse or more focused. This entropy component is 897 

important because two genes can show similar divergence but opposite changes in transcript 898 

complexity. In parallel, Dirichlet -multinomial modeling with DRIMSeq provided statistical evidence of 899 

differential transcript usage 16 . These analyses are not redundant: JSD/dIF/entropy prioritize effect 900 

size and architecture, whereas DTU prioritizes statistical redistribution of isoform proportions. 901 

Together with ORF prediction and transcript -to -genome coordinate lifting 17 , this framework converts 902 

long -read RNA from an isoform catalog into a consequence -aware model of isoform and predicted 903 

coding -output remodeling. Another important advantage of this work is the demonstration of the 904 

power of long read multiomics in resolving how genomic events can be reflected at the gene, 905 

isoform, or even protein level . A common problem in multi -omics is that overlap is mistaken for 906 

mechanism. If all DNA events are used as the denominator, many silent or non -quantifiable events 907 

dilute DNA -to -RNA reflection. If only RNA -altered genes are considered, broad DNA context can be 908 

overinterpreted as causal support. We therefore separated global DNA events from RNA -detected 909 

genes, patient -matched RNA -detected patient -gene pairs, and RNA -quantifiable patient -gene pairs. 910 

We also distinguished high -specificity DNA –RNA/ORF support from directional or layer -concordant 911 

support and broad DNA context. Such a framework allowed us to understand the complex and 912

Biologically, the study supports a model in which recurrent gliomas preserve core driver 915

and mutant -isoform -linked candidates. The broader implication is that precision oncology in recurrent 938

patient -specific DNA -to -RNA -to -isoform -to -ORF remodeling chains. 940

Patient cohort 943 

Among participants enrolled in a study approved by the Ethics Committee of Tohoku University 944 

Hospital (2025 -1-1029), six patients with IDH -mutant astrocytoma were selected based on the 945 

availability of paired tumor tissues resected at initial diagnosis and recurrence. Tumor specimens 946 

were flash -frozen in liquid nitrogen immediately after surgical resection and stored at −80°C until 947 

nucleic acid extraction. Peripheral blood samples were collected before the initial surgery in EDTA -948 

2Na blood collection tubes, and whole blood was stored at −80°C until DNA extraction. Written 949 

informed consent was obtained from all participants. Cohort information are presented in 950

DNA extraction 953 

High -molecular -weight genomic DNA was extracted using the Nanobind PanDNA kit (PacBio, Cat. 954 

No. 103 -260 -000) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. For tumor specimens, approximately 10 955 

mg of frozen tissue was dissected using a scalpel and homogenized by 10 strokes using a Dounce 956 

homogenizer. The homogenate was then transferred to a new tube, and the remaining extraction 957 

steps were performed according to the kit protocol. For matched blood samples, high -molecular -958
