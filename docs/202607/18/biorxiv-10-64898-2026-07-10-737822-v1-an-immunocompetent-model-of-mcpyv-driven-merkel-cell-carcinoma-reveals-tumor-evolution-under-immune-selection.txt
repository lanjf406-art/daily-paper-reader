Abstract 1

Merkel cell carcinoma (MCC) is a neuroendocrine skin tumor that is frequently driven by 2integration of Merkel cell polyomavirus (MCPyV). In MCC, the MCPyV genome is truncated, but 3expression of the viral tumor antigens, truncated large tumor antigen (LTT) and small tumor 4antigen (sT), is maintained and drives uncontrolled proliferation. We introduced constitutive 5expression of the MCPyV T antigens (TAs) into primary mouse dermal fibroblasts (MDFs) to 6determine whether these cells are susceptible to MCPyV-driven transformation. TA expression 7alone in MDFs induced key MCC markers, cytokeratin-20 (CK20) and Sry-box transcription factor 82 (SOX2), and promoted anchorage-independent growth indicative of cellular transformation. 9Subcutaneous implantation of TA-transformed fibroblasts produced high-grade MCC-like tumors 10 that grew persistently in immunodeficient NSG mice but not in immunocompetent C57BL/6 11 mice. Serial in vivo passaging of the tumor cell line enhanced tumor growth, reduced expression 12 of p53-target genes and MHC-1, and was accompanied by a shift in T antigen isoform 13 expression, with decreased LTT and increased sT expression. Our data demonstrate that MCPyV-14 driven tumors acquire immune-evasive adaptions during tumor progression in vivo and suggest 15 that the anti-tumor immune response exerts selective pressure in MCC that favors expression of 16 sT rather than LTT. The model established in this study provides a unique platform for studying 17 evolution of MCPyV-driven tumors under immune pressure and identifying mechanisms of 18 immune evasion in MCC that could be used to develop new therapeutic strategies. 19 

Significance Statement 20 

MCPyV tumor antigen expression transforms mouse dermal fibroblasts to generate MCC-like 21 tumors. Serial in vivo passaging reveals tumor evolution under immune pressure, providing a 22 model to study immune evasion mechanisms in MCC. 23

Introduction 27 

28 

Merkel cell carcinoma (MCC) is a rare neuroendocrine tumor of the skin with poor clinical 29 outcomes and a rising incidence(1-3). Surgery is the first-line treatment option for local MCC but 30 patients often present with more advanced disease(4). MCC frequently metastasizes to lymph 31 nodes and other distant sites where systemic treatments such as immune checkpoint inhibitor 32 therapy are needed(5). While the PD-L1/PD-1 immune checkpoint has been a successful strategy 33 in treating advanced MCC(5, 6), almost half of all patients do not respond to these 34 treatments(7). Improved experimental models are needed to better understand MCPyV-driven 35 oncogenesis and identify new therapeutic strategies. 36 In 2008, a small DNA virus, Merkel cell polyomavirus (MCPyV), was found to be integrated into 37 the host genome in about 80% of MCC cases(8). The MCPyV genome has two coding regions, 38 which are expressed at different time points in the viral life cycle. The early region encodes the 39 nonstructural proteins large T antigen (LT), small T antigen (sT), 57kT antigen, and alternative 40 large T antigen open reading frame (ALTO), and the late region encodes the capsid proteins VP1 41 and VP2. Both regions are under the control of the non-coding regulatory region (NCRR). The 42 MCPyV genome is integrated in MCC resulting in a truncated form of LT (LTT). In MCC with 43 integrated MCPyV (MCPyV+ MCC), the tumor mutational burden is relatively low when 44

3compared to MCC without integrated MCPyV (MCPyV- MCC)(9, 10). This contrast suggests that 45 integration of MCPyV is an important factor in oncogenesis of MCPyV+ MCC. Expectedly, 46 expression of the viral T antigens (TAs) is maintained as a key feature of the tumor and is 47 required for continued proliferation(11). 48 Both LTT and sT possess oncogenic functions and have been demonstrated to have transforming 49 capabilities in vitro (12, 13). The major oncogenic function of LTT involves inhibition of the tumor 50 suppressor retinoblastoma protein (Rb). Truncation of the MCPyV genome removes most 51 functional regions of LT but retains an LxCxE motif that is conserved in polyomaviruses(14, 15). 52 In MCPyV+ MCC, the LxCxE motif of LTT binds to Rb and prevents it from repressing E2F 53 transcription, leading to uncontrolled progression through the cell cycle(16, 17). sT has multiple 54 oncogenic functions, impacting both translation and transcription in the host cell. sT has been 55 shown to dysregulate cap-dependent translation by reducing turnover of hyperphosphorylated 56 eukaryotic translation initiation factor 4E-binding protein 1(4E-BP1)(12). Hyperphosphorylation 57 of 4E-BP1 was then demonstrated to be necessary for sT-induced transformation(12). Previous 58 work has also shown that sT can interact with transcription factors MYCL and EP400 to induce 59 transcriptional changes and indirectly inhibit the tumor suppressor tumor protein 53 (p53)(18, 60 19). Expression of sT has also been shown to modulate the interferon response which may 61 contribute to immune evasion in MCC(20). 62 Several mouse models of MCC have been created by expressing MCPyV TAs under the control of 63 epithelial promoters, resulting in epithelial hyperplasia and increased cellular proliferation(21, 64 22), and the development of cutaneous papillomas in some mice(23). Other models combining 65 MCPyV TA expression with additional genetic alterations, including Merkel cell lineage 66 reprograming and loss of tumor suppressors such as p53 or Rb, have achieved tumor growth in 67 mice that closely resembles human MCC(24-27). Most existing MCC mouse models assume an 68 epithelial origin of MCC, but human dermal fibroblasts (HDFs) represent a compelling alternative 69 candidate because they are the only cell type currently known to support the complete MCPyV 70 life cycle in vitro (28). Recently, MCPyV DNA was detected in the dermal papillae of healthy skin, 71 suggesting that the reservoir for MCPyV in the skin may reside in the dermal compartment of 72 the hair follicle(29). MCPyV+ MCC lacks the UV mutational signature characteristic of epidermal 73 tumors, suggesting that the cell of origin may reside in a UV-protected niche such as the 74 dermis(30). These observations suggest that dermal fibroblasts could be susceptible to MCPyV-75 mediated oncogenic transformation. To address this, we investigated whether expression of 76 MCPyV TAs alone is sufficient to transform primary mouse dermal fibroblasts (MDFs). We 77 demonstrate that TA-expressing MDFs undergo oncogenic transformation, exhibiting 78 anchorage-independent growth, expression of canonical MCC markers including cytokeratin-20 79 (CK20) and sry-box transcription factor 2 (SOX2), transcriptional programs characteristic of MCC, 80 and formation of MCC-like tumors in vivo .81 Although MCPyV+ MCC is considered highly immunogenic owing to its high expression of viral 82 proteins, the tumor also possesses several immunosuppressive mechanisms(31). Our results 83 show that MCPyV TAs were sufficient to transform MDFs, but the transformation did not confer 84 sufficient immune-evasive properties to support progressive growth in immunocompetent 85 C57BL/6 mice. The inherent immunogenicity of the model provided an opportunity to 86 investigate how MCPyV-driven tumors adapt under host immune pressure. We performed serial 87 

in vivo passaging of the TA-transformed cells, which selected for improved tumor growth, 88

Isolation of mouse dermal fibroblasts 97 

Ear tissue was obtained from C57BL/6 mice. Tissue was submerged in 70% ethanol for 10 98 seconds and air dried for 5 minutes at room temperature. The tissue was cut into several pieces 99 and placed into a solution of Dulbecco’s phosphate buffered saline (DPBS) (Life technologies, 100 14190-136) containing 10mg/mL Dispase II (Sigma, D4693) and 5X antibiotic-antimycotic (Life 101 technologies, 15240-062), and incubated for 16 hours at 4 ºC. The tissue was then briefly rinsed 102 in DPBS before being minced and incubated in a solution of DMEM/F12 (Life technologies, 103 113200-33) containing 1.5mg/mL Collagenase IV (Life technologies, 17104-019) and 5X 104 antibiotic-antimycotic at 37 ºC for 4 hours. The resulting suspension was then poured through a 105 100μm cell strainer (Biologix ,15-1100) into a 50mL conical tube (Corning, 352098). The strainer 106 was then rinsed with DMEM/F12 and the cells were centrifuged at 180g for 5 minutes. Cells 107 were resuspended in DMEM (Life technologies, 11965-084) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine 108 serum (FBS) (Cytiva, SH30071.03) and 5X antibiotic-antimycotic. Cells were maintained in 109 medium containing 5X antibiotic-antimycotic for the first three passages, after which the 110 concentration was reduced to 1X. 111 

Generation of mouse dermal fibroblasts stably expressing MCPyV TAs 112 

To generate a lentiviral vector for expressing MCPyV tumor antigens, the truncated MCPyV TA 113 sequence from the WaGa cell line was cloned into a modified version of the pTRIPZ lentiviral 114 vector under control of the human EF1a promoter. A puromycin resistance gene under control 115 of an internal ribosome entry site was placed downstream of the TA gene. To generate 116 lentivirus, HEK293T cells at 70% confluence in a 10cm dish were transfected with 2.5μg the 117 envelope plasmid pMD2.G, 7.5μg of the packaging plasmid pSPAX2, and 10μg of either the 118 lentiviral vector containing MCPyV TAs or an empty vector control. Transfection was performed 119 using lipofectamine 2000 (Thermo fisher scientific, 11668-027). Briefly, 45μL of lipofectamine 120 2000 was added to 500μL Opti-MEM (Life technologies, 31985-062) and incubated for 5 minutes 121 at room temperature. Following the incubation, an additional 500μL of Opti-MEM containing 122 the plasmid DNA was mixed into the solution. The transfection mixture was incubated for 20 123 minutes at room temperature before being added directly to the HEK293Ts. After 16-18 hours, 124 the media on the HEK293Ts was replaced with fresh media. At 48 hours post-transfection, the 125 supernatant was filtered through 0.45μm PVDF filters and hexadimethrine bromide (Sigma, 126 H9268) was added to a final concentration of 6μg/mL. The supernatant was then added to MDFs 127 in culture and incubated for 24 hours before the media was changed. Cells were selected with 4 128 μg/mL of puromycin (Sigma, P8833) for 1 week. 129

RNA-seq 226 

Bulk RNA sequencing was performed using Plasmidsaurus. Cells were washed in PBS and then 227 detached using a non-enzymatic detachment solution (Sigma, C5914). Cells were counted using 228 a hemocytometer, and 100,000 cells were pelleted and resuspended in 50μL of RNA/DNA Shield 229 (Zymo, R1100-50). The samples were sent to Plasmidsaurus where RNA was extracted and 230 sequenced using a 3’ end counting approach with Illumina library prep including unique 231 molecular identifiers (UMIs). Reads were filtered using FastP with poly-X tail trimming, 3' 232 quality-based tail trimming, a minimum Phred quality score of 15, and a minimum length 233 requirement of 50 bp. Reads were then aligned to the reference genome using STAR aligner and 234 BAM files were coordinate sorted using samtools. UMI based deduplication was done using 235 UMICollapse. FASTQ files containing deduplicated reads were generated using samtools and 236 transcripts were quantified using Salmon. Transcript quantification data was imported into 237 RStudio using the tximeta package and summarized to gene-level data. The counts were then 238 normalized using DESeq2’s vst function. Genes were ranked for fast gene set enrichment 239 analysis (fgsea) by taking the difference in the normalized counts between the two groups being 240 compared. The fgsea R package was used to determine if there was gene enrichment in 241 MSigDB’s hallmark gene sets and graphs were generated using ggplot2. 242

Image analysis and quantification 295 

Tissue sections were analyzed using TissueLab(33), an integrated medical imaging analysis 296 platform supporting deep learning-based and custom computational pipelines for high-297 throughput tissue quantification, to assess expression of the MCC tumor marker CK20 across 298 multiple sections. 299 The analysis proceeded in three primary stages: segmentation, spatial masking, and intensity 300 quantification. Nuclei segmentation was performed on DAPI-stained sections using a deep 301 learning-based approach, generating precise contour coordinates for each detected nucleus, 302 including those in high-density cellular regions. Quantification was carried out using a custom 303 Python-based analysis pipeline integrated within the TissueLab platform. 304 For the cytoplasmic marker CK20, which exhibits both cytoplasmic and perinuclear localization, a 305 morphological dilation strategy was employed to detect and quantify the perinuclear CK20 306 signal. Specifically, a 15-pixel perinuclear ring was generated by dilating each nuclear contour 307 and subtracting the original nuclear area, yielding a standardized region for measuring average 308 CK20 pixel intensity. 309 Tumor marker positivity for individual cells was determined using fixed intensity thresholds 310 (CK20 ≥ 15), established based on global intensity distributions across the dataset. These 311 thresholds were applied uniformly across all samples to enable consistent comparative analysis 312 of tumor-positive cell populations. 313

MCPyV tumor antigens induce oncogenic transformation of primary dermal fibroblasts 317 

To determine whether MCPyV TAs can induce oncogenic transformation in primary dermal 318 fibroblasts, MDFs were engineered to express MCPyV T antigens using a lentiviral vector 319 containing the truncated T antigen sequence from the MCC cell line WaGa. This specific T 320 antigen sequence was chosen because WaGa is considered to be representative of MCC 321 tumors(34). Stable TA-expressing cells (MDF-TA) were generated by puromycin selection (Figure 322 1A). MCPyV T antigen expression inhibits Rb but not p53 in mouse dermal fibroblasts 385 

LTT has previously been shown to inhibit the tumor suppressor Rb by binding via its LxCxE motif, 386 while sT is thought to be responsible for inhibition of another key tumor suppressor, p53(15, 387 19). These findings are consistent with LTT-mediated 395 inhibition of Rb occurring independently of Rb phosphorylation. resulted in palpable tumors in 5 of 6 mice by day 6 (Figure 4A). On day 6 and 7, three mice 421 

were euthanized and the tumors were removed to isolate tumor-derived cells (Figure 4B). 422 

In the remaining mice with MDF-TA-12 tumors, tumor growth peaked around day 8–9 but 423 

subsequently regressed, and viable tumor cells could no longer be recovered by day 12. 424 

Histological analysis demonstrated highly cellular tumors composed of poorly 425 

differentiated cells, consistent with human MCC morphology (Figure 4C). 426

12 cells were injected into immunodeficient NOD-scid-gamma (NSG) mice. In this setting, 428

4B). The tumors grew aggressively, with several mice reaching humane endpoints by day 430

critical role in controlling growth of TA-transformed fibroblasts in immunocompetent hosts. 436

Discussion 484 

485 

MCPyV tumor antigens are essential drivers of MCPyV+ MCC, but many aspects of MCPyV -486 driven oncogenesis remain unclear. MCPyV+ tumors don’t usually possess obvious oncogenic 487 mutations outside of MCPyV integration, yet transformation of primary skin cells by MCPyV TAs 488 alone has not been demonstrated(9, 10). Our data demonstrate that expression of the MCPyV 489 TAs is sufficient to transform primary MDFs into cells that share molecular and phenotypic 490 similarities with MCC. The TA-transformed MDFs exhibited anchorage-independent growth, 491 expressed MCC-associated markers, displayed transcriptomic changes characteristic of MCC, 492 and formed tumors in vivo . Unlike most previously described models, which rely on epithelial 493 lineage reprogramming or additional engineered oncogenic alterations to achieve tumor 494 formation, expression of MCPyV T antigens alone was sufficient to generate MCC-like tumors. 495 This enabled us to monitor tumor progression without introducing additional genetic drivers. 496

14 Our work has thus established an immunocompetent model for studying MCPyV-driven 497 tumorigenesis and tumor evolution under immune pressure. 498 We found that the transformed fibroblasts acquired multiple MCC-like molecular features. 499 Notably, we detected expression of the key MCC markers CK20 and SOX2 (Figure 2A, 2B, 2C). 500 Expression of the neuroendocrine genes CHGA, ENO2, NEFL, SYP, and NCAM1 was detected in 501 the transformed cells as well, although expression was generally low and could not be 502 confirmed at the protein level for all neuroendocrine genes (Figure 2A, 2D-2H). In contrast, 503 these cells lacked expression of ATOH1, the classical Merkel cell lineage transcription factor 504 (Figure 2A). Although ATOH1 is considered a key regulator of the Merkel cell lineage, Fan et al. 505 demonstrated that ATOH1 is not actually required for proliferation of MCC cells(37). Moreover, 506 ATOH1 expression varies substantially among MCC tumors, and higher ATOH1 expression has 507 been associated with more advanced disease (38). Despite lacking ATOH1, this model 508 recapitulates many key features of MCPyV-positive MCC while providing a genetically simple, 509 immunocompetent system for studying MCPyV-driven tumor evolution under host immune 510 selection. We detected expression of both LTT and sT in the transformed cells, although there 511 was a noticeable skew toward LTT compared to the more balanced LTT/sT expression in WaGa 512 cells (Figure 3B, 3C). We were unable to detect expression of sT protein in the MDF cell lines or 513 MCC cell lines, although this issue has been reported on previously and has been attributed to 514 low endogenous expression(39). Vimentin expression confirmed the mesenchymal identity of 515 the transformed cells (Figure 1C), highlighting a distinction from human MCC. However, RNA-516 seq analysis of the clonal MDF-TA cell lines, MDF-TA-4.1 and MDF-TA-12, indicated a negative 517 enrichment score in EMT-related genes, suggesting a partial shift towards an epithelial 518 transcriptional program following MCPyV TA expression (Figure 3A). Dermal fibroblasts 519 comprise several distinct subtypes with different biological properties, and the susceptibility of 520 dermal fibroblasts to MCPyV-mediated transformation may therefore not be uniform across all 521 dermal fibroblast subtypes(40, 41). Consistent with this possibility, several neuroendocrine 522 markers were detected at low levels in the vector control cells, suggesting that some fibroblast 523 heterogeneity is retained in vitro (Figure 2C, 2E, 2F). Accordingly, we cannot determine whether 524 TA-expression directly induces neuroendocrine gene expression or whether fibroblast 525 subpopulations with pre-existing neuroendocrine features are preferentially transformed by 526 MCPyV. 527 Transcriptomic analysis of the TA-expressing MDFs revealed activation of proliferation-related 528 pathways associated with MCC. Transcription of E2F targets was the most enriched gene set in 529 all TA-expressing MDFs when compared to the vector control cells (Figure 3A), consistent with 530 LTT-mediated inhibition of Rb and activation of E2F-dependent transcription(16). Enrichment of 531 the G2/M checkpoint gene set was also observed in all MDF-TA cells, reflecting their highly 532 proliferative phenotype (Figure 3A). MYC target enrichment was observed in the two clonal 533 MDF-TA cell lines, consistent with the known ability of sT to interact with MYCL and promote 534 oncogenesis by altering transcriptional regulation in MCC(18). In contrast, although aberrant 535 mTOR signaling is a feature of MCC, increased mTOR pathway activity was observed only in 536 MDF-TA-12 cells compared with the vector control(12, 36, 42). Interestingly, Rb appeared to be 537 inhibited in all TA-expressing cells, whereas p53 remained functional (Figure 3D, 3F). This likely 538 reflects the LTT-dominant balance of T antigen expression in our model, as LTT has previously 539 been shown to activate p53, while sT suppresses p53 function(19). 540

15 We successfully established tumors using the MDF-TA-12 cells in both NSG and C57BL/6 mice 541 (Figure 4A). Histological analysis revealed highly cellular, poorly differentiated tumors (Figure 542 4C). In NSG mice, tumor cells infiltrated nearby muscle tissue, indicating aggressive growth 543 (Figure S5B). Tumor tissue exhibited uniform LTT expression (Figure 4G), but expression of CK20 544 was more heterogeneous, with some cells exhibiting the characteristic perinuclear dot-like 545 pattern (Figure 4G). Although tumors grew progressively in NSG mice, they regressed in C57BL/6 546 mice after approximately one week, indicating that the transformed cells alone were insufficient 547 to overcome immune surveillance in an immunocompetent host. 548 MCPyV+ MCC is considered a highly immunogenic tumor owing to expression of the MCPyV TAs, 549 yet successful tumors acquire multiple mechanisms to evade immune surveillance (43). 550 Downregulation of MHC-1 expression is frequently observed in MCC, allowing tumor cells to 551 escape recognition by cytotoxic T cells(44). STING is also silenced in MCPyV+ MCC and may 552 contribute to immune evasion(45). In some cases, MCC tumors express the checkpoint protein 553 PD-L1, which contributes to T-cell dysfunction and exhaustion(5, 6). Importantly, these immune-554 evasive mechanisms have not been shown to arise as a direct result of MCPyV TA expression 555 and may instead reflect adaptations acquired during tumor evolution under immune pressure. 556 Consistent with this idea, transformation of fibroblasts in vitro may lack the necessary selective 557 pressure to induce the immune-evasive changes associated with MCPyV+ MCC. To determine 558 whether the MDF-TA-12 cells could acquire these adaptations, we subjected the cells to serial in 559 

vivo passaging in immunocompetent C57BL/6 mice. While we were able to achieve improved 560 tumor growth and delayed tumor clearance, the tumors ultimately regressed. Serial in vivo 561 selection resulted in decreased MHC-1 expression and reduced expression of p53 target genes 562 (Figure 5C-E), indicating that immune pressure promotes adaptive remodeling of multiple 563 immune-related pathways. Although we hypothesized that in vivo selection pressure might 564 upregulate PD-L1 in the tumor cells, unexpectedly we observed significant decreases in PD-L1 565 expression following serial in vivo passage (Figure 5F). This finding suggests that immune 566 pressure does not simply select for a single immune-evasion mechanism but instead drives 567 broader remodeling of the tumor state. 568 One of the most interesting findings of this study was the coordinated shift from LTT towards sT 569 expression during serial in vivo passaging (Figure 5A, 5B). Rather than simply selecting for 570 reduced antigen presentation, our findings suggest that immune pressure may also reshape viral 571 oncogene expression. This observation is particularly intriguing because sT is considered the 572 dominant transforming oncogene in MCPyV-positive MCC and is required for maintenance of 573 the transformed phenotype(12). Moreover, LTT and sT have distinct functions in MCC biology, 574 including opposing effects on p53 signaling and inflammatory responses(19, 20), while sT is also 575 known to stabilize LTT(46). Immune pressure during serial in vivo passaging may favor sT 576 expression, and it seems plausible that immunoediting occurring during MCC oncogenesis may 577 influence splicing of the T antigen transcript to fine tune the relative abundance of each isoform 578 and achieve an optimal balance of the two oncogenes. Notably, decreases in MHC-1 expression 579 and p53 target gene expression preceded detectable changes in LTT/sT expression (Figure 5), 580 indicating that immune selection drives coordinated remodeling of multiple pathways, including 581 antigen presentation, p53 signaling, and viral oncogene expression, rather than selecting for a 582 single immune-evasion mechanism. Importantly, some adaptations seen in this model are 583 consistent with adaptations thought to occur during MCC progression and highlight the value of 584 this immunocompetent model for studying tumor evolution under immune pressure.
