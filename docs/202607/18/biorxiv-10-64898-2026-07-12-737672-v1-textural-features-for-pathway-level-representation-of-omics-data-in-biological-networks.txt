# Abstract 8

More than 50 years ago, Haralick and co -authors proposed a family of gray -level co -occurrence 9

statistics that became known as textur al features. These features are widely used in image 10 

analysis, but their application to biological networks has remained limited because cellular 11 

networks are sparse, irregular graphs rather than regular pixel grids. 12 

This work presents a network -adapted version of Haralick texture analysis for generating pathway -13 

level features from gene -level omics profiles. The resulting profiles reduce dimensionality and can 14 

be used as candidate predictors of anti -cancer drug response. Performance of these features is 15 

compared with original gene expression variables and with pathway features from network 16 

enrichment analysis (NEA), whose robustness has been demonstrated previously. Although 17 

technically simpler than NEA, Haralick features showed comparable sensitivity. More importantly, 18 

selected Haralick features were preserved between in vitro drug screens and clinical treatment -19 

associated survival analyses, supporting their potential use for prioritizing robust pathway -level 20 

drug -response correlates. 21

# Introduction 57 

Pathway enrichment analysis, used to summarize individual molecular profiles at the pathway 58 

level, is a popular approach in omics data interpretation. It helps characterize sets of genes that are 59 

differentially expressed or otherwise altered, for example in pathological conditions (Huang et al. 60 

2009) . It can be particularly useful for characterizing individual samples when pathway scores 61 

provide an alternative to original gene -level omics profiles, an approach initially presented as 62 

single -sample gene set enrichment analysis, ssGSEA (Barbie et al. 2009) . This can reduce 63 

dimensionality from thousands of genes to hundreds, or fewer, pathways. In settings where 64 

pathological alteration events are individually sparse or poorly detectable, summarizing them 65 

across samples and patients can be beneficial, especially in cancer, which is known for extreme 66 

heterogeneity at all molecular levels (Heng 2015) .67 

Pathway features can be evaluated in individual samples by accounting for the functional state of 68 

their member genes. However, a more common and simpler approach has been to analyze gene set 69 

enrichment, GSEA (or overrepresentation, ORA), i.e. quantifying overlap between genes in a 70 

pathway and genes that distinguish a biological condition (Subramanian et al. 2005) (Huang et al. 71 

2009) .72 

Modern human cellular networks, for example those derived from FunCoup (Alexeyenko and 73 

Sonnhammer 2009) , STRING (Szklarczyk et al. 2015) , or PathwayCommons (Rodchenkov et al. 74 

2020) , contain much more information than databases of discrete pathways. Such networks can 75 

include millions of edges, mostly supported by experimental evidence but not confined within 76 

pathway limits, and can therefore provide a richer biological context for sample characterization. 77 

We previously proposed network enrichment analysis (NEA) (Alexeyenko et al. 2012) (Jeggari and 78 

Alexeyenko 2017) , which evaluates the overrepresentation of network links between pathway genes 79 

and sample -specific genes, or altered gene sets (AGSs) rather than shared genes as in ORA. NEA is 80 

more complex than ORA because it uses a global interaction network and accounts for an 81 

additional parameter, the node degree of each gene. This brought higher sensitivity and greater 82 

feature robustness. It is worth noting that global networks probably have high false positive and 83 

false negative rates: many declar ed links do not exist, while many existing links remain unknown. 84 

However, this problem is equally relevant to network -free methods, which may include irrelevant 85 

genes in pathways or false positives from omics datasets. In both cases, the purpose of enrichment 86 

analysis is to evaluate signal strength against a noisy background. Enrichment analysis is facilitated 87 

by the availability of multiple expert -curated pathway databases and ontologies. Many functional 88 

gene sets (FGSs) provided by such resources could, either by design or by coincidence, be relevant 89 

to biological functions, conditions, and diseases. This makes the identified correlates not only 90 

statistically significant, but also biologically interpretable. 91

A more mechanistic alternative to enrichment would be to characterize pathway functional states, 92 

perturbations, or damage by monitoring the activity of key genes. However, pathway -state 93 

quantification, as in SPIA (Tarca et al. 2009) , PARADIGM  (Vaske et al. 2010) , Pathifier  (Drier et al. 94 

2013) , PROGENy  (Schubert et al. 2018) , or  VIPER (Alvarez et al. 2016) , is limited either by the 95 

requirement to know pathway maps and interaction modes precisely or by ignor ing the broader and 96 

deeper network context available from multi -source resources such as those mentioned above. 97 

The present work implements and analyzes a trade -off between pathway enrichment and pathway 98 

state approaches, while accounting for the global cellular network context. 99 

Texture features were proposed by Haralick and co -authors in 1973 (Haralick et al. 1973) and have 100 

been widely used to analyze pixel maps of satellite images (Moya et al. 2019) , wood patterns 101 

(Kobayashi et al. 2015) , biological tissues, and cellular compartments (Brynolfsson et al. 2017) . To 102 

my knowledge, Barker -Clarke and co -authors were the first, and so far the only team to apply 103 

Haralick textural features to biological network analysis. Using a collection of simpler example 104 

graphs, they showed that the features expectedly varied with graph topology (Barker -Clarke et al. 105 

2022) .106 

Here, Haralick texture features are tested for their ability to capture clinically relevant molecular 107 

landscapes. The statistical criterion is whether pathway -level correlates of clinical phenotypes can 108 

be detected and then preserved across experimental and clinical datasets. Molecular marker 109 

discovery is a major use case for omics data, but cancer correlates are often poorly reproducible 110 

because relevant molecular events are heterogeneous and sparse at the individual -gene level. 111 

Moving from genes, mutations, methylation events, or expression values to pathways can reduce 112 

dimensionality by summarizing sparse events into interpretable functional units. NEA applications 113 

have show n that network context can improve sensitivity and robustness compared with network -114 

free enrichment methods (Alexeyenko et al. 2012) (Franco et al. 2019) (Brink et al. 2019) 115 

(Alexeyenko et al. 2022) . As with PCA, pathway -level profiling reduces the number of variables and 116 

may limit overfitting in downstream multivariate models; unlike PCA, it preserves biological 117 

interpretability and facilitates visualization. 118 

The usability of in vitro models of drug response has been repeatedly questioned because 119 

molecular landscapes of cancer cell lines were found to deviate substantially from those of primary 120 

tumors (Domcke et al. 2013) . Although a more comprehensive analysis demonstrated overall 121 

consistency of molecular aberrations between primary tumors and relevant cell lines (Iorio et al. 122 

2016) , the clinical relevance of the discovered in vitro correlates was not investigated. A 123 

discouraging comparison between two in vitro screens was also published (Haibe -Kains et al. 124 

2013) , although later work showed that it was not statistically impeccable (Franco et al. 2019) . The 125 

latter work also demonstrated that concerns about cancer cell lines arise when individual gene 126 

profiles are considered and can be partly addressed at the pathway level. The performance of NEA -127 

based features was comprehensively tested against existing pathway methods, both network -128 

based and network -free (Franco et al. 2019) . NEA exceeded the alternatives in statistical power, for 129 

example because ORA and related methods suffered from sparse set overlaps, and in the 130

vitro and clinical response profiles. A technical complication in NEA was the need to delineate 132 

individual, sample -specific AGSs, which required optimization and fine -tuning of AGS derivation 133

performance of textural features against original gene -expression profiles and NEA features. 135 

Comparisons with other enrichment techniques have been performed previously and are therefore 136 

not repeated here. 137 

Unlike the example analyses of Barker -Clarke and co -authors , which were performed on whole 138 

networks, the present study is driven by the practical need of identifying clinically relevant and 139 

usable correlates. The pipeline therefore generates pathway -level texture features from pathway -140 

specific subgraphs of the global human network. Sample -specific feature values are computed 141 

from gene -expression values assigned to pathway nodes. In parallel, NEA features are generated for 142 

the same pathway collection, and original gene -expression features are used as predictors in the 143 

same statistical models. The key comparison criteria are sensitivity, specificity, and reproducibility 144 

of drug -response correlates within and across in vitro and clinical datasets. 145 

The compared methods are evaluated in single -feature models, with the expectation that the most 146 

robust features could later be used in multivariate predictors. Because multivariate prediction 147 

would require independent validation, this study focuses on the reproducibility of single -feature 148 

correlates. First, feature classes are compared in cancer cell -line data while controlling the false 149 

discovery rate. Next, identified correlates are tested for reproducibility in a lternative in vitro 150 

screens. Finally, the most challenging comparison asks whether in vitro correlates are preserved in 151 

clinical follow -up data from patients treated with the same drugs. 152 

# Results 153 

In the first step of the algorithm, network node values are discretized, usually into  𝐺 =2 n levels (Fig. 154 

1A). Next, a  𝐺 × 𝐺  matrix of level co -occurrences is calculated for neighbor nodes. 155 

Topologically, raster images analyzed by Haralick's method are regular graphs (Chen 1997) , in 156 

which every image pixel has exactly four adjacent neighbors, or eight if diagonals are counted. Even 157 

if the analysis is expanded to more remote neighbors, graph regularity is preserved. A crucial 158 

difference in biological networks is that their node degrees are highly irregular (Fig. 1B), usually 159 

following power -law or lognormal distributions (Clauset et al. 2009) . To accommodate this 160 

property, the original code from (Kobayashi et al. 2015 ; ) was modified. Another difference is that 161 

neighboring pixels can optionally be matched at four alternative angles from 0˚ to 135˚, whereas 162 

orientation is irrelevant for a network with undirected edges. 163 

In this work, the analysis is restricted to pathway -specific subgraphs of the global network. In the 164 

default HTF mode, Haralick texture features are calculated only from edges connecting pathway -165 

member genes. The alternative HOF mode additionally includes boundary edges in which only one 166 

of the two connected genes belongs to the analyzed pathway (Fig. 1C vs. 1D). 167

Unlike texture features , network enrichment analysis does not account directly for the activity of 168 

pathway genes (Fig. 1E). Instead, each cell line is characterized by an altered gene set (AGS). 169 

Network links between member genes in a pathway or functional gene set (FGS) of another type 170 

and genes in the AGS are then summed and normalized by values expected by chance, based on 171 

the cumulative node degrees, i.e. total numbers of links in the global network, of the AGS and FGS. 172 

Beyond trivial significance estimation, normalization gives higher importance to less connected, 173 

and hence potentially more context -specific, gene nodes. As an alternative to conventional multi -174 

node FGSs, NEA can also consider single -node FGSs, treating individual genes as “potential 175 

pathways” (Franco et al. 2019) . This option, GNEA, was also included in the comparative analysis. 176

Preservation of drug sensitivity correlates between in vitro drug screens 247 

The analysis above tested the overall ability of feature classes to explain drug response, with false 248 

positive rates addressed only through formal multiple -testing correction. For practical use, 249 

however, correlates should remain stable under changes in screening methodology, batch 250 

structure, and biological composition. Robustness was therefore evaluated using three 251 

independent drug -screen datasets: GDSC1, GDSC2, and CTRP v.2. In total, 138 drugs were shared 252 

by at least two screens. For each shared drug, agreement between two screens was estimated as 253 

the Spearman rank correlation between drug -feature association p -values. Because tumor site of 254 

origin strongly affects molecular phenotypes and drug response, site was included as a covariate in 255 

a two -factor model, with the feature effect estimated as a separate term. In parallel, univariate 256 

models were applied to seven site -specific cell -line subsets. 257

Analysis of TCGA clinical cohorts required a more complex statistical design. Because disease 273 

stage at diagnosis strongly influences patient survival, “stage ” was included as a covariate in Cox 274 

proportional -hazards models together with “drug ” and “feature ” terms. Statistical significance of 275 

the main “feature ” term indicated an association with survival irrespective of treatment. By this 276 

criterion, certain HTF and HOF features, such as con and var , outperformed other feature classes 277 

(Fig. 4A). 278 

The practical objective, however, is to identify features that are potentially predictive of drug 279 

response. Such a property should appear as a survival difference among patients who received a 280 

given drug, but not among patients who did not receive it. To evaluate this effect, a “drug × feature ”281 

interaction term was included in the model. A significant interaction indicates that higher or lower 282 

feature levels are associated with altered survival under treatment. The model is illustrated by the 283 

four -stratum Kaplan -Meier plots in Fig ure 5: survival differed by feature level among treated 284 

patients but not among untreated patients. With respect to the interaction term, HTF/HOF features 285 

generally performed below NEA/GNEA, although selected features were competitive in the LUAD 286 

and KIRC cohorts (Fig. 4B). 287 

Using this framework, we then addressed the most relevant and challenging question: to what 288 

extent are drug -feature correlates detected in CCLE preserved in TCGA survival analyses? Features 289 

of the same methods and classes were generated from gene expression by applying the same 290 

pipeline to CCLE and TCGA datasets. The analysis was restricted to drugs represented in both in 291 

vitro screens and clinical treatment records. After requiring that a drug had been administered to at 292 

least seven patients in a cohort, up to ten drugs were available per cohort and 20 drugs were 293 

available in total. The same drug could occur in up to five cohorts, but each cohort was analyzed 294 

separately. The p -value distributions indicated that the best HTF and HOF features performed at 295 

approximately the same level as NEA features (Fig. 6; Supplementary Files 2 and 3 ). 296

# Discussion 478 

Compared with raw gene -expression features, the proposed Haralick -type texture features and 479 

higher -order features produced pathway -level profiles with markedly reduced dimensionality and 480 

better preservation of drug -response correlates across datasets. Their performance was broadly 481 

comparable to NEA/GNEA rather than clearly superior to it. The main advantage of the new 482 

approach is therefore not an absolute gain in robustness, but its technical simplicity: it avoids 483 

explicit construction of altered gene sets, operates directly on quantitative node values, and can be 484 

extended by defining additional graph -level texture features. This makes the method a practical 485 

complement to NEA/GNEA and a flexible source of candidate variables for downstream predictive 486 

modelling. 487 

The observational nature of TCGA treatment data is a major limitation of this study. Although tumor 488 

stage was included in the clinical analysis, administration of specific drugs could still be 489 

confounded by unmeasured clinical conditions and unknown factors. Neither the proportional -490 

hazards assumption nor e.g. the requirements for Mendelian randomization analysis were fully met, 491 

making unbiased estimation of drug -by -feature effects unfeasible. Nonetheless, the analytical 492 

setup served the comparative purpose of the study: evaluating whether different methods and 493 

method -specific feature classes preserve in vitro correlates in clinical data. The absence of 494 

randomized clinical controls was also addressed by random permutation tests. By this criterion, 495 

preservation of the correlates discovered in vitro was better for pathway -level NEA and HTF features 496 

than for gene -level GNEA and, especially, original gene -expression profiles (Fig. 6). 497 

A practical advantage of Haralick features over NEA is that the magnitude, and therefore potential 498 

sensitivity, of several Haralick features did not depend on pathway size or on the total /mean 499 

pathway node degrees. This was particularly clear for the best -performing features sha , sav , and 500 

var , and also for con , but not for den or f13 (Supplementary File 4). In contrast, absolute NEA 501 

values correlated with pathway size and total node degree , while, a s expected from NEA 502 

normalization , n o correlation with mean node degree was observed. 503 

Haralick and NEA features share one important property: both account for sample -specific gene 504 

activity. However, NEA considers only most altered genes and ignores activity of pathway genes 505 

unless they are also included in the AGS. When pathway -level features we re calculated with NEA, 506 

normalization by node degree wa s required because node degree sum s var y across sample -507 

specific AGSs. Haralick features d o not use AGSs , so node -degree variability across samples is 508 

absent for a fixed pathway and normalization not required for within -pathway comparisons. 509 

> .CC-BY 4.0 International license made available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted July 17, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.07.12.737672 doi: bioRxiv preprint

Normalization would, however, be needed for direct comparisons between pathways or between 510 

whole networks with different degree distributions. Analyses such as those of Barker -Clarke and 511 

co -authors, which compared whole networks with different topologies and degree distributions, 512 

would therefore benefit from explicit degree adjustment. 513 

However, even node -degree normalization in NEA may not fully account for higher -order topological 514 

properties. The relative independence of HTF/HOF features from such properties is therefore 515 

valuable, although cross -pathway comparisons of raw HTF/HOF magnitudes are not necessarily 516 

interpretable. Further development of the method should address this need with a suitable 517 

normalization technique. 518 

The ability to vary or extend the set of textural features is another advantage of the Haralick 519 

approach and remains an avenue for future mechanistically oriented network analysis work. 520
