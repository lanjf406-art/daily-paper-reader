# Abstract 1

2

Current transcriptomics –metabolomics integration approaches are either limited by poor 3

interpretability or constrained by incomplete prior knowledge, preventing the systematic 4

identification of regulatory metabolites. Here, we present Lemonite, a data ‑driv en and 5

interpretable framework for integrating bulk transcriptomics and metabolomics data to uncover 6

regulatory metabolites acting on gene modules. Lemonite extends module network inference to 7

jointly associate transcription factors and metabolites with ge ne programs, without requiring prior 8

differential analysis or complete metabol ome annotation. To contextualize predictions, we 9

construct ed a comprehensive gene /protein –metabolite knowledge graph integrating over 370 000 10 

metabolite –gene /protein and 2.1 mill ion protein –protein interactions. Applied to glioblastoma 11 

(n=99) and inflammatory bowel disease (n=75) cohorts, Lemonite identifie d over 50 functionally 12 

coherent gene modules per disease, revealing established and previously uncharacterized 13 

metabolite –gene regulatory relationships. In glioblastoma, myo ‑inositol and phosphatidylcholines ,14 

together with IRF6, regulate mesenchymal ‑like immune programs , which upon integration with 15 

single ‑cell transcriptomics are primarily expressed in tumor -associated macrophage s and 16 

monocytes. In inflammatory bowel disease, regulatory metabolites were prioritized that change 17 

the expression of their predicted target genes in colonic epithelial cells in vitro . Overall , Lemonite 18 

provides a principled framework to explore the genome ‑wide regulatory potential of the 19 

metabolome and to generate biologically interpretable, experimentally testable hypotheses from 20 

multi ‑omics data. 21

# Introduction 1

Life on Earth is shaped by the dynamic interplay between different biomolecules that enable s2

cells to maintain homeostasis and respond to changing environments. These molecular networks 3

are tightly interwoven such that signals propagate across multiple molecular layers , thus 4

maintaining homeostasis or orchestrating coordinated response s to pertur bation. In this regard, 5

the cellular metabolome has traditionally been considered the endpoint of regulatory cascades, 6

acting as a phenotypic readout and implicit ly integrating upstream genomic, epigenomic, 7

transcriptomic and proteomic regulatory phenomena 1,2 . Today , a paradigm shift emerges as8

researchers increasingly recogniz e the regulatory potential of the metabolome , with studies 9

show ing that metabolite s affect protein stability 3, protein -protein interaction networks 4,10 

transcription facto r activity (TFA) and specificity 5, and act as substrates for histone modifications 11 

and chromatin re organization 6,7 . For example , the presence of citric acid cycle enzymes in the 12 

nucleus has been shown to directly influence nuclear acetyl -CoA and histone acetylation levels, 13 

having a downstream regulatory effect on several pluripotency genes 8. Such observations call 14 

for a multi -modal system s-orien ted approach to study gene regulation, where not only nucleic 15 

acids and proteins, but also small molecule metabolites are considered. 16 

For this purpose, we define ‘regulatory metabolites’ as small biomolecules originating from 17 

metabolic processes within the diet -host -microbiota axis , which have the potential to influence the 18 

expression levels of a specific set of target genes or proteins, either directly or indirectly. In 19 

contrast to transcriptional regulation of cellular metabolism, limited efforts have foc used on 20 

uncovering the regulatory potential of the metabolome, especially in high -throughput contexts 9,10 .21 

While many computational methods exist to model metabolism using transcriptomics or 22 

proteomics data 11 –14 , ma ny of these tools do not consider metabolomics data itself but leverage 23 

transcriptomics or proteomics data as a proxy for metabolic enzyme acti vity . Consequently , there 24 

is currently a lack of both data and data integration approaches that allow studying the genome -25

Lemoni te is an interpretable, data -driven multi -omics integration methodology that 20 

identifies regulatory metabolites and target genes 21 

To overcome the aforementioned limitations, we developed a multi -omics data integration 22 

strategy tailored towards, but not limit ed to, bulk transcriptomics and metabolomics datasets by 23 

building on the existing module network inference software suite LemonTree 34 . First, t ranscription 24 

factor act ivities (TFAs) were calculated on normalized, highly variable gene selected, scaled 25 

transcriptomics data using DecouplR and the CollecTri TF -target gene network encompassing 43 26 

175 signed interactions for 1 186 TFs (Methods) 30,54 . By default 100 independent model -based 27 

Gibbs samplers simultaneously inferred co -expression clusters from transcriptomics data, after 28 

which the results were combined into consensus modules through spectral edge clustering 34 . Per 29 

regulator type (TF, metabolite , lipid, other ), an ensemble of regulatory programs was inferred non -30 

linearly for each module based on decision trees leveraging TFA (or TF expression levels) and 31 

normalized and scaled meta bolite and lipid abundances (or other omics if provided). For each 32

# Discussion 1

Metabolites (including lipids), along with nucleic acids and proteins, play pivotal roles within 2

complex, interdependent molecular and cellular networks. As such, phen otypical variation in 3

complex living systems does not arise from a single omics layer acting in isolation but from the 4

dynamic interplay among these interconnected components (Klein et al., 2021; Ball, 2025). 5

Metabolic reprogramming in cancer, for instance , is not limited to changes in metabolite levels: 6

these in turn actively affect chromatin accessibility and gene expression by providing substrates 7

and cofactors for chromatin -modifying enzymes, such that metabolites act as true molecular 8

'regulators' 109 . Yet current multi -omics integration methods largely fa il to capture this regulatory 9

role of metabolites directly. Lemonite addresses this gap by 1) building an extensive 10 

protein/metabolite -gene knowledgebase, the Lemonite KG, which unifies information across 11 

databases under a single unified metabolite identif ier (HMDB ID), 2) including all metabolites —12 

also un - or ambiguously annotated ones — in the data integration process, 3) requiring no prior 13 

differential analysis or feature selection before integration, 4) performing integration in a fully data -14 

driven ma nner, unconstrained by known metabolite -gene interactions or pathways, and 5) 15 

producing an interpretable metabolite -TF -module network that is further contextualized by the 16 

Lemonite KG. Together, these design choices distinguish Lemonite from existing appro aches, 17 

which typically require choosing between unbiased, data -driven discovery and mechanistic 18 

interpretability (see below). 19 

Multi -omics data integration methods traditionally fall into one of two categories. Data -driven 20 

methods such as MixOmics 20 , MOFA+ 23 , NMF 37,110 or MOMA 111 identify hidden underlying 21 

patterns in the data and allow unbiased exploration across a broad range of omics, but suffer from 22 

low interpretability and are sensitive to noise 1,2 . Prior -knowledge -based methods such as 23 

COSMOS(+) or joint pathway analysis are more easily interpretable, but restricted to known 24 

pathways and i nteractions 26,28 , typically require a pre -specified set of 'relevant features' (e.g. from 25

Applied to our GBM and IBD cohorts, DIABLO an d MOFA+ recovered biologically relevant 9

Several limitations warrant discussion. First, the high degree of incompleteness and 6

complementarity across metabolite -gene databases , likely in part driven by the understudied 7

nature of these inte ractions , argues against restricting integration to prior knowledge from the 8

outset . None of the metabolite -gene pairs selected for in vitro validation, for instance, were 9

annotated as 'interacting' in the Lemonite KG itself, which is why we did not use th e KG as ground 10 

truth for precision/recall benchmarking as is traditional for GRN inference methods (Pratapa et al., 11 

2020; Badia -i-Mompel, 2024; Nguyen et al., 2024). Second, experimental validation was 12 

performed in a single cell line (HT29) , and given that Lemonite modules display cell -type -specific 13 

expression patterns (Fig. 4C), this design likely underestimates true positives as well as producing 14 

false positives, and results should be interpreted with this caveat in mind rather than as a definitive 15 

positi ve/negative rate. Third, Lemonite's current regulator assignment integrates omics pairwise ,16 

i.e. TFs and metabolites independently from each other , which may miss higher -order 17 

dependencies detectable only through cooperative modeling. Finally, like all dat a-driven 18 

approaches, Lemonite's statistical power depends on cohort size; the moderate size of the GBM 19 

and IBD cohorts analyzed here constrains the robustness of module -level and regulator -level 20 

estimates, and larger cohorts will be needed to validate and extend these findings. 21

spatial metabolomics and proteomics 114 , which, combined with single -cell transcriptomics and 23 

chromatin accessibility data, promise unprecedented resolution for studying cellular development 24 

and behavior. Integration methods tailored to these single -cell and spatial multi -omics modalities 25

Figure 2. Lemonite performs data -driven, interpretable integration of bulk transcriptomics 22 

and metabolomics data. Lemonite requires coupled data as input and was developed for the 23 

integration of bulk transcriptomics and metabolomics data, but also accepts other omics data 24 

types (proteomics, phosphoproteomics, copy number variation, microbiomics, epigenomics…). 25 

Data are preprocessed, genes are clustered based on co -association into gene modules and 26 

transcription factor activities (TFA) are inferred. Regulatory programs are then predicted for each 27 

gene module by an ensemble of decision trees leveraging TFA scores (of TF expression if TFA 28 

not available) and metabolite abundances (and abundances of additional omics if provided). 29 

Lemonite then selects the most relevant regulators based on rank and score and prioritizes gene 30 

modules with their respective regulators based on module coherence (i.e. coexpression), module 31 

differential expression, PPIs with in gene modules, functional annotation analysis on module 32 

genes and regulators, regulator -to -module scores and known interactions involving regulators 33 

and module genes (see next section and Methods). 34
