Abstract Diversity and dynamics of immune cell receptor repertoires (IRRs) are two factors at 12 

the functional heart of adaptive immunity that together make IRRs difficult to grasp. Moreover, 13 

measurements are compounded by various sources of experimental noise. Here we propose a 14 

computational framework ( ClustIRR ) for uncertainty-aware quantitative analysis of IRR structure 15 

and dynamics. ClustIRR maps multiple IRRs across replicates, time points, or conditions onto a 16 

joint graph induced by immune receptor sequence similarity. It then detects communities on the 17 

joint graph (CJs). Based on CJs as reference structures across IRRs, ClustIRR then performs 18 

quantitative Bayesian analyses of differential CJ occupancy. Additionally, ClustIRR integrates 19 

single-cell gene expression data to link community expansion with transcriptional activation 20 

signatures. We demonstrate the capabilities of ClustIRR with the joint analysis of multiple T cell 21 

receptor repertoires in several example applications: (1) quantitative changes due to antigen 22 

challenge, (2) longitudinal dynamics during cancer immunotherapy, (3) V(D)J recombination 23 

biases in human vs murine repertoires that pre-adapt IRRs for pathogen responses. ClustIRR is 24 

freely available as open source software from the bioconductor repository. 25 26 

## Introduction 27 

Adaptive immunity relies on highly diverse immune receptor repertoires (IRRs) to recognize patho-28 

gens and malignant cells. Here we focus on T cell receptor (TCR) repertoires. TCR diversity arises 29 

through V(D)J recombination, generating an estimated 10 14 possible receptor variants ( Murugan 30 

et al., 2012 ). Within individuals, TCR repertoires are organized into clonotypes–subpopulations of 31 

cells sharing identical TCR sequences generated through antigen-driven clonal expansion. While 32 

clonally expanded T cells recognize identical antigens, accumulating evidence indicates that TCRs 33 

with non-identical but similar CDR3 sequences often recognize the same epitopes ( Glanville et al., 34 

2017 ; Dash et al., 2017 ). Such cross-reactivity has a number of consequences: for instance, even 35 

if clonotypes are not shared between individuals or samples, the respective repertoires may still 36 

recognize the same antigens. Conversely, the same TCR may recognize multiple antigens. In both 37 

directions cross-reactivity makes data interpretation more difficult. 38 

High-throughput sequencing (HTS) of TCR repertoires ( Woodsworth et al., 2013 ; Heather et al., 39

et al., 2021 ; Zhang et al., 2020 ; Valkiers et al., 2021 ; Pogorelyy et al., 2019 ). The architecture of 44 

TCR repertoires—defined by community composition and abundance—encodes critical functional 45 

information. For instance, successful cancer immunotherapy is associated with the expansion of 46 

tumor-antigen-specific communities, while viral exposure elicits cognate TCR communities. Con-47 

sequently, the functional interpretation of TCR repertoires hinges on the ability to quantitatively 48 

track community occupancy dynamics across repertoires, rather than relying on static community 49 

identification within isolated samples. 50 

However, achieving robust quantitative tracking requires to overcome significant hurdles rooted 51 

in the biological and technical nature of TCR repertoire data. First, quantifying changes at the clono-52 

type level is confounded by vast diversity and limited sampling depth. Stochastic V(D)J recombina-53 

tion and experimental noise mean that identical clonotypes are rarely shared across repertoires, 54 

preventing direct detection of the same clone across conditions. Second, while coarse-graining 55 

by partitioning into communities mitigates clonotype sparsity, it introduces a structural challenge: 56 

communities must be defined consistently across samples to be comparable. Existing clustering 57 

tools typically operate on single repertoires in isolation, producing incompatible community labels 58 

that preclude longitudinal or cross-condition quantitative comparison. 59 

Third, IRR sequencing data are inherently count-compositional, sparse, and noisy. These prop-60 

erties extend to community occupancy estimates. Standard differential abundance tests for TCR 61 

repertoires often focus on clonotypes and rely on qualitative comparisons based on point esti-62 

mates ( Rudqvist et al., 2018 ) or simple univariate approaches, such as beta-binomial counts re-63 

gression ( Rytlewski et al., 2019 ; Mayer-Blackwell et al., 2021 ) or Fisher’s exact test ( Balachandran 64 

et al., 2017 ). Such approaches can mis-characterize uncertainty by overlooking key variance com-65 

ponents, failing to distinguish biological expansion from stochastic fluctuation. 66 

Bayesian hierarchical models offer a rigorous alternative, but these models have been rarely de-67 

veloped for differential abundance analysis in TCR repertoires. Existing Bayesian methods primar-68 

ily focus on inferring latent clonotype frequencies from bulk sequencing read counts ( Puelma Touzel 69 

et al., 2020 ; Koraichi et al., 2022 ). While these methods represent important advancements, they 70 

typically require biological replicates at each time point to quantify natural variability—a condi-71 

tion often unmet in clinical or longitudinal studies. Tools like scCODA (Buettner et al., 2021 ) or 72 

tascCODA (Ostner et al., 2021 ) address compositional bias in single-cell data but are designed for 73 

annotated cell types rather than sequence-defined TCR communities. They are also ideally suited 74 

for moderate dimensionality (typically < 100 categories), whereas TCR communities can number in 75 

the thousands. Consequently, there is currently no unified method that combines joint community 76 

definition across samples with probabilistic quantification for high-dimensional IRR data. 77 

Here we introduce ClustIRR , a computational framework that addresses these interconnected 78 

challenges through model-based quantification of community occupancy in IRRs. While we focus 79 

on TCR repertoires, ClustIRR is equally applicable to B cell receptor (BCR) repertoires. Recognizing 80 

that robust quantification is contingent upon consistent community definitions, ClustIRR first con-81 

structs a joint graph integrating multiple repertoires across replicates, time points, or biological 82 

conditions. In this graph, nodes correspond to clonotypes, and edges reflect CDR3 sequence simi-83 

larity based on pairwise alignment scores. ClustIRR employs community detection algorithms (e.g., 84 

Leiden ( Traag et al., 2019 ), Louvain ( Blondel et al., 2008 )) to identify densely interconnected TCR 85 

groups suggesting shared antigen recognition. This process defines Communities on the Joint graph 86 

(CJs) that persist across repertoires. 87 

To quantify dynamics, ClustIRR models cell counts per CJ across repertoires using a hierarchical 88 

Bayesian Dirichlet-Multinomial framework. By explicitly accounting for the count-compositional 89 

nature of the data, this approach quantifies both relative CJ occupancy and differential occupancy 90

## Results 105 

Detection of EBV- and MART1-antigen reactive T-cell communities from single cell 106 

datasets 107 

To validate ClustIRR , we applied it to five single-cell TCR repertoires (Dataset 1, Methods), including 108 

three from healthy donors (C1, C2, C3) and two antigen-stimulated repertoires: Epstein-Barr virus 109 

(EBV; repertoire E) and MART1 (repertoire M). ClustIRR should show expansion of EBV and MART1 110 

specific CJs, respectively, in the stimulated repertoires compared to repertoires from healthy donors. 111 

ClustIRR constructed a joint graph comprising 89,710 nodes (T-cell clonotypes) connected by 112 

approximately 13 million edges, where edge weights reflected the average CDR3 𝛼𝛽 sequence simi-113 

larity scores between clonotype pairs (Methods). Application of the Leiden algorithm (Potts model, 114 

resolution parameter 𝑟 = 1 ) identified 10,301 CJs. Among these, 3,038 were singletons, while the 115 

largest CJ contained 1,339 clonotypes representing 29,491 cells, 99.5% of which originated from 116 

the EBV-stimulated repertoire (E). Among non-singleton CJs, the maximum unweighted distance 117 

between any two nodes (i.e., the CJ diameter) was 2 (Supplementary Figure S1B). This indicates 118 

that the CJs are compact and well-connected, reflecting conserved CDR3 sequences. Importantly, 119 

connectivity within most CJs was driven by high similarity in either CDR3 𝛼 or CDR3 𝛽 sequences, but 120 

rarely both simultaneously (Supplementary Figure S1C). This pattern suggests that antigen speci-121 

ficity is primarily encoded by a single chain, permitting flexible pairing with diverse partner chains. 122 

Furthermore, this observation aligns with the principle that TCR chain pairing is nearly indepen-123 

dent, which makes the convergent generation of identical 𝛼𝛽 pairs highly improbable ( Dupic et al., 124 

2019 ). 125 

To quantify repertoire overlap, ClustIRR computed pairwise cosine similarities (CS) using CJ oc-126 

cupancy vectors (cell counts per CJ) for each repertoire pair (Fig. 1A). Unstimulated controls had 127 

elevated inter-repertoire similarity from CS ≈ 0.5 between C3 and either of C1 and C2 to CS ≈ 0.9 128 

between C1 and C2. In contrast, antigen-stimulated repertoires overlapped much less with con-129 

trols (CS: 0.03–0.09 for E; 0.18–0.3 for M) and with each other (E vs. M: CS = 0.01). 130 

We quantified repertoire-specific CJ expansion using ClustIRR ’s Bayesian model, which expresses 131 

the effect of repertoire 𝑥 on CJ 𝑖 occupancy by the coefficient 𝛽 𝑖𝑥 . The sign and magnitude of 𝛽 𝑖𝑥 132 

values indicate expansion ( 𝛽 𝑖𝑥 > 0) or contraction ( 𝛽 𝑖𝑥 < 0) in repertoire 𝑥 (Methods). Given that 133 

adaptive immunity responds to antigenic challenge through clonal expansion, we hypothesized 134 

that expanded CJs in repertoires E and M would be enriched for EBV- and MART1-specific clono-135 

types, respectively. Consistent with this, 𝛽 𝑖𝑥 distributions for E and M had heavy tails with large 136 

positive values beyond 5, in contrast to healthy controls (Fig. 1B-C) with 𝛽 coefficients below 5. 137

M contained at least one EBV- or MART1-specific T cell clonotype, respectively. Notably, several 139

Integration with scRNA-seq data reveals signatures of T-cell activation signatures 172 

in expanded CJs 173 

The combination of single-cell gene expression (GEX) profiles with the TCR data allows for the iden-174 

tification of correlations between GEX and TCR space. Here, we compared the GEX profiles of CJs 175 

with their expansion scores ( 𝛽 ) across repertoires C1, C2, C3, E, and M. 176 

Clustering of GEX profiles with scBubbletree (Kitanovski et al., 2024 ) identified four robust, tran-177 

scriptionally distinct cell clusters, visualized as bubbles at the tips of a dendrogram (Fig. 2A). Over 178 

99% of cells from unstimulated samples (C1, C2, C3) were assigned to bubble 0 (Fig. 2B), which 179 

represented the largest cluster in the dataset (76,000 cells; 36.3% of total cells). This homogeneity 180 

underscores the transcriptional similarity of unstimulated cells. Notably, C1, C2, and C3 collectively 181 

contributed 99% of cells in bubble 0 (each sample accounting for 33% of the cluster), while sam-182 

ples E and M contributed only 0.2% and 0.1%, respectively (Fig. 2C). In contrast, stimulated samples 183 

E and M populated different clusters: 66% of cells from sample E and 88% from sample M went 184 

to bubbles 1 and 2, respectively, and the remaining cells from E and M co-clustered in bubble 3, 185 

with sample E contributing >85% of cells in this cluster (Fig. 2C). Hence, bubble 3 provides a unique 186 

opportunity to investigate cells with converging transcriptional profiles in response to stimulation 187 

by different antigens. The relative occupancy of expanded CJs e1–e5 and m1–m5 across bubbles 188 

mirrored overall E/M distributions, indicating no bubble specific enrichment. 189

Benchmarking 370 

To evaluate the computational scalability and parameter sensitivity of ClustIRR , we performed bench-371 

marking analyses varying the repertoire depth and the global minimum identity (GMI) threshold 372 

using paired TCR 𝛼𝛽 CDR3 sequences. GMI specifies the minimum pairwise amino acid sequence 373 

identity required between two CDR3 sequences to retain an edge in the similarity graph. We mea-374 

sured the CPU time for the primary processing function ( clustirr , dominated by similarity search) 375 

and the CJ detection function ( detect_communities ), peak random access memory (RAM) usage, 376 

and the resulting number of detected CJs. 377 

First, we assessed the impact of repertoire depth using subsampled datasets ranging from 378 

1,000 to 100,000 paired clonotypes, fixed at a GMI threshold of 0.8 (Supplementary Figure S15A– 379 

B). As expected, computational time scaled super-linearly with input size, reflecting the pairwise 380 

nature of sequence similarity calculations. The primary processing time increased from approxi-381 

mately 17 seconds for 1,000 clonotypes to 2.4 hours for 100,000 clonotypes (Supplementary Fig-382 

ure S15A). CJ detection time also increased with depth, from 1.7 seconds to 11 minutes, but re-383 

mained a minor fraction of the total runtime. Peak memory usage scaled similarly, with clustirr 384 

requiring approximately 0.1 GB for 1,000 clonotypes and 13 GB for 100,000 clonotypes, while 385 

detect_communities required 0.03 GB and 7 GB, respectively (Supplementary Figure S15B). No-386 

tably, the number of detected CJs grew linearly with clonotypes on the log-log scale in both CPU and 387 

RAM panels, indicative of power-law scaling. This suggests that CJ discovery increases predictably 388 

with sampling effort rather than saturating, reflecting the underlying hierarchical structure of the 389 

repertoire where new CJs continue to emerge at higher sequencing depths. 390 

Second, we evaluated the effect of the GMI threshold (0.6 to 0.95) on a fixed repertoire of 391 

5,000 paired clonotypes (Supplementary Figure S15C). The primary processing time remained sta-392 

ble across GMI values ( 170 seconds), confirming that the computational bottleneck lies in the ini-393 

tial similarity search, which precedes threshold filtering. In contrast, CJ detection time decreased 394 

markedly with increasing GMI, dropping from 18 seconds at GMI=0.6 to 0.7 seconds at GMI=0.95. 395 

This reduction corresponds to a sparser similarity graph at stricter identity thresholds. Concur-396 

rently, the number of detected CJs increased eight-fold (from ≈590 to ≈4,950) as the GMI threshold 397 

rose, reflecting increased fragmentation of the graph into smaller, tighter sequence groups. These 398 

results demonstrate that while repertoire depth dictates overall runtime and memory, the GMI 399 

parameter allows users to tune CJ resolution and downstream clustering speed without affecting 400 

initial processing costs. 401

TCR repertoire analysis was performed with ClustIRR (R-package, version 1.9.38). The main input to 553 

ClustIRR is a table with TCR clonotypes from a TCR repertoire, where each clonotype contains three 554

sizes to facilitate analysis of TCR repertoires generated by bulk TCR sequencing (e.g., Dataset 2). 557 

ClustIRR performs the following steps: 558 

1. Compute similarities between T cell clonotypes 559 

2. Construct a joint similarity graph ( 𝐽 )560 

3. Detect Communities on the Joint graph (CJs) 561 

4. Analyze Differential CJ Occupancy 562 

(a) Between individual TCR repertoires with model 𝑀 563 

(b) Between groups of TCR repertoires from biological conditions with model 𝑀 ℎ564

Computing similarities between TCR clonotypes 566 

ClustIRR aims to quantify the similarity between pairs of T cell clonotypes based on their CDR3 se-567 

quences. Given a TCR repertoire with 𝑛 clonotypes, we have to perform 𝑛 2 pairwise comparisons 568 

of CDR3 𝛼 and CDR3 𝛽 sequences. HT-seq technology allows us to profile TCR repertoires with over 569 

a million clonotypes ( 𝑛 > 10 6) in a single study. Thus, pairwise CDR3 sequence comparisons us-570 

ing algorithms such as the Needleman-Wunsch global sequence alignment algorithm may require 571 

prohibitive computational resources. 572 

To address this, ClustIRR employs Basic Local-Alignment Search Tool (BLAST, Altschul et al. 573 

(1990 )). Briefly, a protein database is constructed (method makeblastdb ) from all CDR3 sequences, 574 

and each CDR3 sequence is used as a query (method blastp ). This enables fast sequence similarity 575 

searches. By default, only CDR3 sequences matches with ≥ 80% sequence identity to the query 576 

are retained. This step reduces the computational and memory requirements, without impacting 577 

downstream CJ analyses, as CDR3 sequences with lower typically yield low similarity scores. 578 

For matched CDR3 pair, an alignment score ( 𝜔 ∈ ℤ) is computed using BLOSUM62 substitution 579

## (5) Probabilistic modeling of CJ occupancy Figure 6. ClustIRR workflow. (1) TCR sequencing (TCRseq) data are collected from multiple repertoires across longitudinal time points or biological conditions. 2) The main ClustIRR input is a table of TCR clonotypes for each repertoire, defined by CDR3 𝛼 and CDR3 𝛽 amino acid sequences and clonal sizes (cell counts). (3) 

ClustIRR constructs a joint similarity graph where nodes represent clonotypes and weighted edges connect clonotypes based on pairwise CDR3 𝛼 and CDR3 𝛽 sequence similarity. This graph integrates clonotypes from all repertoires into a single structure. (4) Graph-based clustering algorithms (e.g., Leiden or Louvain) identify densely connected groups of clonotypes, defined as Communities on the Joint graph (CJs). The number of cells in each CJ is quantified to form a CJ occupancy matrix 𝑌 𝑘 ×𝑟 , where 𝑘 indexes CJs and 𝑟 indexes repertoires. This matrix is used to fit a hierarchical Bayesian Dirichlet-Multinomial model. The model estimates posterior distributions for relative CJ occupancies ( 𝛽 coefficients), providing mean estimates along with 95% credible intervals (CIs).
