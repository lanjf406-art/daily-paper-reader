Abstract 29 

Colorectal cancer (CRC) remains a leading cause of cancer mortality, with most cases refractory to 30 

immunotherapy. Distinguishing tumor -induced from steady -state mucosal T cell responses has been a 31 

critical barrier to understanding antitumor immunity in CRC. Using orthotopic transplantation of CRC 32 

organoids with and without metastatic potential, combined with temporal T cell fate -mapping, we show 33 

that non -metastatic tumors elicit early recruitment of CD8 αβ ⁺ and CD4 ⁺ T cells that acquir ed cytotoxic 34 

and Th1 -like programs, whereas pro -metastatic tumors induce a naïve -like, hypoactivated state. Tumor -35 

infiltrating CD4 + T cells underwent clonal expan sion , including clones recognizing microbial and dietary 36 

antigens. T cells in physical contact with tumor cells, identified by uLIPSTIC, were enriched for expanded 37 

and cytotoxic clones . Fate -mapped T cells from non -metastatic tumors suppress ed tumor growth in an 38 

IFN --dependent manner, whereas pro -metastatic tumor -derived T cells fail ed to do so. Mechanistically, 39 

pro -metastatic tumors downregulate d MHCII, and Ciita targeting in non -metastatic organoids reduced 40 

CD4 ⁺ clonal expansion and led to tumor progression . Together, these findings define divergent early T 41 

cell trajectories associated with CRC metastatic potential, indica ting that ineffective local immune 42 

engagement precedes metastatic dissemination. 43

Introduction 57 

The intestinal mucosa harbors the largest immune compartment in the mammalian body, where resident 58 

and recruited immune cells must maintain tolerance to commensal and dietary antigens while preserving 59 

their ability to mount effective responses against pathogens and malignant transformation 1,2 . Uncontrolled 60 

epithelial proliferation leads to colorectal cancer (CRC), the second leading cause of cancer -related 61 

deaths worldwide 3–5. More than 85% of human CRC cases arise from accumulating sporadic or hereditary 62 

mutations in the tumor suppressor genes Apc, Tp53, Smad4 , and the oncogene Kras 3,6,7 . Although 63 

immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) has transformed the treatment of certain solid tumors, most CRCs, 64 

particularly those with a proficient mismatch repair system (pMMR), remain refractory to immunotherapy 65 

and are prone to metastasis 8–10 . In contrast, mismatch repair -deficient (dMMR) tumors harbor a high 66 

mutational burden that promotes immunogenicity and responsiveness to T cell -mediated therapies 9,10 .67 

This dichotomy underscores the pivotal yet incompletely understood role of local T cell responses in 68 

shaping CRC progression and its metastatic propensity 11 .69 

Although adaptive immunity can eliminate nascent tumor cells, it can also become functionally silenced 70 

or co -opted in an evolving tumor microenvironment (TME) 12 –14 . In the gut tissue, this challenge is 71 

compounded by the constant need to restrain damaging immune activation toward abundant luminal 72 

antigens, creating an environment in which tumor -specific responses must arise amid pre -existing 73 

tolerance mechanisms 15 –18 . However, how T cells integrate these conflicting cues, especially during the 74 

early stages of tumor growth and metastatic dissemination, remains unclear 11 . Previous studies have 75 

largely focused on either highly immunogenic dMMR CRC models or non -physiological subcutaneous 76 

transplants, providing limited insight into gut -resident or metastasis -associated T cell responses 19,20 .77 

Additionally, polyclonal T cell responses in non -metastatic versus pro -metastatic CRC, distinct from 78 

“background” gut immune responses, remain unexplored. 79 

To address these gaps, we combined orthotopic CRC models with distinct metastatic potentials and a 80 

temporal T cell fate -mapping strategy that enabled discrimination between pre -existing intestinal T cells 81 

and newly recruited potential antitumor clones. This approach allowed us to compare the recruitment, 82 

activation, and clonality of T cells in non -metastatic and pro -metastatic tumors within their native 83 

microenvironments. We found that these tumor types elicit fundamentally distinct early T cell activation 84 

responses, ranging from robust effector differentiation and clonal expansion to naïve -like or 85 

hypoactivated states, highlighting a previously unrecognized divergence in local immune engagement 86 

that precedes metastatic progression. 87

Results 89 

Immune profiling of orthotopic CRC models with distinct metastatic potential 90 

To investigate T lymphocyte infiltration in colorectal tumors and adjacent healthy colon epithelium, we 91 

implemented colonoscope -guided orthotopic models of CRC 21,22 . We injected the following mouse tumor 92 

organoid lines in the colonic walls of immunocompetent B6 mice: 1) AKP, harboring Apc –/–, Kras G12D , and 93 

Tp53 –/– mutations 21,23,24 , and 2) pro -Metastatic (pro -Met) line which was generated by multiple in vivo 94 

passaging of the AKP line ( Figures 1A, S1A and B ). For each of these transplanted tumor organoid 95 

lines, we assessed colon tumor growth, liver metastasis, and T cell phenotype at an early stage (two 96 

weeks post -tumor injection) and a late stage (6 -7 weeks after tumor injection) of CRC development. This 97 

temporal analysis enabled us to determine when differences in tumor aggressiveness and immune 98 

infiltration first emerge and how they evolve over time. While the average colon tumor diameter was 99 

comparable between both tumor organoid models at the early CRC stage, pro -Met tumors were larger at 100 

the late CRC stage ( Figure 1B ). Liver macrometastases were observed only in the pro -Met model at the 101 

late CRC stage ( Figures 1C and S1C ). These observations establish AKP and pro -Met as matched 102 

primary CRC models that diverge sharply in metastatic behavior, providing a framework for further 103 

investigation of their primary TME. 104 

To determine how tumor morphology and immune infiltration differ across models and time points, we 105 

performed histopathological analysis of colons of mice bearing AKP and pro -Met tumors at both early 106 

and late stages. We performed hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining combined with 107 

immunohistochemistry (IHC) for tdTomato (to demarcate tumor cells) and CD4 and CD8 to assess T cell 108 

infiltration in colon lesions ( Figure 1D ). Both tumor types were consistent with adenocarcinoma in most 109 

of the analyzed tumors, with invasion extending into the muscularis at an early stage ( Figure 1D ). In the 110 

late stage, both models were uniformly classified as adenocarcinomas and exhibited transmural 111 

extension from the mucosa to the serosa ( Figures 1D and S1D ). To quantify tumor progression, we used 112 

a tumor severity score by integrating necrosis, fibroplasia, and epithelial dysplasia grades. Using this 113 

metric, AKP tumors exhibited an increase in severity as they progressed from early to late stages, 114 

reflecting progressive histopathological worsening over time. In contrast, pro -Met tumors did not exhibit 115 

a significant stage -dependent change, consistent with the presence of a more advanced histopathological 116 

state early on. Early pro -Met tumors scored intermediate, which was not significantly different from either 117 

early AKP tumors or late pro -Met tumors, whereas late AKP and late pro -Met tumors were 118 

indistinguishable ( Figure 1E ). IHC assessment indicated that both tumors were highly infiltrated with 119 

CD4 + T cells and, to a lesser extent, with CD8 + T cells in the early stage. However, in the late stage, CD4 +

120 

and CD8 + T cells were mostly detected at the tumor periphery, with less infiltration inside the tumor bed 121

A fate -mapping system to identify tumor -induced T cells in orthotopic CRC 204 

The orthotopic CRC -uLIPSTIC system is a powerful tool for capturing direct cellular interactions during 205 

the short window of substrate administration. However, a significant roadblock in characterizing adaptive 206 

immune responses to tumors in polyclonal systems is the inability to distinguish recently recruited T cells 207 

responding to tumors (or tumor -related perturbations) from tissue -resident T cells that occupy the 208 

transplantation site before tumor introduction. This distinction is particularly challenging in the intestinal 209 

environment, given the vast number of resident lymphocytes that participate in ongoing immune 210 

responses against luminal antigens 30,31 . To address this challenge and complement the uLIPSTIC 211 

system, we combined orthotopic CRC models with a fate -mapping approach to track peripheral T cell 212 

recruitment and activation in the colon 21,22,32,33 . Tamoxifen treatment of Sell CreERT2 X Rosa26 CAG -LSL -tdTomato 

213 

(iSell Tomato ) mice permanently labels naïve T cells expressing L -selectin ( Sell , CD62L +) as Tomato +. This 214 

strategy allowed us to enrich for “ex -naïve” (CD62L – Tomato +) T cells and follow the fate of tumor -induced 215 

T cells 32,33 .216

Impaired early T cell response is not sufficient to drive metastasis of AKP tumors 255 

The early T cell phenotypic differences between the AKP and pro -Met models prompted us to investigate 256 

whether the more inflamed T cell environment in AKP tumors could impact their metastatic progression. 257 

First, we determined whether the absence of metastasis after AKP tumor transplantation reflects an 258 

intrinsic inability of AKP cells to colonize the liver. Upon intrasplenic injection 35,36 , both AKP and pro -Met 259 

tumor lines were equally capable of forming liver metastases, indicating that AKP tumors are competent 260 

to grow in the liver when given direct access , and that their non -metastatic outcome in the orthotopic 261 

model is likely due to impaired dissemination rather than inability to colonize the liver ( Figure S2E ). 262 

We then directly investigated the role of T cells in shaping the AKP primary tumor and metastatic 263 

outcomes. We orthotopically injected AKP and pro -Met tumors into immunodeficient Rag1 –/– (RKO) mice, 264 

which lack functional T and B cells. By six weeks post -injection, AKP and pro -Met colon tumors in RKO 265 

mice reached sizes comparable to those observed in immunocompetent hosts. Consistent with their 266 

phenotypes in immunocompetent mice, pro -Met tumors formed liver metastases, whereas AKP tumors 267 

remained nonmetastatic ( Figure S2F ). To specifically test the impact of T cells on the observed AKP 268 

phenotype, we performed antibody depletion of CD4 + or CD8 + T cells in C57BL/6 mice transplanted with 269 

AKP tumor organoids ( Figure S2G ). Efficient depletion of CD4 + or CD8 + T cells was confirmed by flow 270 

cytometry ( Figure S2H ). At the early time point, AKP tumor sizes were comparable across the groups. 271 

However, at the late stage, CD8 -depleted mice developed significantly larger tumors, whereas CD4 + T272 

cell -depleted mice showed intermediate tumor sizes between the isotype control and CD8 + T cell -273 

depleted conditions ( Figure S2I ). As expected, CD4 + T cell depletion impaired CD8 + T cell activation, 274 

reducing their IFN - production and PD -1 expression, whereas CD8 depletion had minimal effects on 275 

CD4 + T cell activation ( Figures S2J and K). Nonetheless, liver metastases were not detected in any of 276 

the experimental groups, suggesting that the intrinsic transcriptional programs in AKP and pro -Met tumors 277 

likely play a dominant role in determining their metastatic fate. Although neither CD4 ⁺ nor CD8 ⁺ T cell 278 

targeting was sufficient to drive metastatic progression in AKP tumors, our data reinforce the role of T279 

cells in shaping the primary TME. This prompted us to investigate how CD4 ⁺ and CD8 αβ ⁺ T cells 280 

functionally adapt to distinct AKP versus pro -Met TMEs, independent of their capacity to determine 281 

metastatic outcomes. 282

To resolve how early T cell adaptation programs diverge in AKP versus pro -Met CRC models, we focused 286 

on the fate -mapping i Sell Tomato approach, which enables tracking of polyclonal “ex -naïve” T cells labeled 287 

at the time of tumor transplantation and during the earliest stages of tumor development. This approach 288 

allowed us to assess how newly recruited T cells adapt to the TME. i Sell Tomato mice were transplanted 289 

with AKP or pro -Met organoids, or subjected to a sham procedure ( see Figure 3A ). Two weeks later, we 290 

sorted CD62L – Tomato + T cells isolated from colon tumors and non -tumor epithelium and performed 291 

single -cell RNA and T cell receptor (TCR) sequencing (scRNA/TCR -seq) using the 10x Genomics 292 

Chromium platform to define the transcriptional programs induced by each tumor type. UMAP 293 

visualization and unsupervised clustering of fate -mapped CD4 + T cells classified them into eight 294 

transcriptionally distinct clusters across tumors and colonic IEL (Figure 4A). Clusters 0 and 5295 

corresponded to cytotoxic/ Th1 cells from the IEL and tumor, respectively, marked by the high expression 296 

of genes such as Nkg7, Ccl5, Cxcr3, Tbx21, Ifng, Cd44, Cd226, and Bhlhe40 . In contrast, cluster 1 was 297 

marked by the expression of Sell, S1pr1, Igfbp4, Il7r, and Ccr7, which are classical markers of naïve T 298 

cells. Cluster 3 also expressed markers associated with stemness, such as Tcf7 and Slamf6. Clusters 1 299 

and 3 showed downregulation of activation markers, such as Cd44, Il2ra, and Pdcd1 (Figures 4A –C). 300 

AKP CD4 + tumor -infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) and adjacent IEL were enriched in cytotoxic/Th1 clusters, 301 

whereas pro -Met CD4 + TILs were predominantly represented in naïve -like clusters 1 and 3 ( Figures 4A –302 

D). Of note, although our sorting was enriched for ex -naïve CD62L – Tomato + T cells, cells in clusters 1 303 

and 3 retained a naïve -like profile at the transcriptional level. 304

Cluster 7 included regulatory T cells (Tregs) defined by the expression of Foxp3, Il10 , and Gzmb . Pro -308

Discussion 502 

We provided a detailed characterization of CD4 + and CD8 + T cell clonality, activation states, and cellular 503 

interactions across CRC models with distinct metastatic potential. Using orthotopic CRC models coupled 504 

with temporal T cell fate mapping, we uncovered divergent early activation trajectories of tumor -infiltrating 505 

T cells that reflect the metastatic potential of tumors. Non -metastatic AKP tumors elicited a strong 506 

cytotoxic and Th1 -skewed immune response in recently recruited T cells, accompanied by enhanced 507 

CD4 + T cell clonal expansion. This contrasts with the naïve -like and regulatory phenotypes of T cells 508 

recruited to pro -metastatic tumors during the same early window, suggesting an accelerated state of 509

immune hypoactivation 13,43 . These findings contribute to the growing understanding that antitumor 510 

immunity in CRC is heterogeneous, varying widely with cancer stage, tumor -intrinsic factors, and tumor 511 

environmental cues, and is critical in predicting disease outcomes. 512 

Previous studies have shown that the overall immune infiltrate and its spatial organization, defined as 513 

Immunoscore, outperform microsatellite instability status in predicting patient survival 44 –46 . Our data 514 

further demonstrate that both the quality and quantity of early T cell infiltration and activation differ 515 

markedly between non -metastatic and pro -metastatic settings. Additionally, early recruited T cells, 516 

particularly CD8 + T cells, retained their initial activation state as CRC progressed, whereas CD4 + T cells 517 

exhibited a gradual decline in their activation state. These findings resonate with recent observations in 518 

human CRC, where early stage pMMR tumors displayed robust T cell infiltration and activation that 519 

diminished as tumors advanced and metastasized 47 . These findings reflect the importance of early 520 

immune activation and suggest that it may predict cancer outcomes. 521 

The distinct CD4 + and CD8 + T cell clonal dynamics explored in our study have multiple important 522 

implications. First, the distinct CD4 + T cell clonal expansion observed in the two tumor models indicates 523 

that differences in TCR clonality may be associated with disease outcome. A previous study reported that 524 

increased TCR clonal expansion in metastatic melanoma patients was predictive of their response to ICB 525 

treatment and overall survival 48 . Another study suggested that ICB treatment can increase clonal 526 

expansion in pMMR CRC patients to levels comparable to dMMR CRC, highlighting that localized clonal 527 

expansion may still occur in pMMR tumors 47 . These studies evaluated TCR clonality considering total 528 

tumor -infiltrating T lymphocytes without distinguishing between CD4 + and CD8 + T cell subsets 47,48 . Our 529 

data revealed that CD4 + T cells undergo greater clonal expansion than CD8 + T cells in the non -metastatic 530 

AKP model. This points to a potential contribution of certain CD4 + T cell clones in shaping antitumor 531 

immunity, although the outcomes of CD4 + and CD8 + T cell depletion or immune -deficient experiments 532 

suggest that the repertoire alone cannot determine the metastatic outcomes. 533 

In addition to the TCR clonal dynamics observed in tumor tissues, our findings revealed extensive clonal 534 

overlap between CD4 + T cells infiltrating tumors and adjacent non -tumor colon epithelium, particularly in 535 

the non -metastatic AKP model. These findings, including the microbiota - and food -antigen specificities of 536 

some of our expanded clones, suggest that intestinal antitumor responses can co -opt pre -existing TCR 537 

repertoires utilizing ongoing mucosal immune circuits to either potentiate or subvert antitumor responses. 538 

This hypothesis is further supported by a recent study demonstrating that colonization with segmented 539 

filamentous bacteria (SFB) enhances ICB efficacy in mice bearing B16 tumors expressing SFB -derived 540 

antigens 49 . This enhancement was mediated by the reprogramming of SFB -specific “ex -Th17” CD4 + T541 

cells toward a Th1 -like phenotype characterized by IFN - and TNF -α secretion, which in turn boosted 542

Limitations of the study 586 

Our study was based on orthotopic models that reproduce key features of primary and metastatic CRC, 587 

although they do not capture the earliest stages of tumorigenesis. Furthermore, the in vivo passaging 588 

process used to derive the pro -Met line from the AKP line likely selected mutations that increased 589 

metastatic capacity while concurrently overriding antitumor immunity in the pro -Met line. Although 590 

additional CRC models, such as MC38 and MC38 LVM2, were examined, differences in aggressiveness, 591 

kinetics, and metastatic behavior limited direct comparisons. Additionally, our fate -mapping approach 592 

effectively identified approximately 60% of recently recruited T cells; hence, incomplete labeling may 593 

underreport rare activation patterns. We also acknowledge that the CD62L – Tomato + population is 594 

heterogeneous and may include ex -naïve T cells that were activated outside the TME before 595 

subsequently migrating into the tumor. Therefore, not all labeled cells can be assumed to have been 596 

primed in response to tumor -derived antigens. Moreover, the identity of the antigens recognized by 597 

several top expanded clones remains unknown, precluding a definitive link between the observed clonal 598 

expansion and antitumor immunity. 599
