Summary 24 

Multicellularity is a well -documented microbial response to  stress , however its role as 25 

an adaptive survival  strategy  in cancer remains unresolved. Here we reveal that drug 26 

stress, such as  paclitaxel  treatment, enable rapidly (within 24 -48  hours) and efficiently 27 

(~ 20-40%) convert single mouse breast  4T1  cancer cells into clonal multicellular 28 

spheroids, ultimately generating multicellular masses. Notably, multicellularity is 29 

reversible: upon stress removal, most of them restore a unicellular lifestyle that quickly 30 

becomes dominant. This transient multicellular state shields cells from hostile niches, 31 

functions as a drug -tolerant persistence (DTP) reservoir, and fuels post -therapy relapse, 32 

revealing multicellularity as a facultative  evolutionary pivot for fitness gain. 33 

Importantly, blocking primordial germ cell (PGC) specification suppresses the 34 

multicellularity  transition.  Our findings reveal that certain cancer cells enable  adopt 35 

unicellular –multicellular life cycle  through phenotypic plasticity, dynamically adapting 36 

to microenvironmental shifts to maximize fitness.  This discovery reframes cancer 37 

evolution and the drug -tolerant persistence (DTP) state, highlighting multicellularity as 38 

an adaptive, stress -inducible survival strategy against therapy. 39

Instruction 51 

Recent oncology data reveal that a principal driver of cancer relapse is not the 52 

acquisition of resistance -conferring mutations, but rather the reversible, non -genetic 53 

“drug -tolerant persistence” (DTP) state that provides only transient protection 1,2 .54 

Strikingly, microorganisms can be rapidly switched by microenvironmental stress into 55 

multicellular lifestyle, whether aggregative biofilms or clonal cysts 3-6, as a core 56 

adaptive strategy that transiently buffers them against lethal antibiotic exposure.  We 57 

propose that cancer cells may  adopt  an analogous program to enter a DTP state.  Here, 58 

we report that chemotherapeutic stress alone —typified by paclitaxel (PTX) —is 59 

sufficient to drive a reversible unicellular -to -clonal -multicellular transition in a fraction 60 

of malignant cancer cells, often characterized by the co -emergence of oocyte -like and 61 

blastomere -like phenotypes, thereby  driving cells  in to  a robust DTP state. Our findings 62 

redefine the nature of the DTP state and frame cancer adaptive evolution as the re -63 

activation of an ancient developmental program. 64

Lead to tumor relapse 143 

Regar dless of whether they originated from short -term drug -exposed suspension 144 

cultures or long -term drug -exposed adherent cultures, the daughter cells displayed 145 

heightened morphological plasticity  (Fig s. 2 g and 2i ) and retained robust tumorigenicity, 146 

forming embryonic, highly plastic tumors —including teratomas (Fig s. 3a -3d , S2a-c). 147 

In vivo , 4T1  cultures pre -treated for only 2 days formed tumors rapidly, although 148 

subsequent growth was markedly slower (Fig s. 3e  and  3f , Table S 8). Conversely, 149 

cultures pre -treated for 14 days remained tumorigenic, but tumor initiation were 150 

significantly delayed (Fig s. 3e  and  3f ), similar to th e clinical lag that often precedes 151 

relapse after chemotherapy 1,2 . The tumorigenic potential of  4T1  cultures declined in 152 

proportion to  PTX  exposure time (Fig s. 3e  and  3f , Table S 9). H&E sections revealed 153 

that drug -treated explants retained the histologic  morphology  of control tumors  (Fig. 154 

3g ), yet displayed amplified plastic features (Fig.  S2d), consistent with the heightened 155 

plasticity frequently seen in  clinical relapse specimens 7. Collectively, our findings 156 

reveal that the multicellular state enables reversion to a unicellular lifestyle and thereby 157 

drives post -therapy relapse. 158

Discussion 326
