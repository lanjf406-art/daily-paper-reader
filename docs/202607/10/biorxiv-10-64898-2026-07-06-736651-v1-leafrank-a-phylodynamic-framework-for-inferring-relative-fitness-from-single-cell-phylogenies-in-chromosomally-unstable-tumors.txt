SUMMARY 10 

Tumors contain cancer cells with diverse growth potentials that shape evolutionary trajecto-11 

ries, yet this fitness diversity remains difficult to quantify in cases of whole-genome dupli-12 

cation (WGD) and chromosomal instability. We present LeafRank, a mathematical frame-13 

work that leverages single-cell DNA-seq phylogenies to infer the relative fitness of individ-14 

ual cells. Using a multi-type branching process model, LeafRank integrates full tree topol-15 

ogy, including branch lengths and bifurcation patterns, to estimate marginal fitness probabil-16 

ities under punctuated evolutionary regimes driven by rare driver events. To account for el-17 

evated aberration rates following WGD, we introduce a tree-rescaling strategy that adjusts 18 

for lineage-specific genomic instability. Unlike methods focused on predefined subclones, 19 

LeafRank ranks all sampled cells, enabling flexible assessment of growth heterogeneity. Sim-20 

ulations demonstrate high accuracy across spatial and non-spatial virtual tumors. Applied 21 

to ovarian cancer, LeafRank reveals directional and parallel selection in WGD tumors and 22 

identifies recurrent copy number events enriched in high-fitness lineages. WGD lineages do 23 

not show immediate growth advantages but acquire fitness through subsequent alterations. 24

# INTRODUCTION 25 

Cancer evolution is the process by which cancer cells, expanding from a single founder cell, 26 

diverge by accumulating somatic aberrations, including single nucleotide variants ( SSNV )1,2 27 

and copy number alterations ( SCNA )3,4 , that can drive disease progression and resistance to 28 

treatment. The concept of somatic evolution, first articulated in Nowell’s seminal model of can-29 

cer evolution 5, posits that subpopulations, or subclones, defined by distinct somatic aberrations 30 

within an established tumor can exhibit different survival and proliferation rates 6, corresponding 31 

to differences in cellular fitness under a given microenvironment. 32 

Fitness diversity among single cells within a patient tumor at the time of sampling reflects 33 

both cell-intrinsic growth potential driven by somatic variants and selective pressures imposed 34 

by the tumor microenvironment. Rapidly proliferating lineages indicate adaptive success at 35 

the time of sampling 7, whereas slowly cycling cells may exhibit cancer stem-like properties 8–10 36 

or retain the potential to fuel treatment resistance 8,10,11 . Therefore, characterizing this fitness 37 

diversity is critical for addressing fundamental questions in cancer biology, including measur-38 

ing selection pressures within the native tumor environment 12,13 , identifying somatic variants 39 

associated with fitness differences 14 , and predicting future adaptive potential 15 . However, in 40 

tumors with chromosomal instability ( CIN ), fitness diversity remains poorly characterized, and 41 

the fitness effects of SCNAs, including whole-genome duplication ( WGD ), remain elusive in 42 

patient tumor contexts. 43 

Theoretically, subclonal fitness can be quantified by tracking the growth dynamics of cancer 44 

cell populations, ideally through longitudinal sampling during tumor evolution 16 . However, cur-45 

rent technological constraints largely restrict such measurements to in vitro systems, mouse 46 

models 16,17 , or liquid tumors 18 . Consequently, despite the recognized importance of fitness 47 

diversity within solid tumors, methodologies that can infer subclonal fitness from patient tumor 48 

samples while preserving the native tumor context remain limited. 49 

The relative fitness of a cell carrying an acquired somatic variant is commonly assumed to 50 

be reflected in the population size of the subclone harboring that variant, conditional on the time 51 

at which the variant arises 19 . Variant allele frequencies ( VAF ) of SSNVs measured from bulk 52 

sequencing have therefore been used as a surrogate for subclone size, providing evidence 53 

for detectable positive selection during subclonal evolution 12,20,21 . However, such analyses 54 

have been largely restricted to diploid genomic regions because SCNAs can confound VAF 55 

measurements, thereby excluding tumors with CIN phenotypes. 56 

Notably, advances in single-cell DNA sequencing (scDNA-seq) have substantially improved 57 

the resolution of cell-to-cell genomic variation 22 , enabling the reconstruction of phylogenetic 58 

trees of detectable somatic variants in individual cells. Within such trees, subclone size can be 59 

estimated from the number of cells assigned to a given clade, while the birth time of a subclone 60 

can be approximated from branch lengths, or from the number of variants accumulated along 61 

the corresponding lineage, under appropriate molecular-clock assumptions. Both SSNVs and 62 

SCNAs have been used for phylogenetic reconstruction. For SSNVs, tree-sampling strategies 63 

have been developed 23,24 to mitigate the impact of allelic dropout (ADO) in scDNA-seq data. 64 

In contrast, SCNAs can be robustly detected by leveraging read counts across large genomic 65 

regions, and the introduction of amplification-free scDNA-seq protocols 25,26 has improved res-66 

olution of SCNAs to the megabase level. Despite the violation of infinite-sites assumption in 67 

SCNA evolution 27 , several methods use probabilistic frameworks to reconstruct SCNA histo-68

With phylogenetic trees derived from scDNA-seq data, mathematical modeling has begun 72 

to link information embedded in tree topology, including subclone sizes and branch lengths, to 73 

the population dynamics of cancer cells, enabling phylodynamic inference of subclonal fitness 74 

landscapes. SCIFIL infers fitness by modeling subclone sizes using a deterministic ordinary 75 

differential equation formulation of a branching process 31 . FiTree approximates subclone size 76 

distributions with a multi-type branching process under assumptions of large time and small 77 

mutation rates, and infers the fitness of specific genomic variants within a Bayesian frame-78 

work that requires phylogenetic trees from multiple patients 32 . In contrast, cloneRate infers 79 

fitness by modeling subclone birth times or branch lengths using coalescent theory derived 80 

from a supercritical branching process 33 . SCPhyloX uses the Poisson distribution 34 to model 81 

the expected branch length, including leaf-to-root and leaf-to-progenitor distances, to detect 82 

subclones under positive selection and estimate associated selection coefficients under as-83 

sumptions of structured population growth 35 . Collectively, these methods rely on approxima-84 

tions of branching process models, and focus on predefined subclones and typically assume 85 

uniform fitness within each subclone. Additional parameters, such as mutation rates, are also 86 

known to influence fitness inference 36,37 . Although joint estimation of multiple parameters has 87 

been proposed 38 , inferring absolute subclonal fitness levels remains challenging. 88 

Rather than focusing on the absolute fitness values of specific subclones, we ask whether 89 

the relative fitness ranking of sampled single cells can be inferred from a phylogenetic tree 90 

reconstructed from genomic aberrations in a patient tumor. Leveraging a multi-type branch-91 

ing process model, we developed LeafRank , a probabilistic algorithm that analyzes the full 92 

topology of phylogenetic trees derived from scDNA-seq data. Our approach incorporates es-93 

tablished features of phylogenetic trees, including branch lengths and subclone sizes, while 94 

simultaneously accounting for additional unobserved factors that shape tree structure. 95 

We designed LeafRank to accommodate the high CIN rates associated with WGD. LeafRank 96 

assumes branch lengths (aberration counts) are proportional to time. However, because base-97 

line copy number ( CN ) dictates the substrate for subsequent alterations, ongoing CIN can al-98 

ter aberration rates between lineages and violate this molecular clock assumption. Subclonal 99 

WGD is an extreme case that can significantly raise the baseline aberration rate 39–41 . To ad-100 

dress this, we implemented a tree-rescaling approach that converts the input phylogeny into 101 

an ultrametric tree, allowing us to evaluate and correct for this molecular-clock violation. 102 

This work is inspired by the non-cancer framework proposed by Neher et. al. which lever-103 

ages influenza virus phylogenies to infer the relative fitness of viral strains under the assump-104 

tion of small mutational fitness effects in a constant-sized population 19,42 . In contrast, we model 105 

cancer growth as an expanding population in which fitness differences between cell types can 106 

be large. Moreover, LeafRank does not assume the homogeneity of fitness within a subclone, 107 

and allows users to adjust the input parameters for the multi-type branching process model, 108 

thereby improving the flexibility in the evaluation of fitness diversity within the patient tumor. 109 

Using both non-spatial and spatial simulations 43 , we demonstrate that the inferred fitness rank-110 

ings are generally robust to the choice of input parameters, with sensitivity arising only under 111 

specific parameter regimes. Applying LeafRank to copy number event trees from ovarian can-112

# RESULTS 117 

LeafRank: A phylodynamic framework for inferring relative fitness from single 118 

cell trees 119 

We developed LeafRank , a probabilistic framework for single cell phylogeny, inferring the rela-120 

tive fitness of sampled tumor cells. LeafRank takes as input a phylogenetic tree reconstructed 121 

from somatic aberrations, including SCNAs detected via scDNA-seq, alongside parameter con-122 

figurations defined by a multi-type branching process model 45 (Figure 1A). The framework out-123 

puts the marginal probability of fitness types for leaf cells, conditioned on the observed tree 124 

topology and model configuration. 125 

Internally, LeafRank utilizes a multi-type branching process model that accommodates tran-126 

sitions among discrete fitness states within an expanding population. This framework explicitly 127 

defines the probability density that an ancestral cell (an internal node), possessing a specific 128 

fitness type, generates the observed descending subtree (Figure 1B). Specifically, LeafRank 129 

decomposes this probability calculation into branch and bifurcation propagator functions. This 130 

formulation distinguishes our work from previous non-cancer frameworks 42 , which rely on con-131 

tinuous diffusion approximations of small fitness effects. Crucially, this exact decomposition 132 

provides the foundation necessary to implement a message-passing algorithm, accelerating 133 

the computation of marginal fitness state distributions for each sampled cell (Figure S1). No-134 

tably, the marginal fitness distribution of sampled cells takes into account the full information 135 

embedded in the tree topology, while the multi-type branching process framework naturally ac-136 

counts for the stochastic nature of the clonal expansion dynamics. Detailed implementation of 137 

the LeafRank algorithm is provided in the Methods and Supplemental Text. 138 

Topological signatures of clonal expansion inform fitness ranking predictions 139 

To verify LeafRank’s performance on ideal data, we first tested it on the ground-truth phylo-140 

genetic trees and parameter configurations derived from virtual tumors whose growth satisfies 141 

our underlying assumptions (See Methods). Because LeafRank assumes branch lengths rep-142 

resent elapsed evolutionary time, the input tree should be approximately ultrametric; that is, 143 

it should exhibit relatively consistent leaf-to-root distances when the samples originate from a 144 

single time point. We simulated virtual tumors using the multi-type branching process model, 145 

and randomly sampled single cells once the tumor reached a million cells. By recording the 146 

birth and death times of each cell during the simulation, we are able to reconstruct an accurate 147 

input tree, termed as a true time ultrametric tree or TT tree, as demonstrated in Figure 2. The 148 

simulation setting is detailed in Methods. 149 

LeafRank recovered fitness rankings in virtual tumors with high fidelity. Predicted fitness 150 

values were highly correlated with the ground truth, where 35 of 50 tumors exhibited a rank 151 

correlation exceeding 0.7 (Figure 2A). This result indicates that LeafRank accurately translates 152 

tree topology into cellular level fitness diversity, effectively distinguishing high-fitness lineages 153

Study design and LeafRank framework (A) The input for LeafRank consists of (1) a recon-structed single cell phylogenetic tree (binary bifurcation) derived from somatic aberrations (e.g., point mutations or copy number alterations) and (2) a multi-type branching process model parameter config-uration. Cancer cell expansion is governed by birth ( b) and death ( d) rates corresponding to one of V discrete fitness types. During division, daughter cells accumulate passenger aberrations at rate μ and may transition to higher fitness states at a driver aberration rate ν. In addition to the parameters govern-ing the branching process, the model incorporates a time-scale normalizing parameter τ and a single cell sampling probability ρ. The tree shape T integrates both branch lengths and bifurcation patterns, where 

VL and VN represent the fitness types of leaves and internal nodes, respectively. (B) Message pass-ing algorithm in LeafRank. To derive the marginal distribution of fitness types for sampled cells (leaves), LeafRank decomposes the probability calculation into the branch and bifurcation propagators. As shown in the row panels, the “up-message” (rootward) recursively collects the conditional probabilities of the subtree descending from each node. Conversely, the “down-message” (leafward) updates each node the marginal probability of its fitness types, conditioned on the observed phylogeny “above” the node, including all ancestral and sibling lineages. This bi-directional message passing facilitates the efficient computation of the marginal fitness distribution of all leaf cells. (C) Benchmarking via non-spatial and spatial simulations. We observe that LeafRank’s sensitivity in identifying high-fitness lineages is tied to the real-157 

ized size of the corresponding lineage. In cases where driver aberrations occur extremely late, 158 

the resulting sublineages may lack sufficient time to expand or reach the detection threshold 159 

for sampling. Consequently, LeafRank failed to identify these isolated, high-fitness cells when 160 

their topological signal was not yet reflected in the sampled tree. This is exemplified by the case 161 

where LeafRank predicted a uniform fitness landscape, reflecting a sampled population domi-162 

nated by fitness-homogeneous cells (Figure 2C). Here, emerging high-fitness lineages had not 163 

achieved sufficient size to be distinguished from the background. Consequently, the absence 164 

of a measurable topological signal led to a low correlation with the ground-truth rankings. 165 

In contrast, cell lineages that failed to achieve sufficient clonal expansion were ranked as 166 

low-fitness, regardless of their early emergence. While these clones may branch early in the 167 

phylogeny, their limited sampled clone size indicates a lack of competitive advantage in growth, 168 

allowing LeafRank to correctly distinguish them from high-fitness expansions. In all the virtual 169 

tumor samples, LeafRank correctly located the lower fitness cells. 170

Robustness of LeafRank to parameter uncertainty and evolutionary model spec-182 

ification 183

input parameters), we next assessed its resilience to parameter uncertainty. In practice, these 185 

parameters often vary by biological context and are characterized by significant estimation 186 

uncertainty. We therefore examined how LeafRank responds to variations in these inputs to 187 

ensure the robust translation of tree topology into fitness rankings. By perturbing each param-188 

eter across a biologically plausible range, we found that while individual fitness values were 189 

affected in diverse ways, the overall ranking inferred by LeafRank remained largely consistent. 190 

Furthermore, by characterizing the effect of input parameter tuning on the probability distri-191 

bution of predicted fitness values, we provide guidelines for achieving an informative ranking 192 

through the evaluation of fitness distribution. 193

To provide practical guidelines for parameter optimization, we characterized how input set-213 

tings shape the resulting fitness landscape, reconstructed from the inferred distributions across 214 

all sampled cells (Figure 3C). We observed two distribution patterns that emerge under spe-215 

cific parameter regimes, revealing the conditions under which rankings become uninformative. 216 

These observations provide a framework for tuning LeafRank parameters to maximize the res-217 

olution and accuracy of the inferred fitness rankings. 218 

The first pattern is ranking instability, characterized by closely related cells, such as those 219 

in sibling lineages, exhibiting sharply contrasting inferred fitness values. This pattern suggests 220 

that, under the current parameter regimes, LeafRank may be overly sensitive to stochastic 221 

noise embedded in the tree, leading to unstable predictions. For lineages with such erratic 222 

inferred fitness distributions, this excessive sensitivity to stochastic noise can distort fitness 223 

estimates at the individual leaf level, rendering the internal fitness structure biologically implau-224 

sible. This instability typically arises when the cell sampling parameter ρ is set too small, the 225 

driver aberration rate ν is set too large, or the number of fitness types V is too small to resolve 226 

the underlying fitness structure. 227 

The second pattern is boundary saturation, where a substantial fraction of lineages are in-228 

ferred to have fitness at or near the preset minimum or maximum. This saturation reduces 229 

the resolution of the inference, obscuring the identification of high-fitness clones when most 230 

lineages are pushed to the maximum, or low-fitness clones when they are compressed at the 231 

minimum. This pattern suggests that the chosen parameters systematically deviate from the 232 

ground truth, forcing the inference to assign extreme values to reconcile the observed phyloge-233 

netic tree. Specifically, this arises when the time-scaling factor is either too small or too large, 234 

or when the birth/death rates are incongruent with the tree topology, such that tumor evolution 235 

along the given tree would require cells to have implausibly extreme fitness values. 236 

Although each pattern may yield non-ideal fitness distributions, their individual effects on 237 

ranking accuracy, as measured by Spearman correlation, are minimal (Figure 3A). Empirically, 238 

boundary saturation reduced ranking accuracy more substantially than ranking instability as it 239 

flattens the tails of the distribution, obscuring differences between lineages with exceptionally 240 

high or low fitness. The most substantial deterioration in accuracy is observed primarily when 241 

both patterns occur simultaneously (Figure 3C, circled), highlighting the inherent robustness of 242 

LeafRank under unknown parameter settings. This robustness provides significant flexibility. 243 

For instance, if a user wants to focus on low-fitness lineages, they can increase the time scale 244 

τ to drive most lineages toward the maximum fitness state, thereby accentuating the contrast 245 

with the lowest-fitness lineage. Because the ranking remains stable, the lowest-fitness lineage 246 

can be consistently identified even when most lineages are concentrated at the highest fitness 247 

due to the chosen parameter configuration. 248 

LeafRank provides curated parameter presets that users can adapt to achieve informative 249 

rankings (see Methods). Throughout the analyses in the manuscript, including applications to 250 

patient data, we implemented a distribution based quality control workflow to ensure that the 251 

resulting fitness distributions remained free of the two diagnostic patterns mentioned above. 252

Robust fitness ranking in the presence of whole genome duplication and spatial 253 

sampling bias 254 

To evaluate LeafRank under more realistic conditions, we tested its performance on trees con-255 

taining noise and distortions typical of single-cell DNA sequencing data from tumor samples. 256 

While our numerical studies in previous sections used true time (TT) tree, empirical phyloge-257 

nies are generally distance-matrix (DM) based, with branch lengths representing genomic di-258 

vergence accumulated through events such as SSNVs and SCNAs. This discrepancy is often 259 

manageable under a constant molecular clock assumption 46,47 , whereby genomic divergence 260 

serves as a proxy for evolutionary time. However, in tumors exhibiting CIN, whole genome 261 

duplication (WGD) frequently occurs and violates this assumption. By doubling the DNA sub-262 

strate, WGD accelerates the rate of subsequent genomic aberrations, thereby distorting branch 263 

lengths 39–41 . Therefore, genomic divergence in trees involving both WGD – and WGD + popu-264 

lations is no longer an appropriate proxy for evolutionary time. To account for aberration rate 265 

heterogeneity following WGD, we developed a modified chronos 46 algorithm to recalibrate DM 266 

trees under a state-dependent molecular clock model that assigns distinct aberration rates 267 

to WGD – and WGD + branches (see Methods and Supplemental Text). The resulting WGD-268 

adjusted ultrametric trees closely approximated the underlying TT trees. 269 

When evaluating noisy reconstructed trees, WGD-adjusted trees substantially outperformed 270 

unadjusted DM trees in both non-spatial and spatial simulations (Figure 4F,G). Across all sim-271 

ulations, TT trees yielded the highest Spearman correlations with the true fitness ranking, pro-272 

viding an idealized upper-bound reference for inference accuracy. The WGD adjustment re-273 

covered rankings that closely matched this TT-tree benchmark. More specifically, the WGD 274 

adjustment corrected the spurious fitness inflation observed in WGD – lineages. As shown in 275 

two representative virtual tumors (Figure 4B,C), DM trees systematically distorted the inferred 276 

fitness landscape. In these cases, the model misinterpreted the relatively shorter branches of 277 

WGD – lineages as evidence of rapid expansion. By recalibrating branch lengths, the WGD-278 

adjusted trees reduced this bias and restored correlations close to those obtained from the TT 279 

trees. 280 

The branch length recalibration assumes that aberration counts follow a Poisson distribu-281 

tion. To evaluate this assumption, we used the temporal information from the WGD-adjusted 282 

ultrametric trees to calculate the expected Poisson-based branch length distribution, which rep-283 

resent the expected number of events per branch. We then compared it against the observed 284 

distribution from the unadjusted DM trees (see Methods and Supplemental Text for details). As 285 

shown in Figure 4D,E, the observed branch length distribution aligns closely with the distribution 286 

implied by the chronological time in the WGD-adjusted tree. 287 

To evaluate LeafRank’s robustness against spatial structure-induced topological variations, 288 

we tested the framework on 3D virtual tumors 43 . Spatially structured populations result in sam-289

Lineage fitness landscapes of ovarian cancer reveal ongoing selection following 305 

truncal WGD 306 

We applied LeafRank to an scDNA-seq dataset of high-grade serous ovarian cancer (HGSOC) 307 

comprising treatment-naive samples collected from both primary and metastatic sites 44 (see 308 

Methods). This cohort exhibits highly recurrent WGD characterized by both truncal and sub-309 

clonal events, including cases of independent, parallel WGD alterations within a single patient’s 310 

tumor lineage. The recurrent yet variable distribution of WGD provided an ideal framework to in-311 

vestigate the fitness landscape of genome doubled cells and the specific selective advantages 312 

conferred by WGD. 313 

We first investigated whether WGD, occurring as a truncal event, acts as a dominant driver 314 

to produce a uniform fitness landscape across the resulting population, or if subsequent out-315 

growth involves strong selective events occur post-WGD. To this end, we analyzed ten patients 316 

with truncal WGD 44 whose samples were collected from the same anatomical site (including 317 

nine metastatic lesions, Figure S3 and Figure S4), minimizing sampling biases caused by spa-318 

tial divergence. In these tumors, because WGD is a truncal event, we hypothesized that lin-319 

eages arising after WGD evolve at a similar SCNA rate. Consequently, we converted DM trees 320 

reconstructed using MEDICC2 28 into ultrametric trees using the constant rate chronos method 321 

(see Methods). LeafRank produced robust ranking inferences from both the original DM trees 322 

and the transformed ultrametric trees, while the rootogram test indicated that the constant rate 323 

model adequately captured SCNA accumulation in the majority of truncal WGD tumors (Fig-324 

ure S3 and Figure S4). For consistency, we present results based on the ultrametric trees. 325 

Details about the analysis are provided in the Supplemental Text. 326 

LeafRank uncovered substantial fitness jumps during the tumor growth in over half of these 327 

patient tumors. In metastatic tumor OV-105 (sampled from omentum), for example, we defined 328 

16 potential fitness types (0-15) with birth rates increasing by a multiplicative coefficient of 1.1. 329 

LeafRank revealed three major fitness groups, identified as types 6, 9 and 10, emerging through 330 

successive fitness changes toward the upper portion of the phylogenetic tree (Figure 5). This 331 

pattern resembles directional selection 49 , in which fitness rises as cells approach a specific 332 

aneuploidy state. Notably, these transitions varied in magnitudes. A major leapfrog occurred 333 

from the ancestral type 0 to type 6, followed by another significant jump to type 9, and a final 334 

incremental step to type 10. These results suggest that post-WGD evolution is characterized 335 

by ongoing selection rather than neutral expansion. The discrete fitness changes imply the 336 

presence of specific driver events coincident with lineage branching (Figure 5A). Furthermore, 337 

the heterogeneity in selective advantage confirms that a multi-type branching process is more 338 

appropriate than a continuous weak fitness-gain model 42 for capturing inter-clonal disparities. 339

# DISCUSSION 457 

Cancer cells can exhibit substantial intratumor heterogeneity in fitness, and single-cell phylo-458 

genetic trees reconstructed from SSNVs and SCNAs can encode structural patterns that reflect 459 

this diversity. In this study, we introduce LeafRank , a probabilistic framework for ranking cellu-460 

lar fitness from single-cell phylogenetic trees. Unlike existing fitness-estimation approaches in 461 

cancer 32,33,38 , which often focus on the absolute fitness effects of specific genomic alterations 462 

or predefined subclones, LeafRank estimates the relative fitness ranking of sampled cells at 463 

the leaves of the tree. By combining a multitype branching-process model with a message-464 

passing algorithm, LeafRank integrates information encoded in the single-cell phylogeny, in-465 

cluding branch lengths and bifurcation patterns, to produce robust cell-level fitness estimates. 466

LeafRank provides a generalizable framework for fitness inference from single-cell phylo-479 

genies. First, it does not assume uniform fitness within a subclone, allowing selection within 480 

a given lineage to be resolved. Second, the message-passing algorithm provides an efficient 481 

framework for propagating information along the tree and can flexibly incorporate prior knowl-482 

edge or model assumptions. Third, because LeafRank provides fitness estimates for every 483 

sampled cell, it enables high-resolution comparisons of populations grouped not only by ge-484 

nomic alterations but also by other functional features, such as gene-expression profiles or 485 

epigenetic states, when multiomic data are available. This flexibility facilitates the study of the 486 

determinants of growth-rate variation in cancer. For example, by overlaying transcriptional sig-487 

natures onto fitness rankings, one can identify pathways associated with the clonal expansion 488 

of high-fitness lineages, thereby linking genomic architecture to cancer phenotype. 489 

A key challenge when inferring fitness in clinical samples is the reliance on an evolutionary 490 

molecular clock. In patient tumors with CIN, lineages can exhibit accelerated rates of SCNAs 491 

and SSNVs, severely violating linear temporal assumptions 39–41 . To resolve this, LeafRank 492 

incorporates a tree-rescaling strategy capable of navigating the piecewise linear tempos intro-493 

duced by macro-evolutionary events. By decoupling the aberration rates of WGD + and WGD –494 

lineages and optimizing a joint likelihood function, the framework effectively charts indepen-495 

dent rate heterogeneity onto a common temporal scale. This normalization allows LeafRank to 496 

robustly analyze phylogenies that would otherwise confound standard molecular-clock models. 497 

By analyzing recently published single-cell WGS data from HGSOC patients 44 , LeafRank 498 

identified cell populations with distinct selective advantages within tumors characterized by 499 

truncal WGD. While these tumors were predominantly sampled from metastatic sites, signs 500 

of selection were also evident in a primary tumor (OV-118) as well as in the subclonal WGD+ 501 

lineages of two additional primary cases. This indicates that the inferred selective advantages 502 

may reflect ongoing evolutionary pressures operating both early in tumor development and 503 

post-dissemination, potentially facilitated by WGD-associated CIN. This detectable selection 504 

underlying HGSOC expansion contrasts with previous bulk-sequencing studies of colorectal 505 

cancer that proposed an ‘effective-neutral’ model for some patients 12,58 . This divergence likely 506 

highlights the enhanced resolution of scDNA-seq, which unmasks subclonal fitness differences 507 

that are otherwise obscured by bulk averaging. Furthermore, while early bulk studies focused 508 

primarily on SSNVs in diploid regions, our framework leverages SCNA-based phylogenies to 509 

capture growth dynamics. We note, however, that when bulk sequencing is extended to multi-510 

region sampling, subclonal selection has indeed been detected in colorectal cancers with CIN 13 .511

in HGSOC tumors are consistent with existing hypotheses and empirical findings in cancer and 532 

other biological contexts 56,57 , suggesting that WGD primarily confers long-term evolutionary 533 

advantages rather than immediate fitness gains. 534 

In summary, by integrating information encoded in single-cell phylogenies, LeafRank cap-535 

tures fine-scale evolutionary dynamics during cancer growth. It provides a generalizable tool 536 

for translating tree structure into relative fitness rankings of single cells, offering a powerful 537 

framework for identifying the aberrations that drive cancer clonal expansion. 538 

Limitations 539 

LeafRank has several limitations. First, it is most informative in evolutionary regimes in which 540 

subclonal selection drives heterogeneous fitness levels across sampled cells. In such settings, 541 

the tree structure deviates from expectations under neutral evolution and contains a detectable 542 

signal of differential growth. By contrast, LeafRank is less informative for tumors evolving under 543 

strict neutrality, or for tumors that appear effectively neutral after a recent selective sweep, 544 

because sampled cells may show little residual tree-structural evidence of fitness differences. 545 

In these cases, LeafRank may return nearly uniform or weakly resolved fitness rankings rather 546 

than identifying distinct high-fitness lineages 12 .547 

Despite the utility of our tree rescaling framework, LeafRank’s performance remains bound 548 

to the quality of the input ultrametric tree. While using accumulated somatic aberrations serves 549 

as a pragmatic molecular-clock proxy, the underlying mutational processes can fluctuate wildly 550 

across lineages and over time. Consequently, in tumors with intricate subclonal WGD histo-551 

ries, our modified chronos implementation may only partially mitigate severe rate distortions, 552 

potentially reducing the reliability of downstream fitness rankings. Resolving these topologi-553 

cal ambiguities in highly rearranged genomes remains an open challenge. Ultimately, over-554 

coming this limitation will likely depend on integrating emerging multi-omic profiling 60 or CpG 555

LeafRank: model setup 568 

A binary phylogenetic tree, denoted by F, records the evolutionary history from a common 569 

ancestor cell to all N–1 internal nodes and N leaf nodes. Such a tree can be reconstructed using 570 

algorithms such as neighbor-joining 62 or MEDICC2 28 based on single-cell genomic profiles. As 571 

illustrated in Figure 1, we denote the tree topology as T , which includes the branch topology 572 

(the topological information describes the sibling relationships among cells) and the branch 573 

length information (the evolutionary distance, measured by the number of SSNVs or SCNAs, 574 

between ancestor and descendant cells). Note that under an assumed genomic aberration 575 

rate, the branch length information can be converted into estimates of the evolutionary time 576 

elapsed along each branch. 577 

Temporal calibration of branch lengths often relies on the molecular clock assumption 63 ,578 

under which genomic aberrations are assumed to accumulate at a relatively constant rate over 579 

time. Under this assumption, we denote the constant neutral aberration rate by μ. While this 580 

assumption is widely adopted in the context of SSNVs, it may not necessarily hold for SC-581 

NAs. For example, SSNV and SCNA rates can increase substantially following WGD 39–41 . In 582 

a later subsection and in the Supplemental Text, we further discuss our approach to this issue, 583 

particularly with respect to WGD events, and provide corresponding validation analyses. 584 

To model the underlying cellular evolutionary dynamics, we employ a multitype branching 585 

process model 45 . Under this model, we assume that there are V cell types, each characterized 586 

by distinct birth rates (b i) and death rates (d i), where i ∈ {1, · · · , V}. The cell types are ordered 587 

by increasing fitness such that b i+1 > b i. For simplicity, we assume the death rate d i is constant 588 

across all cell types. We use a backward-time convention for the phylogenetic tree, with the 589 

sampled cells observed at time t = 0 and the root located at time t = T. Thus, T denotes 590 

the elapsed time between the common ancestor and the sampled population. The biological 591 

process starts from a single type-1 cell at the root and evolves toward the sampled population. 592 

To model driver events, we further assume that a type i cell can divide into one type i and 593 

one fitness-advantaged type i + 1 cell at rate ν, which we refer to as the driver aberration 594 

rate. Notably, this rate ν is assumed to be much smaller than the neutral aberration rates 595 

μ, reflecting the rarity of driver events in cancer evolution. At the sampling time, we assume 596 

a uniform sampling scheme in which each living cell has an equal chance ρ ≪ 1 of being 597

Using LeafRank to analyze distance-matrix based phylogenetic tree 743 

Our proposed algorithm, LeafRank, assumes a reconstructed phylogenetic tree whose branch 744 

lengths reflect the elapsed time between ancestral and descendant nodes. For samples col-745 

lected at a single time point, an ultrametric tree therefore provides the ideal input. In practice, 746 

however, phylogenetic trees are more commonly reconstructed using DM-based methods, such 747 

as neighbor-joining or MEDICC2 28 , because aberration distances can be directly inferred from 748 

genomic data. Under a traditional molecular clock assumption, where aberrations accumulate 749 

at a constant rate, genomic divergence is directly proportional to elapsed time. This alignment 750 

allows distance-based trees to be interpreted within a temporal framework and utilized directly 751 

by LeafRank. However, this assumption is frequently violated by macro-evolutionary events 752 

such as WGD, which can induce CIN and trigger heterogeneous aberration rates across dis-753 

tinct lineages. To address this limitation, we developed a procedure to recover ultrametric trees 754 

under a state-dependent molecular clock model, accompanied by goodness-of-fit diagnostics 755 

and a parameter configuration framework for the transformed trees. 756 

Converting a DM tree into an ultrametric tree 757 

To estimate an ultrametric tree, we adopt the chronos function 46 in R. The chronos function 758 

constructs an ultrametric phylogenetic tree from DM tree under a molecular clock assumption 759 

coupled with a Poisson model. Specifically, it assumes the number of aberrations follow the 760 

Poisson distribution with mean proportional to the product of evolutionary time and a constant 761 

aberration rate. Using maximum likelihood scheme, it jointly infers a constant aberration rate 762 

and branch-specific evolutionary time to maximize the likelihood of observing aberration dis-763 

tances along each branch (see Supplemental Text). 764 

To capture the potential heterogeneity in aberration rates between WGD + and WGD – pop-765 

ulations, we propose a state-dependent molecular clock model. In this model, we classify tree 766 

branches as WGD – and WGD + branches according to the WGD status of its descendant node. 767 

In other words, we make the following assumption: 768 

Assumption (Anc. ). For each WGD + clade, the WGD event is assigned to the ancestral end of 769 

the branch leading to the earliest node inferred to be WGD +. Consequently, the branch leading 770 

to this earliest WGD + node is labeled as WGD +, together with all branches descending from 771 

this node. 772 

For an internal node, if both daughter lineages are WGD +, then the parent node is inferred to be 773 

WGD +, under the assumption that WGD status is irreversible. This irreversibility assumption is 774 

consistent with the minimum-event framework used by MEDICC2 28 : a return from a genome-775 

doubled state to a diploid-like state would require many copy-number losses and is therefore 776 

not favored under a parsimonious copy-number-event reconstruction. 777 

We then modified the ultrametric tree-inference framework using the following state-dependent 778 

Poisson model: 779 

Assumption (Poi. ). The WGD + population accumulates SCNAs according to the same Pois-780 

son process as the WGD – population, but with an approximately twofold higher aberration rate. 781 

This assumption does not impose an exact twofold relationship between the alteration rates of 782 

WGD + and WGD – branches. Instead, the two rates are estimated from the data. 783

nomic bins (500 kb each). Regions exhibiting heterogeneous directions across patients, 868 

demonstrating both increased and decreased CN in high-fitness cells, are filtered out. 869 

• Recurrence: The region passes the significance threshold (p < 0.01) in at least three 870 

independent patients. 871
