Abstract 24 PARPi are effective therapy for BRCA1/2 mutant cancers, yet recurrent PARPi resistance 25 frequently develops. The underlying mechanism of PARPi resistance remains largely unresolved. 26 Here, we identify STN1, a component of the CTC1/STN1/TEN1 (CST) complex, as a modulator 27 of PARPi resistance in BRCA2-deficient cells. RNA-seq analysis of PARPi-resistant cancer cells 28 from BRCA2-mutated backgrounds shows largely distinct transcriptomic profiles with limited 29 overlap, suggesting multiple routes to resistance. Notably, STN1 is consistently upregulated in 30 resistant cells. We observe that overexpression of STN1 enhances Olaparib resistance in multiple 31 BRCA2-deficient cell lines and alleviates DNA damage under replication stress. Mechanistically, 32 we find that STN1 overexpression increases RAD51 loading to stalled replication forks while 33 restricting MRE11 recruitment in BRCA2-deficient cells, thereby protecting stalled forks from 34 nascent-strand degradation. Furthermore, STN1 overexpression rescues the accumulation of 35 ssDNA gaps, a major determinant of PARPi sensitivity in BRCA2-deficient cells. Taken together, 36 these findings suggest that elevated STN1 levels can partially compensate for BRCA2 loss by 37 stabilizing stalled replication forks and limiting ssDNA gap accumulation. Our study uncovers a 38 STN1-dependent pathway of replication stress tolerance that promotes PARPi resistance 39 independently of homologous recombination restoration, highlighting STN1 as a potential 40 biomarker and mechanistic contributor to therapeutic resistance in BRCA2-mutated cancers. 41

Introduction 43 Poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase inhibitors (PARPi) have emerged as an effective targeted 44 therapy for tumors with defects in homologous recombination repair (HRR) (1-5). The most 45 frequently altered HRR genes are BRCA1 and BRCA2 , although pathogenic alterations in other 46 HRR-associated genes, including PALB2, BARD1, BRIP1, RAD51C, and RAD51D, can also 47 confer HRR deficiency in subsets of tumors (4,6). Clinically approved PARPi include Olaparib, 48 Niraparib, Rucaparib, Talazoparib, and most recently Saruparib. These agents primarily target 49 PARP1, with varying activity against PARP2, thereby disrupting PARP-dependent DNA damage 50 responses. Inhibition of PARP catalytic activity prevents PARylation and the efficient resolution 51 of DNA lesions, while PARP trapping stabilizes PARP-DNA complexes that impede replication 52 fork progression and promote replication-associated DNA damage. Increasing evidence indicates 53 that persistent single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) replication gaps are a major determinant of PARPi 54 sensitivity and contribute to synthetic lethality independently of, and in addition to, replication-55 associated double-strand breaks (DSBs) (6-9). In HR-deficient cells, the inability to repair these 56 replication-associated lesions results in genomic instability and ultimately cell death (6). 57 Exploiting this synthetic lethal interaction between PARP inhibition and HRR deficiency, PARPi 58 have been approved for the treatment of ovarian, breast, prostate, and pancreatic cancers harboring 59 BRCA1/2 mutations or other biomarkers of HRR deficiency (6,10,11). 60 Despite significant clinical benefit of PARPi, therapeutic resistance frequently develops, 61 leading to disease relapses or progression and posting a major challenge to patient survival (12,13). 62 Accumulating evidence indicates that PARPi resistance arises through diverse genetic and non-63 genetic mechanisms that may include reversion mutations restoring BRCA function, epigenetic 64 reactivation of silenced genes, restoration of HR, loss of PARP1 expression or mutations that 65

In addition, tumor cells can evade 66 PARPi-induced cytotoxicity by protecting stalled replication forks, thereby limiting replication-67 associated DNA damage, or by reducing the formation or persistence of toxic PARP-DNA 68 complexes (10,15). 69 BRCA1 and BRCA2 play distinct and essential roles in maintaining genome integrity 70 (18,19). BRCA1 primarily promotes DNA end resection at DSBs during the initiation of HR (20), 71 whereas BRCA2 facilitates RAD51 loading onto ssDNA to complete HR repair (21,22). Beyond 72 HR, BRCA2 protects stalled replication forks by promoting RAD51 loading, suppresses ssDNA 73 gap formation, and preventing MRE11-mediated degradation of nascent DNA (6,8,21-26). 74 Restoration of BRCA1/2 through either secondary mutations or other mechanisms that re-establish 75 HRR represents a major cause of acquired PARPi resistance (15,17,27-29). Additional resistance 76 mechanisms involve alterations in DNA damage response (DDR) signaling, replication fork 77 protection, and checkpoint pathways (30,31). To counteract such resistance, recent therapeutic 78 strategies aim at disrupting these compensatory DDR pathways to restore PARPi sensitivity 79 (30,32-34). 80 The CST complex, comprised of CTC1, STN1 and TEN1, is an 81 oligonucleotide/oligosaccharide-binding fold (OB-fold)-containing ssDNA-binding that binds to 82 ssDNA and ss/dsDNA junctions (35,36). Initially characterized for its role in telomere 83 maintenance, CST has emerged as an important regulator of genome stability by promoting fork 84 protection and modulating DSB repair (37-39). At telomeres, CST facilitates DNA polymerase α85 (POL α)-mediated C-strand fill-in and limits telomerase activity through the POT1-TPP1 complex 86 to prevent G-strands overextension (40-42). Development of PEO1-R 129 Olaparib-resistant PEO1-R cells were established from the parental PEO1 line through 130 intermittent, incremental in vitro exposure to Olaparib. The drug concentration was gradually 131 increased from 0.5 to 20 μM over a six-month period. At each concentration, cells were treated 132 with Olaparib for 48 hours, after which the medium was replaced with drug-free complete medium. 133 The cells were then allowed to recover and proliferate until reaching approximately 80-90% 134 confluence. Once stable growth was maintained for at least one to two passages at each 135 concentration, the Olaparib dose was doubled, and the treatment cycle was repeated until the final 136 concentration was achieved. 137

RNA sequencing and data analysis 139 Bulk RNA sequencing was performed on three independent biological replicates for each 140 cell line. Total RNA was extracted using the RNeasy Mini Kit (Qiagen) following the 141 manufacturer’s protocol. RNA integrity was assessed using an Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer, and 142 samples with RNA integrity number (RIN) ≥ 9 were used for library preparation. Poly(A)+ RNA 143 enrichment, sequencing library preparation and quantification were performed by Novogene 144 (Sacramento, CA). Illumina NovaSeq 6000 PE150 platform was used to generate paired-end reads. 145 After sequencing, raw data underwent QC to remove low-quality reads, reads with excessive “N”, 146 and adapters to produce high-quality reads, which were then aligned to the Homo sapiens reference 147 genome (GRCh38/hg38) using HISAT2 software (version: 2.2.1) for accurate read mapping with 148 the transcriptome. Gene expression levels were normalized as FPKM (fragments per kilobase of 149 transcript sequence per million mapped reads) values to determine the transcript abundance in each 150 sample. Differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were identified using thresholds of fold change ≥151 1.5, log2 fold change ≤ -0.58 or ≥ 0.58, and p-value < 0.05. Further analysis was performed using 152 R programming language within R software (version: RStudio 2025.09.1+401). Visualization of 153 differential expression data through volcano plots and verification of sample grouping results 154 through PCA (Principal component analysis) were performed using ggplot2 (version: 4.0.1). Heat 155 maps were constructed using the pheatmap package. The common overlapping DEGs were 156 identified through the Venn diagram using VennDiagram package. Pathway enrichment analysis 157 using KEGG (Kyoto encyclopedia of genes and genomes) was conducted using Bioconductor 158 packages. 159

RNA-seq analysis of Olaparib-resistant BRCA2-deficient cells reveals distinct and 296 

overlapping transcriptional changes in resistant cells 297 To uncover novel mechanisms underlying PARPi resistance in BRCA2-deficient cells, we 298 sought to take a screening approach by analyzing differentially expressed genes (DEGs) in PARPi-299 resistant versus sensitive cells, with the goal of identifying candidate genes and pathways that 300 contribute to PARPi resistance. The PEO1 and PEO4 cell lines are a pair of high-grade serous 301 ovarian cancer cell lines derived from the same patient at different stages of chemotherapeutic 302 treatment. While PEO1 is BRCA2-deficient due to a germline nonsense mutation and sensitive to 303 PARPi, PEO4 is BRCA2-proficient and resistant to PARPi due to a revertant mutation in BRCA2. 304 We also developed a new Olaparib-resistant cells (PEO1-R) by chronically exposing PEO1 cells 305 to gradually increasing concentrations of Olaparib over a period of over 6 months, as described in 306 Materials and Methods. Cell proliferation assay showed a significant increase in the IC50 of PEO1-307 R cells (2.198 μM) compared to the parental PEO1 cells (0.1124 μM), validating its PARPi 308 resistance (Fig. 1A). 309 We then performed RNA-seq using these three isogenic cells, PEO1, PEO4, and PEO1-R. 310 For each cell line, three independent biological replicates were analyzed. Through this analysis, 311 we found that PEO1-R and PEO4, although both resistant to PARPi, showed distinct transcriptome 312 profiles (Fig. 1B and Supplementary Fig. S1). We identified 2,512 DEGs in PEO1-R and 8,087 313 DEGs in PEO4 compared to the parental PEO1. Venn diagram analysis revealed substantial 314 overlap of DEGs (777 genes) between PEO1-R and PEO4 (Fig. 1C). The distinct transcriptome 315 and DEG profiles in PEO1-R and PEO4 cells suggest that PARPi resistance could arise through 316 more than one molecular route rather than a single, uniform change. Discussion 599 Although PARPi selectively target HRR-deficient cancers, resistance frequently emerges, 600 limiting their clinical efficacy. Understanding the molecular mechanisms that enable HR-deficient 601 cells to bypass replication stress and survive PARPi treatment remains a central challenge. In this 602 study, we identify a previously unrecognized mechanism that STN1 overexpression facilitates 603 BRCA2-deficient cells to acquire PARPi resistance. STN1 is a component of the ssDNA binding 604 protein complex CST. Previously we have demonstrated that CST directly interacts with RAD51 605 and promotes RAD51 recruitment to stalled replication forks to suppress MRE11-mediated 606 nucleolytic degradation (43,75). Using RNA-seq, we identify that STN1 is upregulated in PARPi-607 resistant BRCA2-deficient ovarian cancer cells. Our work shows that overexpressing STN1 608 functionally compensates for the loss of BRCA2 by restoring RAD51 loading to stalled forks, 609 suppressing MRE11 access to forks and nascent strand degradation, diminishing the formation of 610 ssDNA gaps, and reducing the accumulation of γH2AX foci, indicating that STN1 overexpression 611 decreases replication stress-induced DNA damage in BRCA2-deficient cells. Taken together, our 612 results support a model that STN1 overexpression partially compensates for the loss of BRCA2, 613 thereby attenuating the cytotoxic consequences of PARP inhibition. 614 Our finding suggests that STN1 may offer an alternative fork protection pathway by 615 decreasing the dependence on BRCA2 for maintaining fork stability and enabling cells to tolerate 616 PARPi-induced replication perturbations. While BRCA2 plays essential roles in both fork 617 protection and HRR, our findings suggest that bypassing the fork protection defect and/or ssDNA 618 gap accumulation may promote acquired PARPi resistance.
