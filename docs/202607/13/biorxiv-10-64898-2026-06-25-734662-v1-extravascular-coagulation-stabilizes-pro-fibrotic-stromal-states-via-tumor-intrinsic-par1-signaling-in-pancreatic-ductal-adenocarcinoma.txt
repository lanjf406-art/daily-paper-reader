ABSTRACT 31 Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) exhibits a desmoplastic stroma with context-dependent 32 tumor-restraining and tumor-promoting functions, highlighting the need to selectively reprogram 33 stromal states. Although intratumoral coagulation activity is frequently observed, its role in driving 34 these states remains unclear. Here, we identify extravascular coagulation as a tumor-amplified 35 regulatory module that stabilizes pro-fibrotic stromal states via tumor-intrinsic protease-activated 36 receptor-1 (PAR1) signaling. To establish clinical relevance and enable mechanistic interrogation, 37 we combined human tumor bioinformatics with a cross-scale experimental workflow integrating 38 microphysiological tumor-stroma (MPTS) models and in vivo systems to define and test this 39 regulatory axis. Analysis of The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) revealed heterogeneous F2R 40 (PAR1) expression across tumors, with elevated expression associated with fibrotic transcriptional 41 programs and reduced survival. Consistently, thrombin induced coordinated pro-fibrotic programs in 42 tumor cells and cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs), which were recapitulated in 43 microphysiological models where tumor-intrinsic PAR1 was required for amplification of 44 extracellular matrix deposition and CAF activation. Mechanistically, PAR1 signaling amplified tumor-45 stroma communication, in part through induction of TGF-β1-dependent pathways, establishing a 46 reinforcing feedback loop that stabilizes fibrotic remodeling. Pharmacologic inhibition of PAR1 47 suppressed pro-fibrotic CAF states, reprogrammed stromal states and attenuated tumor 48 progression across microphysiological and in vivo models. These findings establish extravascular 49 coagulation as a systems-level regulator of stromal state architecture in PDAC and define a cross-50 scale framework for targeting tumor-stroma regulatory circuits. 51 52

INTRODUCTION 57 Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is one of the most lethal malignancies, characterized by 58 a highly desmoplastic tumor microenvironment (TME) 1, 2 . This stroma, composed of dense 59 extracellular matrix (ECM) and activated stromal cells such as cancer-associated fibroblasts 60 (CAFs), exerts context-dependent tumor-restraining and tumor-promoting functions 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9 .61 While stromal targeting strategies have been extensively explored, approaches that broadly deplete 62 CAFs or ECM components have yielded limited clinical benefit and, in some cases, accelerated 63 disease progression. These findings highlight the dual roles of the desmoplastic stroma in PDAC 64 and underscore the need for therapeutic strategies that selectively reprogram stromal states rather 65 than eliminate them. 66 Emerging evidence indicates that extravascular coagulation is a prominent and underappreciated 67 feature of the PDAC TME. Leaky tumor vasculature 10, 11, 12, 13 permits circulating coagulation factors 68 to enter the tumor interstitial space, where tissue factor expressed by pancreatic cancer cells 69 (PCCs) initiates local activation of the coagulation cascade. This leads to the generation of 70 thrombin, a central serine protease that not only drives fibrin deposition but also engages cellular 71 signaling pathways through protease-activated receptors (PARs), particularly PAR1. PAR1 is 72 expressed across multiple compartments of the TME, including tumor cells, CAFs, endothelial cells, 73 and immune cells 14 , and has been implicated in tumor proliferation, invasion, and immune 74 modulation 15, 16, 17, 18 . These observations suggest that coagulation may function not only as a 75 byproduct of tumor progression but as an upstream regulator of stromal signaling dynamics. 76 Despite these observations, whether extravascular coagulation functions as a regulatory module 77 that governs stromal state dynamics, rather than merely a byproduct of tumor progression, remains 78 unclear. 79 A major challenge in resolving this question lies in the complexity of tumor-stroma interactions and 80 the distributed expression of PAR1 across multiple cell types. The co-existence of tumor, stromal, 81 vascular, and immune compartments complicates the attribution of cell type-specific contributions to 82 fibrosis and desmoplasia. Moreover, species-dependent differences in PAR1 signaling limit direct 83 translation of findings from murine models to human systems. As a result, a mechanistic 84 understanding of how tumor-intrinsic PAR1 signaling amplifies tumor-stroma communication and 85 coordinates CAF-mediated stromal activation across biological scales is lacking. 86 In this study, we address these challenges using a cross-scale experimental framework that 87 integrates human tumor bioinformatics, microphysiological tumor-stroma models, and in vivo 88 validation. Coagulation-fibrosis coupling defines a poor-prognosis stromal state and establishes a 101 

cross-scale tumor-stroma-coagulation framework in PDAC 102 To assess whether coagulation signaling is active in human PDAC, we examined PAR1 and tissue 103 factor (TF) expression in pancreatic tumors. Immunohistological analysis revealed that both 104 proteins were strongly upregulated in tumor tissues ( Fig. 1A ), accompanied by elevated expression 105 of the fibrosis marker α-SMA. In contrast, normal adjacent pancreas exhibited minimal expression 106 of TF, PAR1, and α-SMA, consistent with activation of extravascular coagulation and desmoplastic 107 signaling in PDAC. Additional tissue samples are presented in the supplement information ( Fig. 108 

S1 ). 109 Analysis of The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) PDAC cohort revealed heterogeneous expression of 110 F2R, encoding protease-activated receptor-1 (PAR1), across tumors ( Fig. 1B ). Patients were 111 stratified into quartiles based on F2R expression, with the top 25% defined as F2R-high and the 112 bottom 25% as F2R-low, while intermediate expression levels comprised the remaining tumors. 113 Kaplan-Meier analysis demonstrated that patients with F2R-high tumors exhibited significantly 114 reduced disease-specific survival (DSS) compared with the F2R-low group, which maintained 115 approximately 50% survival up to 60 months (p=0.05). Patients with intermediate F2R expression 116 displayed reduced survival relative to the F2R-low group, but significantly better outcomes than 117 those with F2R-high tumors, establishing a graded relationship between F2R expression and 118 disease progression. The association between elevated F2R expression and poor survival suggests 119 that coagulation-dependent stromal states may contribute to clinically aggressive disease 120 phenotypes. 121 Given the enrichment of fibrotic transcriptional programs in PDAC tumors and the central role of 122 transforming growth factor-β (TGF-β) signaling in regulating CAF activation and ECM remodeling 19, 123 20, 21 , we next examined the relationship between F2R and TGFB1, a key mediator of fibrotic 124 signaling ( Fig. 1C ). Across tumors, F2R expression exhibited a modest but significant positive 125 correlation with TGFB1 expression (Pearson r = 0.39; Spearman ρ = 0.20), indicating transcriptional 126 coupling between coagulation signaling and fibrotic programs. To assess the clinical relevance of 127 this association, patients were further stratified based on combined F2R and TGFB1 expression. 128 Kaplan-Meier analysis ( Fig. S2 ) showed that survival patterns were largely dictated by F2R 129 expression, with TGFB1 expression refining, but not overriding, F2R-defined risk groups. Thus, 130 F2R-high/TGFB1-high group showed significantly reduced survival relative to the F2R-low/TGFB1-131 low group. These results suggest that concurrent activation of coagulation and fibrotic signaling 132 programs identifies a clinically aggressive fibrosis-associated stromal state. 133

To further define the transcriptional programs associated with elevated PAR1 signaling, gene set 134 enrichment analysis (GSEA) was performed comparing F2R-high and F2R-low tumors ( Fig. 1D ). 135 F2R-high tumors exhibited strong enrichment of extracellular matrix organization (NES = 2.61) and 136 TGF-β signaling pathways (NES = 2.46), accompanied by enrichment of coagulation-related gene 137 programs (NES = 1.83). These programs represent dominant transcriptional outputs relative to 138 coagulation signaling itself, positioning coagulation as an upstream regulatory input within a tumor-139 stroma-coagulation axis rather than a primary transcriptional program. 140 Based on these observations, we hypothesized that thrombin-PAR1 signaling amplifies tumor– 141 stroma communication within a tumor-stroma-coagulation axis and contributes to desmoplastic 142 remodeling of the tumor microenvironment ( Fig. 1E ). Specifically, thrombin is proposed to activate 143 PAR1 in both pancreatic cancer cells (PCCs) and CAFs, promoting CAF proliferation, extracellular 144 matrix deposition, and CAF-mediated contractility. We further posited that PAR1 suppression may 145 reprogram the PDAC stroma without compromising tumor-restraining functions, motivating 146 development of a system enabling compartment-resolved perturbation of PAR1 signaling. 147 To enable mechanistic interrogation of this axis, we developed a microphysiological tumor–stroma 148 (MPTS) model that recapitulates extravascular coagulation within a controlled PDAC 149 microenvironment ( Fig. 1F ). In this system, thrombin introduced through the perfusion channel 150 mimics its accumulation in the tumor interstitium and establishes controlled exposure to 151 coagulation-dependent stimuli across the collagen type I matrix, exposing the tumor and stromal 152 compartments to physiologically relevant coagulation-dependent stimuli. Confocal imaging and 153 three-dimensional reconstruction confirmed the formation of integrated PCC-CAF networks within 154 the matrix, with extensive cell-cell contact and alignment ( Supplementary Video 1 ), consistent with 155 physiologic tumor-stroma architecture. 156 To investigate whether coagulation signaling contributes to the fibrotic PDAC tumor 157 microenvironment, we implemented a cross-scale translational workflow centered on the tumor– 158 stroma–coagulation axis, integrating MPTS models and in vivo systems for systematic target 159 identification, validation, and efficacy evaluation ( Fig. 1G ). This framework enabled controlled 160 genetic and pharmacologic perturbation of PAR1 to interrogate coagulation-dependent tumor-161 stroma communication. Tumor-derived KPC cells from Kras G12D/+ ; p53 R172H/+ ; Elas CreER/+ mice were 162 used to generate PAR1 knockout (KPC PAR1 KO ) lines alongside wild-type controls (KPC PAR1 WT )163 as previously reported 15 , while murine cancer-associated fibroblasts (mCAFs) enabled modeling of 164 reciprocal tumor-stroma interactions. Human PDAC cell lines were subsequently used to evaluate 165 pharmacologic PAR1 inhibition in MPTS models and to establish xenograft systems for in vivo 166 validation. 167

Thrombin induces coordinated pro-fibrotic signaling and tumor-stroma-coagulation coupling 178 

in murine systems 179 To determine whether coagulation signaling modulates tumor and stromal responses, we examined 180 the effect of thrombin on genetically engineered murine PCC and CAF populations - KPC Par1 KO ,181 KPC Par1 WT and murine cancer-associated fibroblasts (mCAFs). Quantitative PCR analysis 182 confirmed that PAR1 expression increased in KPC WT cells following thrombin stimulation, 183 whereas expression was minimal as expected in KPC Par1 KO cells ( Fig. 2A ). Thrombin treatment 184 also induced PAR1 expression in mCAFs, extending this response across both tumor and stromal 185 compartments. 186 We next performed transcriptomic profiling to define thrombin-induced signaling programs. RNA-187 seq analysis was conducted on KPC Par1 WT and mCAF cell lines. GSEA revealed that thrombin 188 stimulation in KPC Par1 WT cells enriched KRAS signaling, TGF-β signaling, and epithelial-to-189 mesenchymal transition (EMT) pathways, consistent with PDAC progression and stromal 190 remodeling ( Fig. 2B ). In parallel, mCAFs exhibited enrichment of inflammatory response, KRAS 191 signaling, TGF-β signaling, angiogenesis, and coagulation pathways, indicating coordinated but 192 compartment-specific transcriptional activation within a fibrosis-associated tumor-stroma context. 193 To further delineate shared transcriptional responses, we analyzed genes within commonly 194 upregulated pathways, focusing on TGF-β and KRAS signaling. Hallmark genes associated with 195 these pathways are shown in the supplement information ( Fig. S3 ). Heatmap analysis of differential 196 gene expression, in conjunction with pathway enrichments, revealed induction of pro-fibrotic and 197 signaling-associated genes across both compartments ( Fig. 2C ). In KPC cells, some of the 198 upregulated genes included Tgfb1 , Tgfbr1 , Thbs1 , Furin , Col1a1 , Cd44 , Serpine1 , and Csf2 , while 199 mCAFs showed increased expression of Tgfb1 , Thbs1 , Itga5 , and Serpine1 . These transcriptional 200 changes support establishment of a pro-fibrotic stromal signaling state. 201 The induction of Tgfb1 in both tumor cells and CAFs supports a paracrine signaling axis, with 202 tumor-derived ligands acting on stromal cells, while receptor-level regulation appears to be primarily 203 tumor-intrinsic. Other enriched genes map onto functional modules of fibrotic remodeling and 204 fibrinolysis: Thbs1 promotes activation of latent TGF-β 22, 23 , Furin mediates proteolytic activation of 205 TGF-β and related factors 24, 25, 26 , and Serpine1 (expressed as Plasminogen Activator Inhibitor-1, 206 PAI-1) inhibits fibrinolysis and stabilizes fibrin-rich matrices 27, 28 . Notably, the lack of significant 207 induction of Tgfbr1 in CAFs and the reduction of Col1a1 expression in mCAFs indicate that 208 thrombin-PAR1 signaling alone is insufficient to directly drive canonical fibroblast activation, 209 supporting a model in which tumor-derived signals dominate stromal activation within the tumor-210 stroma-coagulation axis. 211

Tumor-intrinsic PAR1 signaling and heterogeneous stromal responses in human PDAC 233 

models 234 To ensure that our murine-based MPTS recapitulates the tumor-stroma-coagulation axis in human 235 systems, we cultured human PDAC cell lines Panc10.05, Panc1, and MIA PaCa-2 alongside the 236 cancer-associated fibroblast cell line CAF19. The cell lines were selected based on varying levels of 237 TF and PAR1 expression, as determined by qPCR, ranging from low in Panc10.05 to high in MIA 238 PaCa-2 as compared to human pancreatic ductal epithelial cells (HPDE) ( Fig. S5 for TF and Fig. 239 

3A for PAR1). This variation enables systematic interrogation of PAR1-dependent tumor-stroma 240 coupling under thrombin stimulation while maintaining CAF19 as a constant stromal component, 241 providing a controlled framework to resolve tumor-intrinsic contributions to stromal activation. 242 PAR1 mRNA expression in PCCs generally demonstrated increase in response to thrombin 243 treatment ( Fig. 3B ). These cell lines were introduced into the MPTS platform and continuously 244 exposed to 0.2 U/mL thrombin for eight days. In monoculture, thrombin treatment did not 245 significantly affect the growth or morphology of PDAC cells. In contrast, CAF19 exhibited a notable 246 increase in proliferation under thrombin stimulation, while PDAC cell lines remained largely 247 unchanged ( Fig. 3C ), shifting the dominant response toward the stromal compartment under 248 isolated conditions. 249 We next evaluated PAR1-dependent tumor-stroma coupling using co-culture models of human 250 PCC-CAF pairs. Thrombin treatment did not significantly increase PCC growth across all tested 251 combinations ( Fig. 3D ). CAF19 proliferation was not uniformly enhanced under co-culture 252 conditions, despite the increase observed in monoculture. A significant increase in CAF growth 253 (~1.2-fold) was observed only in the MIA PaCa-2-CAF19 co-culture following thrombin treatment, 254 accompanied by a more elongated CAF morphology consistent with an activated state. Given that 255 MIA PaCa-2 cells express the highest levels of PAR1, this response localizes stromal activation to 256 tumor-intrinsic PAR1-high contexts and again suggests that heterogeneity in the tumor leads to 257 differential activation of the stromal compartment. 258 In contrast to murine systems ( Fig. 2 ), where thrombin stimulation promoted robust tumor growth 259 through PAR1-dependent tumor-stroma coupling, human models exhibited selective stromal 260 activation, linked to tumor-intrinsic PAR1 expression. Across these systems, thrombin-PAR1 261 signaling engaged a conserved tumor-stroma-coagulation axis, while downstream phenotypic 262 outputs varied according to baseline signaling states and tumor context. These suggest that tumor 263 proliferation represents only one facet of the thrombin-PAR1 axis and may not represent the 264 predominant functional output while other fibrosis-associated microenvironmental programs may be 265 affected, including ECM remodeling and stromal activation. 266

Tumor-derived TGF-β1 mediates PAR1-dependent stromal activation and ECM remodeling 276 In addition to effects on proliferation through PAR1 signaling, the RNA-seq analysis ( Fig. 2 )277 identified a suite of genes important in tumor-stroma crosstalk that were upregulated in response to 278 thrombin. To validate these findings in human PCCs and CAFs, we performed RT-qPCR to 279 measure mRNA levels of key genes identified through differential expression analyses, focusing on 280 the MIA PaCa-2–CAF19 pair, which exhibited the most robust response to thrombin ( Fig. 3 ). In MIA 281 PaCa-2 cells, thrombin treatment significantly increased expression of fibrosis-associated genes, 282 most prominently TGFB1 ( Fig. 4A ). CSF2 (colony stimulating factor 2) also showed a trend toward 283 increased expression, suggesting a potential role in modulating tumor-stroma and immune 284 interactions. This induction identifies TGFB1 as a dominant downstream effector of tumor-intrinsic 285 PAR1 signaling within the tumor-stroma-coagulation axis, with PAR1 functioning as a control node 286 governing tumor-derived TGF-β signaling. 287 We next examined whether thrombin or TGF-β1 directly activates CAFs. In contrast to tumor cells, 288 thrombin treatment did not significantly alter fibrotic gene expression in CAF19 ( Fig. S6 ), separating 289 stromal activation from direct thrombin signaling. Given the induction of TGFB1 in tumor cells and 290 the minimal direct response of CAFs to thrombin, we tested whether tumor-derived TGF-β1 could 291 mediate stromal activation. CAF19 cells were treated with exogenous TGF-β1 ( Fig. 4B ), which 292 significantly increased expression of COL1A1 and ITGA5, consistent with enhanced fibrotic activity 293 and integrin-mediated signaling 29, 30 . TGFB1 expression was also upregulated in CAFs following 294 TGF-β1 stimulation, indicating a reinforcing signaling loop. 295 To further evaluate this mechanism, we measured secretion of total (latent and active) TGF-β1 by 296 ELISA. Thrombin treatment increased TGF-β1 secretion in MIA PaCa-2 cells, whereas other PCCs 297 and CAF19 showed minimal changes ( Fig. 4C ). This effect was again most pronounced in MIA 298 PaCa-2, consistent with its high PAR1 expression, positioning tumor cells as the dominant source 299 of TGF-β1 within this system. We next assessed downstream functional consequences by 300 measuring extracellular matrix production. ELISA analysis of soluble collagen revealed that MIA 301 PaCa-2-CAF19 co-cultures exhibited the highest collagen levels and the greatest increase following 302 thrombin treatment, whereas monoculture conditions showed minimal changes ( Fig. 4D ), linking 303 ECM remodeling to PAR1-dependent tumor–stroma coupling. 304 Immunofluorescence analysis further resolved CAF activation states. αSMA expression increased 305 in CAF19 following direct thrombin exposure and TGF-β1 treatment ( Fig. 4E ). TGF-β1 induced a 306 stronger increase than thrombin alone, placing tumor-derived TGF-β1 downstream of PAR1 as the 307 dominant driver of CAF activation. 6B ). This observation contrasts with the anti-tumor effects of PAR1 KO in KPC orthotopic models 357 previously reported 15 . The differential effects observed between genetic PAR1 deletion and 358 pharmacologic PAR1 inhibition likely reflect distinctions between mechanistic ablation and 359 therapeutic perturbation of an established TME. In these murine systems, genetic deletion of Par1 360 in KPC cells prevents engagement of tumor-stroma coupling from the time of tumor implantation, 361 revealing the mechanistic sufficiency of tumor-intrinsic PAR1 signaling in driving tumor-promoting 362 stromal interactions. In contrast, VPX treatment is initiated after establishment of tumor architecture 363 and stromal organization, where CAF populations, ECM deposition, and TGF-β1-mediated 364 feedback loops may already partially sustain fibrotic signaling independently of acute PAR1 activity. 365 Pharmacologic inhibition therefore preferentially reprograms stromal states rather than inducing 366 immediate collapse of primary tumor burden. This distinction further supports a model in which 367 thrombin-PAR1 signaling functions as an upstream regulator of stromal state establishment and 368 maintenance within the tumor-stroma-coagulation axis. 369 Histological and spatial transcriptomic analyses revealed substantial reorganization of tumor 370 architecture following VPX treatment. DISCUSSION 445 Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is characterized by a highly desmoplastic tumor 446 microenvironment in which stromal components exert both tumor-promoting and tumor-restraining 447 functions 4, 31 . This duality has limited the success of strategies aimed at broadly depleting the 448 stroma, highlighting the need for approaches that selectively reprogram stromal states. In this 449 study, extravascular coagulation emerges as a regulatory module that stabilizes pro-fibrotic stromal 450 states through tumor-intrinsic PAR1 signaling. Integration of human tumor data, microphysiological 451 models, and in vivo validation defines a tumor-stroma-coagulation axis that links coagulation 452 signaling to stromal remodeling and tumor progression. 453 Coagulation signaling is commonly interpreted as a consequence of tumor-associated vascular 454 leakage 32, 33 . The present results instead position coagulation as an upstream regulator of tumor-455 stroma communication. Elevated F2R (PAR1) expression in human PDAC associates with fibrotic 456 transcriptional programs and poor survival, while pathway-level analysis resolves coordinated 457 activation of extracellular matrix, TGF-β, and coagulation signaling. These programs dominate over 458 coagulation itself at the transcriptional level, placing coagulation as a regulatory input that organizes 459 downstream stromal remodeling. 460 The clinical associations observed in this study further support the relevance of the tumor-stroma-461 coagulation axis in PDAC progression. Elevated F2R expression correlates with reduced disease-462 specific survival, with divergence of survival curves occurring within the 12~30 month interval, 463 consistent with patterns of early recurrence reported following pancreaticoduodenectomy. Prior 464 clinical studies 34, 35 have shown that perioperative therapy timing critically influences outcomes 465 within this window, suggesting that early disease progression is driven by biologically aggressive 466 tumor states. The association between high F2R expression and adverse outcomes provides a 467 molecular framework for these observations, linking coagulation signaling to tumor-promoting 468 stromal architecture. Notably, the combined F2R-high/TGFB1-high phenotype is associated with 469 particularly poor survival, consistent with tumors characterized by aggressive desmoplastic 470 remodeling and increased risk of early metastasis. This expression signature may therefore serve 471 as a biomarker for risk stratification and selection of neoadjuvant therapeutic strategies. Because 472 F2R and TGFB1 jointly capture both coagulation and fibrotic signaling programs, this signature may 473 provide a clinically actionable framework for identifying tumors with highly activated fibrosis-474 associated stromal states. 475 Microphysiological tumor-stroma (MPTS) models resolve how this regulatory input is translated into 476 functional behavior. Thrombin induces coordinated transcriptional programs across tumor and 477 stromal compartments and promotes PAR1-dependent tumor-stroma coupling. Tumor growth 478

The 542 integration of these independent model systems enables systematic identification, perturbation, and 543 validation of signaling axes across biological scales. MATERIALS AND METHODS 576 

Cell Culture and Reagents 577 Murine pancreatic cancer (KPC2) and cancer-associated fibroblast (mCAF) cell lines were isolated 578 from genetically engineered KPC mice (LSL-Kras G12D/+ ; LSL-Trp53 R172H/+ ; Pdx1-Cre). Human PDAC 579 cell lines (Panc10.05, Panc1, MIA PaCa-2) and CAF lines (CAF19, 02-hT) were cultured under 580 standard conditions in RPMI or DMEM/F12 supplemented with 2.05 mM L-glutamine, 100 μg/mL 581 penicillin/streptomycin, and 5% or 10% FBS. Cells were maintained below 80% confluence and 582 used within defined passage limits. Cryopreservation was performed using 90% FBS and 10% 583 DMSO. Tumor and CAF cells were fluorescently labeled with TdTomato and GFP, respectively. 584 

RT-qPCR 585 Gene expression was quantified by RT-qPCR following RNA isolation and cDNA synthesis. 586 Expression levels of genes of interest were normalized to 18S RNA using the ΔΔ Ct method. Primer 587 and probe sequences are provided in Supplementary Methods. 588 

RNA-seq and transcriptomic analysis 589 Total RNA was isolated from KPC tumor cells and mCAFs following thrombin stimulation under 590 defined experimental conditions. Cells were serum-starved (1% FBS, 24 h) prior to stimulation with 591 thrombin (1 U/mL, 24 h; n = 3 per group). RNA samples were quality-checked and sequenced using 592 paired-end Illumina platforms with a minimum depth of 30 million reads per sample. Raw 593 sequencing reads were subjected to quality control, including adapter trimming and filtering based 594 on Phred score and read length. High-quality reads were aligned to the mouse reference genome 595 (GRCm38) using splice-aware aligners. Gene-level counts were generated using feature-based 596 quantification tools. Differential expression analysis was performed using the edgeR framework with 597 multiple testing correction. Genes meeting false discovery rate (FDR) thresholds were considered 598 differentially expressed. Pathway enrichment analysis was conducted using Ingenuity Pathway 599 Analysis (IPA) and Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) with MSigDB gene sets to identify 600 thrombin-PAR1-associated transcriptional programs. Co-expressed gene clusters were identified, 601 and heatmaps were generated to visualize expression patterns across conditions. Detailed 602 procedures are described in Supplementary Methods. 603 

Microphysiological tumor-stroma (MPTS) model 604 Microfluidic MPTS devices were fabricated using soft lithography, as described previously 50, 52, 53 , to 605 reconstruct fibrosis-associated tumor-stroma signaling under controlled coagulation conditions in a 606 3D tumor microenvironment. Tumor cells and CAFs were embedded in type I collagen and exposed 607 to thrombin and/or PAR1 inhibitor (vorapaxar) under controlled flow conditions. Detailed fabrication 608 and culture procedures are described in Supplementary Methods. 609
