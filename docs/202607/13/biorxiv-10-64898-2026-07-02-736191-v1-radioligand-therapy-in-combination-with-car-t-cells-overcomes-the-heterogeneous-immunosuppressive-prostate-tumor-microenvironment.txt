Abstract: 43 

> 177

Lu-PSMA-617 (Pluvicto TM , Lu-177 RLT) is an FDA-approved targeted radioligand therapy 44 

(RLT) for metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC), but its durability of response 45 

to this singular approach poses a challenge to the field. Chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cell 46 

therapy has revolutionized clinical practice for hematological malignancies, but its clinical 47 

development for solid tumors, including mCRPC, has been encumbered by antigen heterogeneity 48 

and the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME). Here, we evaluate the therapeutic 49 

combination of Lu-177 RLT and PSCA-CAR T cells to overcome these barriers. In human 50 

xenograft and mouse syngeneic prostate cancer models with homogeneous or heterogeneous 51 

antigen expression, the sequential administration of Lu-177 RLT, cyclophosphamide (Cy), and 52 

PSCA-CAR T cells improves tumor control and prolongs survival compared to monotherapies. 53 

Mechanistically, Lu-177 RLT alone or with Cy remodels the TME by promoting pro-inflammatory 54 

myeloid responses and activating endogenous T cells, while enhancing CAR T cell activation and 55 

effector function. We additionally evaluated 225 Ac-PSMA-617 RLT as an emerging approach in 56 

combination with CAR T cells and observed anti-tumor responses, supporting its potential as an 57 

alternative RLT partner. These findings support RLT as an immune priming strategy to enhance 58 

CAR T cell therapy and provide a rationale for clinical translation of this combination in mCRPC. 59

INTRODUCTION 62 

Metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC) remains a lethal disease, with a median 63 

survival of 25.6 months ( 1). Despite recent advances, no therapy can reliably eliminate mCRPC 64 

tumors or prevent relapse. Recurrences are largely driven by multiple treatment-resistance 65 

mechanisms or emergence/selection of therapy-resistant clones, highlighting the need for 66 

combinatorial strategies that can overcome resistance to therapy and provide long-term durability 67 

in response. 177 Lu-PSMA-617 (Pluvicto TM , Lu-177) is a recent FDA-approved radioligand therapy 68 

(RLT) that represents a major clinical advance for patients with mCRPC, selectively targeting the 69 

highly expressed prostate-specific membrane antigen (PSMA) to deliver tumor-directed radiation 70 

(2). While Lu-177 RLT improves survival in patients, responses are rarely durable ( 3, 4 ). 71 

Resistance is thought to arise from tumor antigen heterogeneity and intrinsic or acquired 72 

radioresistance, thereby limiting long-term efficacy. 73 

Chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cell therapy is a cellular immunotherapy that engineers patient 74 

T cells to target tumor-associated cell surface antigens. Several CAR targets are being clinically 75 

evaluated for the treatment of mCRPC ( 5-7 ). Our group has demonstrated safety and promising 76 

responses of CAR T cells targeting prostate stem cell antigen (PSCA) for mCRPC ( 8). However, 77 

responses are constrained by antigen heterogeneity and a profoundly immunosuppressive tumor 78 

microenvironment (TME), highlighting the need for novel combinatorial and/or T cell engineering 79 

approaches that simultaneously address both heterogeneity and immune suppression ( 9). 80 

Among the numerous potential combination strategies, radiotherapy and immunotherapy have 81 

emerged as ideal combinatorial candidates for the treatment of mCRPC. For instance, recent 82 

studies suggest that Lu-177 RLT can induce immunomodulation and sensitize tumors to immune 83 

checkpoint blockade (ICB), which is being investigated clinically ( 10-12 ). In parallel, evidence has 84 

shown that focal radiation therapy (RT) shifts the immune landscape by increasing antigen 85 

presentation and enhancing CAR T cell trafficking, infiltration, and active functionality ( 13-15 ). Our 86 

group has an ongoing phase 1b trial evaluating metastasis-directed radiotherapy (MDRT) in 87 

combination with PSCA-CAR T cells for the treatment of mCRPC (NCT05805371). 88 

Building on these insights, here we preclinically evaluate the efficacy and safety of combining Lu-89 

177 RLT with PSCA-CAR T cell therapy in human xenograft and mouse syngeneic prostate 90 

cancer models. We perform a comprehensive assessment of immunological changes upon single 91 

or combination treatment, integrating flow cytometry and single-cell RNA sequencing. Our data 92

RESULTS 101 

PSMA and PSCA are heterogeneously expressed in prostate cancer 102 

We first evaluated expression of PSMA and PSCA using clinically relevant models and publicly 103 

available datasets. Immunohistochemistry and bulk RNA-sequencing (RNA-seq) analysis of the 104 

MURAL collection of prostate cancer patient-derived xenografts (PDXs) ( 16 ), which includes both 105 

primary and metastatic tumors representing diverse pathological states, revealed variability in 106 

PSMA and PSCA expression both across different PDX models and within individual tumor 107 

sections ( Figure 1a-c ). To extend the findings, we incorporated the Human Prostate Single Cell 108 

Atlas (HuPSA), which compiles 74 samples from public datasets with cell type annotations ( 17 ). 109 

Co-expression of PSMA and PSCA was observed in certain cell types (prostate adenocarcinoma, 110 

AdPCa), whereas other cell types exhibited heterogeneous expression patterns with limited 111 

overlap between PSMA and PSCA (Figure 1d-e ). Such intra- and inter-tumor antigen 112 

heterogeneity highlighted a key limitation of single-antigen targeted therapies, namely, tumor 113 

relapse driven by clonal escape of antigen-negative or antigen-low populations. Notably, although 114 

PSMA and PSCA are commonly associated with adenocarcinoma, their expression was also 115 

detected in mixed neuroendocrine/adenocarcinoma phenotypes ( Figure 1a ). Collectively, these 116 

data reveal substantial antigen heterogeneity in prostate cancer, providing a strong rationale for 117 

PSMA and PSCA dual-targeting therapeutic strategies. 118

models 121 

To evaluate the therapeutic potential of combining PSMA-targeted RLT with PSCA-targeted CAR 122 

T cells, we engineered the human prostate cancer cell line PC3-PIP (PSMA-positive) to express 123 

human PSCA and firefly luciferase (ffLuc). Following intracardiac injection to model metastatic 124 

disease, these cells established metastases in the liver and bone of NSG mice, which were 125 

detected by 68 Ga-PSMA-11 positron emission tomography/computed tomography (PET/CT) and 126

not produce complete responses ( Figure 2c-d ). In contrast, sequential administration of RLT 130 

followed by PSCA-CAR T cells markedly enhanced therapeutic efficacy, leading to tumor 131 

regression and long-term survival in 40% of mice. The combination therapy was well-tolerated, 132 

with no significant changes observed in body weight ( Figure 2e ). To further assess the 133

day 18 (13 days post-RLT and 9 days post-CAR T cell infusion) ( Figure 6a ). We were able to 335 

detect CAR T cells, either PSCA-CAR or TAG72-CAR, in tumors ( Figure 6b ). Comparing RLT + 336 

Cy + PSCA-CAR group with the Cy + PSCA-CAR group, CAR T cells exhibited increased 337 

expression of activation markers ( Tnfrsf9 and Il2ra ) and effector genes most notably Ifng (Figure 338 

6c ). Differential gene expression analysis further revealed upregulation of genes associated with 339 

activation ( Tnfsf4 , Tnfsf13 , Fos , and Ltbr ), stemness ( Tcf7 ), TCR signaling ( Hck , Pik3cb ), and 340 

effector function ( Batf2 , Cd63 , and Ifi30 ) ( Figure 6d ). Pathway enrichment analysis of CAR T 341 

cells suggested that RLT may enhance CAR T cell programs related to cytokine production, 342 

activation, and chemotaxis, particularly toward myeloid populations (Figure 6e ). M1-like 343 

macrophages in the RLT + Cy + PSCA-CAR group showed enrichment of pathways associated 344 

with immune cell chemotaxis, extending our earlier observations of RLT- and Cy + RLT-mediated 345 

myeloid remodeling ( Figure 6f, 3n, & 4c ). Additionally, interferon-related pathways and cytokine 346 

signaling associated with CD4 + T cell regulation were observed. Of note, there was an enrichment 347 

within macrophages and monocyte populations with respect to interferon pathways and cytokine 348 

production important for CD4 T cells regulation, suggesting coordinated crosstalk between T cell 349 

and myeloid cell compartments in response to the combination therapy ( Figure S10a-b ). To 350 

further investigate inter-cellular interactions, we performed cell-cell communication analysis. 351 

Comparing signaling information flow from M1-like macrophages to CAR T cells, we observed 352 

near-complete dominance of MHC-II, MIF, and ADGRL signaling in the RLT + Cy + PSCA group, 353 

indicating enhanced antigen presentation and inflammatory communication toward CAR T cells 354 

(Figure 6g ). In contrast, the Cy + PSCA group showed higher relative contribution from CD200 355 

and CD48 pathways, which are associated with immune regulation and suppressive signaling, in 356 

the absence of RLT. Beyond M1-like macrophages, we also observed enrichment of IL16 and 357 

TNF ligand-receptor interactions originating from M2-like macrophages and monocytes towards 358 

CAR T cells, further supporting the coordinated network of myeloid-CAR T cell communication 359 

that may contribute to the enhanced efficacy of the combination therapy ( Figure S10c-d ). 360 

Collectively, these findings suggest that RLT enhances CAR T cell activation and coordinates 361 

proinflammatory myeloid cell responses. 362

Combination of Ac-225 RLT with CAR T cells enhances anti-tumor efficacy in prostate 364

While beta-emitter Lu-177 has gained FDA approval, increasing attention has been directed 366 

toward the alpha-emitter Ac-225 as a promising RLT strategy for treating mCRPC ( 22 ). 225 Ac-367 

PSMA-617 (Ac-225 RLT) delivers a high radiation dose per decay and is particularly effective 368 

against small-volume disease, such as micrometastases and circulating tumor cells ( 23 ). In the 369 

metastatic human xenograft model, NSG mice were injected intracardially with PC3-PIP-PSCA-370 

ffLuc cells, followed by Ac-225 RLT treatment (15 kBq), and PSCA-CAR T cells (0.2 x 10 6)371 

administered 4 or 6 days after RLT ( Figure 7a ). Ac-225 RLT monotherapy resulted in minimal 372 

tumor control, whereas combination with PSCA-CAR T cells produced greater control and a more 373 

durable anti-tumor response ( Figure 7b ). Survival analysis demonstrated that the combination 374 

significantly improved survival compared to RLT alone (P = 0.0004), with a trend toward improved 375 

survival compared to PSCA-CAR T cell monotherapy (P = 0.0952) ( Figure 7c ). We further 376 

evaluated this combination in the antigen-heterogeneous syngeneic prostate cancer model. 377

were treated with Ac-225 RLT (18.5 kBq), followed 3 days later with Cy (100 mg/kg), and 1 day 379 

later with PSCA-CAR or control TAG72-CAR T cells (1 x 10 6) ( Figure 7d ). Consistent with prior 380 

observations, PSCA-CAR T cells alone induced transient tumor control ( Figure 7e ). The addition 381 

of Ac-225 RLT as a priming strategy improved tumor control. We observed a trend toward a higher 382 

proportion of responders in the RLT + Cy + PSCA-CAR group compared to the non-targeting CAR 383 

T cell control, supporting the importance of antigen-specificity. Survival analysis demonstrated 384 

that the combination strategy significantly improved overall survival compared to RLT (P = 0.0015) 385 

or PSCA-CAR T cell monotherapy (P = 0.0492). Although the overall body weight change did not 386 

exceed the 20% threshold, we observed treatment-associated weight loss in groups receiving Ac-387 

225 RLT in combination with Cy, independent of CAR T cell treatment ( Figure 7g ). Collectively, 388 

these findings demonstrate that alpha-emitter RLT enhances anti-tumor activity in combination 389 

with CAR T cells, while further optimization of Cy dosing may be warranted to improve tolerability 390 

in combination regimens for future clinical development. 391

DISCUSSION 393 

In this study, we evaluated the efficacy and safety of combining RLT with CAR T cell therapy 394 

across multiple prostate cancer models. This approach was designed to address two major 395 

barriers limiting current treatments in prostate cancer: tumor antigen heterogeneity and the 396 

immunosuppressive TME. In metastatic human xenograft models, the combination demonstrated 397 

anti-tumor activity and tolerability in clinically relevant immunodeficient settings that partially 398 

recapitulate aspects of mCRPC. These findings were validated in immunocompetent mouse 399 

models, where antigen-heterogeneous tumors were more effectively controlled by the 400 

combination of RLT, Cy, and CAR T cells. Mechanistically, Lu-177 RLT, particularly in 401 

combination with Cy, reprogrammed the tumor myeloid compartment toward a proinflammatory 402 

state characterized by enhanced antigen presentation programs and chemokine-mediated 403 

recruitment pathways. These changes were associated with activation of endogenous T cells and 404 

enhanced CAR T cell activation, effector gene expression, and cytokine signaling. Together, 405 

these findings identify myeloid cell-T cell crosstalk as potential mechanism by which RLT primes 406 

the TME to support adoptive cell therapy and sustain anti-tumor responses. Importantly, this 407 

strategy integrates clinically relevant components, including PSMA-targeted Lu-177 RLT, an FDA 408 

approved therapy for mCRPC, and PSCA-targeted CAR T cells with demonstrated safety and 409 

bioactivity in phase 1 clinical trials. These features support the clinical translatability of this 410 

combination approach. In addition, we evaluated the alpha-emitter Ac-225 RLT as an alternative 411 

radioisotope and observed enhanced anti-tumor activity at lower dose in combination with CAR T 412 

cells. As clinical experience expands with Ac-225-based, or other radioisotope-based RLT, these 413 

insights may inform the development of next-generation combination strategies with 414 

radiopharmaceuticals and CAR T cell therapies. 415 

Herein, we employed both human xenograft and mouse syngeneic preclinical models to capture 416 

different aspects of prostate cancer biology. However, these models only partially reflect the 417 

complexity of antigen expression observed in human disease. Analyses of patient-derived 418 

xenografts and public datasets indicate that PSMA and PSCA expressions are highly 419 

heterogeneous, with tumors often exhibiting variable or discordant expression rather than the 420 

uniformly high co-expression seen in engineered PC3 models or the non-overlapping expression 421 

in the RM9 mixture models. Moreover, PSMA and PSCA are predominantly associated with 422 

adenocarcinoma, and their expression is frequently downregulated in neuroendocrine prostate 423 

cancer (NEPC, more commonly treatment emergent), which accounts for up to 25-30% of 424 

mCRPC ( 24-26 ). Although we observed co-expression of these antigens in tumors with mixed 425 

adenocarcinoma/neuroendocrine features, a substantial fraction of tumor cells may lack both 426

Lessons from combining RLT or external beam radiation therapy (EBRT) with immunotherapy 432

Radiation administered after CAR T cell infusion has been associated with inferior outcomes 434 

compared to its use as a priming strategy ( 27 ). Non-ablative doses of ionizing radiation induce 435 

immunogenic cell death, enhance antigen presentation, and promote proinflammatory response 436 

(28-30 ). This evidence supports the design in which RLT precedes adoptive cell transfer. Although 437 

radiation within the tumor may partially impair CAR T cell viability ( 31, 32 ), a subablative dosing 438 

of radiation allows surviving CAR T cells to expand within a primed, cytokine-rich 439 

microenvironment, thereby promoting anti-tumor responses. 440 

The choice of radioisotope also influences immune priming, with alpha-emitters such as Ac-255 441 

potentially inducing more cytotoxic and immunomodulatory effects than beta-emitters ( 33 ). 442 

Regarding the timing, CAR T cells require several days to traffic to tumor sites, typically reaching 443 

peak accumulation within 1–2 weeks post-infusion. In this study, we administered RLT 4-days 444 

prior to CAR T cell infusion, based on our observations that PSCA-CAR T cells require 445 

approximately 3-4 days to reach the tumor site ( 21 ), coinciding with the partial decay of Lu-177 446 

RLT (physical half-life of approximately 6.65 to 6.73 days). Extending the interval from 4 to 6 days 447 

did not significantly alter therapeutic efficacy in human xenograft models, although longer intervals 448 

could not be evaluated in the mouse syngeneic models due to rapid tumor growth kinetics. In 449 

addition to timing, tumor vasculature may further influence the outcomes, as reduced 450 

angiogenesis can limit CAR T cell trafficking despite improving tumor control. Accordingly, 451 

functional approaches, such as longitudinal tracking of CAR T cell expansion and localization 452 

using bioluminescence imaging or CD8-targeted immuno-PET, may provide a more direct means 453 

to optimize treatment scheduling ( 34 ). 454

MATERIALS AND METHODS 472 

Study design 473 

This study was designed to evaluate the therapeutic efficacy and safety of combining RLT and 474 

CAR T cells in preclinical models of prostate cancer. Experiments were performed using both 475 

xenograft and immunocompetent syngeneic mouse models with homogenous or heterogeneous 476 

antigen expression. All in vivo studies were performed using 5-8 week old NSG or hPSCA-KI 477 

C57BL/6 mice, using 3-10 mice included within each group for all therapy and survival studies, 478 

and at least three mice per group for ex vivo assessment to ensure statistical power. In xenograft 479 

studies, NSG mice bearing metastatic tumors were treated with Lu-177 or Ac-225 RLT, followed 480 

by PSCA-CAR T cell infusion. In syngeneic studies, hPSCA-KI C57BL/6 mice bearing 481 

subcutaneous RM9-derived tumors received Lu-177 or Ac-225 RLT, followed by Cy 482 

lymphodepletion and CAR T cells. Before RLT and CAR T cell treatment, mice were randomized 483 

based on bioluminescence imaging or tumor volume to ensure an evenly distributed average 484 

tumor burden across each group. Treatment groups were compared to appropriate controls, 485 

including untreated mice, CAR T cells alone, or RLT/Cy with non-targeting CAR T cells in 486 

combination. Animal survival was based on the endpoints (tumor volume >1000mm 3 or ulceration, 487 

or signs of distress including weight loss and impaired mobility). Mechanistic investigation 488 

included immune profiling by flow cytometry and scRNA-seq, as well as safety assessments 489 

including body weight, hematologic parameters, serum chemistry, and histopathology. 490 

Investigators were not blinded to group allocation. 491

In vitro cell binding and ex vivo biodistribution 569 

For in vitro binding assay, cells (RM9-WT, RM9-PSMA-ffLu, RM9-PSCA-ffLuc, and RM9-PSMA-570 

PSCA-ffLuc) were seeded 2-3 days prior to the assay. Cells were detached using 0.25% trypsin, 571 

washed once with 1X PBS, and resuspended in HBSS at the desired concentration (1 x 10 7 /mL 572 

for RM9-PSMA-PSCA-ffLuc, or 1.4 x 10 7 /mL for the other cell lines). Cells were prepared in serial 573 

1:2 dilutions in triplicate and incubated with Lu-177 RLT (~6.5 x 10 5 cpm) at 37°C for 2 hours. 574 

Following incubation, supernatants were collected, and both the cell pellet and supernatant were 575 

measured using a γ-counter (Hidex AMG). The percentage of binding was calculated using decay-576 

corrected count: (pellet – blank) / (pellet + supernatant) * 100%. 577 

For the ex vivo biodistribution study, RM9-PSMA-ffLuc-bearing male C57BL/6 mice were 578 

administered with a non-therapeutic dose of Lu-177 RLT (0.37 MBq) via retro-orbital intravenous 579 

(i.v.) injection. At 0.1, 0.5, 2, 4, 6, and 168 hours post-injection, animals (n = 3 per time point) 580 

were euthanized, and tumors and organs (spleen, kidneys, gastrointestinal tract, liver, and 581 

muscle) were collected and weighed. Samples and the standard injected Lu-177 RLT were 582 

allowed to decay for 48 hours prior to γ-counting, and radioactivity was measured using a γ-583 

counter. Tissue uptake was decay-corrected and expressed as percentage of injected dose per 584 

organ (% ID/organ). Time integrated coefficients (TIAC) for tumor and organs were estimated in 585 

MATLAB (Natick) as the area under the curve of the fraction of injected activity (non-decay 586 

corrected) vs. time curve using trapezoidal, mono- and bi-exponential fits. These calculated TIACs 587 

were then imported to the sphere model of OLINDA/EXM dosimetry package, and the output of 588 

absorbed dose vs. mass data was used to create a subset of data for masses near the appropriate 589 

organ mass. A biexponential function was then fit to the dataset to estimate the organ absorbed 590 

doses in mGy/MBq. 591
