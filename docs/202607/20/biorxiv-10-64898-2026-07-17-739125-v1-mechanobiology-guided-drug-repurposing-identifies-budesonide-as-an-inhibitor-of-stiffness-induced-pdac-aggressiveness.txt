ABSTRACT 1

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) develops within a desmoplastic and stiffened 2

microenvironment that critically shapes tumor progression and therapeutic resistance,  yet these  features 3

are  not  reproduced  by conventional rigid plastic culture systems. Here, we leverage a  tuneable 4

bioengineered platform that mimics stromal stiffening to investigate how mechanical cues regulate 5

PDAC cell  behaviour  and to identify pharmacological strategies that counteract stiffness -driven 6

malignancy. We show that increasing matrix stiffness promotes key hallmarks of PDAC aggressiveness, 7

including enhanced cell spreading, focal adhesions maturation, and cytoskeletal tension.  Notably,  we 8

identify the glucocorticoid budesonide as a selective suppressor of stiffness -induced malignant 9

phenotypes. Transcriptomic profiling reveals that budesonide counteracts stiffness -associated gene 10 

programs, prominently affecting pathways governing cytoskeletal dynamics, nuclear envelope 11 

organization, and YAP nucleocytoplasmic transport. Consistently, budesonide reduced force 12 

transmission to the nucleus, restoring nuclear wrinkling and constraining nuclear size and shape.  These 13 

effects are mediated through both glucocorticoid receptor -dependent and  -independent mechanisms, 14 

revealing  a previously unrecognized mode of action. Together, our findings establish mechanical context 15 

as a critical determinant of PDAC vulnerability and identify budesonide as a candidate for therapeutic 16 

repurposing to target stiffness –driven cancer progression. 17

INTRODUCTION 1

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is a deadly disease that is predicted to become the second 2

leading cause of cancer –related death by 2030 1. The dismal prognosis of pancreatic cancer is primarily 3

caused by the fact that 90% of  tumours  are diagnosed at an advanced stage, with systemic metastases in 4

over 50% of cases, compounded by the absence of effective screening methods 2. The hallmark of PDAC 5

is the profoundly altered  tumour  mechanical microenvironment (TMME) 3, characterized by extensive 6

collagen deposition and extracellular matrix (ECM) remodeling 4. The desmoplastic ECM, enriched in 7

fibrillar collagen and glycosaminoglycans 5, evolves into a highly organized and stiff structure 5–7,8

fostering physical and biochemical cell –cell and cell –matrix interactions that drive  tumour 9

progression 5,7,8  and therapy resistance 6,9 –12 . Cells sense alterations in the mechanical properties of the 10 

TMME and undergo coordinated structural changes 13  from focal adhesions that mediate direct 11 

interactions with the ECM, to the cytoskeleton and its force –generating machinery, up to the nucleus, 12 

where mechanical inputs can regulate transcriptional activity 14 –16 . As a result, these mechanical forces 13 

are transduced into intracellular  signalling  programs via mechano –transduction pathways, among which 14 

the YAP/TAZ complex acts as a key transcriptional effector driving proliferation, survival and therapy 15 

resistance 17 –21 .16 

Building on these insights, increasing efforts have been focused on the development of therapeutic 17 

approaches aimed at counteracting stiffness –induced aggressive traits in cancer cells. However, 18 

molecules able to avoid the influence of the TMME on cancer cells are so far not available 22 , in part due 19 

to the lack of model systems that accurately mimic the TMME.  In this context, the development of 20 

bioengineered platforms that  better  mimic the impact of  the  stiff  TMME on cancer cells is becoming an 21 

increasing field of interest 23,24 .22 

Our work presents an experimental approach to model PDAC aggressive features through 23 

mechanobiology principles 18,20 , using bioengineered platforms that recreate TMME conditions with 24 

controlled stiffness. Beyond providing a more physiologically relevant model, this platform also serves 25 

as a predictive tool for therapeutic testing.  Among candidate compounds , the  FDA –approved 26 

glucocorticoid budesonide,  widely  used  in the treatment of  asthma 25  and inflammatory diseases 26 , has 27 

recently emerged as a context –dependent regulator  of  PDAC cell behavior 27 , suggesting  a previously 28 

unidentified antitumor activity beyond its  well –established role as an anti –inflammatory 29 

glucocorticoid 28 .30 

Here, we find that budesonide antagonizes the acquisition of the stiffness –driven malignant traits in 31 

PDAC by  remodelling  cytoskeletal organization and modulating nuclear architecture,  signalling 32

RESULTS 1

Stiffer substrates promote aggressive  behaviour  in pancreatic cancer cells 2

To interrogate the mechanosensory capacity of pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) cells, we 3

engineered polyacrylamide (PAAm) hydrogels spanning a physiologically relevant stiffness range 4

(Young’s moduli: 0.2, 10, and 27 kPa; Supplementary Fig. 1A). These substrates recapitulate distinct 5

mechanical states of the pancreatic microenvironment, with soft matrices (0.2 kPa) reflecting 6

physiological or sub –physiological tissue compliance, and intermediate (10 kPa) to stiff (27 kPa) 7

matrices  modelling  progressive stromal  remodelling  associated with disease progression 29 . While prior 8

studies have examined selected pancreatic cell responses to matrix stiffness, a comprehensive view of 9

the coordinated biological, mechanical, and molecular adaptations elicited across defined mechanical 10 

contexts is still lacking. To address this gap, we performed an integrated characterization of PDAC cell 11 

responses to  tuneable  substrate stiffness. 12 

PANC -1 cells were first seeded on gelatin –coated substrates (1.5 × 10⁴ cells/cm²) and, after 72 h, 13 

detached and replated at low density (2.5 × 10³ cells /cm²) onto PAAm hydrogels of defined stiffness. 14 

Cells were  analysed  48 h post –seeding (Supplementary Fig. 1B). To determine how substrate mechanics 15 

regulate s cell –matrix interactions, we quantified focal adhesion organization and cell spreading by 16 

immunofluorescence imaging of paxillin and vimentin/F –actin, respectively.  PANC -1 cells cultured on 17 

soft substrates (0.2 kPa) displayed sparse and poorly developed focal adhesions, indicative of weak cell –18 

matrix coupling  (Fig. 1A, B).  In contrast, increasing substrate stiffness promoted the formation of 19 

elongated, well –defined adhesion complexes, accompanied by a progressive increase in focal adhesion 20 

area (Fig. 1A, B), consistent with strengthened adhesive engagement.  Accordingly , cell spreading 21 

progressively increased with substrate stiffness, as reflected by a marked increase in projected cell area 22 

(Supplementary Fig. 1C).  This was accompanied by the emergence of prominent actin stress  fibres  and 23 

a reinforced vimentin network, supporting enhanced cytoskeletal tension and mechanotransductive 24 

coupling with the  ECM  (Supplementary Fig. 1C). The observed cytoskeletal  remodelling  was paralleled 25 

by changes in cellular mechanics, as quantified by atomic force microscopy (AFM) –based force 26 

spectroscopy (Fig. 1C). PANC -1 cells progressively increased their stiffness in response to substrate 27 

rigidity (median: 0.31 kPa at 0.2 kPa; 0.62 kPa at 10 kPa; 1.01 kPa at 27 kPa), indicating adaptive 28 

mechanical tuning to the extracellular environment (Fig. 1C). 29 

Stiffness –dependent reinforcement of focal adhesions and cytoskeletal organization are key determinants 30 

of traction force generation and cell motility 30,31 . Thus, we next investigated how substrate mechanics 31 

regulate s PANC -1 cell migration. Time –lapse microscopy combined with single –cell tracking over 48 h 32

Collectively, these findings advance current understanding by providing a comprehensive, multi –scale 22 

characterization of pancreatic cancer cell mechanoadaptation , highlighting how matrix stiffness 23

Budesonide prevents the stiffness –driven transcriptional changes and impacts nucleoskeletal 13 

organization of pancreatic cancer cells 14 

To gain further mechanistic insight into the effects of budesonide on stiffness –driven PDAC phenotypes, 15 

we performed RNA –seq profiling of  PANC -1 cells  cultured on soft and stiff substrates, as well as 16 

budesonide –treated cells  maintained  on stiff substrates (Fig. 4A). Principal component analysis revealed 17 

clear segregation of the three conditions, indicating distinct transcriptional states ( Fig. 4B ).  Comparison 18 

of  PANC -1 cells on stiff versus soft substrates identified 2073 differentially expressed genes 19 

(Supplementary Fig. 5A ), reflecting transcriptional adaptation to increased matrix stiffness.  David g ene 20 

ontology and pathway enrichment  (padj ≤0.05) analyses  of deregulated genes highlighted processes 21 

linked to regulation of  metabolic process,  cytoskeletal organization,  epithelial to mesenchymal transition, 22 

regulation of  cell adhesion  mediated by integrin,  migration, and intracellular  signalling , as well as 23 

enrichment of Hippo  signalling  and cell cycle pathways (Fig. 4C, D ; Supplementary Fig. 5 B,  C;24 

Supplementary Table 1),  consistent with the phenotypic changes observed. 25 

We next compared the transcriptomic profiles of PANC -1 cells cultured on stiff substrates following 26 

budesonide treatment or vehicle  control and  identified 4271 differentially expressed genes 27 

(Supplementary Fig. 5A). Of note, cross –comparison with stiffness –regulated genes (2073 ;28 

Supplementary Fig. 5A ) revealed that approximately 50% of the stiffness –deregulated genes were 29 

inversely modulated by budesonide (Fig. 4E, F ; Supplementary Table 2),  supporting  its ability to 30 

counteract stiffness –driven programs associated with malignant phenotypes. 31

5I, M).  To further dissect these changes , we quantified nuclear height and the major and minor axes of 22

DISCUSSION 1

Matrix stiffening is a defining feature of PDAC and a key driver of malignant cell  behaviour  through 2

activation of mechanotransductive pathways. Here, using  tuneable  bioengineered platforms that 3

recapitulate physiologically relevant stiffness ranges, we identify the FDA –approved glucocorticoid 4

budesonide as a previously unrecognized modulator of PDAC mechanobiology. Budesonide effectively 5

counteracts stiffness –driven phenotypes, including enhanced cell spreading, focal adhesion maturation, 6

cytoskeletal organization, migration and proliferation 7,45 –48 . These findings demonstrate that 7

pharmacological targeting of mechanoadaptive programs can be achieved using an already clinically 8

available compound and highlight the importance of incorporating mechanical cues into preclinical 9

models. 10 

While individual aspects of stiffness –induced responses, such as YAP activation 17 –21 , cytoskeletal 11 

remodelling 6,7,13,14,47  and increased proliferation 45,49 , have been described in various  tumour 12 

contexts 18,20,48,50 , a comprehensive and integrated characterization of these processes in PDAC has 13 

remained limited. By decoupling matrix stiffness from other microenvironmental variables, our  tuneable 14 

platforms enabled a multi –scale analysis linking extracellular mechanics to cytoskeletal organization, 15 

nuclear architecture and transcriptional regulation. Within this framework, matrix stiffening induces a 16 

coordinated phenotypic program consistent with the establishment of a self –reinforcing 17 

mechanotransductive  circuitry 8,16,32,46,51 –53 .18 

A central insight of this study is that budesonide disrupts stiffness –induced mechanotransductive 19 

programs. Our data indicate that this occurs early at the level of cytoskeletal organization, impairing 20 

force transmission toward the nucleus and selectively limiting downstream mechanoresponsive 21 

signalling , including YAP activation. These effects are consistently observed in both established and 22 

patient –derived PDAC models, underscoring their robustness and translational relevance. 23 

Recent studies have highlighted the context –dependent activity of budesonide in PDAC, with 24 

pronounced antiproliferative effects in  3D  systems but limited activity in conventional  2D  cultures 27 . Our 25 

findings further suggest that this apparent context dependency may largely reflect differences in the 26 

mechanical microenvironment rather than intrinsic drug efficacy, emphasizing the importance of 27 

incorporating physiologically relevant stiffness cues into preclinical models.  More broadly, our results 28 

support the concept that matrix mechanics is a critical determinant of drug response in PDAC 3,4,9,12,24 .29 

At the nuclear level, our data  provide the first evidence, to our knowledge,  that budesonide promotes a 30 

‘soft –like’ nuclear state despite persistent mechanical cues, pointing to the nucleus as a key target of its 31 

activity. By weakening cytoskeletal –nucleoskeletal coupling, budesonide limits force transmission and 32

abundance,  induced a higher wrinkling of the nuclear envelope , and constrained nuclear enlargement and 2

role of the nucleus as a mechanosensitive organelle that integrates extracellular forces into transcriptional 6

outputs 32,41 –44,51,54 , our findings suggest that budesonide interferes with a mechanotransduction axis 7

linking matrix stiffness to the nucleus and eventually on malignant gene expression programs. 8

Transcriptomic profiling strongly supports this model. Budesonide reversed a broad set of stiffness –9

induced transcriptional changes associated with proliferation, migration, cytoskeletal  remodelling , and 10 

aggressive cell states. Thus, the phenotypic effects observed at the cellular and nuclear levels converge 11 

with genome –wide transcriptional rewiring, indicating that budesonide does not simply block isolated 12 

downstream pathways but resets a broader mechanoadaptive state. This system –level reprogramming 13 

may be particularly relevant in PDAC, where stromal stiffening and desmoplasia continuously reinforce 14 

tumor cell plasticity and therapeutic resistance 6,7,9 –13 .15 

Our findings further reveal a complex involvement of GR  signalling . Glucocorticoid  signalling 16 

encompasses both canonical GR –dependent transcriptional effects and non –genomic activities 55 , and our 17 

data further uncover a previously unappreciated role for GR  signalling  in regulating PDAC cell 18 

mechanics, while also supporting the existence of GR –independent mechanisms underlying budesonide 19 

activity. Our results therefore suggest that budesonide engages these pathways in the context of PDAC 20 

mechanobiology 28 . Notably, budesonide, similarly to cholesterol, is known to modulate the structural 21 

properties of lipid bilayers, including lipid order and packing 56 . By altering plasma membrane fluidity, 22 

budesonide may affect membrane organization and receptor clustering, ultimately impairing cell –matrix 23 

coupling and mechanosensing 57,58 . While this possibility warrants further investigation, GR  silencing 24 

experiments uncovered an unappreciated role for GR  signalling  in regulating PDAC cell  behaviour , in 25 

particular in cell motility . These results are also supported by the recent evidence that GR regulates 26 

ovarian cancer  cell motility 59 . Moreover,  our results  suggest that the effects of budesonide on nuclear 27 

organization are largely independent of GR .28 

Collectively, our data identify matrix stiffness as a dominant regulator of PDAC cell phenotype and 29 

establish budesonide as a pharmacological agent capable of functionally uncoupling  tumour  cells from 30 

pathological mechanical cues. Rather than directly targeting oncogenic drivers, budesonide perturbs the 31 

biomechanical circuitry that sustains malignant phenotypes, revealing mechanotransduction as a 32

Several limitations should be acknowledged. Although our platforms capture  tuneable  stiffness, they do 4

(significance threshold p < 0.05). Normalization was achieved by dividing counts by sample –specific 19 

size factors, calculated as the median ratio of each gene’s read count relative to its geometric mean. Gene 20 

Ontology (GO) enrichment was performed with the DAVID tool (https://david.ncifcrf.gov/# ). 21 

Furthermore, pathway analysis was carried out in R for KEGG, Biological Process (BP), and Reactome 22 

pathways. KEGG enrichment was conducted using the clusterProfiler package in R, ranking genes by 23 

differential expression values. For Reactome analysis, the reactome.db  package was applied to map 24 

differentially expressed genes to relevant pathways. Only genes with an adjusted p -value ≤ 0.05 were 25 

considered for pathway enrichment. Results from differential expression and pathway analyses (KEGG, 26 

BP, and Reactome) were integrated to obtain a broader understanding of the biological processes 27 

involved. 28
