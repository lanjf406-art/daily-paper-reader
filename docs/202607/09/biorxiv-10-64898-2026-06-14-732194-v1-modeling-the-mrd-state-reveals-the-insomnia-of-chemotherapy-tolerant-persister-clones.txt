Abstract 22 

Regardless of the success of clinical surgery, disseminated tumor cells (DTCs) can persist in distant 23 

organs, with a fraction surviving chemotherapy, which can result in minimal residual disease (MRD), 24 

a relevant reservoir for metastatic relapse. Yet, the cellular states that enable the survival and outgrowth 25 

of MRD remain poorly defined. Here, using a patient-derived tongue cancer organoid (TCO) model, 26 

we recapitulated the key features of chemotherapy-tolerant DTCs by culturing TCOs under growth-27 

factor deprivation and chemotherapeutic stress conditions that mimic the metastatic tissue environment. 28 

Clonal-level analyses revealed a distinct subset of cells that retained proliferative capacity without 29 

entering a therapy-induced cytostatic state (hereafter referred to as cycling persisters, CPs). CPs 30 

exhibited coordinated activation of the IFN signaling pathway, Xenobiotic metabolism, and 31 

inflammatory signaling pathways, defining a transcriptional and metabolic program that enables 32 

sustained proliferation of CPs under the poor conditions. Consistently, a cell population with similar 33 

features was identified in metastatic tissues from patients. Longitudinal clonal tracking demonstrated 34 

that early metastatic recurrence is more likely driven by CPs, suggesting a novel mechanism that differs 35 

from the prevailing view that relapse arises from reactivation of dormant non-CPs. Our findings 36 

highlight a critical therapeutic oversight in relapse prevention. 37

Introduction 40 

Despite significant advances in multimodal cancer therapies, recurrence remains a major clinical 41 

challenge. Even after seemingly successful treatments, distant relapses can occur in a significant 42 

number of patients. This is primarily due to disseminated tumor cells (DTCs), which evade 43 

conventional detection methods 1-3 . It is widely considered that a fraction of DTCs survive 44 

chemotherapy by entering a dormant, growth-restricted state 4-9 , and metastatic relapse has been 45 

attributed to the reawakening of those cells 10-12 . However, direct evidence for such abrupt reactivation 46 

in patients remains limited, and it is unclear whether dormant DTCs represent a uniform population 47 

with equal relapse-initiating capacities. Rather, DTC populations are likely heterogeneous 9,13,14 ,48 

although the specific cellular states that ultimately give rise to clinically manifest metastases remain 49 

poorly understood. In this context, DNA damage and mutational processes can occur in non-dividing 50 

cells, while the emergence of relapse-initiating clones generally requires subsequent proliferative 51 

expansion. How such clones arise from largely growth-restricted populations of DTCs remains an open 52 

question, raising fundamental issues about the cellular states and evolutionary dynamics that underlie 53 

metastatic recurrence. Thus, defining the cellular state that confers a relapse-initiating capacity on a 54 

subset of DTCs is critical for understanding the biological basis of metastatic recurrence and for 55 

developing effective prevention strategies. 56 

Using patient-derived tumor organoids (PDTOs), previous studies, including studies from our 57 

group, demonstrated that certain PDTO lines exhibit robust drug tolerance not through stable resistant 58 

clones but through transient, mutation-independent persister states 15-17 . Here, we established an in vitro 59 

PDTO platform with AI-based clonal tracking to examine how tumor cells survive and maintain 60 

proliferation under minimal-niche conditions after chemotherapy that mimic those in metastatic sites. 61 

Our findings show that cell fate is determined early on. Some cells maintain proliferation as cycling 62 

persisters (CPs), while others enter growth arrest as non-CPs or die in a stochastic manner. This study 63 

proposes an “insomnia” model, distinct from the conventional “long-term dormancy and awakening ”64 

model, thereby defining a novel chemotherapy-induced DTC phenotype that contributes to metastatic 65 

relapse. 66

A small subset of DTC clones retains proliferative capacity after chemotherapy 70

In vitro modeling of DTCs using patient-derived organoids 92 

Adult epithelial stem cells are maintained within highly specialized, spatially restricted niches that 93 

provide growth cues, such as Wnt ligands, epidermal growth factor (EGF) family members, Notch 94 

ligands, and bone morphogenetic protein (BMP) modulators, in a concentrated, localized manner 18,19 .95 

Those signals are supplied by defined cellular sources, including specialized epithelial cells and niche-96 

associated stromal components, in a tissue-dependent manner, and are confined to precise 97 

microanatomical locations rather than being uniformly distributed throughout the tissue. Consistent 98 

with this principle, the normal tongue epithelium proliferated and formed organoids (hereafter referred 99 

to as TNOs) in culture medium supplemented with niche factors (NFs), including Noggin, EGF, and 100 

R-spondin 1 (hereafter referred to as NF-sufficient medium). Further addition of several factors to the 101 

medium, including FGF2, forskolin, Prostaglandin E2, A83-01, nicotinamide, and CHIR99021 102 

(hereafter referred to as TNO-optimal medium), improved the growth of TNOs. In contrast, when NFs 103 

were removed (niche-deprived medium), organoids failed to form and did not recover even after 104 

exposure to the TNO-optimal medium several days later (Supplementary Fig. 1A–D). These findings 105 

were also replicated in normal esophageal squamous epithelial cells (ENOs; Supplementary Fig. 1E, 106 

F). 107 

On the other hand, DTCs that extravasate into secondary organs are unlikely to encounter a 108 

pre-formed niche. Instead, they lodge in regions with poor niche conditions. Consistent with this, 109 

clinical observations and experimental models show that DTCs commonly enter a dormant, non-110 

proliferative, low-metabolic state 6,7,9,20,21 .111 

Supporting this concept, despite their malignant nature, cancer cells remain dependent on 112 

defined niche-derived signals for optimal survival and proliferation. This dependence is exemplified 113 

by the requirement for exogenous NFs, such as Wnt ligands, EGF, and other epithelial stem cell-114

Notably, while most TCO lines exhibited reduced growth under NF-deprived conditions, some 122 

cells appeared to survive. Those cells resumed proliferation when re-exposed to an NF-sufficient 123 

medium (Fig. 2C, D). Thus, the capacity of a surviving fraction of cells to withstand NF deprivation 124 

without permanently losing their proliferative potential distinguishes tongue cancer cells from their 125 

normal epithelial counterparts (Supplementary Fig. 1A–D). Furthermore, TCO organoid lines 126 

established from metastatic lymph nodes (hereafter referred to as TLOs) frequently exhibited 127 

comparable survival under both NF-deprived and NF-sufficient conditions (Fig. 2B). Similar results 128 

were observed when comparing paired TCO and TLO lines derived from the same patient (Fig. 2E, F), 129 

demonstrating a substantially reduced dependence of TLOs on exogenous NFs. Consistently, NF-130 

independence increased over the long term in some TCO lines cultured under NF-deprived conditions 131 

(Fig. 2G, H). Considering that metastatic lesions are sustained by cancer cell clones derived from 132 

primary tumors that have acquired the ability to adapt to microenvironments with poor niches, such as 133 

those encountered in lymph nodes, it is plausible that cancer cell clones that survive prolonged culture 134 

of primary TCOs under NF-deprived conditions exhibit key functional features of DTCs. 135 

Contrary to the prevailing belief that DTCs remain dormant 4-9 , analysis of post-treatment 136 

metastatic specimens revealed that a subset of tumor cells retains proliferative capacity even after 137 

chemotherapy (Fig. 1D, E). We therefore modeled this clinically relevant state in vitro by culturing 138 

tumor cells under NF–deprived and chemotherapeutic conditions that mimic at least in part the niche-139 

poor, treatment-exposed metastatic microenvironment. First, we examined the impact of NF-deprived 140 

conditions on the response to cisplatin (CDDP), a standard chemotherapeutic agent for oral cancer. 141 

IC50 values were calculated from the dose-response curves for each TCO after 10 days of culture in 142 

NF-sufficient medium or in NF-deprived medium, and CDDP was administered from day 4 to day 7. 143 

Notably, all TCOs showed a marked increase in IC50 when cultured in NF-deprived medium compared 144 

to NF-sufficient medium (Supplementary Fig. 2A), which suggests that DTCs residing in an NF-145 

deprived environment display enhanced resistance to chemotherapy. A similar increase in 146 

chemotherapeutic resistance under these culture conditions was also observed in an esophageal 147 

squamous cell carcinoma (ESCC) model (Supplementary Fig. 2B). 148

assays based on bulk ATP measurements, such as CellTiter-Glo, have a couple of limitations. Since the 150 

assay requires reagent-induced cell lysis, continuous long-term tracking of the same well is not feasible. 151 

In addition, these assays provide only bulk measurements of cell viability and therefore do not permit 152

responses 187 

Although a fraction of cells at metastatic sites retain the ability to proliferate even after systemic 188 

chemotherapy (Fig. 1D, E), snapshot analyses of pathological specimens cannot determine the 189 

subsequent fate of these cells. To directly interrogate the long-term fate of CPs versus non-CPs 190

following exposure to chemotherapeutic drugs, we developed a novel in vitro clonal-tracking system 191 

that enables the precise monitoring of individual tumor clones. This system allows us to study their 192 

distinct proliferative behaviors and contributions to relapse. In this system, after AI-assisted 193 

segmentation (Supplementary Fig. 3A-D), individual clonal aggregates were labeled, and their 194 

centroids were computationally registered across serial images. This alignment procedure reliably 195 

tracked the same clones throughout the entire observation period (see Methods). Representative 196 

outputs are shown in Fig. 3A, demonstrating accurate and stable clonal identification over time. 197 

Using this system, we monitored thousands of individual clones immediately after exposure to 198 

chemotherapeutic agents across multiple TCO lines, including TCO3, TCO8, TCO12, TCO18, TCO21, 199 

TCO32, and TCO42. Changes in two-dimensional clonal areas were quantified based on microscopic 200 

images from day 4 (treatment initiation) to day 13 (Fig. 3B, Supplementary Fig. 5A,B). Clones whose 201 

area increased by more than twofold from day 7 to day 13 were defined as CPs and are highlighted in 202 

red. In contrast, clones exhibiting less than a twofold increase were classified as non-CPs or dead 203 

(gray). The frequency of CP formation varied markedly between TCO lines: TCO8, TCO21, TCO32, 204 

and TCO42 generated relatively abundant CPs (hereafter referred to as CP-rich TCOs), whereas TCO 205 

lines TCO3, TCO12, and TCO18 hardly produced CPs (hereafter referred to as CP-poor TCOs) (Fig. 206 

3C). 207 

Next, we tracked the long-term fate of CPs in CP-rich TCO lines from day 13 to day 31. Notably, 208 

clonal behaviors became increasingly polarized over time. By day 31, the surviving clones were 209 

primarily composed of cells that had either expanded minimally or grown more than tenfold compared 210 

to their size on day 7. These results imply that the proliferative capacity of CPs that emerge after 211 

chemotherapy exposure is heterogeneous. Not all CP clones maintain continuous growth, and only a 212 

subset of them can evade growth arrest, retaining the ability to proliferate and form large aggregates 213 

over time (Fig. 3D, E, Supplementary Fig. 6, 8A, B). In addition, we observed that non-CP clones in 214 

CP-rich TCOs began to proliferate slowly from day 13 onward under NF-deprived conditions, a 215 

phenomenon we defined as “slow-awakening”, characterized by a greater than twofold increase in area 216 

relative to day 7. “Slow-awakening” clones were only rarely observed in CP-rich TCOs (Fig. 3F, G, 217 

Supplementary Fig. 8C, D) and were negligible in CP-poor TCOs (Supplementary Fig. 7A-C). Finally, 218 

we classified clones as "disappearing" or "surviving" at day 31 and quantified the extent of expansion 219 

among surviving clones between day 13 and day 31. Notably, among clones that expanded by more 220 

than tenfold, the vast majority had already increased in size by at least twofold between day 7 and day 221 

13 (Fig. 3H). This trend was consistently observed across other CP-rich TCOs (Supplementary Fig. 6). 222 

Together, our data suggest that CPs arise from pre-existing clones whose molecular state 223 

enables their survival without entering a cytostatic, dormant program. Those clones maintain the 224 

capacity to proliferate under cytotoxic stress, challenging the conventional “long-term dormancy and 225 

awakening” model by demonstrating that persistent proliferation, rather than dormancy, underlies 226 

long-term survival (Fig. 3I). 227

CPs exhibit MRD features of chemotherapy-treated patients 229 

To elucidate the molecular basis of the emergence of chemo-resistant CPs under NF-deprived 230 

conditions, cancer cells derived from CP-rich or CP-poor TCO lines were either exposed to 231 

chemotherapeutic agents under NF-deprived conditions or were maintained under drug-free, NF-232 

sufficient conditions, and interline differences in gene expression profiles were analyzed. 233 

Unsupervised principal component analysis (PCA) revealed that samples clustered primarily by 234 

organoid line rather than separating into distinct groups based on CP-rich versus CP-poor status and 235 

culture conditions, including NF-sufficient versus NF-deprived with chemotherapeutic treatment (Fig. 236 

4A). 237 

Next, we compared the gene expression profiles of cancer cells derived from CP-rich or from 238 

CP-poor lines under NF-deprived conditions in the presence of chemotherapeutic agents. Gene set 239 

enrichment analysis (GSEA) revealed that the most significantly upregulated pathways in CP-rich lines, 240 

compared to CP-poor lines, included the INTERFERON_ALPHA_RESPONSE and 241 

INTERFERON_GAMMA_RESPONSE pathways. In addition, the E2F_TARGETS pathway, which 242 

reflects proliferative activity, was activated, whereas genes associated with the P53_PATHWAY, which 243 

is implicated in growth arrest and cell death following chemotherapy, were suppressed. These findings 244 

indicate that CPs retain their proliferative capacity even under highly stringent culture conditions. 245 

Consistently, the activation of genes related to cell cycle checkpoints (G2M_CHECKPOINT) was also 246 

observed. Moreover, multiple metabolic pathways, including the 247 

REACTIVE_OXYGEN_SPECIES_PATHWAY, XENOBIOTIC_METABOLISM, GLYCOLYSIS, 248 

and ADIPOGENESIS, were upregulated, which suggests that transcriptional metabolic adaptation is a 249 

key feature underlying CP emergence across different lines (Fig. 4B). We also compared the gene 250 

expression profiles of CP-rich and CP-poor lines before and after CDDP exposure. Several pathways, 251 

including the IFN SIGNAL PATHWAYS, XENOBIOTIC_METABOLISM, GLYCOLYSIS, 252 

FATTY_ACID_METABOLISM, and INFLAMMATORY_RESPONSE, remained consistently active 253 

in CP-rich lines regardless of CDDP treatment. In addition, compared to CP-poor lines, the following 254 

pathways were relatively weak before treatment but were more strongly induced after drug exposure 255 

in CP-rich lines: cell proliferation (E2F_TARGET, G2M_CHECKPOINTS, MITOTIC_SPINDLE), 256 

inflammatory signaling (COAGULATION, TNFA_SIGNALING_VIA_NFKB, COMPLEMENT), 257 

and epithelial–mesenchymal transition (EPITHELIAL_MESENCHYMAL_TRANSITION, 258 

TGFB_SIGNALING). The activation of these pathways suggests their involvement in establishing a 259 

cellular state permissive for CP emergence (Fig. 4C). Previous studies have reported that cancer cell 260 

clones surviving chemotherapy adopt a diapause-like metabolically dormant state 27-30 . In contrast, CPs 261 

observed in our model exhibited active transcriptional metabolic adaptation, indicating that they do 262 

not conform to the previously proposed model in which low metabolic activity enables evasion of 263 

chemotherapeutic effects. Furthermore, our findings suggest that pre-existing transcriptional states 264 

facilitate the rapid induction of adaptive responses required for CP emergence. 265

Discussion 308 

In this study, we established culture conditions that recapitulate the metastatic tumor 309 

microenvironment and applied them to a diverse panel of TCO lines. Under these conditions, we 310 

identified a population of CPs that survive chemotherapy while retaining their proliferative capacity. 311 

We further characterized these CPs based on their gene expression profiles. In addition, we identified 312 

CP-associated gene markers and examined their spatial expression patterns in patient-derived 313 

metastatic tissues, demonstrating the presence of a similar cell population in metastases from 314 

chemotherapy-treated patients. These cells are likely to represent a population capable of contributing 315 

to early disease recurrence. Collectively, our model provides a valuable platform for investigating the 316 

molecular basis of cancer recurrence. 317 

Our findings are not readily reconciled with a simple long-term dormancy-and-awakening 318 

model, in which DTCs persist in a deeply quiescent state and subsequently re-enter the cell cycle to 319 

drive metastatic outgrowth 10-12,32 . Instead, our results suggest that the proliferative potential of 320 

individual clones is largely determined prior to chemotherapy exposure, and that treatment-induced 321 

damage dictates whether clones with proliferative potential accelerate their expansion (insomnia) or 322 

clones lacking the potential enter a durable, non-expanding state. Following extended culture (1 323 

month) under NF-deprived conditions combined with chemotherapy exposure, subsequent restoration 324 

of NF-sufficient conditions failed to induce robust re-expansion of previously non-proliferative clones 325 

(Supplementary Fig. 4C, D). These observations underscore the importance of the “insomnia” model 326 

in the post-treatment dynamics of tumor cells and suggest that, at least under these conditions, the 327 

conventional “long-term dormancy and awakening” model is rare. 328 

At the same time, several limitations of our study should be considered. Organoid culture 329 

systems are inherently constrained in duration and do not fully recapitulate the long-term persistence 330 

of DTCs in patients. It therefore remains possible that prolonged survival within native 331 

microenvironments could induce additional cellular adaptations that enable delayed reawakening, as 332 

proposed in the long-term dormancy model. 333 

Despite the success of clinical surgery and chemoradiotherapy, DTCs persist as MRD in tissues 334 

distant from the primary tumor site. The suppression of recurrence could be achieved by keeping MRD 335 

in a sleeping state. Given that this represents a key concept for future cancer control strategies, our 336 

model will serve as a vital tool for elucidating the mechanisms underlying CPs and for developing 337 

control methods based on those mechanisms, thereby helping prevent recurrence. 338

Microscopic imaging and deep-learning 422 

Microscopic images were acquired using Cell3iMager NX (SCREEN). To enable longitudinal 423 

quantification of viable organoid clone areas (defined as areas of cell clusters detected in microscopic 424 

images) without sacrificing the wells, a deep-learning-based image analysis approach was employed. 425 

Organoid clones were first annotated in bright-field images by manual labeling. These annotations 426 

were then used to train a deep learning model to extract organoid clones irrespective of their viability 427 

status, and a trained model was generated for subsequent analysis. Next, based on the model file, PI 428 

staining was used to annotate dead (PI-positive) and viable (PI-negative) cells. The annotated dataset 429 

was then used to train a deep learning model to discriminate between viable and dead cells solely from 430 

bright-field images. In the initial output, the AI classified the clones into four groups based on their 431 

positive/negative status for PI staining and the results of AI initial predictions. Type I: clones labeled 432 

as “alive” with PI fluorescence intensity values < 20, Type II: clones labeled as “dead” with PI 433 

fluorescence intensity values ≥ 10, Type III: clones labeled as “dead” despite PI fluorescence intensity 434 

values < 10, Type IV: clones labeled as “alive” despite PI fluorescence intensity values ≥ 20. Types I 435 

and II were excluded from additional training. In contrast, Type III clones were retrained as “alive” for 436 

additional training. In particular, considering that expanding organoids naturally incorporate internal 437 

dead cells even within viable clusters 34,35 , clones labeled as Type IV were generally categorized as 438 

“alive”, while morphologically obvious dead cells and debris were manually re-annotated. These deep-439 

learning models were run repeatedly until significant correlations were observed between PI-positive 440 

clones predicted as “Dead” (Type II) and all clones predicted as “Dead” (Types II and III) both for area 441 

and for clone count. In addition, we verified that the average accuracy for area and clone count reached 442 

to over 95%. To minimize noise, debris and objects smaller than 100 μm 2 were excluded from the 443 

clone recognition. To detect MRD clones, sequential clone imaging was performed every 3 days from 444 

day 1 to day 31 after seeding. 445
