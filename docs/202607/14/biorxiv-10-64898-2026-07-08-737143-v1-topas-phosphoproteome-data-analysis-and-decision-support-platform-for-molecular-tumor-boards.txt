Abstract 34 

The molecular tumor board (MTB) is central to precision oncology, providing personalized 35 

treatment recommendations based on molecular profiles of patient tumors . Genomics is 36 

instrumental for MTBs but often fail s to identify clinically actionable targets, a gap that 37 

phosphoproteomic s can fill . We present the tumor proteome activity status (TOPAS) platform , an 38 

end -to -end analysis pipeline that converts terabytes of phosphoproteomic data into patient -specific 39 

reports for MTB discussions, focusing on clinically relevant signaling linked to oncogenic 40 

mechanisms and therapeutic targets. Designed to scale with growing cohorts, the platform 41 

integrates data from 1,998 tumor samples to support patient - and cohort -level hypothesis 42 

generation. A web portal handles quality control, calculates TOPAS scores , identifies tumor 43 

antigens and immune checkpoint s, and offers interactive analyses of differential protein abundance 44 

and outlier detection . The TOPAS platform is open source, addresses a critical unmet need and 45 

facilitates broader adoption of phosphoproteomics in precision oncology in the future .46 

Main 47 

Retrospective molecular tumor profiling initiatives such as The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) 148 

and the Clinical Proteomic Tumor Analysis Consortium (CPTAC) 2 have demonstrated conceptual 49 

benefits of analyzing pan -cancer cohorts over single cancer types. Examples include the 50 

identification of mo lecular signatures, cancer (immune) subtypes , or signaling patterns that span 51 

entities or demarcate certain entities 3, 4 . The pan -cancer perspective is particularly relevant for the 52 

analysis of patients suffering from the large variety and molecularly heterogeneous group of rare 53 

or otherwise hard -to -treat cancers. For many patients , standard -of -care systemic therapy options 54 

are either limited or not available at all 5, 6 . Molecular tumor boards (MTBs) aim to make 55 

therapeutic recommendations for such cases based on their indiv idual genomic and , sometimes, 56 

transcriptomic profiles 5, 7 . Despite noteworthy successes, identifying or predicting actionable 57 

oncogenic drivers from nucleotide data remains challenging , and clinical trials conducted so far 58 

only showed moderate patient benefit 5, 6, 8, 9 . Because many cancers are driven by aberrant protein 59 

expression or signaling pathway activity , proteomics and phosphoproteomics (from here 60 

collectively referred to as phosphoproteomics) are important additional data layers . In an 61 

accompanying paper 10 , we demonstrate that adding this component to MTBs is not only 62 

technica lly feasible but also enhances treatment recommendations across many tumor entities. 63 

Here, we present the enabling end -to -end and open -source data analysis platform that turns raw 64 

phosphoproteomic data into patient -specific reports to support decision -maki ng in MTBs and 65 

enables gaining novel insights into the tumor biology of rare cancers such as alveolar 66 

rhabdomyosarcoma (ARMS) in a robust, rapid , and scalable manner. 67

Integrating phosphoproteom e data into pros pective weekly MTB meetings required the 70 

development of a new and bespoke data processing pipeline that followed three major design 71 

principles (Fig. 1a ).72 

First, it needed to place data collected for an individual patient into an ever -growing background 73 

cohort of samples from the same or different tumor entities. This design increases statistical power 74 

and confidence in the biological and clinical interpretation of the profiles over time . The current 75 

data set comprise s ~4 TB of raw mass spectrometry (MS) data ( 17,64 0 MS runs ) collected from 76 

1,998 tumor samples from 1,852 patients . Analyzing all data for peptide and protein identification 77 

and quantification in a single step each week was not feasible with reasonable computing 78 

resources. Instead, each batch of 8 or 9 patients plus two or three common cell line quality control 79 

(QC) samples (multiplexed by s table isotope labeling using tandem mass tags, TMT , Methods )80 

was searched separately. Results from 29 4 such batches were combined using mass spectra 81 

clustering by SIMSI -Transfer to reduce missing data between batches 11 and applying the picked 82 

protein group approach 12 to control the false discovery rate (FDR) of protein identification at 1%. 83 

This led to the quantification of 13, 069 protein -codi ng genes and 148,224 phosphopeptides , of 84 

which 8,192 and 22,414 respectively were detected in ≥50% of all patient samples (Extended 85 

Data Fig. 1a ). Samples with poor TMT labeling (<90%) , phosphopeptide enrichment (<80%), or 86 

a summed TMT intensity of 5-fold below QC samples in the same batch were removed (Fig. 1b ,87 

Extended Data Fig. 1b , Methods ) and n ormalization was applied to reduce batch effects ( Fig. 88 

1b, Methods ). 89 

Second, to aid molecular oncologists with data interpretation, the data volume per patient had to 90 

be reduced to the essentials and analyzed using easily explainable methods rather than potentially 91 

more powerful, but less intuitively accessible ones. For instance, whenever available, proteins and 92 

phospho -sites of interest were annotated using prior knowledge from databases and the scientific 93 

literature. In addition, tumor proteome activity status (TOPAS) scores and kinase substrate 94 

phosphorylation scores were calculated for 18 receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs) and 2895 

intrace llular kinases (ICKs) (Fig. 1c ). The data processing pipeline takes about one day to create 96 

>2,000 annotated patient -specific reports from the raw data using off -the -shelf computing 97 

hardware ( Fig. 1d ). 98 

Third, the data needed to be accessible in a simple f ashion. This was achieved by importing 99 

abundance values, annotations and phosphoproteome scores into an interactive TOPAS Portal 100 

(Fig. 1 e) that molecular oncologists use in tandem with a patient -specific tabular report (< 5 MB, 101 

downloadable from the portal, Extended Data Table 1 ) to obtain an in -depth appreciation of 102 

potentially dysregulated protein expression and pathway signaling in a patient’s tumor. This 103 

information is summarized into a single page and discussed alongside the genomics and 104 

transcriptomics data for the same patient in the weekly MTB. 105

Measuring and managing batch effects 128 

Addressing technical variation is critical when integrating data over the course of months to years. 129 

Five MS instruments were deployed on the project , and we observed performance drifts over time ,130 

leading to batch effects ( Fig. 2a) that were also apparent at the level of tumor entities and 131 

individual proteins ( Extended Data Fig. 2). The conventional approach to T MT batch correction 132 

is to use a common reference sample (here two or three cell line QC samples) contained in every 133 

batch 14 . However, this approach would have resulted in missing >5,000 pro teins detected in 134 

patients, which were insufficiently abundant in the QC samples, an effect exacerbated by the 135 

biological heterogeneity of a pan -cancer cohort. Moreover, batch -correction methods run the risk 136 

of introducing art ifacts which can complicate da ta interpretation 15 . Instead, we addressed batc h137 

effects as follows ( Methods ): To overcome missing data in QC samples, we computed quasi -label -138 

free quantification (LFQ) intensities 2, 16 for any sample (QC or patient) by distributing the 139 

precursor intensity (produced in the MS1 spectrum) across the relative contributions of the TMT 140 

reporter fragment ion intensities (produced in the MS3 spectrum; Extended Data Fig. 3a). This 141 

approach has the additional benefit of allowing comparison of cohorts that do not share a common 142 

reference sample. For protein quantification, we observed that summing all peptide intensities of 143 

a protein produced substantial batch effects ( Extended D ata Fig. 3b). This is because peptides 144 

identifying a particular protein in different samples are not necessarily the same 17 . Using the 145 

MaxLFQ approach 17 on the quasi -LFQ intensities for protein quantification alleviated these issues. 146 

We found that median z -sco red MS intensities of all proteins for a patient were correlated with the 147 

number of proteins identified in a given batch. This effect was minimized by using only proteins 148 

detected in ≥70% of samples for median centering normalization (Extended Data Fig. 3c). 149 

Combining these three steps led to improved clustering of samples from similar entities such as 150 

alveolar rhabdomyosarcoma (ARMS) and embryonal rhabdomyosarcoma (ERMS) ( Fig. 2b, 151 

Extended Data Fig. 2a, b). F it -for -purpose repeatability across batches was assessed for full 152 

process replicates of the same patient sample (n=3, 18 % median CV for proteins, 23 % for 153 

phosphopeptides; Fig. 2c , 7 additional patients in Extended Data Fig. 4) as well as QC sample 154 

replicates (n=5 2, 25 % median CV for proteins and 26 % for phosphopeptides ; Fig. 2c ). Across 155 

patients measured in the same TMT channel but across 49 different batches , median CVs were 156 

much larger ( 65 % for proteins and 92 % for phosphopeptides ), demonstrating that technical and 157 

biological abundance variations co uld be distinguished for the majority of cases. These figures of 158 

merit provide important guidance for assessing differential abundance data between patients or 159

Distilling biologically relevant information 177 

The automated processing steps up to this point reduced the data collected per patient 2,000 -fold 178 

(Fig. 1a ). However , further curation and prioritization of the information is required to derive 179 

interpretable tumor biology. An important challenge is deciding which signals are reliable and 180 

which represent technica l limitations . To address the latter , protein quantifications based on fewer 181 

peptides than expected and qua ntifications below the dynamic range of the MS signal within the 182 

batch were flagged (Methods, Fig. 3a ). The few m issing values for phosphopeptides within a 183 

batch were imputed at the limit of the dynamic range , and p rotein missing values within a batch 184 

were flagged as tentative evidence of absence. In addition , for each protein and phosphopeptide, 185 

quantification robustness was assessed by the ratio of biolo gical over technical CV ( Fig. 3b ,186 

Methods ). This led to their classification as robust (ratio > 2), var iable (1 < ratio < 2), unreliable 187 

(ratio < 1) or uncharacterized (detected in too few technical replicates). 188 

A straightforward way to prioritize aberrant biological signals is to analyze abundances of known 189 

entity biomarkers, tumor antigens, oncogenes, tu mor suppressors, drug targets or phospho -sites, 190 

annotations of which were obtained from OncoKB, KinHub, ADCdb, PSP and the literature 20 -22 191 

(Fig. 3c, Extended Data Table 2 ). To evaluate if a patient show s abnormal abundance of a protein 192 

or phosphopeptide of interest, one might compare the molecular profile to that of nearby healthy 193 

tissue 23 . In a precision oncology setting, healthy control samples are not systematically available ,194 

and the tissue of origin may be unknown , e.g. , for cancers of unknown primary (CUP) or many 195 

rare subtypes. Detecting differences in the phosphoproteomes of proliferating cancer cells and 196 

fully differentiated (non -proliferating) normal cells does not necessaril y imply that they are 197 

oncogenic signals. Therefore , we chose all samples contained in th is study as a background cohort 198 

and computed z-score s for each protein or phosphopeptide for each patient sample relative to the 199 

cohort (Methods ). Z -scores ≥ 2 were typ ically considered for further investigation (Fig. 3 d),200 

though clinically relevant results below this threshold were still reported if , e.g. , tumor cell content 201 

was low. Analyte abundance relative to other patients from the same (or closely related) entity as 202 

well as tissue topology (tumor/metastasis site) w ere also taken into account 10 .203

Tumor proteome activity stat us (TOPAS) scoring 215 

An exciting potential of patient phosphoproteome data is to infer dysregulation of kinase activity 216 

and affected signaling pathways , and s everal tools have been developed for this purpose (KSEA, 217 

INKA, PTM -SEA, KSTAR , ROKAI )24 -28 . These methods strongly depend on reliable kinase -218 

substrate relationship (KSR) annotations but particularly f or intracellular kinases (ICKs), KSR 219

between direct kinase substrates and phosphorylation mediated by downstream kinase activity 29 .221 

To alleviate these issues , we built a series of TOPAS scores as follows ( Fig. 4a , Methods ). For 18 222 

receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs), we developed TOPAS -RTK scores by summing up three 223 

subscores: 1) kinase protein abundance (overexpression can be a mechanism of overactivity 30, 31 ),224 

2) abundance of (important) kinase activity -indica ting phosphorylation ( intersection of sites 225 

curated from the literature , respond ing consistently to drug perturbation 29, 32 , and 226 

hyperphosphorylated in patients with oncogenic mutations , Methods ), and 3) total abundance of 227 

kinase phosphorylation ( Fig. 4b , Extended Data Table 3 ). Adaptor protein phosphorylation s by 228 

RTKs are not considered in the score because they are not systematically known and the same 229 

adaptors are often shared by several RTKs, leading to ambiguity. The 28 developed ICK -TOPAS 230 

scores sole ly relied on the summed abundance of high -quality substrates. These were compiled 231 

from coherent behavior in a large -scale dose -dependent phosphoproteomics screen in response to 232 

kinase inhibitors recently developed by the authors 29 . Depending on the kinase, ICK -TOPAS 233 

scores were based on few or many known substrates ( Fig. 4 c). The robustness of each score varies ,234 

as not all proteins or ph ospho -sites comprising the scores are equally stably detected across 235 

patients ( Fig. 4 d). Analogous to phospho peptide or protein abundance, TOPAS scores are 236 

computed as z -scores across the cohort to simplify interpretation. 237 

To verify the ability of TOPAS scores to detect dysregulated RTK signaling in patients, we 238 

analyzed 94 cases from our own cohort and 79 from the CPTAC cohorts . The se patients carry 239 

known oncogenic genomic alterations with therapeutic implications in nine RTKs ( ALK, EGFR, 240 

ERBB2, FGFR1, KIT, MET, PDGFRA, PDGFRB, RET ) and were defined to represent ground 241 

truth ( Fig. 4 e, Methods, Extended Data Table 4 ). Although far from perfect, TOPAS -RTK 242 

performed better than INKA and both were far superior to several alternatives mentioned above 243 

(Fig. 4 f). T he TOPAS sub -scores were of broadly equal performance making TOPAS robust 244 

against relying on one metric alone (Extended Data Fig. 5a). In addition, results of TOPAS 245 

scoring were consistent between patients in our cohort and those in CPTAC ( Extended Data 246 

Fig. 5b ). To validate TOPAS scores by experiment , we treated five cell lines with known 247 

oncogenic driver alterations in EGFR (A431, NCI -H1975) , ERBB2 (NCI -H87) and FGFR2 248 

(KATOIII, MFM223) with afatinib (EGFRi) or futibatinib (FGFR2i) ( Fig. 4 g). Indeed , each cell 249 

line showed a significant TOPAS score for its driver kinase , which was reduced upon the 250 

respective drug treatment. Extending this analysis to ICK signaling activity in the RAF -MEK -251 

ERK and PI3K -AKT pathways showed that their TOPAS was generally also reduced upon RTK 252 

inhibition (Fig. 4 h). In t hree cases, the PI3K -AKT pathway was not inactivated upon kinase 253 

inhibition, likely due to mechanisms that decouple the RTK from the downstream pathway: 254 

A431 ( low PTEN levels ), NCI -H1975 (PIK3CA mut ation) , and MCM223 ( PIK3CA mutation) .255 

As expected, k inase activities in other pathways remain ed unaffected ( Extended Data Fig. 6). 256

Patient -centric analysis in the TOPAS Portal 275 

To enable molecular oncologists to interrogate and interpret patient phosphoproteomes, we built 276 

the TOPAS Portal, an interactive web -based decision support system comprised of seve ral 277 

dashboards , each addressing specific aspects of the data (Fig. 5a, https://topas -278 

portal.kusterlab.org/ ). Additionally, patient -specific reports can be downloaded in tabular format 279 

for offline investigation and manual annotation of results. Using data from a testicular teratoma 280 

patient (P1306) as an example , we illustrate how the portal and reports can be used to prepare for 281 

the MTB meeting. First, the overall data quality for the patient and the TMT batch in which the 282 

patient was contained is assessed ( Fig. 5b). This includes checking if i) the summed intensities of 283 

peptides and phosphopeptides are in the acceptable range, ii) the QC samples contained in the 284 

same batch cluster with the QC samples of all other batches in a principal component analysis 285 

(PCA) and i ii ) other patients in the same batch who a re typically from different entities do not 286 

cluster together in the PCA. For P1306, all three criteria were met. Failing QC criteria can lead to 287 

excluding the patient or the entire batch. Alternatively, one can cho ose to continue the analysis 288 

with consider ation of the problematic data quality. In the target identification step, aberrant kinase 289 

activity can be explored using TOPAS scores , and aberrant abundance of biomarkers relevant for 290 

treatment recommendations , such as tumor antigens, immune checkpoint pr oteins, immune cell 291 

infiltration , or kinase substrate phosphorylation , can be considered (Fig. 5c). In our example ,292 

EGFR should be considered because of an outstanding TOPAS score. ERBB2 had a TOPAS score 293 

just below threshold , indicating potential HER1 -HER 2-heterodimer signaling. CLDN6 is a 294 

noteworthy tumor antigen in this patient because an outstanding response to CLDN6 -specific 295 

CAR -T cell treatment has been reported in a teratoma patient before 33 . For target verification ( Fig. 296 

5d), each TOPAS subscore is evaluated individually to check if it supports the conclusion of 297 

aberrant kinase activity implied by the overall TOPAS score. This includes looking up phospho -298 

site annotations in PhosphositePlus ( PSP ) and their reported relevance for kinase activity. For 299 

P1306, all TOPAS subscores suppo rted high EGFR activity. Next , the molecular oncologist 300 

summarizes the results in a target evidence table ( Fig. 5e) that lists individual proteins that are 301 

clinically actionable , along with the molecular evidence derived from the phosphoproteom e data. 302 

Pote ntial inclusion of the patient in an ongoing clinical trial is also documented. Results from all 303 

four steps are presented in the MTB, supported by plots downloaded from the portal, and discussed 304 

alongside the genomics and transcriptomics data collected for the same patient. Final target 305 

recommendation is made by accredited clinical oncologists. 306

Cohort -centric analysis in the TOPAS Portal 320 

Molecular data collections on pan -cancer cohorts are highly valuable for discovering common and 321 

distinct aspects of tumor biology. In the prospective setting of a n MTB , this is particularly true as 322 

it may drive therapy decisions. Creating new hypotheses is especially important for rare or 323 

otherwise hard -to -treat cancers , a focus of our precision oncology program . The portal offers 324 

functionalities to i) detect molecular subgroups within or across entities, ii) compare groups of 325 

patients by genomic alteration , iii) detect proteins, kinases , or pathways exhibiting outlier behavior 326

The TOPAS platform was developed to assist scientists and clinicians in ga ining therapeutically 406 

actionable insights from analyzing the phosphoproteomes of individual cancer patients or cohorts 407 

in a prospective MTB setting. Such data analysis pipelines for genomics and transcriptomics 51, 52 

408 

as well as clinical decision support systems (CDSS) for genomics 53 -55 have been available to MTBs 409 

for some time, but no comparable tools existed for phosphoproteom e data . While the CPTAC data 410 

analysis pipeline (CDAP) 56 , paire d with PANOPLY 57 , a cloud platform offering analysis tools for 411 

cohort -wide investigations, follows a unified approach, it is currently not possible to analyze or 412 

contextualize the profiles of individual patients. The same is true for the Clinical Knowledge Graph 413 

(CKG), which integrates data processing, functional annotation , and visualization within a414 

knowledge graph database 58 . In addition, a ll available tools today have only been used for 415 

retrospective cohort analysis . To derive tailored options for each patient in ne ed of therapy, 416 

independent of whether it is a common, well -studied , or a rare cancer subtype , or even a cancer of 417 

unknown primary, retrospective cohort studies are insufficient. 418 

The TOPAS platform specifically addresses this gap and is the first tool with demonstrated ability 419 

to analyze the phosphoproteomes of a large number of prospective MTB cases. While the platform 420 

is computationally efficient, the main current bottleneck is the time needed by molecular 421 

oncologists to interpret and understand the data for each case well enough to arrive at a treatment 422 

recommendation. This can be challenging because it is often unclear which level of over - or 423 

underactivity of a signaling component (protein, phospho -site, kinase, pathway, etc.) may 424 

constitute an actionable target in the complex biological and disease journey context of this 425 

individual patient. The simple approach used here of prioritizing parameters with a z -score of ≥2 426 

carries a risk for both false positives and negatives. In addition, the full potential of the TOPAS 427 

scoring concept is currently underutilized because of the lack of comprehensive, high -confidence ,428 

and cancer -context -aware kinase -substrate relationships. Recent computational advances in kinase 429 

motif analysis 59 and large -scale phosphoproteome profiling of drug perturbation e xperiments 29, 32 430 

can be expected, at least in part, to alleviate this issue. 431 

The TOPAS platform is under continuous development and we are happy t o help other s set it up 432 

in their MTB environment . In the future, visualizations that overlay the signaling components for 433 

each patient directly onto curated pathway diagrams 60 will become available . Additionally, we are 434 

collecting training data from manual evaluation s of dysregulated signaling pathways in patients to 435 

develop machine learning models that can provide faster decision support. By close ,436 

multidisciplinary collaboration, we anticipate that phosphoproteome profiling alongside the open -437 

source TOPAS analysis platform has the potential to become a widely used component in precision 438 

medicine programs around the globe. 439

## Peptide and protein identification and quantification by MaxQuant 20 

For peptide and phophopeptide identification and quantification of the MTB and CPTAC cohorts, raw mass 21 

spectrometry files were searched with MaxQuant (v.1.6.12.0 for Batch 1-229 and all CPTAC datasets, 22 

v2.1.3.0 for Batch 230-336) against the human SwissProt+TrEMBL database (97,086 protein sequences 23 

including isoforms, downloaded Nov 2020) and the common contaminants database from MaxQuant 5. To 24 

distinguish CDKN2A p14 and p16 splice variants, CDKN2A entries had their gene name extended by the 25 

respective splice variant names CDKN2A-p14 and CDKN2A-p16. The proteome and the phosphoproteome 26 

datasets were searched separately and per TMT batch. A custom automated pipeline was set up in Python 27 

to start a MaxQuant search as soon as all raw files for a batch were measured. 28 

29 

Default parameters of MaxQuant were used with the following changes. Searches were performed using 30 

the ‘MS3 reporter ion’ experiment type and ‘TMT11plex’ labels. Isotope impurity correction factors 31 

provided by the manufacturer were used for each TMT lot. Searches included up to three missed cleavage 32 

sites, a minimum peptide length of 6, maximum peptide mass of 6,000 and a fragment ion tolerance of 0.4 33 

Da (ITMS). PSM-level FDR was set to 1% and protein level to 100% (for protein level FDR control see 34 

below). For the phosphoproteome, phosphorylation of serine, threonine, and tyrosine was set as variable 35 

modification. CPTAC data were searched with the same parameters except the experiment type was set to 36 

‘MS2 reporter ion’. 37

## TOPAS pipeline 47 

Software and performance 48 

The TOPAS pipeline was implemented in Python 3.9.13 and is available at 49 

https://github.com/kusterlab/topas-pipeline . A Docker image is automatically created with every git 50 

commit. The pipeline performs the following steps (outlined in further detail below): quality control, MS2 51 

identification transfer, normalization and imputation, protein grouping (proteome only), protein or 52 

phosphopeptide quantification, fold-change and z-score calculation, functional annotation, TOPAS scoring 53 

and report creation. System and unit tests were implemented to ensure stability and maintainability. The 54 

pipeline is run in a Docker container by passing it a configuration file in TOML format with input/output 55 

paths and settings for the pipeline run. Compute-intensive steps of the pipeline were optimized to use 56 

multiple cores and a pipeline run of ~2,000 patients is completed in ~24 hours using ~250GB of RAM. The 57 

pipeline is routinely run on a Linux server with 64 cores and 512GB of RAM which can run several pipeline 58 

runs in parallel if needed. 59 

60 

Quality control 61 

Batches with TMT labeling efficiency <90% (calculated by ‘summed intensity TMT-labelled 62 

peptides’/‘summed intensity all peptides’) or mean IMAC enrichment efficiency <80% (calculated by 63 

‘summed intensity phosphopeptides’/‘summed intensity all peptides’) over the 12 fractions were excluded. 64 

Furthermore, samples for which the ‘summed TMT reporter intensity’ was <20% (threshold chosen 65 

empirically) of the mean ‘summed intensity of TMT reporter intensity’ of both reference channels were 66 

assigned a “QC failed” label and excluded from further analyses. Additionally, samples were also excluded 67 

from further analyses if the genomics or transcriptomics data detected contaminations or if the sample 68 

identity could not be ascertained (termed excluded by ‘other’). Samples with a tumor cell content <20%, 69 

severe DNA degradation, documented long cold ischemia time (>30 min) or RNA library preparation 70 

failure were assigned a ‘limited tissue quality’ label. 71

Caution flags for quantification values 136 

Besides the “imputed” and “partially imputed” flags described above, three additional flags were employed. 137 

First, false identification and inaccurate quantification of peptides can distort protein quantifications, 138 

especially when based on few peptides. To detect such problematic cases, we performed a linear regression 139 

for each protein, modeling its intensity as a function of the detected peptide count C across samples. Protein 140 

quantification with a residual of 2 × 1.1 𝐶−1 standard deviations above the predicted protein intensity were 141 

flagged as “pepcount_OOR”. The multiplicative factor 1.1 𝐶−1 was empirically determined to produce more 142 

lenient thresholds as the peptide count increases. Second, quantification values below 1/100 th of the 143 

maximum quantification value in the batch were flagged as “quan_OOR”, again informed by the 144 

observation that ratios smaller than that can typically not be reliably measured by TMT. Third, if a protein 145 

was not detected in a channel but detected in one or more other channels in that batch, the missing 146 

quantification value was flagged as “detected in batch” to indicate a potential true absence. 147

## Tumor proteome activity status (TOPAS) scores 174 

Kinase abundance score 175 

The kinase abundance score of a patient is the z-score of that patients’ kinase abundance computed relative 176 

to the background cohort consisting of all other patients, and is computed independently for every kinase. 177 

178 

Activity indicating phosphorylation score 179 

For the activity indicating phosphorylation score a set of phosphopeptides (typically, well-known 180 

autophosphorylation sites) are selected as the intersection of sets of phosphopeptides i) uniquely attributed 181 

as substrate of the RTK according to PhosphoSitePlus, ii) regulated by a drug selectively targeting the RTK 182 

in a decryptM experiment 14, 15 and iii) differentially abundant between patients with a known oncogenic 183 

alteration in the RTK versus the rest of the MTB cohort. If one or two of these sets were unavailable for an 184 

RTK, an intersection was done with the remaining sets. For each patient, the log10 fold change is computed 185 

relative to the background cohort for each phosphopeptide included in the set. To arrive at the protein 186 

phosphorylation scores, the sum of log10 fold changes are z-scored across all patients, resulting in 187 

standardized scores that can be compared to each other. 188 

189 

Protein phosphorylation score 190 

For the protein phosphorylation score, all phosphopeptides on the protein are considered, excluding 191 

phosphopeptides shared with other proteins. As with activity indicating phosphorylation scores, the protein 192 

phosphorylation scores is the sum of log10 fold changes, z-scored across all patients. 193 

194 

Substrate phosphorylation score 195 

For substrate phosphorylation scores, we first removed the protein expression contribution for each 196 

phosphopeptide by fitting a log-log-linear model with a bounded slope between 0 and 1, and subtracted the 197 

regressed linear protein expression effect from the phosphosite abundance. For the substrate 198 

phosphorylation score, all phosphopeptides annotated as a substrate of the kinase according to Bayer et 199 

al. 15 , detected in at least 1 QC channel for 67% of batches (i.e. those for which bridge normalization was 200 

performed) are considered. For MEKs, MAP2K3/6, CHEK1 and AAK1, only the seed sites were used as 201 

kinase substrates. As with activity indicating phosphorylation scores, the substrate phosphorylation scores 202 

is the sum of log10 fold changes, z-scored across all patients. 203 

204 

TOPAS scores 205 

The TOPAS-RTK scoring consists of three subscores: kinase abundance, activity indicating 206 

phosphorylation and protein phosphorylation. The TOPAS-ICK scoring consists of a single subscore: 207 

substrate phosphorylation. For each TOPAS score, the subscores are summed up with the activity indicating 208 

phosphorylation subscore weighted doubly. Finally, the summed scores are again z-scored across all 209 

patients to arrive at a standardized TOPAS score for each of the 18 RTKs and 28 ICKs for each patient. 210
