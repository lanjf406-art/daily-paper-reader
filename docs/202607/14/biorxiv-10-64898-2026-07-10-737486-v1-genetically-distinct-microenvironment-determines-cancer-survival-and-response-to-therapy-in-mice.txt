ABSTRACT 17 

Genetic uniqueness of the tumor microenvironment significantly influences cancer 18 

growth, survival, and response to therapy, independent of the cancer cell's intrinsic 19 

properties or the adaptive immune system. Using genetically di stinct Rag1 -/- mouse 20 

models, this study shows that different strains exhibit varied tumor growth kinetics and 21 

survival outcomes when xenografted with identical leukemic and solid tumor cell lines. 22 

This study further highlights the critical role of the myeloid immune compartment and 23 

shows that disrupting both lymphoid and myeloid systems alters cancer progression. 24 

The se results also reveal that the tumor microenvironment can permanently alter cancer 25 

cell phenotypes and significantly affect chemotherapy efficacy, as seen with Cisplatin's 26 

varying effects across strains. These findings underscore the importance of considering 27 

genetic background in preclinical cancer models, suggesting that re liance up on a single 28 

mouse strain may lead to incomplete conclusions about cancer biology and treatment 29 

efficacy. 30

INTRODUCTION 36 

Cancer remains one of the leading causes of death world wide , with its incidence 37 

rising in developing nations over recent decade s (ARNOLD et al. 2017; SUNG et al. 2021; 38 

WILKINSON AND GATHANI 2022) . Despite many therapy advancements, there is significant 39 

inter -individual variability in patient survival and disease progression in cancers resulting 40 

from t he same genetic mutation (COATE et al. 2010; TALSETH -PALMER AND SCOTT 2011) .41 

This suggests that the microenvironment surrounding the cancer cell – and the host 42 

genetics underpinning its characteristics – may determine the outcome of the disease 43 

(ANDERSON AND SIMON 2020; DE VISSER AND JOYCE 2023) . A microenvironment is 44 

comprised of the molecules, cells , and structures that support unique cells and tissues ,45 

such as the immune cells, stromal cells, signaling molecules, and vasculature 46 

surrounding a tumor (ANDERSON AND SIMON 2020) . This definition can be broadened to 47 

consider the entire organism as the microenvironment for liquid cancers , such as 48 

leukemia. Abnormal cells, such as cancer cells, can alter their microenvironment which 49 

can in tu rn influence how cancer cells grow and spread (WU AND DAI 2017; DE VISSER AND 50 

JOYCE 2023) . As such, the microenvironment poses an attractive target for improving our 51 

understand ing how cancer cells form and developing new strategies for cancer treatment 52 

(WU AND DAI 2017; THAKKAR et al. 2020; XIAO AND YU 2021) .53 

The relationship between cancer cells and their surrounding microenvironment is 54 

bidirectional: while cancer cells can remodel their environment, the microenvironment can 55 

also shape tumor growth and gene expression profiles (SARGENT et al. 2022; ELHANANI et 56 

al. 2023; HASHAM et al. 2024) . In patients, u nlike xenografted models, cancer cells , the 57 

microenvironment, and the immune system are all derived from identical genetic 58 

composition. Therefore, it is difficult to determine the exact contribution of the tumor 59 

microenvironment towards growth or response to therapy. In addition, a myriad of factors 60 

influences cancer growth and response to therapy, including the complexity of the human 61 

genome, adaptive immune response, living conditions, and lifestyle/environmental 62 

factors. 63 

Mouse models provide an ideal solution to control not only an organism’s genetics, 64 

but also the environmental factors to which they are exposed, making them well -suited 65

Survival of g enetically distinct immunodeficient (Rag1 -/-) mouse strains with 115 

leukemia 116 

We previously demonstrated that identical human or mouse cancer cell lines, 117 

grown in culture and then xenografted into five genetically di stinct mouse strains, exhibit 118 

markedly different growth kinetics depending on the host background (SARGENT et al. 119 

2022; HASHAM et al. 2024) . Furthermore, these mice were immunodeficient in both T - and 120 

B- lymphocytes due to their Rag 1 nullizygosity (SARGENT et al. 2022; HASHAM et al. 2024) .121 

This data indicates that the genetic microenvironment in which the cancer is engrafted ,122 

independent of the adaptive immune system and the intrinsic nature of the cancer cell ,123 

determines the growth of both solid and liquid cancers. However, it is important to note 124 

that, due to ethical reasons, xenografted solid tumors can only be allowed to grow to a 125 

certain size (e.g., 1000 mm 3) before the mouse must be euthanized. Therefore, it is 126 

important to recognize that the size of the tumor does not necessarily indicate the inability 127 

of a certain strain of mouse to sustain the cancer without symptoms . In general, a p atient 128 

does not typically die from the size of a tumor, but rather what the tumor does to the rest 129 

of the body , and thus tumor size alone is not fully reflective of the disease . However, a 130 

leukemia model can be used to address the question of whether the tumor 131 

microenvironment also influences survivability apart from tumor growth. 132 

To this end, three different strains of Rag1 -/- mice were xenografted with MEC1, a 133 

chronic lymphocytic leukemia cell line , to generate previously validated xenograft models .134 

Th e MEC1 cell line carrie d a luciferase cassette which enabled the longitudinal 135

The genetics of the tumor microenvironment alter cancer cell phenotypes 264 

While we have shown both previously and in this study that the growth kinetics of a given 265 

cancer cell line are dictated by surrounding the microenvironment (Figure s 1 and 2), it 266 

remains unclear whether these effects are transient or permanent : Does the cancer cell 267 

adapt its phenotype in response to the microenvironment ? Does it undergo stable, 268 

heritable changes? Or does the microenvironment selectively support a pre -existing 269 

subclone within the population? This adaptation versus evolution question is important 270 

to determine and to truly understand the role of the tumor microenvironment in the growth 271 

of cancer. To answer this question, we turned to solid tumor models, as leukemia cells 272 

are distributed throughout the bloodstream and lymphoid organs, making it difficult to 273 

isolate a specific site for interrogation without introducing bias in the observed phenotype .274 

For instance, cancer cells in the bone marrow may be readily adapting to the 275 

microenvironment , while the cells in the spleen have been selected for a certain 276 

phenotype. While this would be interesting, it would require investigation beyond the 277 

scope of this study. To move forward, we focused on solid tumor models with the most 278 

extreme phenotypes. Based on prior studies, B6R was selected as the model with the 279 

slowest tumor development, and NR as the model with the fastest tumor growth (SARGENT 280 

et al. 2022) .281 

To test whether cancer cells are merely adapting to the microenvironment or 282 

undergoing permanent changes or clonal selection , we first xenografted MDA -MB -231 283 

clonal cancer cells in both NR and B6R mice. Our previous work has demonstrated that 284 

the NR microenvironment is growth inducive and B6R is growth inhibitive, the latter 285 

primarily due to massive collagen deposits within the tumor (SARGENT et al. 2022) . When 286 

the tumors grew, we passaged them in equal amounts in to both NR and B6R naïve mice 287 

(Figure 4A). Tumor size was measured using calipers over a period of 32 days . To assess 288 

whether cancer cells adapt to their microenvironment or are shaped by it, we first 289 

passaged the MDA -MB -231 cell line from a growth inducive environment (NR) and in to a290 

growth inhibitive environment (B6R) . Tumors derived from this passage exhibited no 291 

difference in growth kinetics of the tumors compared to those maintained in the growth -292

Genetic s of the microenvironment drive chemotherapy treatment efficacy 303 

While understanding how genetic ally distinct tumor microenvironment s influence 304 

cancer growth kinetics is biologically important, the most clinically critical phenotype 305 

remains the response to therapy . It is well established that the same treatment can yield 306 

vastly different outcomes across different patients. However, because a patient’s tumor 307 

shares the same genetic origin as the host, disentangling the specific contribution of the 308 

tumor microenvironment to therapeutic response remains a significant challenge. 309 

Additionally, tumors are often excised after resistance has already emerged, introducing 310 

further complexity due to sub -clonal heterogeneity. To disentangle these factors ,311 

xenograft mouse models afford us the ability to separate/differentiate the genetic 312 

contributions on the microenvironment to tumor growth .313 

For this experiment, we again leveraged the solid tumor system , since models 314 

such as B6R showed low xenograf t growth in leukemia models ( Figure 1), making it 315 

difficult to detect subtle differences between treated and control groups. We selected the 316 

slowest and fastest growing strain s (B6R and NR, respectively ), as previously determined 317 

(SARGENT et al. 2022) . We also tested 129R , another strain shown to support rapid tumor 318 

growth in earlier studies (SARGENT et al. 2022; HASHAM et al. 2024) . All three strains were 319 

xenografted with MDA -MB -231 , which we and others have demonstrated to reliably grow 320 

in these genetic backgrounds . When the tumors reached at least ~30 mm³ , the mice were 321 

treated with Cisplatin , a common chemotherapeutic agent, three times a week for 21 days 322

DISCUSSION 335 

In this study , we used both solid and liquid cancer model s to understand how the 336 

tumor microenvironment influences cancer cell growth, therapeutic response, and overall 337 

survival across genetically di stinct mouse strains . Numerous studies have looked into the 338 

role of tumor microenvironment in both biological models and patient samples (ANDERSON 339

Previously and here in , we have developed and validated a mouse model system 347

reach beyond the standard ethical size threshold , limiting the utility of this model . Although 352 

tumor extractions of these metastatic models can be performed, inherent variability in this 353 

in vivo system and an inability to control for significant mechanical and handling stressors 354 

may compromise the integrity of the animal and the metastatic tumor growth being 355 

evaluated . Importantly, it is also imperative to note that organismal survival rates and 356 

cancer growth kinetics are not equivalent parameter s. It has been observed, commonly 357 

in chronic lymphocytic leukemia and benign hyperplastic growth patients, that the tumor 358 

burden is not correlated with their survival, alluding to the critical role of the 359

demonstrated the clear role of genetic background plays in cancer growth rates (SARGENT 361

In this study, we assessed the impact of growth permissive and growth inhibitive 364 

microenvironment s in one solid tumor and two liquid tumor models . In addition to 365 

proteomic profiling of these microenvironment conditions, we also tested the efficacy of 366 

Cisplatin in a variety of Rag 1-/- strains , demonstrating a clear impact of both 367 

microenvironment and genetic context on treatment efficacy . It is important to note that 368 

the cell lines we have used are well -established in the field and adequately represent the 369 

biological processes associated with these cancers in patients, thus enabling the 370 

reve lation of biological patterns that emerge due to genetic di stinctness of the host .371 

Despite the utility of these model systems , caution need s to be observe d when 372 

generalizing the patterns observed in specific strains and cancer cell lines as directly 373 

representative of the biology and treatment responses for all cancers. Future studies will 374 

aim to test different classes/types of the same cancer , and whether the microenvironment 375 

plays a critical role in determining outcome or therapy response in those models as well .376 

For example , while here we have demonstrated the impact of genetic background and 377 

microenvironment in the triple negative breast cancer cell line MDA -MB -231, future 378 

studies would determine whether a similar pattern emerges with a hormone dependent 379 

MCF cell line or comparable patient derived xenograft at various stages of cancer 380 

progression .381 

An intriguing feature was observed when the myeloid component of the immune 382 

system was disrupted in the leukemia studies . PWKR mice exhibited shorter survival than 383 

NR and CASTR when xenografted with MEC1 cells . However , when Il2rg was genetically 384 

removed from these strains , PRG mice showed longer survival and could carry a higher 385 

tumor burden than NRG or CRG. Il2rg is an essential component of IL -2 and other 386 

cytokine receptor s, and its disruption targets the myeloid component of the immune 387 

system , particularly NK cells (CAO et al. 1995) . Thus , our results have revealed that the 388 

genetic di fferences of the myeloid component of the immune system may play a crucial 389 

role in xenograft rejection , completely independent of the adaptive immune system. 390 

Therefore , these features make PRG an ideal mouse model for study ing leukemias ,391 

especially in efficacy testing of slow -acting compounds . Unlike NRG or other commonly 392 

used strains, the extended disease latency allows sufficient time for delayed therapeutic 393 

effects to emerge .394

Apart from studying cancer growth and effects, xenografted mouse models are 395 

highly pragmatic in pre -clinical testing, particularly in their ability to validate new 396 

compounds , molecules , or combination therapies for potential efficacy in human patients 397 

(BOSENBERG et al. 2023) . Yet , there is an overarching tendency in the field to only evaluate 398 

efficacy using one or two strains of mice prior to determining if a particular compound or 399 

therapy is rejected or accepted as a viable for further testing . We have previously 400 

demonstrated that patient derived xenografts may fail in commonly used mouse strain s401 

such as NSG , but may xenograft ver y well in other Rag1 -/- strains (HASHAM et al. 2024) .402 

Here , we show highly variable treatment outcomes, as Cisplatin treatment exhibited three 403 

distinct effects in three different strains. Cisplatin is a platinum compound that binds to 404 

DNA and upon replication causes unrepairable DNA strand breaks , leading to increases 405 

in cell death and marked effects on replicating cells (PERSE 2021) . As a standard strain ,406 

NSG shows efficacy with Cisplatin , with unsurprising efficacy observed in NR mice due 407 

to the shared genetic background of these models. P redictably , this same compound had 408 

minimal effect on B6R mice , as B6R m odels are known to exhibit slow tumor growth 409 

kinetics since these models form an abundance of collagen deposits which inhibit tumor 410 

growth (SARGENT et al. 2022) . Surprisingly, Cisplatin treatment in 129R mice enhanced 411 

tumor growth, although the mechanisms responsible for this phenotype have yet to be 412 

elucidated . It is possible that 129R mice have reduced or slowed Cisplatin absorption ,413 

and thus these observations are of a hormetic effect , or some other mechanism entirely .414 

Regardless of the mechanism, these findings underscore the importance of including 415 

genetic differences in preclinical research and emphasize the need for caution in 416 

extrapolating the efficacy of a therapy in a single genetic model to inherently 417 

heterogeneous human populations . Further, our findings suggest that the tumor 418 

microenvironment may be essential in determining the fate of a new molecule or 419 

combination of compounds , posing an important consideration for therapy selection in 420 

patient care .421 

Our proteomic studies ha ve shown that the same cell line xenografted in one 422 

mouse strain results in strikingly different proteomic profile s when xenografted in to 423 

another strain (HASHAM et al. 2024) . Th ese data allude to cancer mechanisms promoting 424 

adaptation in order to optimize growth in a given environment , or to the selection for a 425

Overall, our findings in genetic ally distinct models support the concept that the 435 

genetic composition of the tumor microenvironment play s a critical role not only in 436 

modulating cancer growth kinetics , but also in determining a host’s capacity to sustain 437

the genetic background differences of the host when selecting experimental models for 439 

studying cancer biology and evaluating therapeutic efficacy. Neglecting genetic diversity 440

the costly advancement of treatments that only work in narrow genetic context s, thereby 442
