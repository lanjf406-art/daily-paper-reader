Abstract 1

Understanding gene expression requires integrating multiple regulatory layers, because 2

transcript abundance does not necessarily correspond to translational activity or protein 3

abundance. Ribosome profiling and proteomics help distinguish increased translation from 4

ribosome stacking or translational buffering, but no de facto standard framework exists for 5

unsupervised integration of transcriptome, translatome, and proteome profiles. Here, we 6

propose a four-dimensional tensor decomposition-based unsupervised feature extraction 7

approach for tri-omics integration. We applied higher-order singular value decomposition 8

to transcriptome, Ribo-seq, and proteome profiles measured under branched-chain amino 9

acid starvation. The resulting singular value vectors captured relationships among the three 10 

omics layers, including a component consistent with ribosome stacking, where transcrip- 11 

tome and translatome signals increased while proteome signals decreased, and another 12 

consistent with translational buffering, where proteome variation was suppressed despite 13 

transcriptome and translatome changes. Gene selection identified 1,781 genes associated 14 

with ribosome stacking and 227 genes associated with translational buffering. Enrichment 15 

analyses linked the former to translation, post-translational protein modification, RNA 16 

polymerase II transcription, cell cycle regulation, endoplasmic reticulum protein processing, 17 

ubiquitin-mediated proteolysis, and stress-related pathways, and the latter to ribosome, 18 

translation elongation and termination, spliceosome, immune- and stress-related pathways, 19 

and ribosomopathy-associated diseases. Robustness analyses indicated that the results 20 

were not substantially affected by the duplicated proteome replicate or missing-value 21 

handling. Under the tested settings, comparison with MOFA+ and mixOmics suggested 22 

that our approach more directly extracted components interpretable as ribosome stacking 23 

and translational buffering. These results demonstrate that tensor decomposition-based 24 

unsupervised feature extraction is useful for identifying functionally relevant gene clusters 25 

from tri-omics data. 26 

Keywords: BCAA starvation; tri-omics; tensor decomposition 27 

## 1. Introduction 28 

Despite its apparent ease, measuring gene expression is not straightforward. The 29 

purpose of measuring gene expression is to identify genes that are active in a context- 30 

dependent manner. Relating the phenotype to the amount of expressed genes can help us to 31 

infer the functions of the genes. Thus, it is important to assume that more highly expressed 32

genes are more active in some contexts. Nevertheless, expression levels are not always 33 

related to the activity of individual genes. This is because the definition of gene expression 34 

remains unclear. It is typical to measure gene expression as the amount of mRNA. However, 35 

the amount of mRNA is not always equivalent to the activity of the genes, because mRNA 36 

cannot function until it is translated into a protein. One might wonder why we measure 37 

the amount of mRNA rather than directly measuring the amount of protein. Defining gene 38 

expression as the amount of protein creates another problem, as the amount of protein is not 39 

directly regulated [ 1 ]. Most of the molecular machinery that can influence gene expression 40 

is in mRNA layer, e.g. promoter methylation, protein binding to DNA, or non-coding RNA 41 

that can affect the amount of mRNA after its transcription from DNA. Thus, if we define 42 

protein expression as gene expression, we cannot directly relate the molecular machinery to 43 

the amount of gene expression. Furthermore, the amount of mRNA is not always directly 44 

related to the amount of protein present. If translation is halted, the amount of protein will 45 

not increase even if mRNA expression increases. In contrast, even if the amount of mRNA 46 

remains stable, the amount of protein can increase if the speed of translation increases [ 2]. 47 

In addition, compared to our knowledge of the molecular machinery that regulates the 48 

amount of mRNA, we have little knowledge about how the speed of translation is regulated. 49 

Translation speed is strongly related to the amount of ribosome. More ribosome usually 50 

results in more proteins. Thus, it appears sufficient to measure mRNA, ribosomes, and 51 

proteins simultaneously. However, the simultaneous measurement of mRNA, ribosomes, 52 

and proteins is not sufficient to determine gene expression in a context-dependent manner. 53 

More ribosome does not always increase translation speed, as the increased amount of 54 

ribosome could reflect ribosome stacking [ 3 ], which is evidence of decreased translation 55 

speed. Thus, we need to determine how to distinguish between increased ribosomes that 56 

reflect an increased translation speed and those caused by ribosome stacking. 57 

There is a long history of studies related to the interrelationships between mRNA, 58 

ribosome profiling (Ribo-seq) [ 4], and the proteome. Hereafter, we refer to these as the 59 

transcriptome, translatome, and proteome. Initially, Schwanhäusser et al. [ 5 ] proposed that 60 

as little as 40 percent of gene expression is regulated at the transcriptome level, whereas as 61 

much as 60 percent is regulated at the proteome level. Nevertheless, based on tri-omics 62 

(transcriptome, translatome, and proteome) [ 6 ], Jovanovic et al. [ 7] found that variations in 63 

the proteome are usually regulated at the transcriptome level, at least for steady-state and 64 

dynamic responses. In contrast, proteome modeling (changes in cell function) is governed 65 

directly at the proteome level. Thus, translational buffering (TB) [ 8 ] is a central issue in 66 

tri-omics studies. 67 

In 2015, Battle et al. [ 9 ] and Cenik et al. [ 10 ] proposed the concept of TB, referring to 68 

the suppression of proteome variation despite transcriptome variation owing to control 69 

by the translatome layer. This mechanism is useful to maintain proteome stability during 70 

transcriptome variation. Two possible mechanisms underly TB: 71 

• Translation initiation rate model: as mRNA levels increase, translation initiation 72 

factors and ribosomes become rate-limiting, reducing the frequency of translation 73 

initiation per mRNA molecule. Consequently, even with increased mRNA, total 74 

protein synthesis reaches saturation. 75 

• Differential accessibility model: excess mRNA is sequestered into pools not utilized 76 

for translation (P-bodies, stress granules, or specific ribonucleoprotein complexes). 77 

This regulates the proportion of translatable mRNA (Fraction), altering the apparent 78 

translation efficiency. 79 

Several studies have investigated this topic. Blevins et al [11] found the followings 80 

• The proteome is more strongly correlated with the translatome than the transcriptome. 81 

• TB occurs broadly. 82

Tri-omics have also been analyzed at the isoform level [ 12 ] but better proteome accuracy is 85 

required for a better understanding. Lu et al. [ 13 ] found that the high correlation between 86 

the translatome and proteome could be destroyed in some specific cases. Cuevas et al [ 14 ] 87 

considered a non-canonical proteome in tri-omics analysis. Zu et al. [ 15 ] considered plant 88 

tri-omics and confirmed that the proteome is more strongly correlated with the translatome 89 

than the transcriptome. Worpenberg et al. [ 16 ] investigated BCAA starvation in tri-omics 90 

and determined the importance of the dependence of dwell time on the condition. Elpida 91 

et al. [17] also investigated TB using tri-omics. 92 

Despite these numerous studies using tri-omics (transcriptome, translatome, and 93 

proteome) a de facto standard method to integrate tri-omics does not appear to exist. We 94 

propose the use of tensor decomposition (TD)-based unsupervised feature extraction 95 

(FE) [ 18 ]. TD-based unsupervised FE has been successfully analyzed in numerous multi- 96 

omics integration studies; therefore, it would not be surprising if it could be used to 97 

successfully integrate tri-omics. To determine whether TD-based unsupervised FE can 98 

successfully integrate tri-omics, we applied it to the datasets of Worpenberg et al. [16 ], 99 

which contain the largest number of samples among datasets associated with tri-omics 100 

measurements. As a result, we showed that TD-based unsupervised FE successfully 101 

identified functional gene sets associated with high and low translational efficiency and 102 

identified their functional mechanisms. Thus, we can conclude that TD-based unsupervised 103 

FE can satisfactorily integrate tri-omics. 104 

These studies [ 19– 23 ] demonstrate the growing importance of deep representation 105 

learning, graph-based modeling, and foundation models. However, our aim is different: 106 

interpretable unsupervised integration of bulk transcriptome, Ribo-seq, and proteome data 107 

with explicit omics-mode loadings. 108 

## 2. Materials and Methods 109 

2.1. Datasets 110 

The datasets analyzed in this study were generated by Worpenberg et al. [ 16 ], who 111 

investigated codon-specific ribosome stalling during branched-chain amino acid starvation 112 

in NIH3T3 mouse fibroblast cells. Cells were analyzed under six conditions: complete 113 

medium control, leucine starvation, isoleucine starvation, valine starvation, double star- 114 

vation of leucine and isoleucine, and triple starvation of leucine, isoleucine, and valine. 115 

RNA-seq and Ribo-seq data were obtained from matched experimental conditions, and 116 

quantitative proteomics data were obtained from the same starvation design. These datasets 117 

were used to construct the tri-omics tensor described in Section 2.2 118

and loaded into R using the read.csv command. 122

The supplementary file, “GSE291653_Ribo_seq_raw_count.tsv.gz”, was downloaded from 125

2.8. Computational complexity / scalability 261 

TD-based unsupervised FE required limited computational resources in the present 262 

dataset. In the current setup, the tensor contained 18,175 × 6 × 3 × 3 = 981,450 entries. 263 

Runtime was approximately 10 seconds on 12th Intel(R) Core(TM) i7-1270P, with peak 264 

memory below 32 GB. As for the scalability, HOSVD/SVD can be applied to larger problems 265 

by using truncated SVD or irlba. 266 

2.9. Limitations 267 

This study does not provide direct experimental validation of ribosome stacking or 268 

translational buffering. The proposed interpretation is based on latent component patterns, 269 

enrichment analysis, and orthogonal computational evidence. Experimental perturbation 270 

of selected genes and direct measurement of ribosome collision or protein synthesis will be 271 

required in future studies. 272

## 5. Conclusions 562 

In this study, we developed and applied a four-dimensional tensor decomposition- 563 

based unsupervised feature extraction method to integrate transcriptome, translatome, and 564 

proteome profiles obtained under branched-chain amino acid starvation. By representing 565 

genes, experimental conditions, replicates, and omics layers as a single tensor, the proposed 566 

method enabled the direct extraction of components reflecting relationships among mRNA 567 

abundance, ribosome occupancy, and protein abundance. 568 

The analysis identified two biologically interpretable components. One component 569 

was consistent with ribosome stacking, because it represented increased transcriptome and 570 

translatome signals accompanied by decreased proteome signals. Gene selection based 571 

on this component identified 1,781 genes enriched for translation-related processes, post- 572 

translational protein modification, transcriptional regulation, cell cycle suppression, endo- 573 

plasmic reticulum protein processing, ubiquitin-mediated proteolysis, and stress-response 574 

pathways. These enrichments suggest that the selected genes reflect cellular responses to 575 

impaired translation caused by amino acid starvation and ribosomal congestion. 576 

The second component was consistent with translational buffering, because it repre- 577 

sented a pattern in which proteome abundance remained relatively stable despite changes 578 

in transcriptome and translatome profiles. Gene selection based on this component identi- 579 

fied 227 genes enriched for ribosome-related pathways, eukaryotic translation elongation 580 

and termination, spliceosome, immune- and stress-associated pathways, and anemia- or 581 

ribosomopathy-related disease terms. These results suggest that the selected genes are 582 

involved in regulatory mechanisms that buffer protein abundance against transcriptomic 583 

perturbation. 584 

Robustness analyses further supported the reliability of the proposed framework. The 585 

major singular value vectors and selected genes were largely preserved when a different 586 

proteome replicate was duplicated or when missing proteome values were handled using 587 

an alternative substitution strategy. Moreover, comparison with MOFA+ and mixOmics 588 

indicated that these widely used multi-omics integration methods did not extract com- 589 

ponents as clearly consistent with ribosome stacking or translational buffering as those 590 

obtained by tensor decomposition. 591 

Taken together, these findings indicate that tensor decomposition-based unsupervised 592 

feature extraction can effectively integrate tri-omics profiles and identify gene clusters 593 

with coherent biological and functional interpretations. The proposed approach provides a 594 

useful data-driven framework for investigating translational regulation, ribosome stacking, 595 

and translational buffering in multi-layer omics datasets. Future studies applying this 596 

method to additional biological conditions and experimentally validating the selected 597 

genes will further clarify its utility for understanding post-transcriptional and translational 598 

regulation. 599
