SUMMARY 

Colorectal cancer remains a major cause of cancer mortality, and most microsatellite stable tumors derive little benefit from immune checkpoint blockade. Here, we identify a microbiome-dependent mechanism that converts immune-refractory colorectal cancer into a more immunologically responsive state. Using orthotopic mouse models spanning distinct genetic and immunologic contexts, we show that a Helicobacter-containing microbiome suppresses primary tumor growth and limits metastasis. This protective state is associated with increased intratumoral lymphocyte infiltration and stronger effector programs. Mechanistically, microbial exposure induces MHC class II expression in colon cancer cells to promote anti-tumor immunity. Tumor-intrinsic loss of CIITA abrogates microbial protection, whereas enforced CIITA expression is sufficient to increase intratumoral T cell accumulation, restrict progression and metastasis, and sensitize microsatellite-stable tumors to PD-1 and CTLA-4 blockade. In human microsatellite-stable patient-derived organoids, increased cancer-cell MHC-II enhanced interactions with autologous immune cells and increased tumor cell apoptosis. Together, these findings define a microbiome-cancer cell antigen presentation axis that restrains metastasis and overcomes immunotherapy resistance in colorectal cancer.

INTRODUCTION 41 

42 

Colorectal cancer (CRC) remains a leading cause of cancer mortality, driven predominantly by 43 

metastatic disease that is refractory to standard-of-care therapies (Shiels et al., 2025; Siegel et 44 

al., 2026a; Siegel et al., 2026b). Although immune checkpoint blockade has produced durable 45 

benefit in mismatch repair-deficient (dMMR) and microsatellite instability-high (MSI-H) CRC, 46 

most CRCs are mismatch repair-proficient and microsatellite stable (MSS), and the advanced 47 

stages of this disease subset derive little benefit from current PD-1 or CTLA-4 based 48 

immunotherapy modalities (Le et al., 2015; Sharma et al., 2023). Clinical efforts to extend 49 

checkpoint blockade to MSS metastatic CRC have thus far shown limited efficacy, underscoring 50 

the need to identify mechanisms that convert poorly inflamed tumors into lesions capable of 51 

supporting productive antitumor immunity (Eng et al., 2019; Sharma et al., 2023). Experimental 52 

studies further suggest that resistance in MSS CRC is not explained simply by a complete 53 

absence of tumor antigens; rather, these tumors can harbor clonal neoantigens but fail to 54 

support efficient T cell priming, enabling early immune escape (Westcott et al., 2021). 55 

56 

The intestinal microbiota has emerged as an important regulator of antitumor immunity and of 57 

response to immune checkpoint blockade immunotherapy in both preclinical and clinical settings 58 

(Baruch et al., 2021; Daillere et al., 2016; Davar et al., 2021; Gopalakrishnan et al., 2018; Lee et 59 

al., 2022; Matson et al., 2018; McCulloch et al., 2022; Routy et al., 2018; Sivan et al., 2015; 60 

Vetizou et al., 2015). Colonization with Helicobacter hepaticus was shown to promote CD4+ T 61 

follicular helper cell and B cell-dependent antitumor immunity together with the formation of 62 

tertiary lymphoid structures, establishing that defined commensal organisms can reshape local 63 

immune responses in a model of colitis-associated colon cancer (Overacre-Delgoffe et al., 64 

2021). At the same time, the effects of Helicobacter species are context dependent. In other 65 

settings, Helicobacter infection has been linked to chronic intestinal inflammation, mutagenesis, 66 

and colorectal tumorigenesis, underscoring that the consequences of these host –microbe 67 

interactions depend on species, host immune state, and disease context (Fox et al., 2011; Ge et 68 

al., 2019). However, most work in this area has focused on professional antigen-presenting 69 

cells, lymphoid organization, or cytokine-dependent effects on the immune compartment. 70 

Whether microbiota-derived signals also act directly on malignant epithelial cells in established 71

A plausible link between microbial cues and cancer cell immunogenicity is major 75 

histocompatibility complex class II (MHC-II). We previously showed that MHC-II expression in 76 

intestinal epithelial cells is regulated by a Helicobacter -containing gut microbiome and that loss 77 

of epithelial MHC-II promotes intestinal tumorigenesis, supporting a role for epithelial antigen 78 

presentation in immune surveillance at early stages of disease (Beyaz et al., 2021). In other 79 

tumor settings, enforced CIITA-driven MHC-II expression on cancer cells can directly prime 80 

naive CD4+ T cells, and cancer cell-intrinsic MHC-II expression has been associated with 81 

favorable immune responses and improved outcomes in multiple cancers, including melanoma, 82 

lymphoma, colorectal cancer, breast cancer, and more recently, human endometrial cancer 83 

(Bou Nasser Eddine et al., 2017; Cho et al., 2021; Chung et al., 2026; Forero et al., 2016; 84 

Johnson et al., 2016; Riaz et al., 2017; Rodig et al., 2018; Roemer et al., 2018). At the same 85 

time, tumor-cell MHC-II is not uniformly protective. In breast cancer lymph node metastasis, 86 

cancer-cell MHC-II promotes regulatory T cell expansion and metastatic progression (Lei et al., 87 

2023). Furthermore, tumor-specific MHC-II can also support adaptive resistance through its 88 

function as a ligand for T cell co-inhibitory receptor LAG-3, indicating that the output of this 89 

pathway depends on tissue context and immune state (Johnson et al., 2018). Thus, in CRC, it 90 

remains unresolved whether microbiota-dependent regulation of epithelial MHC-II persists after 91 

malignant transformation, whether cancer-cell MHC-II supports productive immunity or tolerance 92 

in established tumors, and whether this axis influences metastatic spread and response to 93 

checkpoint blockade immunotherapy. 94 

95 

Here, we identify a microbiome-dependent mechanism that restores MHC-II expression in colon 96 

cancer cells and converts immune-refractory MSS CRC into a more immunologically responsive 97 

state. Using orthotopic, genetically defined CRC models and autologous human patient-derived 98 

organoid – immune co-culture systems, we show that Helicobacter-containing microbiota induce 99 

cancer MHC-II expression, which promotes anti-tumor immunity against MSS CRC. Cancer cell-100 

intrinsic MHC-II is required for the antitumor effects of the microbiome and, when enforced, is 101 

sufficient to partially phenocopy microbial protection and sensitize MSS tumors to checkpoint 102 

blockade immunotherapy. These findings define a microbiome – cancer antigen presentation 103

Gut microbiome containing Helicobacter species limits CRC progression and metastasis 109 

110 

To test whether a Helicobacter -containing gut microbiome modulates CRC progression, we 111 

used an endoscopy-guided orthotopic implantation model that enables longitudinal assessment 112 

of tumor growth in the colonic mucosa. Using the MC38 cell line, a hypermutated, MSI-H-like 113 

syngeneic CRC model in C57BL/6 mice, we observed a significant reduction in primary tumor 114 

burden in Helicobacter -colonized (H+) mice compared to Helicobacter negative (H-) controls, 115 

as measured by colonoscopy and luciferase activity (Figure S1A-E). The incidence of 116 

metastasis was also reduced in H+ mice (Figure S1F-G). These anti-tumor effects were 117 

reproduced in an independent syngeneic setting using the CT26 tumors in BALB/c mice (Figure 118 

S1H-K), indicating that the anti-tumor effects of H+ microbiome generalize across host strains 119 

and tumor models. 120 

121 

Because transplanted 2D cell lines do not fully capture the genetics, histopathology and 122 

metastatic behavior of human CRC, particularly the predominance of the immune evasive MSS 123 

subtype, we next evaluated genetically-defined organoid-based orthotopic CRC models (Roper 124 

et al., 2017). Implanting genetically-engineered Apc null , Kras G12D , Tp53 null (AKP) organoids into 125 

the colon leads to formation of tumors that faithfully model MSS CRC (Roper et al., 2017; 126 

Westcott et al., 2021). H+ colonization dampened the AKP tumor growth and metastasis (Figure 127 

1A-D, S1L, M), resulting in prolonged overall survival in mice (Figure 1E). 128 

129 

We next asked whether Helicobacter colonization could constrain a more aggressive and 130 

immune evasive MSS model with enhanced metastatic competence. Mice implanted 131 

orthotopically with genetically engineered AKPS ( Apc Knock-down , Kras G12D , Tp53 null , Smad4 null )132 

organoids, develop aggressive and immune-evasive MSS tumors that eventually metastasize to 133 

the liver and other sites at enhanced frequency (Roper et al., 2017; Westcott et al., 2021). In 134 

this model, we found that Helicobacter colonization restricts primary tumor growth (Figure 1F-135 

G), and strikingly, prevented metastasis (Figure 1H-I), leading to significant survival advantage 136 

in H+ mice (Figure 1J). Together, these data identify a Helicobacter-associated gut microbiome 137

Colonization with single Helicobacter species partially restricts CRC progression and 141 

metastasis 142 

143 

We previously found that H+ community contain Helicobacter species H. typhlonius or H. 144 

mastomyrinus (Beyaz et al., 2021). To determine whether individual Helicobacter species 145 

identified in the H+ community are sufficient to restrain tumor progression, we inoculated H-146 

mice with either H. typhlonius or H. mastomyrinus , confirmed successful colonization (Figure 147 

S2A –G), and then orthotopically implanted AKPS organoids. Colonization with either species 148 

reduced primary tumor burden and metastatic incidence (Figure S2H –J) and improved survival 149 

relative to H- controls (Figure S2K). However, these effects were consistently less pronounced 150 

than those observed following whole-flora transfer. These findings indicate that single 151 

Helicobacter species can confer partial protection while additional microbial constituents or 152 

cooperative community interactions may be needed for the full protective effects of whole-flora 153 

transfer. 154 

155 

To determine whether the anti-metastatic activity we observed reflects a broader genus-level 156 

property of Helicobacter , we evaluated additional Helicobacter species that were not detected in 157 

the H+ flora. We inoculated H- mice with H. bilis , H. pullorum , or H. hepaticus and assessed 158 

metastatic dissemination following orthotopic implantation of AKPS tumors. In contrast to H+ 159 

flora, colonization with these species did not reduce metastatic incidence (Figure S2L). 160 

Together, these data indicate that suppression of metastatic progression is not a universal 161 

feature of Helicobacter colonization in mice. 162

To define how Helicobacter colonization remodels the tumor-immune microenvironment at 176 

higher resolution, we performed single-cell RNA sequencing on FACS-sorted CD45+ and 177 

CD45- fractions from enzymatically dissociated AKPS tumors harvested 21 days after orthotopic 178 

implantation in H+ and H- mice. After quality control, we analyzed the CD45+ compartment by 179 

unsupervised clustering and annotated immune populations using canonical lineage markers 180 

(Figure S4A, B). The overall representation of major immune lineages was broadly similar 181 

between groups. However, H+ tumors showed shifts in composition, including increased 182 

macrophage and monocyte populations and enrichment of B lineage states, most prominently 183 

plasma B cells (Figure 2C). Within the T cell compartment, Helicobacter colonization was 184 

associated with coordinated induction of trafficking and effector programs. Intratumoral CD8+ T 185 

cells from H+ tumors showed higher chemotaxis signatures and increased expression of Ccl5 186 

(Figure 2D, E), a chemokine implicated in tumor T cell infiltration programs (Topper et al., 2023). 187 

CD8+ T cells in H+ tumors also exhibited increased effector scores together with higher 188 

expression of Gzma (Figure 2F, G), consistent with enhanced cytotoxic differentiation. CD4+ T 189 

cells similarly demonstrated increased chemotaxis signatures and higher Cxcr6 expression 190 

(Figure 2H, I), a chemokine receptor linked to intratumoral localization and persistence of T cells 191 

(Di Pilato et al., 2021). This was accompanied by increased CD4 effector scores and higher 192 

expression of inflammatory and cytotoxic transcripts including Il17a , Tnf , and Gzma (Figure 2J, 193 

K). 194 

195 

We next used the MC38 model as an orthogonal system to test whether the immune-state shifts 196 

observed in AKPS tumors were conserved. MC38 scRNA-seq recapitulated key features 197 

observed in AKPS, with H+ tumors enriched for plasmablasts (Figure S4C –E) and T cells 198 

skewed toward effector differentiation (Figure S5A-C). Specifically, cytotoxic CD8+ T cells 199 

exhibited increased cytotoxicity gene scores with higher Gzma /Gzmb and reduced exhaustion-200 

associated Pdcd1 /Lag3 (Figure S5A –C) expression. CD4+ T cells showed increased levels of 201 

effector-associated transcripts ( Il18r1 , Jun , Slamf6 ) along with higher Cxcr6 expression (Figure 202 

S5D). These findings demonstrate that Helicobacter colonization is associated with conserved 203 

enhancement of intratumoral T cell infiltration and effector programs across CRC models. 204

T cells, B cells and Cxcr6+ cells are required for microbiome-mediated tumor control 206 

207 

Single-cell RNA-seq analyses revealed enhanced chemotactic and effector programs in CD4 +

208 

and CD8 + T cells from H+ tumors, accompanied by an expansion of mature B-cell populations, 209 

including plasma B cells, in both AKPS and MC38 models. These observations raised the 210 

possibility that adaptive immune cells contribute directly to microbiome-associated restriction of 211 

tumor progression and metastasis. To determine whether adaptive lymphocytes are required for 212 

microbiome-mediated tumor control, we depleted T cells (CD4 + and CD8 +) or B cells (CD19 +

213 

and B220 +) in H+ mice bearing AKPS tumors. T cell depletion resulted in a marked increase in 214 

metastatic incidence and significantly reduced survival compared with non-depleted H+ controls 215 

(Figure 3A, B). Similarly, depletion of B cells increased metastatic burden and shortened 216 

survival relative to H+ controls (Figure 3A, B). Together, these results indicate that both T cells 217 

and B cells contribute to microbiome-associated restriction of metastatic progression. 218 

219 

To further assess the role of adaptive immunity, we evaluated tumor progression and 220 

metastasis in immunodeficient mouse models under H+ conditions, including NSG (lacking T, B 221 

and NK cells), Nude (lacking T cells), and Rag2 KO (lacking both T and B cells) mice. Loss of 222 

adaptive immune components diminished the metastasis control and survival benefit of the H+ 223 

flora across these models (Figure 3C-D). The persistent anti-metastatic effect in Rag2 KO mice 224 

likely reflects a preserved NK-cell mediated surveillance against metastasis (Figure 3D). 225 

226 

In parallel, our scRNA-seq analysis identified consistently elevated Cxcr6 expression in CD4 + T227 

cells from H+ tumors across both AKPS and MC38 models (Figure 2I, Figure S5D). Given the 228 

established association of Cxcr6 with tissue-resident memory T-cell positioning within tumors 229 

(Di Pilato et al., 2021), we tested its functional relevance by implanting the AKPS organoids into 230 

Cxcr6-deficient mice under H+ conditions. Cxcr6 deficiency resulted in increased metastatic 231 

incidence and reduced survival compared to wild-type controls (Figure 3E-F), indicating that 232 

Cxcr6+ cells contribute to microbiome-mediated restriction of tumor progression and metastasis. 233 

Collectively, these results demonstrate that the protective effects of H+ microbiota require 234 

coordinated adaptive immune responses, including both T cells and B cells, and tissue-resident 235 

memory-associated Cxcr6+ cells. 236

Because effective tumor infiltration and cytotoxic activity require proper priming of T cells within 240 

tumor-draining lymph nodes (dLN), we next asked whether Helicobacter colonization enhances 241 

tumor-specific T-cell priming. To directly assess priming potential of CD8 + T cells, we implanted 242 

AKPS organoids expressing low levels of the ovalbumin peptide SIINFEKL (AKPS loSIIN 243 

(Westcott et al., 2021)) into H- and H+ mice, with microbial colonization established 7 days prior 244 

to tumor implantation. T cell responses were analyzed by flow cytometry at days 7 and 14 245 

following tumor implantation. At an early time point (day 7), H+ mice exhibited a pronounced 246 

expansion of CD8 + T cells in the dLN, accompanied by increased numbers of CD4 + T cells, 247 

consistent with enhanced priming in secondary lymphoid organs (Figure S6A-B). This early 248 

expansion preceded a significant increase in CD8 + T cell accumulation within tumors at day 14 249 

(Figure S6C-D), indicating a temporal progression from lymph node priming to intratumoral 250 

effector accumulation. 251 

252 

Because the magnitude of effector T cell priming is reflected by the expansion of antigen-253 

specific T cells expressing cytokines and cytolytic molecules, including granzyme B (GzmB ), 254 

IFN γ and TNF α, (Westcott et al., 2021), we next assessed functional differentiation of T cells in 255 

AKPS tumor bearing mice. In the dLN, H+ tumor-bearing mice displayed increased numbers of 256 

antigen-specific CD8 + T cells expressing Gzmb, IFNγ (Figure 4A-B), and TNFα (Figure S6E) at 257 

both time points, days 7 and 14. Similarly, CD4 + T cells expressing GzmB, IFNγ (Figure 4C-D), 258 

and TNFα (Figure S6F) were enriched in the dLN of H+ tumor bearing mice. In agreement with 259 

this early priming phenotype, H+ mice exhibited increased accumulation of cytotoxic tumor-260 

specific CD8 + T cells within tumors. Consistent with the effector programs observed in the 261 

draining lymph nodes, antigen-specific CD8 + T cells within tumors of H+ mice also exhibited 262 

increased expression of Gzmb, IFN γ (Figure 4E-F) and TNF α (Figure S6G) at both days 7 and 263 

14. Notably, CD4 + T cells expressing GzmB, IFN γ (Figure 4G-H) and TNF α (Figure S6H) were 264 

also significantly enriched in H+ tumors, indicating induction of a cytotoxic transcriptional 265 

program within the CD4 + T-cell compartment in the context of Helicobacter colonization. 266

Next, we tested whether tumor-intrinsic MHC-II mediates microbial anti-tumor immunity. To 278 

selectively ablate tumor MHC-II while preserving MHC-I upregulation, we generated a CIITA 279 

deficient AKPS organoid (CIITA KO). As expected, CIITA KO organoids upregulated MHC-I, but 280 

not MHC-II, in response to IFN γ (Figure S8A-B). Implantation of CIITA KO tumors into H+ mice 281 

resulted in increased tumor volume (Figure 5E-G), increased metastasis (Figure 5H), and 282 

reduced survival (Figure 5I) compared to parental tumors. Immunofluorescence analysis 283 

demonstrated reduced CD3 + T cell infiltration in CIITA KO tumors (Figure 5J-K), accompanied 284 

by loss of tumor-cell MHC-II pathway activity as reflected by reduced CD74 staining (Figure 5L). 285 

Together these finding indicate that tumor-intrinsic MHC-II is required for full microbiome-286 

mediated anti-tumor immunity. 287 

288 

Building on the requirement for tumor cell-intrinsic MHC-II in the H+ setting, we next asked 289 

whether tumor MHC-II alone is sufficient to restrict tumor progression and metastasis, in the 290 

absence of microbial colonization. To this end, we generated an AKPS organoid with elevated 291 

CIITA expression (CIITA OE). The CIITA OE AKPS organoids displayed significantly high MHC-292 

II expression in the absence of external stimuli, such as IFNγ (Figure S8C-D). Using CIITA OE 293 

and parental AKPS organoids, we assessed whether enforced tumor-intrinsic MHC-II 294 

expression confers anti-tumor activity in H- mice. Our results demonstrated that MHC-II 295 

overexpression significantly reduced tumor volume (Figure 6A-C) and metastasis rate (Figure 296 

6D). However these reductions did not result in significant improvement in overall survival 297 

(Figure 6E), indicating that tumor MHC-II is sufficient to partially restrict tumor progression and 298 

metastasis but does not fully recapitulate the survival benefit associated with H+ microbiome 299 

colonization. Tumors derived from CIITA OE organoids contained increased numbers of 300 

intratumoral T cells by immunofluorescence analysis (Figure 6F-G) alongside heightened tumor-301 

cell MHC-II expression as reflected by CD74 staining (Figure 6H). Collectively, these data 302 

demonstrate that tumor-intrinsic MHC-II expression promotes intratumoral T-cell presence and 303 

contributes to restriction of tumor growth and metastasis, and that enforced MHC-II expression 304 

on cancer cells is sufficient to confer partial anti-tumor activity even in the absence of 305 

Helicobacter colonization. 306

MHC-II mediated anti-tumor effects require immune cell trafficking from the lymph node 308 

309 

Our data show that microbiome-induced upregulation of tumor MHC-II enhances T-cell priming, 310 

increases tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes, reduces tumor burden and decreases metastasis 311 

incidence. These findings may suggest that anti-tumor effects of cancer-cell MHC-II depend on 312 

effective immune cell priming within tumor-draining lymph nodes and subsequent trafficking into 313 

the tumor microenvironment. To test this, we implanted either WT or CIITA-overexpressing 314 

(CIITA OE) AKPS organoids and treated mice with vehicle or FTY720, an inhibitor of 315 

lymphocyte egress from lymph nodes. Tumor burden in CIITA OE tumors treated with FTY720 316 

was comparable to CIITA WT controls, indicating that elevated tumor-cell MHC-II alone is not 317 

sufficient to restrict tumor growth when the trafficking of immune cells from the lymph node is 318 

inhibited (Figure S8E-H). 319

Helicobacter colonization after tumor implantation restricts metastasis 321 

322 

Inspired by the microbiome-mediated anti-tumor effects observed in our preclinical models, we 323 

next assessed whether Helicobacter colonization could exert therapeutic benefit when 324 

introduced after tumor establishment. AKPS organoids were implanted into H- mice, and once 325 

tumors were confirmed at day 7, half of the mice were subsequently inoculated with H+ flora. 326 

While H+ colonization after tumor implantation slowed tumor growth by 3 weeks compared to 327 

controls, it did not reduce tumor burden at endpoint (Figure S9A-D) but resulted in a significant 328 

reduction in metastasis incidence (Figure S9E) and improved metastasis-free survival (Figure 329 

S9F) although overall survival was not significantly altered (Figure S9G). These data indicate 330 

that Helicobacter colonization after tumor establishment can restrict metastasis. 331

Augmenting cancer MHC-II expression enhances anti-tumor immunity in autologous 350 

human CRC PDO – immune co-culture system. 351 

352 

Finally, we extended these findings to human MSS CRC using a 3 dimensional (3D) autologous 353 

patient-derived organoid (PDO) – immune cell co-culture system. CIITA overexpression in a 354 

MSS CRC PDO line robustly increased MHC-II expression levels compared to parental WT 355 

PDO line (Figure S10A). Over a 3-day co-culture period, autologous PBMCs exhibited minimal 356 

cytotoxicity toward WT PDOs but efficiently killed CIITA-overexpressing PDOs with elevated 357 

MHC-II expression. To visualize these interactions in real time, we established a confocal live-358 

cell imaging assay using human PDOs co-cultured with autologous PBMCs or autologous CD4+ 359 

T cells (Figure 7G, Figure S10B). Increased tumor-cell MHC-II expression in CIITA-360 

overexpressing PDOs enhanced PBMC colocalization with cancer cells (Figure 7H) and 361 

increased apoptosis in PDOs engaged by immune cells (Figure 7I). CIITA overexpression 362 

similarly increased interactions between PDOs and autologous CD4+ T cells and promoted 363 

apoptosis in cancer cells, although these effects were more modest than those observed with 364 

PBMCs (Figure 7J-K). Together, these findings indicate that increasing cancer-cell MHC-II 365 

expression enhances antitumor immune engagement in human MSS CRC. 366

DISCUSSION 368 

369 

Most deaths from CRC result from metastatic progression, and this clinical burden falls 370 

predominantly on patients with MSS disease, a setting in which checkpoint blockade 371 

immunotherapy has shown limited benefit (Canellas-Socias et al., 2024; Dekker et al., 2019; 372 

Eng et al., 2019; Le et al., 2015). In parallel, studies in human CRC and mouse models suggest 373 

that MSS tumors are not uniformly invisible to the immune system, but instead many fail at an 374 

early step of productive T cell priming and sustained local immune engagement (Galon et al., 375

epithelial antigen-presentation program into this framework by showing that a Helicobacter-377 

containing microbiome induces MHC-II expression in colon cancer cells, enhances intratumoral 378 

T cell accumulation, restrains tumor progression and metastasis, and sensitizes MSS CRC to 379 

immune checkpoint blockade immunotherapy. These findings extend the current models of 380 

microbiota-dependent antitumor immunity beyond effects on T cells, professional antigen-381 

presenting cells, cytokine milieu, systemic anti-tumor immunity and tertiary lymphoid structures 382 

(Baruch et al., 2021; Daillere et al., 2016; Davar et al., 2021; McCulloch et al., 2022; Overacre-383 

Delgoffe et al., 2021; Routy et al., 2018; Sivan et al., 2015; Vetizou et al., 2015). Our results add 384 

a complementary mechanistic layer by showing that microbial signals can act directly on the 385 

malignant epithelial compartment, converting tumor cells into putative antigen presenting cells 386 

with MHC-II expression. 387 

388 

A central implication of our work is that cancer MHC-II is not simply a passive correlate of 389 

inflammatory cytokine exposure, but a causal determinant of antitumor immunity in MSS CRC 390 

(Axelrod et al., 2019; Bou Nasser Eddine et al., 2017; Cho et al., 2021; Johnson et al., 2016; 391 

Rodig et al., 2018). In multiple tumor types, including melanoma, lymphoma, breast cancer, and 392 

endometrial cancer, tumor-intrinsic MHC-II expression has been associated with favorable 393 

immune states, improved clinical outcomes, or increased responsiveness to immunotherapy 394 

(Bou Nasser Eddine et al., 2017; Cho et al., 2021; Chung et al., 2026; Forero et al., 2016; Rodig 395 

et al., 2018). Experimental studies further support functional significance, showing that MHC-II 396 

expression on tumor cells promote anti-tumor immunity, prime naive CD4+ T cells, and that 397 

MHC-II-restricted neoantigens can shape tumor immunity and response to immunotherapy 398 

(Alspach et al., 2019; Beyaz et al., 2021; Bou Nasser Eddine et al., 2017; Kalaora et al., 2021; 399 

Marty Pyke et al., 2018). Consistent with these observations, we find here that deletion of CIITA 400 

in tumor cells blunts microbe-driven enhancement of anti-tumor immunity, whereas enforced 401 

CIITA expression increases intratumoral T cell accumulation, reduces metastasis, and improves 402 

sensitivity to checkpoint blockade immunotherapy even in the absence of the protective 403 

microbiome state. 404 

405 

At the same time, our data reinforce the idea that the functional consequences of tumor-cell 406 

MHC-II expression are context dependent rather than uniformly beneficial across tissues and 407 

disease states (Axelrod et al., 2019). In breast cancer metastasis to lymph nodes, MHC-II-408 

expressing cancer cells can promote immune tolerance and regulatory T cell expansion, 409

415 

Considered together with the companion study by Saad et al., our findings support a model in 416 

which metastasis outcome in CRC is shaped early by the quality of local immune engagement 417 

rather than arising only from late-stage immune collapse (Saad et al., 2026; Westcott et al., 418 

2021). Saad et al. identify divergent early T cell states during orthotopic CRC progression, 419 

whereas our study identifies a microbiome-dependent epithelial mechanism that can bias that 420 

early immune window toward productive tumor control. As such, these two studies are 421 

mechanistically complementary: one defines the T cell trajectories associated with metastatic 422 

competence, whereas the other identifies a microbiome-tumor-cell antigen-presentation axis 423 

that can promote those trajectories toward immune surveillance. 424 

425 

One major unresolved question is the identity of the peptides presented by tumor-cell MHC-II in 426 

this setting. This issue becomes especially interesting in light of work suggesting that antitumor 427 

CD4+ T cell responses can intersect with microbial antigens, bacterial peptide presentation, and 428 

MHC-II neoantigen biology (Alspach et al., 2019; Kalaora et al., 2021; Saad et al., 2026). It 429 

remains unclear whether cancer-cell MHC-II in CRC presents bona fide tumor antigens, mimetic 430 

antigens with structural overlap to microbial epitopes, or a mixed ligand repertoire that allows 431 

tumor immunity to intersect with the preexisting mucosal CD4+ T cell compartment. Resolving 432 

this question will require direct MHC-II immunopeptidomics, paired TCR reconstruction, and 433 

functional validation of candidate ligands across primary tumors, draining lymph nodes, and 434 

metastatic lesions. 435 

436 

Another open question concerns which lymphocyte populations are most important downstream 437 

of the microbiome-MHC-II axis. Our depletion and host-genetics experiments indicate that both 438 

T cells and B cells contribute to the full protective phenotype, and our single-cell analyses point 439 

to enhanced chemotactic and effector programs in both CD4+ and CD8+ compartments 440 

together with expanded B-lineage states in protective microbiome conditions. These 441 

observations are consistent with prior work showing that microbiota-specific T follicular helper 442 

cells and tertiary lymphoid structures can support antitumor immunity in CRC, but they also 443

452 

Our study also has translational implications. Tumor-cell MHC-II may represent both a453 

biomarker and a therapeutic target in MSS CRC. This is consistent with prior studies suggesting 454 

that tumor-intrinsic MHC-II associate with enhanced anti-tumor immunity (Cho et al., 2021; 455 

Chung et al., 2026; Johnson et al., 2016; Rodig et al., 2018; Roemer et al., 2018). Our data 456 

extend this concept by arguing that enforced tumor-cell MHC-II can actively increase MSS CRC 457 

sensitivity to PD-1/CTLA-4 blockade. This raises the possibility that strategies that induce or 458 

stabilize tumor-cell MHC-II, whether through microbial, cytokine, or epigenetic mechanisms, 459 

could be used to convert immune-refractory MSS CRC into a more immunologically responsive 460 

state. 461 

462 

The Helicobacter-containing microbiome in our study should be viewed primarily as a model 463 

system for mechanism discovery rather than as a direct therapeutic target. In our experiments, 464 

single Helicobacter isolates reproduced part of the tumor-control phenotype, but suppression of 465 

metastasis was strongest with the broader transferred community, indicating that cooperative 466 

community effects matter. This point is particularly important because members of the genus 467 

Helicobacter can have divergent effects across mouse models, ranging from antitumor immune 468 

stimulation in CRC models to chronic inflammation and tumor-promoting activity in other 469 

contexts (Fox et al., 2011; Ge et al., 2019; Overacre-Delgoffe et al., 2021), Future work should 470 

therefore identify human microbes, microbial consortia, or microbial products that reproducibly 471 

induce tumor MHC-II and promote antitumor immunity. 472

facilitated by the microbiome. By identifying microbiome-dependent induction of tumor-cell 476
