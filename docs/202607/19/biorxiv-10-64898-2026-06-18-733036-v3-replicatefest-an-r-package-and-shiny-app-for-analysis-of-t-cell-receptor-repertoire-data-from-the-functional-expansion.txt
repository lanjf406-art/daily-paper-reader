Abstract 11 

Motivation : The Functional Expansion of Specific T cell (FEST )-based assays combine short -term 12 

peptide stimulation with TCR sequencing to identify clonotypes that expand in response to specific 13 

antigens. These approaches have proven invaluable for detecting neoantigen -specific T cell 14 

responses, guiding vaccine development, and assessing checkpoint blockade efficacy. However, 15 

variability introduced by biological and technical replicates poses challenges for reproducibility and 16 

interpretation, and existing computational tools do not address replicate -level analysis in these 17 

assays. 18 

Results : We developed replicateFest, a computational framework implemented as an R package and 19 

Shiny web application, to analyze FEST -based TCR -seq data with and without replicates. 20 

replicateFest applies Fisher’s exact test for non -replicate datasets and negative binomial modeling for 21 

replicate experiments, returning adjusted p -values and odds ratios to identify clonotypes significantly 22 

expanded in antigen -stimulated conditions. The framework distinguishes FEST -expanded clonotypes 23 

(relative to a no -antigen control) and FEST -positive clonotypes (expanded compared to all other 24 

conditions). Validation using synthetic datasets confirmed accurate detection of antigen -specific 25 

clonotypes. Application to published HIV -1 epitope stimulation data reproduced original findings 26 

and demonstrated replicateFest’s utility for reproducibility assessment and quality control. 27 

Availability and Implementation : replicateFest is freely available under the Apache -2.0 license as 28 

an R package at  https://github.com/OncologyQS/replicateFest  and as an interactive Shiny application 29 

at  http://www.stat -apps.onc.jhmi.edu/FEST/ .30

1 Introduction 32 

Monitoring antigen -specific T cell responses has become a cornerstone of cancer immunology and 33 

immunotherapy research. The ability to detect and quantify clonal expansion of T cells in response to 34 

tumor -associated or mutation -derived neoantigens provides critical insights into the dynamics of 35 

antitumor immunity and the effectiveness of therapeutic interventions. Traditional approaches, such 36 

as ELISPOT or intracellular cytokine staining, offer functional readouts but lack the resolution to 37 

capture the full diversity and clonality of T cell responses. 38 

Recent advances in high -throughput T cell receptor sequencing (TCR -seq) have transformed our 39 

capacity to characterize T cell repertoires at unprecedented depth. By coupling TCR -seq with antigen 40 

stimulation assays, researchers can identify clonotypes that expand in response to specific peptides, 41 

enabling precise mapping of antigen -specific T cell populations. The Functional Expansion of 42 

Specific T cells (FEST) family of assays exemplifies this approach by integrating short -term peptide 43 

stimulation with TCR -seq to detect antigen -specific clonotypes. These assays use peptides 44 

representing candidate antigens from viruses (ViraFEST), from tumors (tumor -associated antigens, 45 

TAAF EST ), or from mutations (mutation -associated neoantigens, MANAFEST) . The latter was 46 

previously published by our group (1) and included a bioinformatics tool for analysis of FEST -based 47 

TCR -seq data without replicates. We have extended our analysis approach to include FEST designs 48 

using experimental replicates. 49 

Several computational tools have been developed to identify antigen‑responsive TCRs from 50 

stimulation‑based assays by detecting patterns of sequence convergence and clonal enrichment. 51 

ALICE (Antigen‑Led Immune Convergence Engine) (7) operates by statistically identifying TCRs 52 

whose frequency exceeds what would be expected under a generative recombination model, thereby 53 

detecting clonotypes that show significant local enrichment of similar sequences —a hallmark of 54 

antigen‑driven expansion. In contrast, TCRNET (a TCR neighbor enrichment test) (8) employs a 55 

network‑based framework in which each TCR is represented as a node connected to neighbors with 56 

similar CDR3 sequences; antigen‑specific responses are inferred from subnetworks showing 57 

increased connectivity and overrepresentation of related clonotypes following stimulation. 58 

Complementing these approaches, the GLIPH2 algorithm (9) uses motif‑ and global similarity‑based 59 

clustering to group TCRs that share conserved CDR3 patterns associated with common antigen 60 

specificity, while tcrdist3 (10) applies a biologically informed distance metric to build quantitative 61 

similarity maps of TCR repertoires. For single‑cell multi‑omic datasets, TCRscape (11) enables 62 

integrative analysis of full‑length TCR sequences alongside transcriptomic and surface‑protein 63 

profiles, thereby supporting high‑resolution identification and characterization of antigen‑responsive 64 

T cell clones during stimulation assays. While these tools offer complementary strategies for 65 

detecting convergent antigen‑specific TCR signatures and mapping clonal responses to stimulation, 66 

none specifically address replicate -level reproducibility in antigen stimulation assays, highlighting 67 

the need for solutions like replicateFest .68 

In this context, we present replicateFest , a computational framework that includes a Shiny app and an 69 

R package, designed to address these challenges and provide an integrated solution for analyzing data 70 

with replicates in FEST -based TCR -seq studies. This approach enhances the reliability of 71 

immunogenomic data and supports reproducible research in the rapidly evolving field of 72 

immunology and immunotherapy. Additionally, the framework incorporates a previously developed 73 

version for the analysis of FEST -based TCR -seq studies without replicates. 74

2.1 The replicateFest framework 76 

Our analysis framework is designed to analyze TCR -seq data generated from a specific experimental 77 

setup. A typical experiment involved T cells obtained from a patient and cultured in vitro under 78 

multiple antigen conditions. These conditions included stimulation with peptides of interest, such as 79 

mutation -associated neoantigens (MANAs), viral epitopes, or other antigens, as well as a control 80 

condition without peptide to evaluate non -specific T cell expansion. After several days of culture 81 

(depending on the experimental design), DNA is extracted for TCR Vβ CDR3 sequencing. Such 82 

experiments have been previously described in (1,12,13) .83 

The replicateFest data analysis workflow consists of three main steps: importing the FEST -based 84 

TCR -seq data, estimating clone abundance, and filtering the results to select significantly expanded 85 

clones . Users can perform the analysis online using standardized model parameters and filtration 86 

criteria , while a free R package offers the experienced user the ability to customize analysis, 87 

filtration, and output formats. 88 

2.1.1 Importing Data 89 

By default, replicateFest accepts tab -delimited files as exported from the Adaptive Biotechnologies 90 

ImmunoSEQ platform or VDJtools . For the online analysis, file names should follow the format 91 

'sampleID_condition_replicate. tsv ', e .g. , 'sample1_HIV_1.t xt '. During import, templates are 92 

prefiltered to include only productive clonotypes , and nu cleotide sequences translat ing into the same 93 

amino acid sequence are aggregated. T he user has an option to filter out templates with low counts to 94 

reduce the number of clonotypes included in downstream analysis and improve runtime. 95 

2.1.2 Estimating Clone Abundance 96 

Our previously published workflow for unreplicated FEST experiments used odds ratios to estimate 97 

levels of clonal expansion compared to no -peptide or other control, and evaluated significance with 98 

pairwise Fisher's exact test s. In case of data with experimental replicates, we fit a negative binomial 99 

model for each clone to estimate the abundances for all culture conditions simultaneously . The 100 

coefficients in the negative binomial model are interpreted as log odds ratios calculated relative to a 101 

no -pe ptide control. The analysis return s p-values adjusted by Benjamini -Hochberg multiple test 102 

correction (FDR) 103 

2.1.3 Filtering for Significant and Unique Expansions 104 

The software offers several criteria for selecting FEST -expanded clonotypes relative to the reference :105 

(i) expanded in a condition of interest compared with the reference at an FDR less than the specified 106 

threshold (<0.05; default value ), and (ii) hav ing a positive odds ratio above the specified threshold .107 

We also identify a subset of FEST -expanded clonotypes that are expanded in the condition of interest 108 

compared with all other conditions tested in parallel; these are designated FEST -positive clonotypes .109 

Both sets (FEST -expanded and FEST -positive) are saved in output Excel tables, and FEST -positive 110 

clonotypes are visualized in a heatmap. 111

2.2 In silico dataset generation 122 

The replicateFest R package includ es a set of standardized tests based on artific ial data, to ensure the 123 

integrity of the code it self and interactions with dependencies. Briefly, synthetic T -cell receptor 124 

repertoire data were generated by randomly sampling am ino acid CDR3 sequences from the 20 125 

standard residues with lengths between 12 and 16, while nucleotide sequences were generated from 126 

the four canonical bases with lengths between 36 and 45. The background counts of clonotypes were 127 

randomly selected to be around 10 . To simulate peptide -driven clonal expansion, a single clonotype 128 

in each stimulated sample was replaced with a clone that expanded about 100 times from the 129 

background . Details can be found in the R package. 130 

2.3 Analysis of HIV -1 data 131 

We used the T cell sequencing data set with replicates that were previously published in a study of 132 

HIV -1 epitope variants by Chen et al. (13) . The data were downloaded from the Adaptive 133 

Biotechnologies ImmuneACCESS Database,  https://clients.adaptivebiotech.com/pub/chan -2020 -fi .134 

We analyzed the dataset in the R programming environment (v4.6.0) and followed the analysis 135 

performed in the Chen et al. study as closely as possible. We excluded samples with fewer than 5,000 136 

productive reads , leaving 79 samples of 25 peptide conditions and the reference (no peptide) 137 

condition for the downstream analysis. Then w e ran replicateFest (v0.1 .0 ) with the cross -reactive 138 

option enabled, 0.01 for the FDR threshold, and 5 for the OR threshold. The output of the analysis is 139 

presented in the Supplementary Table 1.140 

2.4 Simulation Study 141 

2.4.1 Data Generation 142 

Observed T -cell clone count data were drawn from 79 samples from Chen et al. (13) . We removed 143 

clones found to be FEST -expanded (Supplementary Table 1), as well as clones with fewer than 50 144 

total reads, leaving 351 clones with positive counts but no significant expansion. From the dataset, 145 

we selected 10 peptide conditions with three replicates and the reference (no peptide) condition (n = 146 

33) . For each clone, the mean and variance of counts were calculated across all experimental 147 

conditions (Figure 1), fitting a loess line to model variance as a function of mean. 148 

Simulated clones were generated across a 3 × 3 factorial design defined by three starting frequency 149 

levels (L1, L2, L3; corresponding to the 50th, 75th, and 95th percentiles of the empirical mean 150 

distribution) and three expansion factors (E1 = 2×, E2 = 4×, E3 = 8×) that yielded nine parameter 151 

combinations . The counts were drawn from a negative binomial distribution parameterized by a mean 152 

(𝜇 ) and dispersion (size) parameter, where  size = 𝜇 2 /(𝜎 2 − 𝜇 ). For non -expanded replicates, the 153 

dispersion was derived from the empirical mean –variance relationship at the clone's baseline mean. 154 

For expanded replicates, the dispersion was interpolated using an Akima spline fit to the loess line. 155

Each parameter combination was used to simulate 10 clones, yielding a matrix of 90 simulated clones 156 

(10 clones × 9 combinations ). Each clone was expanded in three replicates of one condition. 157 

Simulated clone counts were appended to the observed background count matrix to produce complete 158 

count matrices. This procedure was repeated 10 times to generate 10 independent simulated datasets. 159 

2.4.2 Application of replicateFest 160 

Each of the 10 simulated datasets was analyzed using replicateFest (v0.1.0) , with the no -peptide 161 

condition (NoPeptide) specified as the reference condition and the default threshold s (FDR < 0.05, 162 

OR > 1) . For 90 simulated clones in each dataset , c lone -level results were tabulated in a 3 × 3 163 

contingency table (frequency level × expansion level), allowing assessment of sensitivity across all 164 

simulated datasets . Detection rates were summarized across 10 simulated datasets as the mean and 165 

standard deviation of the proportion s of correctly identified FEST -expanded clones out of simulated 166 

clones within each cell of the 3 × 3 factorial design (Table 1; Figure 2). 167 

3 Results 168 

3.1 In silico experiment al data 169 

To evaluate the framework in a controlled setting, we generated in silico experimental datasets with 170 

and without replicates (see details in Methods) . Each simulated experiment included five peptide 171 

conditions, with only one clonotype expanded per condition, and one condition without expansion. 172 

replicateFest successfully identified both FEST -expanded and FEST -positive clonotypes in 173 

experiments with and without replicates. These synthetic datasets and corresponding results are 174 

included as test data within the R package. Overall, these results demonstrate that replicateFest 175 

reliably detects clonal expansion under well -defined conditions and provides consistent performance 176 

regardless of the presence of experimental replicates. 177 

3.2 Application to HIV -1 data 178 

We applied our framework to previously published data that was used to identify cross -reactive recall 179 

CD8⁺ T cell clonotypes that respond to autologous HIV -1 epitope variants from the study by Chen et 180 

al (13) . The study demonstrated that memory CD8⁺ T cells can recognize multiple epitope variants, 181 

highlighting the breadth and adaptability of antiviral T cell immunity. By leveraging TCR -seq, t he 182 

authors assessed 25 peptides representing epitope variants of Gag and Nef HIV proteins to evaluate T 183 

cell response to s timuli. The study adapted non -replicated version of the MANAFEST ass ay(12) to 184 

the replicated dataset by applying additional filtering criteria . After applying the criteria, t he authors 185 

reported n ine T cell clon es that significantly expanded in response to stimuli (three clones in 186 

response to Gag epitopes and six clones in response to Nef epitopes ), where five of them were 187 

reported as cross -reactive (Table 1) .188 

To validate reproducibility and assess replicate -level consistency, replicateFest was applied to the 189 

TCR -seq data from th e HIV -1 study , trying to recapitulate the analysis as closely as possible (see 190 

details in Methods) . We ex cluded samples that were excluded in the study, and appli ed similar 191 

threshold s, specifically, for FDR less than 0.01 and OR more than 5 (Supplementary Table 1). We 192 

were able to successfully identify all nine clonotypes reported in the study as FEST -expanded. 193 

Additionally, we identified four out of five cross -reactive clon otypes, and one cross -reactive 194 

clonotype that was not reported as cross -reactive by Chen et all . These minor discrepancies were due 195 

to the different methodology of calling FEST -expanded clonotypes by Chen et al . and replicateFest. 196

3.3 Application to the s imulated data 199 

To evaluate replicateFest performance, we simulated clones with known background levels and 200 

expansion rates from a negative binomial distribution, using empirical count data as a basis for 201 

realistic parameterization (see details in Methods) . We used a 3 × 3 factorial design with three 202 

starting frequency levels (L1, L2, L3; corresponding to the 50th, 75th, and 95th percentiles of the 203 

empirical mean distribution) and three expansion factors (E1 = 2×, E2 = 4×, E3 = 8×). We generated 204 

10 simulated datasets with 90 expanded clonotypes corresponding to nine combinations of the 205 

factorial design (ten clonotypes for each combination) , and analyzed these datasets with replicateFest 206 

under default parameters . After that, we evaluated how many clonotypes out of 90 were FEST -207 

expanded across 10 simulated datasets (Figure 2) and calculated the mean and standard deviation of 208 

the detection rate . At the lowest expansion level (E1, 2×), detection was near zero across all starting 209 

frequency levels, indicating that 2 -fold expansions fall below the method's detection threshold under 210 

the simulated conditions. At moderate expansion (E2, 4×), sensitivity increased substantially with 211 

starting clone frequency, ranging from 0.42 ± 0.10 at L1 to 0.92 ± 0.10 at L3, with the greatest 212 

variability across replicates observed in this regime. At the highest expansion level (E3, 8×), 213 

detection was near -perfect regardless of starting frequency (≥ 0.98 in all cases), with no variability at 214 

L3. Overall, these results indicate that replicateFest exhibits a clear dependence on both clonal 215 

abundance and expansion magnitude, with robust detection achieved primarily at higher expansion 216 

levels and frequencies, thereby defining the practical sensitivity limits of the method under realistic 217 

repertoire conditions. 218

4 Discussion 220 

We introduce replicateFest , a computational framework comprising an R package and an interactive 221 

Shiny application, designed to analyz e FEST -based TCR -seq data. By supporting both replicate and 222 

non -replicate experimental designs, replicateFest provides an integrated solution for identifying 223 

antigen -specific clonotype expansions with appropriate statistical methods. This framework improves 224 

the reliability of immunogenomic analyses and promotes reproducible research in the rapidly 225 

advancing fields of immunology and cancer immunotherapy. 226 

The ability to accurately monitor antigen -specific T cell responses is critical for understanding 227 

antitumor immunity and guiding immunotherapy strategies. FEST -based assays, including 228 

MANAFEST, have enabled sensitive detection of clonotype expansions following antigen 229 

stimulation by leveraging TCR -seq. However, the complexity of TCR repertoires and variability 230 

introduced by biological and technical replicates present significant challenges for reproducibility 231 

and interpretation. replicateFest addresses these challenges by providing a unified computational 232 

framework for analyzing FEST -based TCR -seq data. By incorporating statistical models tailored to 233 

experimental design – Fisher’s exact test for single -replicate data and negative binomial modeling for 234 

replicate datasets – replicateFest ensures robust identification of antigen -specific clonotypes. 235 

Successful testing on the in silico datasets and validation on the previously published HIV -1 epitope 236 

stimulation data demonstrate that replicateFest reliably identifies expanded clonotypes and 237 

reproduce s findings from complex experimental designs. 238

Additionally, t he simulation results provide important insights into the sensitivity and operational 239 

limits of the replicateFest framework under realistic repertoire conditions. Detection performance 240 

was strongly influenced by both the baseline abundance of clonotypes and the magnitude of 241 

expansion, consistent with theoretical expectations for count -based sequencing data. In particular, the 242 

near absence of detection at 2 -fold expansion indicates that modest antigen -driven changes may be 243 

difficult to distinguish from background variability, especially for low -frequency clones. In contrast, 244 

the marked improvement at 4 -fold expansion and the near -perfect detection observed at 8 -fold 245 

expansion demonstrate that replicateFest is highly effective in identifying robust antigen -specific 246 

signals when clonal expansion exceeds the noise threshold inherent to TCR -seq data. The increased 247 

variability observed at intermediate expansion levels further highlights the stochastic nature of 248 

sampling and the importance of replicate -aware modeling. Collectively, these findings emphasize 249 

that both clonal frequency and expansion magnitude are critical determinants of detection power and 250 

underscore the value of incorporating experimental replicates to improve confidence in identifying 251 

true antigen -responsive T cell populations. 252 

Compared to existing TCR repertoire analysis tools such as MiXCR, immunarch, and tcrdist3, 253 

replicateFest fills a unique gap by focusing on replicate -level concordance in antigen stimulation 254 

assays. This functionality is particularly relevant for studies evaluating neoantigen -specific 255 

responses, vaccine efficacy, or cross -reactivity, where reproducibility is essential for downstream 256 

clinical and translational applications. 257 

Future development will focus on expanding compatibility with single -cell TCR -seq data, integrating 258 

paired α/β chain analysis, and implementing automated thresholds for replicate concordance. By 259 

addressing these needs, replicateFest will continue to support rigorous and reproducible analysis in 260 

the rapidly evolving field of cancer immunology and beyond. 261
