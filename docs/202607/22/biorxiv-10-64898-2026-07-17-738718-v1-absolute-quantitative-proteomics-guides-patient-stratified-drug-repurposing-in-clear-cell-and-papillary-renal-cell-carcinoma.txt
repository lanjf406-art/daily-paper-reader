Background 35 

Renal cell carcinoma (RCC) is a highly heterogeneous disease in which distinct molecular 36 

subtypes exhibit characteristic genomic, metabolic, and microenvironmental features that 37 

influence therapeutic response. Substantial inter-patient variability exists within each 38 

subtype, resulting in markedly different clinical outcomes even among tumours of the same 39 

histological category. Proteomics provides a direct readout of tumour biology and pathway 40 

activity, complementing genomic information and enabling the identification of patient-41 

specific actionable vulnerabilities. We applied a Total Protein Approach (TPA)-based 42 

prescriptomics framework that integrates absolute quantitative proteomics with curated 43 

drug–target knowledge to nominate patient-specific drug-repurposing options, positioned as 44 

coadjuvants to the prevailing standard of care. 45 

Methods 46 

Seventeen human kidney tissue specimens, seven clear cell RCC (ccRCC), five papillary 47 

RCC (pRCC), and five normal adjacent tissues (NAT), were retrieved from the publicly 48 

available PRIDE repository (PXD023296) and reanalysed by TPA-based absolute 49 

quantification applied to previously acquired label-free LC-MS/MS data. Differential 50 

expression analysis between each tumour subtype and NAT identified subtype-specific 51 

upregulated proteins, wich were intersected with Therapeutic Target Database (TTD) to 52 

nominate FDA-approved drugs targeting dysregulated proteins as candidate repurposing 53 

strategies. 54 

Results 55 

ccRCC and pRCC produced distinct proteome-wide upregulation profiles consistent with 56 

their known biological drivers. TPA index stratification nominated bempedoic acid (ACLY 57 

inhibitor) and tipiracil hydrochloride (TYMP inhibitor) as patient-stratified candidates for 58 

ccRCC, and auranofin (TXNRD1 inhibitor), bempedoic acid, and mipomersen (APOB-59 

directed antisense oligonucleotide) for pRCC. ACLY was the only top-priority target shared 60 

across both subtypes, pointing to a candidate cross-subtype metabolic vulnerability. 61 

Secondary candidates emerged from protein–protein interaction network analysis in both 62 

subtypes.. 63 

Conclusions 64 

This study presents a quantitative proteomics framework for translating individual-patient 65 

proteomic dysregulation into coadjuvant drug-repurposing hypotheses across the principal 66 

RCC subtypes. By combining the TPA for absolute protein quantification with prescriptomics-67 

guided drug–target mapping, we show that ccRCC and pRCC harbour distinct, individually 68

# Introduction 84 

Renal cell carcinoma (RCC) represents approximately 3% of adult malignancies and is the 85 

most common type of kidney cancer, arising from the renal tubular epithelium 1,2 . RCC 86 

comprises a heterogeneous group of tumours with distinct histopathological features, genetic 87 

alterations, biological behaviour and therapeutic responsiveness 3 . Its principal subtypes, 88 

clear cell RCC (ccRCC) and papillary RCC (pRCC) differ in molecular drivers, clinical course 89 

and treatment sensitivity, yet both are managed by histological category rather than 90 

individual tumour biology 3,4 .91 

Clear cell RCC is the most prevalent subtype, accounting for 70–75% of RCC cases, and is 92 

defined histologically by cells with clear cytoplasm due to glycogen and lipid accumulation 5 .93 

The molecular hallmark of ccRCC is inactivation of the Von Hippel–Lindau (VHL) tumour 94 

suppressor gene, observed in up to 90% of cases, leading to stabilisation of hypoxia-95 

inducible factors (HIF-1 α and HIF-2 α) and upregulation of angiogenic and metabolic 96 

programmes, including VEGF, PDGF and CAIX 6. Treatment of advanced ccRCC has 97 

evolved considerably over the past two decades, from tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs) 98 

targeting VEGFR, exemplified by sunitinib 7 , to single-agent immune checkpoint inhibitors 99 

(ICIs) such as nivolumab 8 , and more recently to dual PD-1/CTLA-4 blockade with nivolumab 100 

plus ipilimumab 9 and to first-line Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors (ICI)–TKI combinations 101 

including pembrolizumab plus axitinib 10 and nivolumab plus cabozantinib 11 .102

Adjuvant TKI therapy has also been explored in resected high-risk disease 12 . Although these 103 

regimens extend progression-free and overall survival, response remains heterogeneous, 104 

and most patients ultimately progress, underscoring the need for molecularly guided 105 

personalisation 13 .106 

Papillary RCC accounts for approximately 10–20% of RCCs. Historically subdivided into type 107 

1 and type 2, papillary RCC is now recognised as a heterogeneous entity, and the 2022 108 

WHO classification no longer recommends this simple binary subtyping. Instead, several 109 

tumours previously grouped within type 2 papillary RCC are now classified as distinct 110 

entities, including FH-deficient RCC, a molecularly defined and aggressive renal carcinoma 111 

frequently associated with hereditary leiomyomatosis and RCC (HLRCC) 4,14 . Tumours 112 

historically classified as type 1 papillary RCC are generally less aggressive and are enriched 113 

for Mesenchymal-epithelial transition (MET) pathway activation through mutation, 114 

amplification or chromosomal gains 14 . MET inhibitors such as savolitinib have demonstrated 115 

clinical activity in MET-driven papillary RCC 15 . Fumarase hydratase (FH)-deficient RCC is 116 

characterised by fumarate accumulation, pseudohypoxic signalling, metabolic rewiring and 117 

epigenetic dysregulation 16–18 .118 

Proteomics-based analysis enables functional characterisation of RCC beyond genomics: 119 

unlike DNA or RNA profiling, proteomics captures dynamic features such as protein 120 

expression levels, post-translational modifications and pathway activity 19 . Several studies 121 

have combined high-resolution mass spectrometry with the Total Protein Approach (TPA) to 122 

identify novel biomarkers and druggable targets in RCC 20,21 . The TPA enables label- and 123 

standard-free estimation of absolute protein abundances by normalising each protein’s 124 

signal to the total proteome signal, making it cost-effective and scalable for large or 125 

exploratory studies 22,23 . Its accuracy, however, depends on comprehensive proteome 126 

coverage, and missing or poorly detected proteins can bias normalisation 22,23 . Inter-127 

laboratory variability and differences in digestion, ionisation and instrument response may 128 

impair comparability, often requiring complementary strategies such as reference protein 129 

normalisation 23,24 For applications demanding precise absolute quantification, isotope-130 

labelled standards or validated immunoassays remain preferable 24 . Nonetheless, TPA 131 

performs well for biomarker discovery and longitudinal analyses when carefully 132 

applied 20,21,25 . For instance, TPA-based profiling identified subtype-specific protein 133 

signatures, PLIN2 for ccRCC and TUBB3 for pRCC, that were subsequently validated by 134 

immunohistochemistry 21 .135 

Prescriptomics, the integration of omics-derived molecular profiles with curated drug–target 136 

knowledge to nominate patient-specific therapies, has emerged as a promising precision 137 

oncology concept 26 . Drug repurposing offers a pragmatic route to expand cancer 138

can shorten development timelines and reduce costs relative to de novo drug discovery 26 . By 140 

integrating TPA-based proteomics with curated drug–target databases, differentially 141 

expressed proteins can be mapped to clinically approved drugs or investigational 142 

compounds, enabling patient-level therapeutic prioritisation 27 . For example, in ccRCC, 143 

activation of hypoxia, angiogenic, glycolytic and PI3K/AKT/mTOR-related programmes 144 

provides a molecular rationale for targeting VEGFR- and mTOR-associated pathways 28 . In 145 

MET-driven pRCC, integrative molecular profiling of the TCGA papillary cohort supports the 146 

mechanistic basis for MET-targeted therapy 29 . In FH-deficient tumours, fumarate 147 

accumulation, redox imbalance and metabolic rewiring suggest potential vulnerabilities 148 

involving mitochondrial metabolism, oxidative stress and related adaptive pathways 30,31 .149 

Resources such as the Therapeutic Target Database (TTD) 27 and large-scale 150 

pharmacogenomic resources such as the Genomics of Drug Sensitivity in Cancer 151 

database 32 complement this strategy by linking molecular alterations to candidate 152 

therapeutic agents. This is particularly relevant in RCC, where pronounced intratumour 153 

heterogeneity and resistance to single-agent therapies limit the durability of monotherapy 33 .154 

In this work, we applied a TPA-based prescriptomics framework to 17 human kidney tissue 155 

specimens spanning the main principal RCC subtypes, ccRCC and pRCC, together with 156 

normal adjacent tissue (NAT). Our work presents two main contributions. First, we use 157 

absolute, rather than relative, protein quantification to rank candidate drug targets, enabling 158 

within-patient prioritisation via a TPA-based index (TPA index ). Second, we perform a159 

personalisation at the patient level, rather than the subtype level, so that two patients with 160 

identical histological diagnoses may receive distinct nominated therapies. Together, these 161 

features establish a proof of concept for proteomics-guided basis for individualised drug-162 

repurposing hypotheses in RCC and a template that could be extended to other 163 

heterogeneous malignancies. 164

Differential protein expression in pRCC 279 

Volcano plot analysis identified 470 upregulated and 347 downregulated proteins in pRCC 280 

relative to NAT (Fig. 3a). Intersecting the upregulated proteome with the Therapeutic Target 281 

Database identified eight targetable proteins associated with FDA-approved drugs (Fig. 3b), 282

TPA-derived abundance, particularly in patients 2, 4 and 5, thereby defining the largest 285 

target-based subgroup. TXNRD1 and APOB also displayed markedly increased absolute 286

patient 3, respectively. Thus, the five pRCC cases were stratified into three principal 288 

molecular profiles: TXNRD1-high, ACLY-high, and APOB-high. Among the remaining 289 

targetable proteins, LDHA and PARP1 showed the fewest TPA index  values above the 290

abundance in subsets of patients, supporting their prioritisation as secondary or tertiary 292 

therapeutic options. Network-based contextualisation of the top-ranked targetable proteins 293 

using STRING and Cytoscape revealed coherent pathway structures (Fig. 3d–f). TXNRD1-294 

associated networks were enriched for cellular response to stress, nuclear events mediated 295 

by NFE2L2 and ferroptosis (Fig. 3d). ACLY-associated networks were primarily linked to 296 

altered metabolic processes, including fatty-acid metabolism and the citrate cycle (Fig. 3e). 297

remodelling, and clearance, cholesterol metabolism, and fatty acids and lipoproteins 299 

transport (Fig. 3f). As observed for ccRCC, these protein- and pathway-level findings were 300 

integrated into the proposed sequential treatment framework for pRCC (Fig. 4), in which first-301 

to third-line therapeutic options are prioritised according to patient-specific TPA index  values. 302

# Discussion 322 

Applied across the two principal RCC subtypes, the TPA-based prescriptomics framework 323 

generated, patient-stratified therapeutic candidates from a single label-free LC–MS/MS 324 

experiment, without isotope labels, spike-in standards, or tumour-type-specific prior 325 

knowledge. In ccRCC and pRCC, proteome-wide upregulation yielded sufficient druggable 326 

signal for target-centric prioritisation via TTD intersection. 327 

In ccRCC, our TPA index stratification highlighted two dominant, patient-separable 328 

signatures centred on ACLY and TYMP (Fig. 4). ACLY-high tumours showed enrichment of 329 

fatty acid metabolism and the citrate cycle, consistent with the oncogenic role of ACLY in 330 

cancer metabolism 38,39 . In this context, bempedoic acid, a first-in-class, oral ACLY inhibitor 331 

approved for hypercholesterolaemia and atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease 40,41 ,332 

emerged as a plausible candidate for experimental evaluation, given its potential to reduce 333 

cytosolic acetyl-CoA availability and thereby constrain lipogenic output. Because bempedoic 334 

acid is a prodrug requiring activation by very-long-chain acyl-CoA synthetase 1335 

(SLC27A2/ACSVL1), whose expression is highest in hepatocytes, tumour-specific target 336 

engagement cannot be assumed and should be confirmed in ccRCC models prior to 337 

therapeutic interpretation, ideally by direct measurement of cytosolic acetyl-CoA depletion 338 

and lipogenic flux. Protein SLC27A2 is present in our ccRCC proteomics datasets. 339 

TYMP-high tumours clustered into networks associated with nucleotide metabolism, 340 

suggesting increased nucleoside turnover and salvage activity in ccRCC cells 42 . This is 341 

consistent with the broader metabolic reprogramming of ccRCC, in which tumour cells rely 342 

on altered metabolic pathways to sustain proliferation and survival 43,44 . In addition, TYMP 343 

has been reported to promote angiogenesis, inhibit apoptosis and modulate epigenetic 344 

regulation, and is overexpressed in several cancer types, including ccRCC 45 . Accordingly, 345 

tipiracil hydrochloride, a potent and selective TYMP inhibitor and component of 346 

trifluridine/tipiracil (TAS-102), which is approved for colorectal and gastric cancer, may 347 

represent a hypothesis-generating approach to limit ccRCC growth, although this requires 348 

dedicated functional validation 46 . Beyond these phenotype-defining candidates, VWF, LDHA, 349 

and TUBB supported exploratory secondary opportunities. VWF is a multimeric plasma 350 

glycoprotein that mediates platelet adhesion to the endothelium and participates in 351 

haemostasis and coagulation-related pathways 47 . Its elevated expression in several cancers 352

Among the tertiary-priority candidates, LDHA and TUBB emerged as the least compelling 357 

opportunities in our ccRCC cohort. LDHA is linked to glycolytic reprogramming, but the only 358 

FDA-approved LDHA-directed therapeutic, nedosiran, is a GalNAc-conjugated small 359

hyperoxaluria type 1 52 ; its biodistribution is deliberately restricted to hepatocytes, so direct 361

supporting the view that this is a broader, pan-RCC metabolic feature 54–56 . ACLY emerged 367 

as a recurrent metabolic hub, being the protein with the highest overexpression in 60% of 368 

patients, consistent with reports showing that papillary renal tumours display increased 369 

mitochondrial activity and altered in situ metabolic characteristics and intracellular lipid 370 

features in pRCC 57,58 . Importantly, ACLY is the only top-priority target shared between 371 

ccRCC and pRCC in our data, positioning it as a candidate cross-subtype vulnerability and 372 

raising the possibility that an ACLY-directed strategy could be evaluated in a histology-373 

agnostic, metabolism-defined subset of renal tumours, subject to the tissue-activation caveat 374 

described above. 375

nuclear events and ferroptosis 58,59 . This supported prioritisation of auranofin, an oral gold-377 

containing drug indicated for rheumatoid arthritis 60 , as a hypothesis-generating candidate for 378 

TXNRD1-high pRCC. Mechanistically, auranofin has been reported to disrupt redox 379 

homeostasis and promote reactive oxygen species accumulation through TXNRD-related 380 

pathways 61 . In addition, auranofin combined with gemcitabine-based chemotherapy has 381 

been reported to enhance antitumour activity in pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma 62 ,382 

suggesting that similar redox-directed strategies may merit evaluation in TXNRD1-high 383 

pRCC. In APOB-high pRCC cases, enrichment analysis highlighted cholesterol, fatty acid 384

lipid handling. Mipomersen, a liver-targeted second-generation antisense oligonucleotide 386

Integrating both RCC subtypes into a unified, top-three priority ranking (Fig. 4) provides a 401 

proof-of-concept for how proteomics-guided prescriptomics could be incorporated into future 402 

precision oncology workflows. By prioritising candidates according to TPA index  and 403 

anchoring each nomination within its dominant pathway context, this framework moves 404 

beyond subtype-level suggestions towards patient-stratified therapeutic hypotheses. 405 

Crucially, these repurposing candidates are positioned as personalised coadjuvants to the 406 

prevailing standard of care, not as replacements for it, so that the clinician retains the current 407 

disease-appropriate backbone (ICI–TKI or MET-directed therapy for advanced ccRCC/pRCC 408 

as indicated) while gaining a molecularly justified, patient-specific add-on. These rankings 409 

are hypothesis-generating and require systematic functional validation, including target 410 

engagement and efficacy testing in appropriate preclinical models, before any clinical 411 

translation. Two of the therapeutic candidates, nedosiran and mipomersen, are GalNAc- or 412 

liver-restricted oligonucleotide therapeutics with negligible renal biodistribution, so their 413 

inclusion should be read as conceptual validation of the LDHA- and APOB-silencing axes 414 

rather than as direct repurposing opportunities; successful translation will require liver-415 

sparing delivery systems. Bempedoic acid requires SLC27A2/ACSVL1-mediated activation, 416 

so the tumour-tissue expression of this activating enzyme should be confirmed before 417 

predicting ACLY engagement in renal tumours. Our approach should be interpreted in the 418 

context of the published proteogenomic RCC literature. The Clinical Proteomic Tumor 419 

Analysis Consortium (CPTAC) and related international efforts have profiled large cohorts 420 

spanning the principal and rare RCC subtypes by integrative proteogenomics. Clark et al. 421 

established the first comprehensive CPTAC clear cell RCC proteogenomic landscape, 422 

linking PBRM1/BAP1 status, immune infiltration patterns and metabolic rewiring to candidate 423 

therapeutic vulnerabilities 70 . Li et al. subsequently extended this framework to ccRCC 424

# Conclusions 451 

This work establishes the TPA as a practical absolute-quantitation engine for personalised 452 

drug repurposing in renal cell carcinoma. By converting the per-patient proteome into a 453 

> TPA index

ranking and intersecting the resulting upregulated proteins with curated drug– 454 

target knowledge, the framework translates individual tumour biology, rather than histological 455 

subtype alone, into a structured shortlist of FDA-approved compounds positioned as 456 

molecularly justified coadjuvants to the prevailing standard of care. The same workflow 457 

accommodates a target-centric mode when an actionable protein crosses the prioritisation 458 

threshold and a pathway-centric mode when none does, so it remains usable across the 459 

heterogeneous biology of the principal RCC subtypes. Applied to 17 patient biopsies, the 460 

framework recovers biologically coherent and patient-specific drug nominations. In ccRCC, 461 

ACLY-high and TYMP-high patient subgroups support bempedoic acid and tipiracil 462 

hydrochloride, respectively, with VWF, LDHA, and TUBB as exploratory secondary options. 463 

In pRCC, the same TPA-driven prioritisation separates TXNRD1-high, ACLY-high and 464 

APOB-high patients, mapping onto auranofin, bempedoic acid and mipomersen as primary 465 

candidates. Each nomination is anchored to a measurable, patient-specific abundance 466 

changes rather than to a population average. These results constitute a proof of concept 467 

rather than a clinical recommendation. 468
