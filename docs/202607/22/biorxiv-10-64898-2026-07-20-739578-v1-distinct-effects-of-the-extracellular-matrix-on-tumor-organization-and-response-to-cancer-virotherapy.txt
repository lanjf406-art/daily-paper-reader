Abstract 15 

Extracellular matrix (ECM) regulates tumor architecture and immune accessibility, but its impact 16 

on virus -based anti cancer therapies is not well understood. In particular, ECM modulation leads 17 

to both enhanced and impaired therapeutic outcomes in vivo , reflecting the complexity of matrix -18 

immune -vi rus interactions. Here , we combine computational modeling with empirical 3D tumor 19 

spheroid -immune in vitro co -cultures to examine how ECM composition and abundance shape an 20 

Alphavirus replicon -based therapy. We show that ECM -induced changes in tumor sphero id 21 

architecture vary by cell -adhesion phenotype but higher matrix abundance consistently reduces 22 

cellular density. Notably , increasing abundance of basement membrane extract or Collagen ‑I23 

reduce d tumor spheroid cell density, which generally enhance d viral infection, T cell activation, 24 

and IFNγ production. However, reduced immune accessibility in collagen -rich environments 25 

suppresse d immune responses despite improved infection, highlighting matrix -specific effects. 26 

Our findings provide insights into E CM's role in virus -based therapies and propose a bottom -up 27 

framework for systematically evaluating its influence on therapeutic efficacy. 28

Introduction 29 

Virus -based therapies show promise in cancer treatment by infecting tumor cells and stimulating 30 

antitumo r immune response s. However, their efficacy depends on optimal function within a tumor 31 

microenvironment shaped by extensive extracellular matrix (ECM) remodeling 1,2 . The specific 32 

ways in which EC M properties influence viral infection and immune activation remain insufficiently 33 

defined .34 

The ECM is an important acellular component of the tumor microenvironment , providing 35 

mechanical support while contributing to biochemical signaling that drives can cer progression 3,4 .36 

In tumor s, the ECM undergoes struc tural and compositional changes, including increased 37 

deposition and cross -linking of fibrillar proteins such as Collagen ‑I and fibronectin. These changes 38 

generate a matrix that is stiffer, denser , with marked region -to -region heterogeneity. Such 39 

variabilit y leads to formation of micro -niches that differently influence immune evasion and tumor 40 

progression. These ECM -related features have direct implication s for virus -based therap ies since 41 

its efficacy depends on effective viral infection and subsequent immune activation. In addition to 42 

structural effects, ECM components and associated signaling pathways can directly influence viral 43 

infection and tumor cell behavior, including integri n-mediated viral entry and matrix -regulated 44 

signaling cascades 5,6 . Studies so far have demonstrated that a dens e or highly cross -linked matrix 45 

can act as a physical barrier, limiting viral penetration into the tumor core and restricting immune 46 

cell access to infected regions 7,8 . Moreover, ECM composition can modulate antigen presentation 47 

and immune signaling, thereby influencing the magnitude and quality of anti tumor immunity. For 48 

example, collagen -rich tumor s have been sho wn to hinder T -cell migration, while altered ECM 49 

organization can reshape dendritic cell activation and cytokine production 9,10 .50 

Despite these observations, much of the current understanding o f ECM -virotherapy 51 

interaction derives from in vivo tumor models, where ECM properties are difficult to control or 52

Immunotherapy Model (SAMI), implemented in R (v4.3.0) 35  to simulate the interaction between 174 

rSFV -viral infection (modeled as self -amplifying sa -mRNA expression) , cancer cells and T cell 175

leading to tumor -killing. Each simulation tracked EGFP expression at a single -cell resolution, its 178 

modulation by microenviro nmental factors, and T cell -mediated cytotoxicity. Simulations were run 179 

with hourly timesteps covering a 24 hour s window to capture EGFP kinetics and immune 180 

interactions over time. Please refer to Supplementary table 1 for model parameters and details 181 

of t he simulations. 182

Results 233 

Modeling cancer cell organization in the presence of extracellular matrix 234 

We studied how the presence, abundance and type of extracellular matrix (ECM) changes 235 

organization of cancer cells in three dimensional space using spheroid -based culture system 236 

(Fig2A) . Cultivation of 22Rv1 cancer cells in basement me mbrane extract (BME) or Collagen -I237 

matrix led to decrease in spheroid density with increasing matrix abundance (Fig2B -C) . The 238 

density remained unchanged over the observed time -period of 24 hours (Fig2D) . Of note, ECM -239 

driven reduction in cell density was mo re pronounced in BME -embedded cultures compared to 240 

those in Collagen ‑I. Cells in BME formed sparsely organized clumps, whereas Collagen ‑I241 

supported the formation of a larger, though less dense spheroid structure ( Fig2B ). 242 

A cell -type dependent effect was o bserved at lower matrix abundance (1%), with an 243 

increase in cancer cell density for PANC -1 and Ca -Ski tumor spheroids (Fig2E and 244 

Supplementary FigS1 ). However, higher matrix abundance (10%) generally decreases density 245 

across multiple cancer cel l lines ( Fig 2E and Supplementary FigS1 ). To explore why 22RV1 and 246 

PANC ‑1 cells display distinct cell -organization and density patterns, we assessed differences in 247 

adhesion ‑related gene expression patterns. Expression of adhesion molecules differed between 248 

cell types a t baseline, where PANC -1 cells expressed more cell -to -matrix adhesion related 249 

molecules, whereas 22Rv1 expressed more cell -to -cell adhesion related molecules (Fig2F) . Using 250 

these insights, we employed an agent -based modeling approach to simulate virtual tu mor 251 

spheroids of different densities (Fig2G) maintaining their density over time (Fig2H) .252

in cell organization on viral infection in the spheroid -based culture system. Cancer cells embedded 270

Incucyte -based real -time microscopy imaging ( Fig3B ). An i ncrease in the frequency of EGFP+ 272 

cells was observed in 22Rv1 tumor spheroids with lower density ( Fig3C -D). Presence of either 273

embedding (Fig3 D).275

Quantifying T-cell activation in the presence of extracellular matrix 303 

We cocultured rSFV -EGFP -infected cancer cells embedded in BME and Collagen ‑I matrices with 304 

HLA -matched PBMCs for 24 hours and quantified T cell activation through flow cytometry (Fig 4A) .305 

Activation was defined as expression of both CD69 and CD107a by helper (CD4+) and cytotoxic 306 

(CD8+) T cells. CD4+T cell activation did not correl ate with changes in cell density, whereas 307 

CD8+T cell activation increased significantly as density decreased (Fig4B) . Cocultures in 308 

presence of 10% BME showed higher CD4+ and CD8+ T cell activation than non ‑embedded 309 

controls. In contrast, embedding in 10% Collagen ‑I produced fewer or no changes in CD4+ T cell -310 

activation and a significant reduction in CD8+ T cell -activation (Fig4C) . These results were 311 

observed across different target tumor cells (Supplementary Fig S3) and were independent of the 312 

rSFV -infectio n or transgene expression (Fig4D and Supplementary FigS3 ), suggesting direct 313 

ECM -mediated effects. 314 

To study antigen ‑specific T cell responses, we cocultured infected ECM ‑embedded cancer 315 

cells expressing MAGE antigen along with CD8+ T cells expressing the corresponding cognate -316 

TCR ‑4F7 receptor (Fig4E) . IFN γ spot ‑forming units, as a measure of T cell activation, increased 317 

as cell density decreased (Fig4F) . Embedding in 10% BME enhanced IFNγ secretion relative to 318 

non ‑embedded cocultures, whereas embedding in 10% Collagen ‑I markedly reduced cytokine 319 

production compared to BME ‑embedded samples (Fig4G) . Again, these effects were independent 320 

of rSFV -infection and were observed across different target tumor cells (Fig4H) .321

Agent -based modelling of the effect of cell organization on T -cell responses 364 

To evaluate how ECM -driven changes in tumor cel l organization influences T ‑cell activation, and 365 

to understand why T ‑cell activation shows weaker correlations with cell -density than infection, we 366 

extended o ur agent ‑based modelling framework to incorporate T -cells. Here, we simulated T ‑cell 367 

infiltration and killing to determine how spatial constraints modulate antitumor immune efficacy. In 368 

particular, we studied the effect of how T -cell migration and killing across different distances 369 

influences T -cell activation and cancer cell survival in the model. As illustrated in Fig6A , a shorter 370 

T-cell killing radius ( Kr), e.g. Kr=10 would lead to killing of 3 target cells, whereas a longer radius, 371 

e.g. Kr=50 would lead to killing of 7 target cells. 372 

Across tumor spheroids of different radii, cancer cell death was distributed 373 

heterogeneously, with killing occurring more often at the periphery in larger, less dense spheroids 374 

(Fig6B) . The frequency of activated T cells per spheroid increased with increasing killing radius 375 

and increasing local neighbor density (Fig6C) , indicating that densely packed regions provide 376 

more accessible targets for serial killing. Similarly, increasing the T ‑cell killing radius led to efficient 377 

killing across tumor spheroids of different densities (Fig6D) . However, at a single cell resolution, 378 

we observed that the killing efficacy of a T -cell decreased with an increasing number of 379 

neighboring cancer cells (Fig6E) correlating with a reduction in overall tumor cell killing (Fig6F) .380 

Together, these simulation s suggest that the effect of cancer cell organization on T ‑cell 381 

activation is partially buffered by spatial limitations on T ‑cell access and killing, explaining the more 382 

modest experimental effects observed. 383

Discussion 392 

In this study, we set out to understand how the extracellular matrix shapes the efficacy of 393 

anticancer virotherapy by regulating cancer cell organization, viral infection, and T ‑cell activation. 394 

ECM remodeling is a hallmark of tumor progression and has com plex effects on therapeutic 395 

resistance and efficay 3,4 . While the influence of ECM -degradation during cancer virotherapy is 396 

explored 39 –41 , its exact influence in viral infection and immune activation remain less defined. By 397 

combining a controlled 3D in vitro system with computational modelling, we show how matrix 398 

composition and abundance modulate the spatial arrangement of cancer cells and, in turn, th e399 

dynamics of viral infection and immune activation. 400 

Our results demonstrate that ECM abundance reorganizes tumor spheroids , thereby 401 

directly influenc ing infection kinetics. Increasing concentrations of BME or Collagen ‑I reduced cell 402 

density, but the magni tude and direction of this effect depended on both matrix type and the 403 

intrinsic adhesion phenotype of the cancer cells. BME, a softer and less structured matrix, induced 404 

pronounced cell -dispersion, whereas the stiffer and more organized Collagen ‑I produce d more 405 

compact tumor spheroid structures. These observations are consistent with prior work showing 406 

that ECM stiffness and composition regulate tumor architecture and cell organization 4,42 .407 

Simult aneously, t ranscriptomic analysis revealed that embedding different cancer cells in the 408 

same matrix can lead to distinct cell organization depending on the distinct adhesion phenotypes :409 

PANC ‑1 cells, which express high levels of cell ‑to ‑matrix adhesion gen es, compact at low BME 410 

levels before dispersing, while 22Rv1 cells, which rely more on cell ‑to ‑cell adhesion, disperse 411 

more uniformly. These findings highlight that ECM abundance and cancer cell adhesion jointly 412 

determine tumor architecture. 413 

Cell reorganiz ation had a direct impact on viral infection. Lower ‑density spheroids exhibited 414 

higher infection frequencies across cancer cell types . Our computational model recapitulated 415

T‑cell activation followed similar but less pronounced trends with respect to cell density . In 430 

22Rv1 spheroids, lower cell density and BME embedding enhanced CD8 +T cell activation and 431 

IFNγ secretion, whereas Collagen ‑I reduced both responses, even below levels observed without 432 

matrix. These differences likely reflect the impact of environmental stiffness on T ‑cell motility and 433 

activation, as rigid matrices , like Collagen ‑I, can restrict immune cell infiltration and 434 

proliferation 9,47,48 . H owever, the weaker correlation between cell density and T ‑cell activation 435 

compared to infection suggested that additional constraints shape immune responses. In depth 436 

T-cell phenotyping supported this interpretation , while matrix presence increased markers of 437 

motility and activation, exhaustion and differentiation signatures diverged sharply by matrix type. 438 

Notably , Collagen ‑I but not BME, increased exhaustion marker expression as noted before 10 ,439 

indicating that ECM composition modulates T ‑cell functional states independently of infection 440

Finally, o ur computational modelling provided mechanistic insight into these observations. 444 

While viral infection was highly sensitive to spatial organization, T ‑cell killing was constrained by 445 

physical access , for example due to a stiffer matrix, and largely saturated at the spheroid 446 

periphery. Killing efficiency decreased with spheroid radius and local neighbor density but 447 

increased with T‑cell killing radius. These simulations suggest that T ‑cell responses are limited 448 

more by spatial accessibility than by antigen availability, explaining why T ‑cell activation effects 449 

were less pronounced and more variable across cell lines. This is consistent with previous 450 

observations that population context and spatial organization strongly influence infection dynamics 451 

at the single -cell level 24 . Together, the modelling and experimental data reveal that ECM -driven 452 

changes in cancer cell density predominantly influence viral infection, whereas ECM -mediated 453 

alterations in matrix stiffness and tissue accessibility primarily regulate T -cell cytotoxicity. Although 454 

prior computational models have explored ECM –virus interactions 23,50 , our work uniquely 455 

integrates these effects with immune dynamics at single -cell resolution. 456 

To conclude , our results demonstrate that ECM composition and abundance regulate 457 

cancer virotherapy through combined effects on cancer cell organization, viral infection, and T ‑cell 458 

activation. By integrating a bottom -up experimental and computational framework , we provide 459 

novel insights into how viral infection is governed primarily by matrix ‑induced changes in cell 460 

organization, whereas T ‑cell activation is directly controlled by matrix composition and stiffness. 461 

This mechanistic separation highlights opportunities to independently optimize infection and 462 

immune engagement in future anticancer virotherapy designs. 463

Limitations 464 

Our bottom -up experimental in vitro system enabled precise control over ECM abundance and 465 

composition, allowing us to isolate their effects on cell organization, infection, and immune 466 

activation. However, this reductionist approach excludes many regulatory factors present in vivo ,467 

including stromal interactions, vascularization, and complex immune networks. In addition, 468 

because we employed rSFV replicons that support only a single round of infection, our study does 469 

not capture how ECM or cell organization might affect multicycle viral propag ation, secondary 470 

infection waves, or long ‑term viral –immune dynamics. As a result, some observations may differ 471 

from in vivo behavior. Computational modeling helped contextualize these findings, but further 472 

integration with more complex systems will be req uired to fully capture ECM ‑immune dynamics 473 

across multiple infection cycles . Despite these limitations, the experimental control afforded by our 474 

in vitro model design was essential for disentangling the individual contributions of ECM 475 

composition, cell org anization, and immune accessibility , providing insights that would be difficult 476 

to resolve in more complex, heterogeneous in vivo settings. 477
