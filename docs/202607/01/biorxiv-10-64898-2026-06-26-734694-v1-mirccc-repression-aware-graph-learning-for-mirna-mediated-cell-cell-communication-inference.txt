# Abstract 

Cell–cell communication analyses usually focus on protein ligands and receptors and therefore miss extracellular vesicle transfer of microRNAs, an important route of signalling in cancer. Here we show that microRNA-mediated communication can be inferred from standard single-cell RNA sequencing by detecting coordinated decreases in the expression of validated miRNA target genes. We developed mirCCC, a computational framework that estimates cell-specific microRNA activity, models cellular sending and receiving capacity for extracellular vesicle transfer, and learns microRNA-resolved communication graphs from transcriptomic data. In synthetic benchmarks with strong confounding signals, mirCCC improved whereas all comparison methods declined. Applied to a human colorectal cancer atlas, mirCCC recovered known colorectal cancer-associated microRNAs and identified stromal- and myeloid-to-epithelial communication converging on a plasticity program linked to TGF-β and Wnt/ β-catenin signalling. These results provide a practical route for studying extracellular vesicle-mediated communication in existing single-cell atlases. 

# Author summary 

Cell-cell communication plays important roles across a wide range of biological processes. Most studies of cell-cell communication focus on interactions between ligands and receptors. However, cells can also use extracellular vesicles to deliver microRNAs (miRNAs), which regulate gene activity in receiving cells and contribute to cancer progression, immune regulation, and changes in the tissue environment. Since standard single-cell sequencing does not directly capture miRNAs, this mode of communication remains largely unexplored. Here we developed mirCCC, a computational method for inferring miRNA-mediated communication from standard single-cell transcriptomic data. Instead of detecting miRNAs directly, mirCCC examines the effects they leave in receiving cells. # Introduction 1

Cell-cell communication (CCC) analysis has developed into a rich computational ecosystem. CellChat 2

combines ligand–receptor prior knowledge with a mass-action model to analyze communication probabilities 3

and network patterns [1]; NicheNet links sender-side ligand signals to receiver-side transcriptional 4

responses [2]. LIANA+ integrates multiple CCC resources into a unified framework [3]; ICELLNET˜ v2 5

emphasizes multi-subunit ligand/receptor complexes [4]. More recent methods extend cell–cell 6

communication analysis beyond classical ligand–receptor models. DeepTalk extends CCC to spatial 7

transcriptomics by integrating spatial context into graph-based inference [5]. CellNEST models relay 8

communication to capture multi-step signaling cascades across intermediate cell states [6]. SigXTALK 9

further expands CCC analysis to pathway-level crosstalk by linking signaling pathways across interacting cell 10 

populations [7]. Despite these advances, most of these methods still rely on protein ligand–receptor 11 

interactions as the underlying paradigm. 12 

However, cell-cell communication is not limited to protein ligand–receptor signaling. Extracellular vesicles 13 

(EVs) serve as long-range carriers transferring miRNAs, proteins, and lipids between cells [8], playing 14 

widespread roles in immune evasion, metastasis, microenvironment remodeling, and therapy resistance [9]. 15 

EV-mediated miRNA transfer is functionally distinct from ligand–receptor signaling because it acts through 16 

post-transcriptional gene repression rather than receptor activation, making it difficult to characterize using 17 

existing CCC frameworks [10]. Furthermore, standard single-cell transcriptomic workflows rely on 18 

poly(A)-based capture and cannot directly measure mature miRNAs [11]. 19 

Computational tools for inferring EV-derived miRNA communication at the single-cell level remain scarce. 20 

miRTalk was the first to formalize this task, integrating scRNA-seq data with EV-related prior information 21 

and miRNA-target interaction databases [12]. However, miRTalk relies on detected miRNA expression in 22 

sender cells, whereas conventional poly(A)-based scRNA-seq provides limited coverage of mature miRNAs. 23 

MIR-gene transcripts may not faithfully reflect mature miRNA activity or EV loading. In addition, miRTalk 24 

mainly summarizes miRNA–target interactions between annotated cell types, limiting its ability to resolve 25 

cell-to-cell heterogeneity and reconstruct miRNA-specific communication links at single-cell resolution. 26 

Based on these considerations, we propose mirCCC, a repression-aware graph learning framework for 27 

inferring miRNA-mediated cell–cell communication from standard scRNA-seq data. Rather than treating 28 

miRNA communication as another class of ligand–receptor events, mirCCC infers communication from 29 

receiver-side target-gene repression patterns consistent with miRNA activity. It first employs signed diffusion 30 

on a miRNA–target bipartite graph to estimate cell-level miRNA activity proxies from target-gene 31 

expression, thereby avoiding reliance on direct miRNA detection in conventional poly(A)-based scRNA-seq. 32 

It then estimates cellular sending and receiving capacities from the expression of EV biogenesis, miRNA 33 

sorting, and RISC pathway genes. On this basis, mirCCC constructs directed graphs for individual miRNAs 34 

to resolve communication heterogeneity among cells within the same annotated cell type and to learn 35 

representations of their miRNA-mediated communication context. Crucially, communication strength is 36 

determined by miRNA-specific proxy–repression matching: a high sender-side proxy for a given miRNA must 37 

coincide with repression of its corresponding target genes in the receiver cell. This requirement reduces false 38 

positives caused by co-expression, cell-state covariation, or nonspecific activation of EV-related machinery. 39 

Overall, mirCCC extends single-cell communication inference beyond protein ligand–receptor signaling and 40 

enables the analysis of miRNA-mediated regulation using routine scRNA-seq data. 41

## Per-miRNA directed graph construction 107 

A key design principle of mirCCC is that miRNAs are modeled independently rather than collapsed into a 108 

single aggregate communication score (Fig. 1c). For each candidate miRNA, we constructed a 109 

miRNA-specific directed sender-to-receiver edge set over the shared cell population and retained the miRNA 110 

identity of each edge in the pooled graph representation. 111

three pathway-based features corresponding to EV biogenesis, miRNA sorting, and RISC activity. 118 

Node features. Each cell was represented by three pathway-based node features: normalized EV 119 

biogenesis activity, normalized miRNA sorting activity, and normalized RISC pathway activity (Fig. 1e). 120 

Sender selection. For each miRNA mk, a miRNA-specific sender score was computed for every cell as the 121 

product of the cell-level emission prior and the normalized proxy of mk: 122 

sender score( mk, i ) = emit prior( i) × proxy 01 (i, m k). (13) This favors cells that both exhibit EV-miRNA emission capacity and show evidence of activity for the 123 

corresponding miRNA. The top-S cells were then selected as sender candidates, where S is a configurable 124 

hyperparameter. 125 

Receiver scoring. Receiver candidates were prioritized using a hybrid repression score that combines two 126 

complementary measures of target-gene suppression through their geometric mean: 127 

repress 01 (mk, j ) = prepress ctrl (mk, j ) × repress rank (mk, j ). (14) The first component was a control-adjusted target-module score. For each miRNA, the weighted mean 128 

expression of its target genes in a given cell was compared with that of expression-matched control genes 129 

selected from the same global expression bins, yielding an adjusted target-module signal. Lower adjusted 130 

target-module expression corresponded to stronger inferred repression and was rescaled to [0 , 1] to obtain 131 

repress ctrl . 132 

The second component was a within-cell rank-based repression score computed from the weighted 133 

normalized ranks of target genes. This term captures ordinal repression patterns and is less sensitive to 134 

outliers in absolute expression magnitude. The geometric mean of the two components therefore combines 135 

absolute repression relative to matched controls with rank-based evidence of target suppression. 136 

To incorporate receiver-side processing capacity, the final receiver absorption score was defined as 137 

absorb( mk, j ) = repress 01 (mk, j ) × risc 01 (j). (15) The top-R cells were then selected as receiver candidates, where R is a configurable hyperparameter 138 

(R = 150 in the default configuration). For each sender cell, directed edges were retained only to its top-L 139 

receiver candidates, where L is also configurable ( L = 10 by default). Candidate edges with low combined 140 

sender–receiver weight were discarded using a minimum threshold of 0.005. 141 

Edge features. Each directed edge ( i → j) associated with miRNA mk carried a six-dimensional 142 

continuous feature vector comprising the base sender–receiver matching weight, the sender’s emission prior, 143 

the receiver’s repression score, the receiver’s RISC capacity, the receiver-side control-adjusted target-module 144 

signal, and the sender’s normalized miRNA proxy score. In addition, each edge carried a discrete miRNA 145 

index k, which was used for learnable miRNA embedding lookup in the graph encoder. 146 

## miRNA-aware Graph Transformer encoder 147 

The miRNA-aware encoder jointly integrates cell-level node features with miRNA-specific edge information 148 

(Fig. 1f). Each miRNA index is first mapped to a learnable embedding of dimension 16, which is concatenated 149 

with the 6-dimensional continuous edge feature vector to form an augmented edge representation. 150

## Self-supervised training via Deep Graph Infomax 157 

Because ground-truth labels for miRNA-mediated communication are unavailable, we trained the encoder 158 

using Deep Graph Infomax (DGI) [20], a self-supervised objective that maximizes agreement between local 159 

node representations and a global graph summary (Fig. 1g). Following the standard DGI formulation, the 160 

graph-level summary vector was computed by applying a sigmoid activation to the mean of all node 161 

embeddings. 162 

Negative examples were generated by a corruption function that independently permuted node features 163 

and continuous edge attributes while keeping the graph topology fixed. This dual corruption yields a 164 

stronger contrastive signal than perturbing only one modality, as the encoder must learn the correspondence 165 

between cellular states and their miRNA-aware communication context. Training then followed a contrastive 166 

objective that distinguishes node embeddings from the original graph from those obtained under corruption. 167 

Specifically, embeddings and the graph summary vector were L2-normalized, and a cosine-similarity 168

## Synthetic benchmark data 193 

To evaluate mirCCC under controlled conditions, we generated two synthetic scRNA-seq benchmark settings, 194 

each containing 2,000 cells, 8 cell types, 2,000 genes, and 3 implanted communication patterns. In the Base 195 

setting, each communication pattern was mediated by 16 ground-truth miRNAs. In the Trap setting, each 196 

communication pattern was mediated by 12 ground-truth miRNAs. Communication signals were injected by 197 

increasing EV biogenesis and miRNA sorting activity in sender cells, increasing RISC activity in receiver 198 

cells, and repressing the corresponding miRNA target genes in receivers. Dropout and expression noise were 199 

added to model technical variability. 200 

Two benchmark settings were used. The Base setting featured uniform miRNA importance, no 201 

orthogonal target-selection trap, no per-miRNA gradient, and predominantly easy negatives (90%). The Trap 202 

setting introduced orthogonal target selection, variable miRNA importance, and graded per-miRNA signals, 203 

with easy negatives reduced to 45%, increasing hard cross-pattern confounders. 204 

Six methods were evaluated: mirCCC and five baselines, including two simple heuristics (Prior-Product, 205 

Matched-Product), one miRNA-oriented (miRTalk-Restored), and two adapted ligand–receptor-style methods 206 

(CellChat-style, NicheNet-style). All were run under the same benchmark framework. miRTalk-Restored is a 207 

paper-faithful reimplementation restoring EVBS-based sender scoring, RISC/RITAC-based receiver scoring, 208 

rank-based target evaluation, optional permutation testing, and specificity calculation, while relying on proxy 209 

miRNA activity. The CellChat-style and NicheNet-style baselines are miRNA-adapted analogs of classical 210 

ligand–receptor methods. Missing ground-truth pairs were completed using the method-specific fill score 211 

defined below, and results were aggregated across user-specified random seeds and settings. 212 

## Benchmark methods and unified evaluation 213 

To ensure comparability across methods, all predictions were converted into a unified cell-pair-level format 214 

containing the sender cell, receiver cell, and communication score. The resulting predictions were merged 215 

with the ground-truth cell-pair labels used in the synthetic benchmark. Ground-truth pairs not covered by a 216 

given method were assigned a score no greater than the minimum score produced by that method. 217 

Performance was subsequently evaluated at both the cell-pair and cell-type levels. 218 

In addition to mirCCC, we implemented five comparison methods. Matched Product calculates the 219 

product of a sender-cell EV-miRNA emission score and the mean receiver-side repression score derived 220 

separately for each miRNA-specific target set. Prior Product instead multiplies the sender-side prior score by 221 

a global receiver-side repression score calculated from the union of all candidate miRNA target genes. 222 

Neither method includes graph representation learning. The distinction between them is that Matched 223 

Product preserves the correspondence between individual miRNAs and their respective target sets before 224 

aggregation, whereas Prior Product collapses all target genes into a single global receiver-side score. 225 

miRTalk-Restored is a benchmark-oriented reimplementation based on the methodological description of 226 

miRTalk [12], rather than a direct execution of the official software. The original miRTalk workflow relies on 227 

detectable sender-side miRNA expression and primarily summarizes communication at the cell-type level, 228 

whereas our benchmark uses standard scRNA-seq data without direct mature-miRNA measurements and 229 

requires scores for predefined individual sender–receiver cell pairs. We therefore reimplemented its core 230 

scoring principles within the unified benchmark framework. The adapted implementation retains the 231 

principal components of miRTalk, including EV biogenesis and sorting-related sender modules, 232

CellChat-style and NicheNet-style were constructed as conceptual adaptations of CellChat [1] and 236 

NicheNet [2], respectively, for the miRNA communication setting. In CellChat-style, sender-side miRNA 237 

emission and receiver-side target repression replace conventional ligand and receptor signals, and 238 

communication scores are calculated using a mass-action formulation with a Hill function at the cell-type 239 

level. In NicheNet-style, a miRNA-target regulatory-potential matrix is constructed and combined with 240 

receiver-side target-gene repression patterns to estimate miRNA regulatory activity, which is subsequently 241 

multiplied by the sender-side emission score to obtain a cell-pair communication score. 242 

The official CellChat and NicheNet implementations were not directly applied because their original 243 

models are designed for protein ligand-receptor communication and do not directly accept miRNA proxies, 244 

miRNA-target repression evidence, or EV-related sender and receiver features as inputs. Similarly, the 245 

original miRTalk workflow relies on detectable sender-side miRNA expression and primarily summarizes 246 

communication at the cell-type level, whereas the present benchmark uses standard scRNA-seq data without 247 

direct mature-miRNA measurements and defines ground truth at the individual cell-pair level. We therefore 248 

implemented task-specific adaptations based on the core modeling principles of each method and 249 

standardized their candidate cell pairs, output structure, and evaluation procedure. Full scoring definitions, 250 

parameter settings, output-completion procedures, and implementation details for all benchmark methods 251 

are provided in S1 Appendix and the accompanying code repository. 252 

Enrichment analysis. To assess whether the top-ranked miRNAs were supported by prior knowledge, we 253 

tested the enrichment of literature-curated CRC-associated miRNAs among the top candidates. Specifically, 254 

the top 20% of miRNAs ranked by mirCCC score along each sender–receiver axis were compared to the full 255 

set of candidate miRNAs using Fisher’s exact test to evaluate whether literature miRNAs were 256 

overrepresented. P-values were reported for each axis to quantify the significance of enrichment. 257 

## Evaluation metrics 258 

Model performance was primarily evaluated using AUROC (area under the receiver operating characteristic 259 

curve) and AUPRC (area under the precision–recall curve) for binary classification of ground-truth 260 

communication cell pairs in the synthetic benchmark. For fair comparison, all methods were evaluated on the 261 

same labeled cell-pair set after completing missing predictions using the method-specific fill score defined 262 

above. 263

conducted on a single NVIDIA GPU. The source code, installation instructions, and example workflows are 267

## Synthetic benchmarking shows robustness to confounding signals 270 

To systematically evaluate the inference accuracy and robustness of mirCCC, we benchmarked it under two 271 

synthetic data settings (see Methods). Both settings contained three ground-truth miRNA communication 272 

axes. In the Base setting, each axis was mediated by 16 miRNAs selected from the miRTalk resource [12]; 273 

sender cells showed increased EV biogenesis and miRNA sorting activity, whereas receiver cells showed 274 

increased RISC activity and systematic repression of the corresponding miRNA target genes. In the Trap 275 

setting, each axis was mediated by 12 miRNAs and included three additional sources of difficulty: (i) 276 

orthogonal target selection reduced target-gene overlap across communication axes, thereby requiring 277

communication calls. In contrast, mirCCC’s repression-aware scoring infers communication by detecting 297 

systematic suppression of target genes in receiver cells. Although decoy cells express the requisite machinery 298 

genes, they lack genuine miRNA–target repression signals and therefore do not yield high scores. These 299 

results demonstrate that mirCCC’s repression-aware scoring provides robustness against confounding signals 300 

that mislead existing approaches in the Trap scenario. 301

## CRC communication landscape reveals an Epi-centered receiver context 302 

To evaluate mirCCC on real data, we applied it to the Pelka et al. CRC single-cell atlas (GSE178341) [21], 303 

selecting MMRp patient C162 comprising 9,994 cells across 7 major cell lineages: Epi, Myeloid, Strom, 304 

T/NK/ILC, B, Plasma, and Mast (Fig. 3a). miRNA–target interactions were obtained from miRTalk [12], 305 

based on experimentally validated data from miRTarBase [16], retaining only 12,731 Functional MTI-level 306 

records. After coverage filtering, 185 miRNAs entered the analysis. 307 

The global communication network inferred by mirCCC revealed that Epi cells serve as a major receiver 308 

of miRNA signals, simultaneously accepting inputs from multiple non-epithelial compartments (Fig. 3b). An 309 

Epi-centric paracrine communication view further delineated the relative contributions of each sender 310 

(Fig. 3c). Quantification of sender-specific contributions showed that Myeloid and Strom together accounted 311 

for approximately 94% of the total paracrine communication score directed toward Epi (Myeloid 60.7%, 312 

Strom 33.2%), far exceeding T/NK/ILC (5.1%), Mast (0.8%), and B (0.1%) (Fig. 3d). This pattern was 313 

remarkably consistent across all top 15 miRNAs, with each showing a Myeloid-plus-Strom contribution of 314 

90–95%. The dominance of these two compartments was further supported by a two-dimensional analysis of 315 

sender capacity. Myeloid and Strom had the highest fractions of active sender cells (23.0% and 22.3%, 316 

respectively) and exhibited the highest mean communication score per sender cell directed toward Epi 317 

(Fig. 3e). 318 

Despite the quantitative difference between Myeloid and Strom in overall sender contribution, the 319 

miRNA–target circuit diagram revealed a key qualitative feature: the top-ranked miRNAs on the Strom →Epi 320 

and Myeloid →Epi axes were highly overlapping (Fig. 3f). The 15 miRNAs with the highest combined scores 321 

generally received substantial contributions from both stromal and myeloid senders. This cross-compartment 322 

convergence suggests that stromal and myeloid cells may deliver regulatory signals to epithelial cells through 323 

a shared miRNA repertoire. Based on these observations, subsequent analyses focus on the Strom →Epi and 324 

Myeloid →Epi axes to systematically assess the biological plausibility of mirCCC predictions. 325 

## Convergent stromal and myeloid miRNAs target an epithelial plasticity module 326 

To evaluate whether mirCCC-prioritized candidate miRNAs along the Strom →Epi and Myeloid →Epi axes 327 

are supported by prior CRC biology, we tested their enrichment among literature-curated CRC-associated 328 

miRNAs. On both axes, these literature-curated CRC-associated miRNAs were 3.2-fold enriched within the 329 

top 20% of mirCCC-ranked miRNAs (7/11, Fisher’s exact p = 1 .1 × 10 −3; Fig. 4b). A similar enrichment 330 

was observed on the Epi →TNKILC axis (7/15, 2.3-fold, p = 0 .013), whereas the Myeloid →TNKILC axis 331 

showed no significant enrichment, serving as an internal negative control. These results indicate that 332 

mirCCC recovers biologically relevant candidates without explicitly incorporating prior knowledge of miRNA 333 

function during inference. 334 

More importantly, the predicted targets of the prioritized miRNAs were not dispersed across unrelated 335 

genes but preferentially converged on an epithelial plasticity-related regulatory module. The miRNA–target 336 

prioritization matrix (Fig. 4a) displays 12 representative candidates, comprising three miRNAs highlighted 337 

for their broad coverage of the curated plasticity gene set–miR-200a, miR-204, and miR-506–and nine 338 

additional high-ranking candidates. Specifically, miR-200a targets ZEB1, ZEB2, SMAD2, SMAD3, and 339 

CTNNB1; miR-204 targets SNAI1, SNAI2, VIM, CDH1, and MMP9; and miR-506 targets CTNNB1, SNAI1, 340 

SNAI2, and VIM. Collectively, these three miRNAs cover 14 of the 15 curated EMT/plasticity-related genes, 341 

forming a coherent target module centered on epithelial plasticity regulation. These predictions are broadly 342 

consistent with prior experimental evidence: miR-200a is a canonical EMT suppressor acting through the 343 

miR-200/ZEB double-negative feedback loop. Additional literature support among the displayed candidates 344 

includes the involvement of miR-34a in EMT-associated invasion programs in CRC and experimentally 345 

validated transfer of CAF-derived exosomal miR-200b-3p, supporting the relevance of the miR-200 family in 346 

this context. Among the 12 displayed candidates, eight have direct or family-level literature support in CRC 347 

or EMT contexts (Table 1). The complete rankings for these two epithelial-directed axes, together with the 348 

Epi-to-TNKILC and Myeloid-to-TNKILC comparison axes, are provided in S1 Table. The table includes 349 

total communication scores, edge counts, mean scores per edge, validated target counts, and 350 

EMT/plasticity-related target annotations. 351

# Discussion and conclusion 369 

In this study, we developed mirCCC, a computational framework for inferring miRNA-mediated cell–cell 370 

communication from standard single-cell transcriptomic data. Unlike existing CCC methods that model 371 

protein ligand–receptor interactions, mirCCC reverse-estimates miRNA activity from target gene repression 372 

patterns and performs representation learning on per-miRNA directed communication graphs, incorporating 373 

the miRNA dimension into single-cell communication analysis. 374 

A key feature of mirCCC is its repression-aware design, which ensures that high-scoring communication 375 

events depend on the concordance between sender proxy and receiver-side target gene repression, rather than 376 

on superficial expression correlations. In synthetic benchmarks, mirCCC was the only method whose 377 

performance improved upon the introduction of confounding cells, demonstrating robustness against false 378 

positives driven by surface-level sender/receiver characteristics. As community interest in miRNA 379 

communication inference grows, establishing standardized benchmark datasets will facilitate systematic 380 

comparison across methods. 381 

Application to real data shows that mirCCC identifies biologically interpretable communication modules. 382 

In a CRC single-cell atlas [21], it recovered known CRC-associated miRNAs and revealed a receiver-centric 383 

program in which stromal and myeloid miRNAs converge on an EMT/TGF-β/Wnt/ β-catenin-related 384
