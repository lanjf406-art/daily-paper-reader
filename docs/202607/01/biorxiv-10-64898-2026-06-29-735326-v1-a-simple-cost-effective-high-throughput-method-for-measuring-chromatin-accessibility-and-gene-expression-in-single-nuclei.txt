Abstract 9

We describe microfluidic -free, droplet -based methods for single -nucleus epigenomic 10 

measurements: Particle -templated Instant Partition single -nucleus assay for 11 

transposase -accessible chromatin using sequencing (PIP -ATAC -seq) and its multiomic 12 

version (PIP -Multiome -seq). We benchmarked these assays by generating data sets 13 

containing thousands of nuclei using cell lines and mouse brains and compared to other 14 

established methods. PIP -Multiome and PIP -ATAC are straightforward to implement, 15 

affordable, and produce high -quality data, providing useful additions to the single -cell 16 

molecular measurement armamentarium. 17 

Main 18 

Chromatin accessibility is associated with “active” regulatory elements that drive gene 19 

expression programs that define cellular identity. Single -nucleus chromatin accessibility 20 

mapping in complex tissues has allowed a mapping of gene regulatory mechanisms 21 

across neuroscience, development, disease, and aging 1–3. Yet widespread adoption of 22 

single cell chromatin accessibility methods has been hampered by the limitations of 23 

existing single -cell methods, which are often prohibitively expensive or technically 24 

demanding with inconsistent results. 25 

Current approaches for mapping chromatin accessibility — with or without co -profiling of 26 

gene expression — fall into three categories, each with notable drawbacks. Plate -based 27 

methods require sorting individual cells into wells from which sequencing libraries are 28 

generated, making them low -throughput and costly. Microfluidics -based methods depend 29 

on specialized, expensive equipment and reagents to encapsulate single cells in droplets. 30 

Finally, split -pool -based methods are technically cumbersome, subjecting nuclei to 31 

multiple rounds of time -intensive reactions that increase ambient RNA contamination, 32 

promote nuclei clumping, and ultimately compromise data quality. A simpler, more 33 

accessible, and cost -effective alternative to these approaches would likely enable a 34

To address these limitations, we developed a novel single -nucleus chromatin accessibility 37 

method built on Particle -templated Instant Partitioning Sequencing (PIP -seq), an 38 

emerging alternative platform for single -cell assays 4. In PIP -seq, uniformly sized beads 39 

serve as physical templates to generate monodispersed, water -in -oil droplets within 40 

minutes through simple vortexing — eliminating the need for specialized microfluidic 41 

hardware. Nuclei and barcoded beads are co -encapsulated within the same droplets, 42 

enabling straightforward single -nucleus library preparation directly from the partitioned 43 

emulsion. 44 

Relative to existing approaches, our method offers several key advantages: it is 45 

substantially more cost -effective than microfluidics -based platforms, considerably higher 46 

in throughput than plate -based methods, and simpler to execute than split -pool strategies, 47 

with reduced nuclei clumping and minimal nucleic acid leakage. The method supports 48 

profiling of chromatin accessibility alone or in combination with the transcriptome. Using 49 

an adapted commercially available kit, this approach can generate data withi n two days 50 

without costly specialized equipment, while reliably producing high -complexity 51 

sequencing libraries — making it broadly accessible to laboratories without extensive 52 

single -cell genomics infrastructure. 53 

We first developed PIP -ATAC -seq, which is carried out in the following steps: (1) Insertion 54 

of sequencing adapters into open chromatin regions within permeabilized nuclei using 55 

hyperactive Tn5 transposase; (2) Compartmentalization of individual nuclei together with 56 

a single barcoded bead via particle -templated instant partitioning; (3) Cell lysis through 57 

fusion of microemulsions containing lysis reagent (4) Release of chromatin fragments 58 

from the nuclei and hybridization to capture oligos on barcoded beads through splint 59 

oligos; (4) Ligation of chromatin fragments onto the beads; (5) Amplification of sequencing 60 

libraries from the beads (Fig. 1A, Fig. S1). 61 

We next extended the method to enable simultaneous profiling of chromatin accessibility 62 

and gene expression from the same nucleus. The PIP -multiome -seq workflow shares the 63 

same step (1) to (3) as PIP -ATAC -seq with the following differences: Upon lysis in step 64 

(4), released chromatin fragments and RNA are captured by oligonucleotides on the 65 

barcoded bead — chromatin fragments via ligation and RNA via reverse transcription into 66 

cDNA — while ATAC -seq fragments are simultaneously extended. ATAC -seq fragments 67 

then undergo linear amplification, are eluted from the beads, and are indexed by PCR. 68 

The beads are then washed and used for whole -transcriptome amplification, after which 69 

cDNA is purified, fragmented, ligated with sequencing adapters, and indexed by PCR. 70

As proof of concept, we tested PIP -ATAC -seq using the human K562 cell line. We 73 

targeted 2,000 cells and sequenced our ATAC -seq fragment library such that the number 74 

of reads was approximately double the number of aligned fragments. Based on read 75 

complexity per cell, we identified 1,800 cells and then applied quality control filtering (Fig. 76 

S3A–E). Of these, 1,682 cells (93.4%) passed quality control, with a median of 41,801 77 

fragments per cell and a median TSS enrichment score of 14.3. The insert size distribution 78 

exhibited clear nucleosome periodicity typical of ATAC -seq data  6 (Fig. S 3 C). 79 

To assess single -cell specificity, we performed a barnyard experiment by mixing human 80 

K562 and mouse 802T4 cells prior to library preparation. We recovered 750 mouse cells 81 

(median 45,923 fragments; TSS enrichment score = 23.5) and 271 human cells (median 82 

44,435 fragments; TSS enrichment score = 15.4), indicating robust library complexity and 83 

data quality for both species. Cross -contamination was minimal, with only 0.27% of 84 

human fragments detected in mouse cells and 0.42% of mouse fragments in human cells, 85 

confirming high single -cell purity. The observed multiplet rate was 8.08% (97/1,200), 86 

similar to the <8% rate specified by the PIP -seq kit; this rate can be reduced by lowering 87 

nuclei input (Fig. 1 B, Fig. S 3 G). 88 

We also validated PIP -multi -seq using frozen K562 cells. RNA libraries contain 1,717 cells 89 

based on elbow plot (Fig . S3 B). ATAC libraries were sequenced to 52.8% saturation, 90 

and 94.1% of cells passing the RNA filter also passed the ATAC filter. From the final 1,615 91 

high -quality cells, we detected a median of 15,428 transcripts (5,740 genes) and 25,958 92 

ATAC fragments per cell, with a median TSS enrichment score of 15.2 (Fig. S 3 D-F). 93 

To evaluate cross -contamination for the multiomic version, we performed a barnyard 94 

experiment mixing human K562 and mouse 802T4 cells. We recovered 556 human and 95 

835 mouse cells with a multiplet rate of 7.5%. Cross -species RNA contamination was low 96 

(2.38% mouse transcripts in human cells; 2.02% in the reverse direction), and 99% of 97 

cells passing RNA filters also passed ATAC filters. ATAC -seq yielded 832 mouse cells 98 

(median 18,681 fragments; TSS = 24.2) and 553 human cells (median 18,257 fragments; 99 

TSS = 15.3), with cross -species fragment contamination of only 1.61% and 1.25%, 100 

respectively, confirming high single -nucleus purity (Fig. 1 C -D, Fig. S 3 H). 101 

A key technical challenge in multi -omic assays is cross -library contamination —102 

particularly ATAC fragments appearing in RNA libraries. We found that replacing the 103 

Nextera Read 2 adapter with a TruSeq Read 2 adapter in the RNA library preparation 104 

effectively resolved this issue for samples with low RNA content. Long -read sequencing 105 

confirmed the RNA library structure, with fewer than 0.54% ATAC -derived fragments 106 

detected in the RNA library (Table S1). 107

To benchmark our method, we compared it against 10x Genomics, the current gold 108 

standard in single -cell assays. To ensure a fair comparison, we obtained 10x data from 109 

ENCODE and published studies using the same K562 cell line, then downsampled all 110 

datasets to a uniform read depth per cell ( 38 ,375 for ATAC; 69 ,00 9 for RNA) based on 111 

the sample with the lowest read depth, and called peaks using bulk ATAC -seq from 112 

ENCODE . To assess the amount of useful information captured by each assay, we first 113 

quantified the number of unique nuclear fragments in peaks per cell. PIP -ATAC -seq 114 

performed comparably to 10x ATAC -seq, while PIP -multiome -seq showed lower numbers 115 

relative to 10x Multiome -seq. However, the 10x Multiome -seq sample exhibited noticeably 116 

lower TSS enrichment, suggesting it may have been over -transposed (Fig. 1F –J). 117 

Importantly, both PIP -ATAC -seq and PIP -Multiome -seq displayed higher TSS enrichment 118 

than their 10x counterparts, indicating greater specificity and reduced background signal. 119 

To further assess library complexity, we simulated the total number of unique fragments 120 

at infinite sequencing depth; both PIP -ATAC -seq and PIP -Multiome -seq were 121 

comparable to 10x (Fig. 1E). On the RNA -seq side, PIP -Multiome -seq yielded similar 122 

median gene counts per cell compared to 10x Multiome -seq (Fig. 1H –I). Furthermore, the 123 

number of unique fragments and detected genes for PIP -ATAC -seq and PIP -Multiome -124 

seq at their original sequencing depths was substantially higher than values reported for 125 

other open -source methods based on benchmarking in literature  7,8 .126 

We then visually examined bulk ATAC -seq signal tracks for our methods. The PIP -ATAC -127 

seq and PIP -Multiome -seq derived tracks were highly concordant with bulk ATAC -seq 128 

from ENCODE and pseudo -bulk tracks from 10x scATAC -seq (Fig. 1J). The tracks also 129 

demonstrated high sensitivity, with minor peaks clearly distinguishable against minimal 130 

background noise. For the bulk RNA -seq track comparison, we generated a standard 131 

PIP -seq RNA library from frozen K562 nuclei; its track was nearly identical to that of our 132 

Multiome -seq, suggesting that the additional steps do not significantly degrade RNA -seq 133 

quality. Taken together, the distinct signal tracks for each modality indicate no detectable 134 

cross -contamination between them. Regarding throughput, 10x can process up to 10,000 135 

cells per lane, whereas PIP -seq can handle between 2,000 and 100,000 cells per sample 136 

depending on the kit size. Despite comparable performance, PIP -seq can be 10 times 137 

less expensive than 10x, particularly when using this large -scale kit (Fig. 1E). 138

13.1, while the RNA -seq library detected an average of 15,177 transcripts and 4,573 143

In sum, particle -templated Instant partitioning is a promising strategy for high quality, large 160 

scale, low -cost single cell sequencing. Here, we present its first application to chromatin 161 

accessibility and multiomics . These applications highlight its potential use as a platform 162 

for further method development. For instance, single -cell CUT&TAG, single -cell DNA 163 

methylation sequencing, and single -cell footprinting may also be portable to this platform, 164 

further increasing the toolkit of single cell molecular characterization methods. 165

PIP -ATAC -seq and PIP -Multiome -seq ATAC Data Pre -processing. We developed a 252 

Snakemake pipeline enabling parallelized data processing to convert raw FASTQ files 253 

to fragment files for downstream analysis (https://github.com/GreenleafLab/PIP -ATAC -254 

Pipeline). The pipeline consists of the following steps (Fig S2) :255 

Quality control. Raw reads were quality -trimmed and adapter sequences were removed 256 

using fastp (v1.0.1) 9. R1 reads containing cell barcodes were processed independently, 257 

while I1 and R2 reads containing genomic sequences were processed as paired files. 258 

Quality reports were generated for each sample. 259 

Cell barcode extraction. Cell barcodes were extracted from R1 reads using PIPseeker 260 

(v3.3.0) with the --chemistry V setting. Barcodes were incorporated into the read headers 261 

of the corresponding I1 and R2 reads using a custom Python script that built an in -memory 262 

lookup table mapping each R1 read to its 16 bp cell barcode. The barcode was embedded 263 

as a CB: tag in the read header to enable barcode -aware downstream processing. Read 264 

headers were also cleaned of extraneous nucleotide sequences appended during 265 

sequencing. 266 

Alignment and filtering. Quality -controlled I1 and R2 reads (with embedded cell barcodes) 267 

were aligned to the reference genome using Bowtie2 (v2.5.4) 10  in --very -sensitive mode 268 

with a maximum insert size of 2,000 bp. Aligned reads were converted to BAM format 269 

and processed with SAMtools (v1.20) 11 , including read name sorting, mate -pair fixing, 270 

coordinate sorting, and barcode -aware PCR duplicate marking using samtools markdup. 271 

Duplicate -marked reads were filtered to retain only properly paired alignments with a 272 

minimum mapping quality of MAPQ ≥ 30. 273 

Fragment file generation. Filtered BAM files were converted to fragment files using a 274 

custom Python script implemented with pysam (v0.23.3). For each properly paired read, 275 

Tn5 transposase insertion sites were identified by applying a +4/−4 bp offset correction 276 

to read start positions, which resolves the 1 bp mismatch introduced by the conventional 277 

+4/−5 bp offset approach and improves base pair -resolution analysis 3. Fragment 278 

coordinates (chromosome, start, end, and cell barcode) were output in BED format, 279 

coordinate -sorted with GNU sort, compressed with bgzip, and indexed with tabix for 280 

efficient random -access queries. 281 

Computational infrastructure. All pipeline steps were executed on an HPC cluster via 282 

SLURM job scheduling, managed through Snakemake's SLURM executor plugin. Conda 283 

environments were used to ensure software reproducibility. 284
