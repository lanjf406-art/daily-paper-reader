Abstract 18 Patient-derived organoids (PDOs) have transformed translational cancer research, 19 allowing tractable models that better represent clinical features than traditional immortalized cell 20 lines. Here we describe two PDOs with differential responses to carboplatin derived from 21 sequential ascites fluid collections from a patient with high-grade müllerian carcinoma, that 22 could not be further subclassified on the omental biopsy. Uterine origin was clinically excluded 23 by pelvic imaging/CT scan of the uterus and absence of vaginal bleeding. Successful derivation 24 from independent collections enabled comparison of intra-patient heterogeneity across 25 sequential ascites samples and demonstrates that PDO efficiency rate is at least partly patient-26 specific or tumor-dependent. We performed long-read whole genome sequencing on the two 27 PDOs, OC104 and OC109, to better characterize the structural variant landscape while also 28 obtaining information on single nucleotide variants and DNA methylation. In addition to 29 confirming single nucleotide variants noted in clinical sequencing (TP53, KRAS, SPOP, 30 PPP2R1A, KMT2D), we identified additional variants in TSC2, NCOR2, and CTNNA2 that are 31 predicted to be likely pathogenic. The spectrum of mutations, particularly the coincident KRAS 32 and TP53, highlighted unexpected overlap with ovarian mucinous carcinoma. We also identified 33 larger insertions and deletions that result in non-synonymous variants in MUC5AC, TPRX1, and 34 BMX, as well as four translocation events, including two that could not have been resolved with 35 short-read sequencing. Differentially methylated promoters between the two PDOs include 201 36 oncogenes and tumor suppressor genes, with HNF1A, MSI2, and SETBP1 having methylation 37 directions consistent with these genes’ roles in platinum response differences observed 38 between the PDOs. Notably, the clonal nature of PDOs produced from two samples taken one 39 week apart is important for the field to appreciate, particularly since they have clonal differences 40 in platinum response. The temporal differences in clonality may indicate a limitation of low 41 volume sampling, however may provide opportunity to longitudinally predict clinical outcomes. 42

Introduction 45 Primary ovarian carcinoma is typically divided into serous, endometrioid, clear cell, and 46 mucinous categories based primarily on morphologic features. While endometrioid and clear cell 47 tumors are both associated with endometriosis and have shared molecular biology, high-grade 48 serous carcinoma is thought to be derived from TP53 mutation-associated transformation of 49 fallopian tube epithelium and the origin of mucinous tumors remains a mystery. Though it lacks 50 an association with endometriosis, serous carcinoma demonstrates discernible phenotypic 51 overlap with endometriosis-associated tumors; mucinous carcinoma is more of an outlier among 52 ovarian cancers with respect to gene expression, though it frequently demonstrates aberrant 53 TP53 and is also associated with hallmarks of low-grade serous tumors, such as KRAS 54 mutation 1–5 . In current practice, the undeniable diagnostic overlap among these entities 55 infrequently affects patient management, because despite such disparate underlying biology, 56 patients with these tumors are generally treated with the same regimen of conventional platinum 57 and taxane-based chemotherapy. Robust models are necessary to explore improved treatment 58 options for these aggressive tumors. 59 Patient-derived organoids (PDOs) of cancer, which are primary tumor cells typically 60 grown in a dome of extracellular matrix with a specialized cocktail of media rich in supplements 61 and inhibitors of senescence, have been shown to more faithfully recapitulate tumor genomics 62 and therapeutic outcome 6–10 . To date, approximately 178 peer-reviewed studies have been 63 conducted using ovarian cancer PDOs 11 , including five studies with Illumina-based whole 64 genome sequencing on a total of 121 PDOs, predominantly representing high-grade serous 65 ovarian carcinomas 8,9,12–14 . Recently, a mucinous ovarian carcinoma-focused study was posted 66 as a preprint, characterizing 19 long-term PDOs by whole-exome sequencing, RNA-seq, and 67 therapy response for 11 chemotherapy drugs 15 . Summary of case 271 The 68-year-old patient with no known relevant medical history or smoking history 272 presented at the ER with a one-month history of bloating, abdominal distension, decreased 273 appetite, fatigue, and constipation. CA125 was elevated at 255 U/mL and abdominopelvic CT 274 demonstrated a 7.7 x 6.8 x 5.4 cm left pelvic mass, with ascites and peritoneal implants. The 275 patient underwent a core needle biopsy of the omentum. The diagnosis was high-grade 276 müllerian carcinoma, supported by marked nuclear atypia and immunophenotypic findings 277 including PAX-8 positivity and a null pattern for p53 ( Figure 1A ). Additional findings included 278 negativity for WT1 and estrogen receptor alpha. More specific classification was not possible, 279 but the findings were compatible with either high-grade serous carcinoma of tubo-ovarian origin 280 or uterine serous carcinoma, with the former being the most likely scenario based on disease 281 spread and incidence in routine clinical practice. Clinically, there was no history of vaginal 282 bleeding, and the uterus was visualized by CT with no indication of abnormality, supporting 283 ovarian origin, for a working diagnosis of at least stage IIIC tubo-ovarian high-grade serous 284 carcinoma. Accordingly, the patient underwent 4 cycles of neoadjuvant chemotherapy with 285 carboplatin/paclitaxel/bevacizumab; by imaging, there was reduction in size of the ovarian mass 286 but increased volume of ascites and possible increase in peritoneal carcinomatosis. Interval 287 debulking was attempted, but the surgery was aborted due to extensive intraabdominal disease 288 involving peritoneal surfaces, bowel, and lesser omentum/porta hepatis. Resection of disease 289 would have required extensive peritoneal resection, extensive bowel resection (possibly total 290

The highest AFs were 0.8966 and 0.9756 for SPOP in OC104 and 328 OC109, respectively, indicating >90% pure cancer organoids. Discussion 474 This study presents a unique case of two PDOs derived from the same patient, with LRS 475 and drug response analyses yielding tumor subtype reclassification. Derivation of two PDOs 476 from a single patient, given otherwise low efficiency rates in generating PDOs from tumor or 477 ascites, suggests the patient-specific nature of PDO generation. While media formulations have 478 been largely developed in endoluminal gastrointestinal cancers, whether they are optimized for 479 success in ovarian cancer PDO development remains uncertain. One recent preprint on 480 mucinous ovarian carcinoma PDOs 15 modified media formulation compared to that previously 481 developed for ovarian cancer PDOs from Hans Clever’s lab 9 . They found that increased B-27 482 supplement, Y-27632 ROCK inhibitor and human-EGF, addition of N-2 supplement and FGF-2, 483 and absence of beta-estradiol, heregulin beta-1, and forskolin improved PDO generation. Our 484 formulation was not specifically optimized for ovarian cancer PDOs, with the main difference 485 being the absence of several growth factors and hormones (FGF-10, FGF-2, beta-estradiol, 486 hydrocortisone, gastrin, heregulin-beta1, and forskolin) and addition of MAPK inhibitor 487 SB202190 to inhibit fibroblast growth. Further work will be needed to ensure that PDO 488 conditions are optimal for ovarian cancer, in addition to other tumor features that influence PDO 489 efficiency rate. 490

22 Long-read sequencing of cancer genomes remains in its relative infancy, with only 33 491 ovarian cancers sequenced in the published literature to date 18–21 . Our work is unique in that we 492 have performed LRS on an ovarian carcinoma with features overlapping high-grade serous 493 carcinoma and the enigmatic mucinous carcinoma, and leveraged several strengths of LRS, 494 examining likely pathogenic single nucleotide polymorphisms, structural variants, and differential 495 methylation. In addition, previous LRS of whole genomes in PDOs are limited in parallel 496 information from manipulation of the PDOs. While one report confirmed ecDNA presence with 497 fluorescence in situ hybridization 22 and another performed karyotyping, ChIP-seq, and Hi-C 23 ,498 each in esophageal PDOs, our study specifically assessed the response of PDOs to carboplatin 499 as a single agent. 500 Of note, examination of the genomics of these two isogenic PDOs from a single patient 501 led us to retrospectively reconsider whether mucinous carcinoma would have been a better 502 classification for this tumor. In hindsight, the original finding of WT1 negativity could be regarded 503 as an argument against a diagnosis of high-grade serous carcinoma, which is usually positive 504 for WT1, but in the setting of advanced tumors it is not uncommon to observe isolated 505 immunophenotypic results that do not support the final diagnosis and it was therefore readily 506 regarded as noncontributory. The pattern of spread within the peritoneum is certainly in keeping 507 with the patterns seen in the serous tumors in clinical practice. In hindsight, the additional 508 finding of KRAS mutation in the clinical sequencing, and the patient’s poor outcome and rapid 509 disease progression are more compelling for a mucinous ovarian carcinoma, which are rather 510 rare, and characteristically aggressive and chemoresistant in the setting of disseminated 511 disease 39 . Classification of an ovarian carcinoma is relatively straightforward in the majority of 512 the patients when a generous sample is available/on a surgical specimen; however, it is much 513 more challenging in a small biopsy specimen as many high-grade carcinomas have overlapping 514 morphology and share abnormalities in p53.
