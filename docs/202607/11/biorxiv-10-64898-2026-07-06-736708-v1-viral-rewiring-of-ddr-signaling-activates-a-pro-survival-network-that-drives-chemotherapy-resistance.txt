ABSTRACT 19 

20 

Radiation and chemotherapy rely on an intact DNA damage response (DDR) to halt cell -cycle 21 

progression and eliminate damaged cells, yet many tumors evade these outcomes and develop 22 

resistance. Adenoviruses remodel host signaling networks in ways that mirror tumor evolution, 23 

providing a powerful system to dissect how DDR pathways are subverted. Here, we identify two 24 

scenarios in which the central DDR kinases ATM and ATR are reprogrammed from enforcing 25 

CHK1/CHK2 -dependent checkpoint arrest to activating a NEMO-NF -κB survival pathway. This 26 

rewiring induces transcriptional programs associated with stress tolerance, anti -apoptotic 27 

signaling, and chemoresistance, and promotes the accumulation of cells with abnormal DNA 28 

content. These findings reveal a previously unrecognized mode of DDR plasticity that generates 29 

a pro -survival state reminiscent of early tumor evolution and suggest how ATM - and 30 

ATR -dependent pathways can be co -opted to promote therapeutic resistance. 31

INTRODUCTION 32 

33 

The DNA damage response (DDR) is initiated when the MRN complex, composed of MRE11, 34 

RAD50, and NBS1, recruits the apical kinases ataxia-telangiectasia mutated (ATM) and ATM-35 

and-Rad3-related (ATR) to sites of DNA lesions, triggering an evolutionarily conserved signaling 36 

network that preserves genome integrity [1-3]. Once activated, ATM and ATR phosphorylate 37 

key transducers, including the checkpoint kinases CHK1 and CHK2, and downstream effectors 38 

such as p53, thereby coordinating cell-cycle arrest, DNA repair, or, when damage is irreparable, 39 

cell death [1-3]. Cytotoxic therapies, including radiation and chemotherapy, exploit this 40 

signaling network to eliminate tumor cells, making intact DDR function essential for therapeutic 41 

efficacy. Yet many tumors evade these outcomes [4], underscoring persistent gaps in our 42 

understanding of how DDR pathways are subverted during therapy resistance. 43 

44 

Adenoviruses (Ads) are widespread human pathogens, whose replication cycle is well 45 

characterized. Although not oncogenic in humans, Ads remodel host signaling networks in ways 46 

that mirror early tumor evolution, making them powerful tools for dissecting how 47 

cancer -relevant pathways are rewired [5, 6]. Ad proteins perturb numerous cellular systems— 48 

including the DDR, NF -κB signaling, and cell -cycle control-that are frequently dysregulated in 49 

cancer [7, 8]. 50 

51 

Here, we use adenovirus infection to uncover two scenarios in which the central DDR kinases 52 

ATM and ATR are reprogrammed from enforcing CHK1/2-dependent checkpoint arrest to 53

E4orf3 rewires ATM-ATR checkpoint signaling to drive abnormal DNA content 63 

64 

Adenovirus infection is widely assumed to inactivate ATM and ATR signaling because the virus 65 

disables upstream DNA damage sensing [9, 10]. The E1B55K-E4orf6 complex targets MRE11 and 66 

RAD50 for proteasomal degradation, while E4orf3 sequesters MRE11 and NBS1 into 67 

higher -order nuclear assemblies [11], collectively disrupting the MRN complex and preventing 68 

canonical DDR activation. Despite this, ATM phosphorylation at Ser1981-a hallmark of ATM 69 

activation [12]-has been consistently observed in Ad -infection [13-15], prompting us to 70 

examine how ATM and ATR behave when their upstream sensors are disabled. 71 

72 

Although HeLa cells harbor chromosomal translocations and other structural abnormalities not 73 

typically present in normal human cells [24], flow -cytometric analysis (Fig. 1A) showed 74 

expected G1, S, and G2/M distributions in mock -infected populations, with only ~1% of cells 75

ATM and ATR impose distinct cell -cycle checkpoints governing abnormal DNA content 105 

106 

Because ATM and ATR inhibition produced opposing outcomes depending on E4orf3 status, we 107 

next examined how each kinase shapes cell -cycle progression, the process most directly linked 108 

to DNA content. In E4orf3(+) infections, inhibition of either kinase prevented further 109 

accumulation of abnormal DNA content (Fig. 1D-G). ATM inhibition caused a pronounced G2/M 110 

accumulation (Fig. 2B, 2C), whereas ATR inhibition blocked progression from G1 to S phase 111 

progression (Fig. 2D). These results indicate that ATM and ATR regulate abnormal DNA content 112 

through distinct, phase -specific checkpoint functions. 113 

114

These findings reveal a clear division of labor: ATR safeguards S -phase entry, whereas ATM 118

Phosphorylated NEMO links ATM to NF-κB signaling during Ad-infection 125 

126 

The observation that ATM is phosphorylated in line with its activation yet fails to signal to CHK2 127 

in E4orf3(+) infections (Fig. 1C) suggests that ATM may redirect its signaling to alternative 128 

substrates. Prior work showed that ATM phosphorylation correlates with active NF -κB, which is 129 

required for abnormal DNA content in E4orf3(+) Ad-infected cells [13]. Because NF -κB 130 

activation depends on the IKK complex and its regulatory subunit NEMO, and ATM is known to 131 

phosphorylate NEMO [20], we tested whether NEMO mediates NF -κB activation during Ad 132 

infection (Fig. 3A) 133 

134 

Western blotting showed that phospho-p50 (serine 337) increased steadily in E4orf3(+) Ad 135 

infections (Fig. 3B), mirroring the rise in phospho -NEMO and the abnormal DNA content 136 

observed in Figs. 1 and 2. In contrast, E4orf3(–) infections showed reduced phospho -NEMO and 137 

no increase in phospho -p50 (Fig. 3B). Confocal microscopy confirmed that phospho -NEMO 138 

localized predominantly in the nuclei of E4orf3(+) cells but was nearly undetectable in E4orf3(-) 139 

and mock-infected control cells (Fig. 3C). 140

Switching from CHK1/2, to the NEMO-NF-κB pathway induces survival and 213 

chemoresistance -associated gene expression 214 

215 

Cells with abnormal DNA content are known to show enhanced viability under stress [27]. 216 

Because redirecting ATM/ATR signaling toward NEMO-NF-κB drives abnormal DNA content, we 217 

next asked whether this signaling shift also reprograms the transcriptional outputs associated 218 

with stress adaptation and chemoresistance. 219 

220 

RNA sequencing of A549 cells infected with E4orf3(+) or E4orf3(-) Ad and treated with an ATM 221 

inhibitor revealed that myeloblastosis proto-oncogene-like 2 (MYBL2) was among the most 222 

significantly upregulated genes in both conditions (fold changes = 2.623 and 2.587, respectively; 223 

Fig. 7A). MYBL2 is a transcription factor essential for cell cycle progression and survival, and its 224 

dysregulation is linked to abnormal DNA content, genomic instability, and poor cancer 225 

outcomes [28]. MYBL2 is activated by Cyclin A/E-CDK2 [28], and we previously demonstrated 226 

that CDK2 is activated in Ad-infected cells [8]. 227

DISCUSSION 240 

241 

Adenovirus infection has long been understood to disable canonical DNA -damage response 242 

(DDR) signaling by dismantling the MRN complex [11], thereby preventing activation of ATM 243 

and ATR [9, 10]. Yet our findings reveal that ATM and ATR are not merely passive bystanders in 244 

this context. Instead, Ad, through E4orf3, repurposes these kinases, redirecting their signaling 245 

away from the CHK1/CHK2 checkpoint axis and toward the NEMO-NF -κB pathway. This rewiring 246 

induces transcriptional programs associated with survival and chemoresistance, revealing a 247 

previously unrecognized plasticity in DDR signaling. 248

A central finding of this study is that ATM activation persists despite MRN disruption, but its 250 

downstream signaling is selectively suppressed in E4orf3(+) infections. Although ATM and ATR 251 

are phosphorylated, their canonical targets, CHK1 and CHK2, are not maintained, revealing a 252 

disconnect between kinase activation and checkpoint enforcement. This uncoupling explains 253 

the striking divergence in cell -cycle phenotypes: E4orf3(-) infections retain a robust G2/M 254 

arrest, whereas E4orf3(+) infections progress to abnormal DNA content. 255 

256 

Our data show that E4orf3 is sufficient to reprogram ATM/ATR signaling, redirecting these 257 

kinases toward NEMO. This is evident in the colocalization of phosphorylated ATM with 258 

phosphorylated NEMO, the loss of ATM-NBS1 interactions, and the preferential formation of 259 

ATM-NEMO complexes during E4orf3(+) infections. In E4orf3(-) Ad-infected cells, blocking ATM 260 

or CHK2 mimics the effect of E4orf3, demonstrating that the key requirement for pathway 261 

switching is not the viral protein itself but the interruption of canonical DDR signaling. 262 

263 

This shift toward NEMO-NF -κB signaling has profound consequences. NF -κB activation is 264 

essential for the accumulation of abnormal DNA content, and its inhibition reverses the effects 265 

of ATM or CHK2 blockade. These findings position NF -κB as the critical effector of the rewired 266 

DDR, transforming what would normally be a checkpoint -enforcing response into a 267 

pro -survival, genome -destabilizing program. 268 

269 

RNA -seq analysis revealed strong induction of MYBL2, a transcription factor strongly linked to 270 

abnormal DNA content, genomic instability, and poor cancer outcomes [28]. MYBL2 activation, 271

Together, these findings support a model in which Ad infection repurposes ATM and ATR from 278 

genome guardians into facilitators of viral replication and host -cell survival. By disabling 279 

MRN -dependent checkpoint signaling and redirecting ATM/ATR activity toward NEMO-NF -κB, 280 

the virus creates a cellular state characterized by abnormal DNA content, enhanced survival, 281 

and resistance to stress. This model unifies previously disparate observations-persistent ATM 282 

phosphorylation, NF -κB activation, abnormal DNA content, and enhanced viability of infected 283 

cells-into a coherent framework for DDR pathway switching. 284 

285 

These insights have important implications for Ad -based therapeutics. E4orf3 -containing Ads 286 

are being evaluated as oncolytic agents ([32, 33], ClinicalTrials.gov), yet E4orf3 -mediated 287 

activation of survival pathways may limit their potency. They are also being evaluated for 288 

vaccine delivery [34-36]. E4orf3 -dependent reprogramming of NF -κB signaling may attenuate 289 

NF -κB -driven inflammatory responses that are critical to vaccine efficacy. These considerations 290 

underscore the need to account for E4orf3’s signaling effects when designing next -generation 291 

Ad vectors. 292

More broadly, our results reveal a previously unappreciated plasticity in DDR signaling. They 294 

demonstrate that ATM and ATR can be reprogrammed not only by viral proteins but also by 295 

perturbations to their downstream effectors, such as CHK2. This raises the possibility that 296 

similar rewiring events occur in cancer, where DDR components are frequently mutated or 297 

suppressed. The parallels between Ad -infected cells and chemoresistant tumor cells—MYBL2 298 

upregulation, CDK2 activation, Bcl -2 induction, and NF -κB dependence—suggest that viruses 299 

exploit vulnerabilities that cancer cells also leverage for survival. Understanding this shared 300 

logic may illuminate new strategies to counteract therapeutic resistance. 301
