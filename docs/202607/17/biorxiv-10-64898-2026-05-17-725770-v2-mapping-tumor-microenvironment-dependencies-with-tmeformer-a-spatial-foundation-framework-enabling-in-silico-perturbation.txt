# Abstract 33 Despite the fundamental role of spatial context in driving tumor progression, most 34 current computational models for virtual perturbation have largely overlooked its 35 importance. Here, we introduce TMEformer, a tumor microenvironment-aware deep 36 learning framework that leverages high-resolution spatial transcriptomics to jointly 37 model intrinsic tumor cell programs and local microenvironmental signals by explicitly 38 incorporating spatial architecture. Validated across diverse tumor spatial 39 transcriptomic cohorts, TMEformer enables virtual perturbations that capture functional 40 dependencies within local cellular ecosystems. Despite being trained on cancer-41 specific spatial datasets, TMEformer outperforms baseline models pretrained on large-42 scale corpora in capturing key tumor transitions, including lineage plasticity and the 43 emergence of therapy resistance. Systematic perturbation analyses prioritize tumor-44 intrinsic transcription factors and TME-derived ligands that drive disease progression, 45 recovering established regulators and revealing novel candidates. Furthermore, TME-46 derived embeddings improve the spatial stratification of tumor cells and align more 47 closely with pathological architecture. Together, TMEformer establishes a general 48 framework for modeling tumors as spatially coupled, perturbable ecosystems. An 49 online TMEformer web platform for perturbation prediction, analysis, and visualization 50 is available at http://www.pradcellatlas.com. 51 52

# Introduction 53 With the advent of single-cell and spatial omics, tumor development and progression 54 are increasingly recognized as emergent properties of complex cellular ecosystems 55 rather than the consequence of tumor cell–intrinsic alterations alone 1, 2 . Intratumoral 56 heterogeneity, lineage plasticity, and therapy resistance reflect the capacity of 57 malignant cells to dynamically adapt to diverse selective pressures 3. These adaptive 58 processes cannot be fully understood without considering the spatially organized 59 tumor microenvironment (TME), particularly in solid tumors, which provides 60 biochemical signals, physical constraints, and intercellular interactions that 61 continuously shape tumor cell states 4. However, analyses for the tumor ecosystem 62 defined by tumor cells and surrounding stromal and immune compartments remain 63 largely descriptive, emphasizing co-localization patterns or static interactions. A central 64 unresolved challenge remains in deciphering how perturbations in tumor-intrinsic or 65 microenvironmental compartments propagate through the spatial network to 66 orchestrate tumor cell states 5, 6 . Addressing this challenge requires computational 67 frameworks that are capable of modeling tumor–TME interactions as integrated 68 systems. 69 Concurrently, transformer-based foundation models have revolutionized single-70 cell representation learning 7, 8 . By pretraining on large-scale single-cell omics data, 71 these models extract generalizable gene regulatory patterns, facilitating downstream 72 tasks such as cell type annotation and batch correction. This paradigm has given rise 73 to the concept of the artificial intelligence virtual cell (AIVC) 9. Leveraging this, in silico 74 perturbations (ISP) enable the simulation of genetic or pharmacological interventions 75 to predict cellular responses, ultimately offering a scalable route to hypothesis 76 generation and mechanistic inference 10 . However, most single-cell models treat cells 77 as isolated entities and often rely on in vitro data input 11 , which may not fully 78 recapitulate the in vivo responses shaped by the complex tumor microenvironment 79 (TME) 12-14 . As spatial transcriptomics (ST) continues to advance, 5 pioneering works 80 like MintFlow 15 and SpaceTravLR 16 have begun to bridge this gap. Nevertheless, 81 considerable untapped potential remains in harnessing non-linear deep learning 82 architectures to capture the intricate, non-canonical regulatory logic underlying cellular 83 organization. 84 Here, we introduce TMEformer, a tumor microenvironment–aware spatial 85 foundation framework. Instead of modeling tumor cells in isolation, TMEformer 86 explicitly incorporates spatial information from high-resolution ST data to jointly learn 87

For each tumor cell, neighboring cells (conceptually like 89 dynamic spatial niche) are aggregated through attention-based mechanisms to 90 generate a compact context token summarizing the local TME, which is subsequently 91 integrated with the embedding representation of corresponding tumor cell and then 92 processed by a shared transformer encoder. Based on this design, versatileISP 93 strategies and tasks are implemented to discover both tumor-intrinsic and TME-94 derived dependencies, enabling cellular ecosystem-aware causal inference. Moreover, 95 the learned TME representations yield interpretable embeddings that capture local 96 tissue architecture and support downstream stratification analysis. Together, 97 TMEformer establishes a generalizable framework for modeling tumors as spatially 98 coupled, perturbable ecosystems and provides a foundation for mechanistic 99 investigation of tumor evolution. 100 101 102 103

1. A TME-aware Spatial Transformer Model for High Resolution Gene Expression 105 

Prediction 106 To model the interplay between tumor cells and their neighboring microenvironment, 107 we introduce a tumor microenvironment-aware single-cell transformer modeling 108 framework and train it on high-resolution spatial transcriptomic data (Fig. 1a ). 109 Specifically, a TME-CEM (TME Context Encoding Module) is designed to enable 110 integration of each tumor cell’s intrinsic transcriptional profile with signals from its 111 immediate spatial microenvironment. For a given tumor cell, the module first 112 represents the initial embeddings of its spatially neighboring TME cells encoded by 113 overall transcriptome and annotated cell types. These TME embeddings are 114 processed through two sequential attention layers to model intercellular 115 communications and aggregated into a single context token, which is then fused with 116 the tumor cell’s intrinsic embedding using a fixed weight ( Methods ). Thus, our TME -117 aware spatial Trans former (TMEformer) encoder infers context-dependent regulatory 118 drivers of tumor heterogeneity, enabling a systems-level view of the tumor ecosystem. 119 It achieves this by integrating spatial architecture to first model the distinct cellular 120 components surrounding each tumor cell and then orchestrate their complex interplay, 121

5. TME embedding captures spatial organization and pathological phenotypes 342 By extracting the learned TME context token embedding (TME-emb) of PCa cells from 343 input layer of TMEformer, we evaluated their utility for tumor stratification and 344 representation from intrinsic transcriptional profiles (EXP-emb) was used as a baseline 345 comparison. Based on the results of unsupervised clustering, we further introduced 346 two metrics—the TME Clustering Index and the Gleason Consistence Index—to 347 quantify spatial coherence and pathological relevance, respectively ( Methods ). As 348 expected, TME-informed clustering consistently showed higher spatial and Gleason 349 coherence across the three treatment-naïve PCa samples with clear Gleason 350 annotation ( Figs. 6a-l ). 351 To further validate the phenotypic relevance of TME-emb in a supervised setting, 352 we designed a modeling pipeline to train Gleason classifiers and evaluate cross-353 sample generalization based on PCa cell representations ( Fig. 6m ). We benchmarked 354 TME-emb against classical dimensionality reduction methods as well as recently 355 proposed single-cell and spatial foundation models. While all models outperformed 356 random prediction, TME-emb consistently achieved the best performance, followed by 357 STACK, Nicheformer, and CellPLM ( Figs. 6n–o , S12 ). Notably, the recently published 358 STACK model also incorporates cellular context based on scRNA-seq, but does not 359 leverage the advantage of spatial transcriptome. Together, these results demonstrate 360 that explicitly modeling spatially restricted TME context yields cell representations with 361 enhanced predictive power. 362 363

PCa cohort collection and spatial transcriptomic profiling 394 Tumor tissues from eight patients with prostate cancer (PCa) were collected and 395 subjected to spatial transcriptomic profiling using the 10x Genomics Xenium platform. 396 The cohort comprised four treatment-naïve (TN) tumors, three tumors obtained 397 following androgen-deprivation therapy (ADT), and one castration-resistant prostate 398 cancer (CRPC) sample. For the four TN samples, Gleason pattern–annotated regions 399 were provided based on clinical pathological assessment. All tissue sections were 400 profiled using the Xenium Prime 5K Human Panel. Unsupervised clustering was performed using the Leiden algorithm, and 409 major cell populations were annotated based on the expression of canonical marker 410 genes referring to published literature 54 . For each cell during model training and 411 prediction, genes were ranked based on their expression levels in descending order, 412 and the resulting gene rankings were converted into token sequences following the 413 processing pipelines of Geneformer 55 .414 415 

TME-aware model designing 416 We developed a tumor microenvironment (TME)–aware transformer model for spatial 417 transcriptomics, termed TMEformer. The model introduces a TME context embedding 418 module (TME-CEM) at the input layer, enabling the explicit incorporation of spatially 419 informed contextual information from each cell’s surrounding microenvironment. For 420 each PCa cell, neighboring cells were first identified by selecting the top K spatially 421 nearest cells (K = 10,000), from which k = 256 cells were randomly sampled without 422 replacement as candidate TME contributors. For each selected TME cell, two types of 423 embeddings were computed to form its initial representation. First, a transcriptional 424 embedding was obtained using a pretrained single-cell representation model (GF_CL, 425 described below). Second, a learnable cell-type embedding was assigned to each cell 426 based on its annotated cell-type label. These embeddings were combined via element-427 wise summation. The resulting TME cell representations were processed through a 428 two-stage attention-based aggregation module. The first stage consisted of a self-429 attention layer that modeled interactions among TME cell tokens and captured higher-430 order relationships within the local microenvironment. The second stage applied an 431 attention-based pooling mechanism with a learnable query vector to aggregate 432 information into a single TME context token. The TME context embedding was then 433 integrated into the target PCa cell representation using a fixed weighting factor (α = 434 0.2), and combined with gene token embeddings and rank-based positional 435 embeddings at the input layer. This combined representation served as the final input 436 to the transformer encoder during model training. 437 438

Background distribution and statistical testing 572 To assess the statistical significance of in silico perturbation effects, background 573 distributions were generated using randomly selected genes under the same 574 perturbation settings as the target perturbations. For single-gene perturbations, 100 575 genes were randomly sampled from the available panel genes. Each gene was 576 perturbed in randomly selected cells, yielding 5,000 background perturbations for the 577 embedding similarity task, with 50 cells perturbed per gene, and 10,000 background 578 perturbations for the marker expression task, with 100 cells perturbed per gene. The 579 resulting ISP scores were used to define the background distribution. For multi-gene 580 perturbations involving m genes, background distributions were generated by 581 randomly sampling gene combinations of size m and applying the same perturbation 582 settings. 583 To compare target perturbations with the background, each tissue section was 584 divided into square patches of fixed size. Within each patch, the ISP score of each 585 target perturbation was compared with the corresponding background score, defined 586 as the mean ISP score of random gene perturbations in that patch. A one-sided 587 Wilcoxon signed-rank test was then used to assess whether target perturbations 588 produced significantly greater effects than random perturbations. By default, 2,000-μm 589 square patches were used for all-sample ISP analyses, whereas 500-μm square 590 patches were used for few-sample ISP analyses. For external datasets generally 591 containing only one sample, 1,000-μm square patches were used. Statistical 592 significance was defined as P < 0.05 by default. For TF and ligand screening analyses, 593 Benjamini–Hochberg (BH) adjusted P < 0.05 was used to determine significance. 594 For marker expression perturbation analyses, overall delta-exp scores were 595 calculated by aggregating effects across marker genes. For individual perturbation 596 analyses, the overall delta-exp score was computed in two steps. First, within each 597 patch, delta-exp scores from target and random perturbations were Z-score 598 normalized separately for each marker gene. Second, the normalized delta-exp scores 599 were averaged across marker genes to obtain an overall delta-exp score for each 600 perturbation. The background overall delta-exp score was defined as the mean overall 601 delta-exp score across random gene perturbations. For batch perturbation analyses, 602 ISP scores from all screened target genes and background perturbations were pooled 603 within the same patch. Scores were then Z-score normalized within each patch 604 separately for each marker gene. For each target perturbation, normalized scores were 605 averaged across marker genes to obtain an overall delta-exp score. 606

TME embedding-based clustering 673 Based on the TME context encoding module of the TMEformer model, a TME context 674 token embedding was computed for each PCa cell. These embeddings (termed TME-675 emb) were used to perform unsupervised clustering, and the resulting TME-driven 676 clusters were compared with clusters derived solely from the intrinsic transcriptomic 677 profiles (termed EXP-emb) of PCa cells. The comparison focused on spatial coherence 678 and pathological consistency. 679 (1) TME Clustering Index 680 To evaluate the spatial coherence of clusters, we quantified how spatially proximal 681 cells within the same cluster were, compared with cells from different clusters. Two 682 metrics were used: (i) Silhouette coefficient: Measures the separation between each 683 cell’s assigned cluster and its nearest neighboring cluster. Higher values indicate more 684 distinct and well-separated clusters in spatial space. (ii) iLISI_graph: Assesses the 685 degree of cluster mixing by quantifying the presence of neighboring cells with different 686 cluster labels. Lower mixing (i.e., lower iLISI_graph values) indicates higher spatial 687 purity of clusters. Together, these two metrics form the TME clustering index 688 (Silhouette - iLISI_graph), which reflects how well the clustering captures spatial 689 structure. 690 (2) Gleason Consistency Index 691 To assess how well the TME-driven clusters correspond to pathological Gleason 692 regions, we computed the Gleason consistency index based on two metrics: (i) 693 Adjusted Rand Index (ARI): Measures the agreement between TME-based clusters 694 and Gleason-labeled regions, adjusted for chance. Higher ARI indicates stronger 695 alignment. (ii) Normalized Mutual Information (NMI): Quantifies shared information 696 between clustering and pathology labels. Higher NMI reflects greater consistency in 697 overall partition structure. Gleason Consistence Index from these metrics (ARI + NMI) 698 jointly evaluate the degree to which TME-derived clustering captures clinically relevant 699 pathological heterogeneity. The above original indexes (Silhouette, iLISI_graph, ARI, 700 NMI) were computed using the scib python package 65 .701 702

# Discussion 743 In this study, we present TMEformer, a tumor microenvironment-aware transformer 744 framework that models tumor cells as components of spatially organized cellular 745 ecosystems. By integrating tumor-intrinsic programs with microenvironmental signals 746 from clinical specimens which capture a rich set of endogenous perturbations such as 747 disease states and tissue niches, TMEformer enables spatially informed in silico 748 perturbation. This approach addresses a central challenge in tumor biology of 749 understanding how perturbations propagate through spatially structured networks to 750 shape cellular states. 751 Across diverse perturbation settings, TMEformer outperformed Geneformer-752 derived baselines, particularly in contexts where tumor evolution is tightly coupled to 753 microenvironmental regulation. Notably, the model captures both tumor-intrinsic and 754 non-cell-autonomous effects, allowing perturbations originating from neighboring cells 755 (e.g. ligand signaling or compositional changes) to propagate and reshape tumor cell 756 states. This provides the approach to dissect multi-level regulatory dependencies 757 underlying neuroendocrine differentiation, castration resistance, and tumor 758 progression. Leveraging this framework, TMEformer recovers established drivers and 759 prioritizes clinically relevant candidates, including TWIST1, whose association with 760 disease progression. The model also uncovers non-obvious intercellular dependencies, 761 such as immunosuppressive myeloid states and Treg-associated signaling, 762 demonstrating its ability to reveal mechanisms that are difficult to capture through 763 descriptive spatial analyses. Beyond perturbation prediction, TME-derived 764 embeddings enhance the representation of tumor cell states by embedding them within 765 their local ecological context. This leads to improved spatial coherence and stronger 766 alignment with pathological architecture, indicating that spatial context provides 767 biologically meaningful information not captured by transcriptome-only models. 768 Notably, the generalization of TMEformer across spatial platforms and cancer types 769 suggests that it captures conserved regulatory principles governing tumor–TME 770 interactions. 771 Despite these advances, several limitations remain. The reliance on predefined 772 spatial neighborhoods and fixed weighting may limit the modeling of long-range or 773 dynamic interactions. In addition, while in silico perturbation enables scalable 774 hypothesis generation, experimental validation is required for newly identified 775 regulators. The current framework is also limited to transcriptomic features and does 776
