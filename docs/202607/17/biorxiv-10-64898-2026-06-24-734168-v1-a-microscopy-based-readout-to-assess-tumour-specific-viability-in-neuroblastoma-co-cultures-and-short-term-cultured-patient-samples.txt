> Abstract

17 

> High-risk neuroblastoma patients face poor survival despite intensive treatment. Drug

18 

> testing  using  patient-derived  models  can  support  therapy  prioritization  for  precision

19 

> medicine  and  drug  development.  Models  incorporating  tumour  microenvironmental

20 

> components, such as co-cultures and short-term cultured patient samples containing

21 

> substantial  non-malignant  cell  fractions,  could  better  recapitulate  microenvironment-

22 

> dependent drug responses. However, conventional viability assays measure the combined

23 

> signal from all viable cells in a well and therefore cannot determine tumour-specific drug

24 

> responses. Here, we establish a microscopy-based readout to quantify cell-type-specific

25 

> viability in two complementary settings: neuroblastoma-PBMC co-cultures and freshly

26 

> dissociated patient tumour samples. In the co-cultures, PBMCs were pre-labelled with a cell-

27 

> tracking dye, and Calcein staining was used to independently quantify the viability of tumour

28

30

31

32

36

37

38 

> the microscopy-based viability readout for co-cultures and patient samples enables scalable

39 

> assessment of tumour-specific drug responses.

> Introduction

42 

> Neuroblastoma arises from neural crest progenitor cells of the developing sympathetic

43 

> nervous system and is the most common extra-cranial solid tumour in children. 1,2  Despite

44 

> intensive multimodal treatment, high-risk neuroblastoma remains associated with poor

45 

> outcome, with 5-year overall survival rates of approximately 50%. 2,3  In addition, survivors

46 

> often experience severe long-term treatment-related toxicity. 4,5  This highlights the need for

47 

> more effective and better tailored treatment strategies.

48 

> Patient-derived organoids are widely used for drug testing in neuroblastoma. 6–8

49 

> Organoid establishment usually takes several months, but once established, they are

50 

> scalable for high-throughput assays which makes them well suited for drug development. 7–9

51 

> Conventional  organoid  monocultures,  however,  lack  components  of  the  tumour

52 

> microenvironment, which can influence drug response and are essential for evaluating

53 

> immune-mediated therapies. 10–12  Co-culture models incorporating immune cells increase

54 

> physiological relevance while retaining scalability. However, these assays require readouts

55 

> that  distinguish  tumour  viability  from  immune-cell  viability  in  the  same  well. 13–16

56 

> Furthermore, drug testing on  ex vivo  short-term cultured patient material, containing

57 

> unknown tumour fractions, is increasingly being explored as a complementary approach to

58 

> molecular profiling for precision oncology. 8,17–20

59

60 

> metabolic activity from all cells present in the well and therefore cannot distinguish tumour

61 

> from non-malignant cells. 8,21,22  Genetic labelling strategies, such as luciferase expression, can

62 

> enable cell-type-specific readouts but require prior modification of the tumour cells which is

63 

> time- and labour-intensive and not feasible for primary patient samples. 16  Therefore,

64 

> scalable, non-genetic, cell-type-specific viability assays are needed to assess drug responses

65 

> in  physiologically  relevant  in  vitro  models.  Immunofluorescent  microscopy-based

66 

> approaches are increasingly compatible with high-throughput workflows and provide a

67 

> solution for tumour-specific viability measurements in co-cultures and patient-derived

68 

> samples. 21,23–25  Tumour-specific markers, already established in diagnostics and therapeutic

69 

> targeting, enable tumour-specific labelling in mixed cell populations. However, expression of

70 

> these individual markers is heterogeneous across neuroblastoma samples, which limits the

71 

> reliability of single-marker approaches. 26–29

72 

> By combining viability dyes with either pre-labelling of PBMCs or a cocktail of

73 

> tumour-specific surface markers, we establish a microscopy-based viability readout that

74 

> enables separate quantification of tumour and non-tumour viability in neuroblastoma-PBMC

75 

> co-cultures and patient samples.

> Neuroblastoma-immune co-cultures

93 

> In short, peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) were obtained from healthy donors

94 

> (Mini Donor Bank; institutional review board approval at UMCU: TC18-774), isolated using

95 

> Ficoll density gradient centrifugation 30  and cryopreserved at -180 °C until use.

96 

> Organoids were seeded in 384-well plates and co-cultured with PBMCs, which were pre-

97 

> labelled with CTO to enable distinction between tumour and immune cells during image

98 

> analysis. Co-cultures were established at an effector-to-target ratio of 1:5 and exposed to

99 

> compounds for 120 hours.

100 

> At readout, viable cells were stained using Calcein Violet (CalceinV) and imaged using the

101 

> high-content confocal microscope Opera Phenix (5x air objective; confocal mode). One fixed

102 

> Z-plane was selected based on comparison with a five-plane z-stack and its maximal

103 

> intensity projection. CellTiter-Glo 3D (CTG) assays were performed after imaging for multiple

104 

> plates, to benchmark the microscopy-based viability readout against conventional whole-

105 

> well viability measurements. 7,8,22

106 

> Image analysis was performed in Harmony software (Revvity). PBMCs were identified

107 

> as  CTO + regions. All viable cells were defined as CalceinV⁺ regions. Viable PBMCs were then

108 

> defined as overlapping CTO +/CalceinV + regions and viable tumour cells as CalceinV + regions

109 

> subtracting the viable PBMC mask. The viability (CalceinV) was quantified using the sum

110 

> pixel intensity of images. Compounds showing intrinsic autofluorescence that interfered

111 

> with signal detection (Suppl. Table 1) were excluded from downstream analyses in the

112 

> affected channel.

117

118

119 

> malignant cells, a neuroblastoma-specific antibody cocktail (NB-cocktail) targeting NCAM,

120 

> L1CAM and B7H3 conjugated to a far-red fluorophore was combined with the viability dye

> Results

133 

> Optimization of a microscopy-based viability readout

134 

> To  enable  microscopy-based  measurement  of  viability  in  neuroblastoma-immune  co-

135 

> cultures, we first optimized and validated technical aspects of the readout method. We

136 

> utilized two organoid lines derived from distinct tumour regions of the same patient:

137 

> AMC691T and AMC691B (Fig. 1A). 6 AMC691T represents the mesenchymal phenotype and

138 

> expresses MHC-I (HLA +), enabling interaction with immune cells, such as cytotoxic T cells and

139 

> natural  killer  cells.  In  contrast,  AMC691B  represents  the  more  typical  adrenergic

140 

> neuroblastoma phenotype and lacks MHC-I expression (HLA -), thereby limiting immune

141 

> recognition. This allowed us to model phenotypic heterogeneity regarding tumour-immune

142 

> interactions while keeping other properties of the models as similar as possible.

143 

> To validate CalceinV as a reliable measure of cell viability, we benchmarked this

144 

> imaging-based readout against the luminescence-based assay CTG, the current standard for

145 

> drug screens in monocultures of organoids. 8 Calcein Violet (CalceinV) reports on esterase

146 

> activity in viable cells, whereas CTG quantifies cellular ATP content (Suppl. Fig. 1A).

147 

> Microscopy-based CalceinV staining clearly distinguished viable (vehicle treated) from non-

148 

> viable (staurosporine treated) cells (Suppl. Fig. 1B). The coefficients of variation in untreated

149 

> wells  were  comparable  between  CalceinV  and  CTG  signals,  indicating  similar  assay

150 

> reproducibility for both readouts (Fig. 1B; Suppl. Fig. 1C).

151 

> .CC-BY-NC 4.0 International license available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted June 25, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.24.734168 doi: bioRxiv preprint To compare drug sensitivities in tumour monocultures, both organoid lines were

152 

> screened using a comprehensive drug library (n = 134 compounds). Comparing area under

153 

> the dose response curve values (AUC), CalceinV-derived drug sensitivity correlated strongly

154 

> with that of CTG (r = 0.88-0.96; Fig. 1C). Despite their phenotypic differences, overall drug

155 

> sensitivity  was  largely  conserved between  AMC691T  and  AMC691B, with  differential

156 

> responses limited to a small subset of compounds (Suppl. Fig. 1D). Eight compounds,

157 

> including prexasertib, showed higher efficacy (ΔAUC ≥ 100) in AMC691B (Suppl. Fig. 1E). In

158 

> contrast, five compounds, including cobimetinib, were more effective in AMC691T (Suppl.

159 

> Fig. 1E). Together, these results demonstrate that the microscopy-based readout provides a

160 

> quantitative and robust measure of cell viability and drug response.

162 

> Distinguishing cell type-specific viability in tumour-immune co-cultures

163 

> To assess cell-type-specific viability in co-culture conditions, peripheral blood mononuclear

164 

> cells (PBMCs) from healthy donors were pre-stained with the proliferation dye CellTracker

165 

> Orange (CTO) and distinguished from tumour cells using an image-based masking strategy

166 

> (Fig. 1D). Downstream analysis pipelines for co-cultures segmented viable organoids as

167 

> CalceinV +/CTO - regions and viable PBMCs as CalceinV +/CTO + regions. Cell-type specific

168 

> CalceinV sum intensities were extracted from both regions. We used this approach to

169 

> distinguish between viable and non-viable fractions for both cell types present in the co-

170 

> culture well.

171 

> We first assessed the baseline killing of the AMC691T and AMC691B by PBMCs.

172 

> Immune mediated differences were expected, since, in contrast to AMC691T, AMC691B is

173 

> HLA -, limiting T-cell recognition. Indeed, AMC691T showed reduced viability in a PBMC co-

174 

> culture compared to monoculture (Suppl. Fig. 2A), consistent with PBMC-mediated killing. To

175 

> achieve ~20% baseline killing, we tested multiple effector-to-target ratios and found 1:5

176 

> consistently optimal across three donors (Suppl. Fig. 2B). In contrast, the growth of

177 

> AMC691B was comparable with and without PBMCs (Suppl. Fig. 2C).

178 

> We assessed the stability of CTO labelling over the 120-hour assay duration, including

179 

> conditions of immune activation that may induce proliferation and subsequent dye dilution.

180 

> PBMC monocultures were stimulated via CD3 signalling to induce TCR-mediated activation.

181 

> Under these conditions, CTO loss was minimal: below 3% of viable cells misclassified as CTO -

182 

> (Fig. 1E). PBMCs were selected for pre-labelling rather than organoids because organoids are

183

It is made The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted June 25, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.24.734168 doi: bioRxiv preprint cultured for an additional 48 hours and generally display higher proliferative capacity,

184 

> potentially resulting in greater dye dilution. These results demonstrate that CTO pre-staining

185 

> enables stable and reliable identification of immune cells throughout the screening assay.

186 

> Finally,  to  enable  high-throughput  screening,  we  automated  the  readout  and

187 

> optimized imaging speed by selecting a single Z plane, as described in the methods section.

188 

> This reduced imaging time approximately two-fold, resulting in ~8 minutes acquisition time

189 

> per 384-well plate, without compromising signal intensity (Suppl. Fig. 2D-E). Collectively,

190 

> these validation experiments established a robust, scalable cell-type specific microscopy-

191 

> based readout suitable for high-throughput drug screening in neuroblastoma-immune co-

192 

> cultures.

> Identifying compound sensitivities in co-cultures using the microscopy-based readout

232 

> Having established and technically validated this microscopy-based readout for cell-type-

233 

> specific drug responses, we next applied it to high-throughput drug testing in organoid-

234 

> PBMC co-cultures (Fig. 2A). In short, organoids were seeded in 384-well plates. Two days

235 

> later ( t = 0 h), pre-labelled PBMCs were added to the organoids and compounds were added

236 

> for 120 hours. At readout, the viability dye CalceinV was added and automated image

237 

> acquisition was performed. The drug library was screened across both conditions in the two

238 

> organoid lines, AMC691T and AMC691B.

239 

> To identify compounds with altered efficacy in the presence of PBMCs, we compared

240 

> drug responses between mono- and co-culture conditions (Fig. 2B). A clear distinction was

241 

> observed between the two organoid models, in accordance with their immune status.

242 

> Specifically, there were several compounds with altered efficacy in co- versus monoculture

243 

> for AMC691T, whereas in AMC691B almost no differences were observed (Fig. 2B). In

244 

> AMC691T, five compounds showed increased efficacy in the co-culture (ΔAUC ≥ 50), and 22

245 

> compounds  showed reduced  efficacy, in  contrast  to  AMC691B  where  only  3 and  2

246 

> compounds  showed  increased  and  reduced  efficacy,  respectively.  No  overlapping

247 

> compounds with altered efficacy were identified between the two organoid lines. In

248 

> .CC-BY-NC 4.0 International license available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted June 25, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.24.734168 doi: bioRxiv preprint AMC691T, AZD5582 demonstrated greater efficacy in co-culture compared to monoculture

249 

> (ΔAUC = 197; Fig. 2C-D; Suppl. Fig. 3), suggesting a PBMC-mediated effect. In line with this,

250 

> AZD5582 did not exert toxic effects on PBMCs at concentrations below 10 μM (Fig. 2C).

251 

> Similarly, vinblastine showed increased efficacy in co-culture (ΔAUC = 50), with PBMCs

252 

> remaining viable at high concentrations (Fig. 2C-D; Suppl. Fig. 3). Together, these results

253 

> demonstrate the applicability of this microscopy-based approach to identify compounds

254 

> with  altered  efficacy  in  co-culture  conditions,  potentially  informing  the  selection  of

255 

> compounds for combination with immunotherapy.

256 

> To identify compounds with tumour-selective efficacy, we directly compared tumour

257 

> cell and PBMC responses from the same wells, as limiting PBMC toxicity may preserve

258 

> immune-mediated anti-tumour activity  in vivo . We identified 6 compounds that were

259 

> consistently more effective in tumour cells in both organoid lines (ΔAUC ≥ 100 in organoids

260 

> vs PBMCs in both lines), comprising 5 microtubule-targeting agents (docetaxel, paclitaxel,

261 

> vindesine, vinblastine and vincristine) and antimetabolite gemcitabine (Fig. 2E). For example,

262 

> docetaxel and paclitaxel induced tumour killing at low concentrations (0.001 μM and 0.01

263 

> μM, respectively), while PBMC viability was unaffected up to 10 μM (Fig. 2F-G). Additional

264 

> tumour-selective compounds were identified in only one of the two organoid lines, with two

265 

> compounds specific for AMC691T and 30 for AMC691B (Suppl. Fig. 4A). The AMC691B-

266 

> specific compounds included prexasertib, decitabine, alisertib and carboplatin, which were

267 

> already more effective in AMC691B monocultures and did not affect PBMC viability.

268 

> Together,  these  results  demonstrate  that  the  microscopy-based  readout  enables

269 

> identification  of  tumour-selective  compounds  that  may  be  relevant  for  combination

270 

> strategies with immunotherapies.

271 

> We next assessed compounds with comparable effects on tumour and immune cells,

272 

> as the toxicity of the compounds on the immune cells may be a reason to not apply them  in

273 

> vivo . Compounds were selected based on efficacy in both cell types (AUC < 400) and limited

274 

> differences in viability reduction between PBMCs and organoids (|ΔAUC| < 100). We

275 

> identified six compounds, the proteasome inhibitors bortezomib and ixazomib, the HDAC

276 

> inhibitors romidepsin and fimepinostat, β-catenin antagonist tegavivint and CDK12/13

277 

> inhibitor SR-4835, with similar activity in tumour cells and PBMCs across both organoid lines

278 

> (Suppl. Fig. 4B-C). No compounds were consistently more toxic to PBMCs versus organoids

279 

> across the full dose-response range (ΔAUC ≥ 100 in PBMCs versus organoid in both lines).

> Development and validation of a microscopy-based readout to determine tumour-specific

322 

> viability in patient-derived samples

323 

> .CC-BY-NC 4.0 International license available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted June 25, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.24.734168 doi: bioRxiv preprint In short-term cultured patient material, which still contains the tumour microenvironment, it

324 

> is essential to assess tumour-specific viability, similar to the co-cultures described above. To

325 

> distinguish tumour from their microenvironmental cells, we developed a microscopy-based

326 

> readout for use in primary patient material.

327 

> To enable drug testing in low-input patient samples, we optimized the assay for

328 

> reduced cell numbers per well. Standard flat-bottom assays typically require ~5000 cells per

329 

> well, which is not feasible for testing multiple compounds on minimally invasive biopsies. 8,22

330 

> Therefore, we adapted the assay to round-bottom 384-well plates to improve cell viability

331 

> and promote spheroid growth from a limited cell density (500 cells/well). Round-bottom

332 

> plates  pose  challenges  for  automated  microscopy  due  to  potential  focus  errors,  as

333 

> consecutive autofocus failures lead to termination of automated image acquisition. To

334 

> address this, we optimized imaging settings using the two organoid models by systematically

335 

> comparing objective magnification and numbers of field-of-view and selecting for the lowest

336 

> amount of focus errors. Imaging with a 10× objective and a single central field resulted in

337 

> zero focus errors, whereas imaging with multiple fields or the 5x magnification increased

338 

> error rates substantially ( n = 28 and 5, respectively; Suppl. Fig. 5A). Additionally, using only

339 

> one field of view imaged 42 wells in 4 minutes versus 13 minutes for the 4 fields of view

340 

> condition. Therefore, we selected the 10x objective with one central field of view, imaging

341 

> the centre of the well while excluding the well edges. These optimized imaging settings

342 

> enabled robust and fast image acquisition in round-bottom plates with a reduced cell input

343 

> of 500 cells per well.

344 

> Patient material is more complex than neuroblastoma-PBMC co-culture systems,

345 

> because tumour cells must be distinguished from multiple non-malignant cell types. As

346 

> these cells cannot be selectively pre-labelled prior to plating, tumour cells were identified

347 

> using a far-red-conjugated neuroblastoma-specific antibody cocktail targeting NCAM, L1CAM

348 

> and B7H3 (NB-cocktail). These markers are widely used in neuroblastoma diagnostics and

349 

> are being explored as therapeutic targets due to their high expression on tumour cells and

350 

> limited expression in surrounding stromal and immune cells. 26–29  By combining these

351 

> markers with the viability dye CalceinAM Green (CalceinAM), it is possible to identify viable

352 

> neuroblastoma cells as NB-cocktail +/CalceinAM + and non-neuroblastoma viable cells as NB-

353 

> cocktail -/CalceinAM + (Fig. 3A).

354 

> .CC-BY-NC 4.0 International license available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted June 25, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.24.734168 doi: bioRxiv preprint We first evaluated the tumour markers both individually and combined on two

355 

> organoid models by quantifying CalceinAM intensity within each marker-defined population.

356 

> The adrenergic organoid line AMC691B showed strong positivity for all three markers,

357 

> whereas its mesenchymal counterpart AMC691T was positive for B7H3 but showed minimal

358 

> expression of NCAM and L1CAM (Fig 3B-C; Suppl. Fig. 5B). Combining all three markers into a

359 

> single neuroblastoma-specific antibody cocktail resulted in robust detection of tumour cells

360 

> in both organoids while PBMC controls showed minimal signal (Fig. 3B). We then tested the

361 

> sensitivity and specificity of the NB-cocktail setup in a co-culture of AMC691B organoids

362 

> with pre-labelled PBMCs and observed that the NB-cocktail distinguished tumour from

363 

> immune cells (Suppl. Fig. 5C-E), with minimal overlap between the NB-cocktail and PBMC-

364 

> specific  eFluor450  signals.  The  combination  of  three  tumour  markers  is  particularly

365 

> advantageous for patient-derived samples, where (individual) marker expression is not

366 

> determined prior to the assay.

> Tumour-specific viability and drug responses in short-term cultured patient samples

369 

> To apply the established microscopy-based readout to  ex vivo  tumour material, we obtained

370 

> three neuroblastoma patient samples (two biopsies and one resection) and one melanocytic

371 

> neuroectodermal tumour of infancy sample (MNTI; resection; Suppl. Table 2). This rare

372 

> paediatric tumour was included to demonstrate broader applicability of the workflow, since

373 

> it was confirmed B7H3 positive by pathology.

374 

> We analysed the viability of the tumour and non-tumour fractions at baseline and

375 

> during short-term culture. The MNTI sample (MaxPatient4) consisted almost entirely of

376 

> tumour cells at  t = 0 h (99%), whereas tumour fractions varied substantially across the

377 

> neuroblastoma samples, ranging from 22% (MaxPatient3) to 66% (MaxPatient2), with

378 

> MaxPatient1 at 55% (Fig. 3D-E; Suppl. Fig. 6A-B). These findings underscore the importance

379 

> of tumour-specific viability measurements, as drug responses of tumour cells may be

380 

> masked by large non-malignant cell fractions. Marker expression also varied between

381 

> patients. MaxPatient2 showed higher expression of B7H3 and NCAM compared to L1CAM,

382 

> whereas  MaxPatient3  was  positive  for  L1CAM  and  NCAM,  but  showed  lower  B7H3

383 

> expression (Suppl. Fig. 6C). Together, these results support the use of the NB-cocktail in

384 

> patient samples with different marker expression profiles and demonstrate the applicability

385 

> of the readout to determine tumour fractions and tumour-specific viability.

386

387 

> drug responses in short-term cultured patient samples. Because of the limited amount of

388 

> tumour material, we selected a small number of compounds to test at a single concentration

389 

> as a proof-of-concept. Staurosporine was included as a cytotoxic compound. Vincristine was

390 

> selected based on the use in standard neuroblastoma treatment regimens and, together

391 

> with cytarabine, showed limited PBMC toxicity in our co-culture experiments. Due to the

392 

> limited availability of viable tumour cells in MaxPatient1, only a single compound was

393 

> tested. Viability was quantified within tumour-specific masks and compared with total cell

394 

> viability (Suppl. Fig. 7A). The low tumour fraction at  t = 0 h for MaxPatient3 (Fig. 3D; Suppl.

395 

> Fig. 6A) was reflected by a clear difference between total and tumour-specific viability at the

396 

> drug screen readout ( t = 72 h; Suppl. Fig. 7A). In contrast, the MNTI sample MaxPatient4

397

398 

> fraction. In addition, the neuroblastoma samples showed limited changes in viability during

399 

> the assay (75-115% viability from 0 h to 72 h), whereas the MNTI sample did proliferate

400 

> (146%).

401 

> Cytotoxicity could clearly be detected using this microscopy-based readout, as

402 

> staurosporine (10 μM) induced cell death compared to the vehicle control (0.25% DMSO;

403 

> Fig. 3F-G; Suppl. Fig. 7A-B). Tubulin inhibitor vincristine (1 μM) and DNA polymerase

404 

> inhibitor cytarabine (10 μM) showed limited efficacy in the neuroblastoma samples, likely

405 

> reflecting the limited cell proliferation (Suppl. Fig. 7A). In contrast, vincristine induced cell

406 

> killing in the MNTI sample, while cytarabine showed minimal activity. Finally, the MNTI

407 

> sample provided sufficient material for larger drug screen of 44 compounds across the full

408 

> dose-response range (Fig. 3H; Suppl. Fig. 7C). Mitoxantrone and daunorubicin were the most

409 

> effective compounds. The high tumour fraction allowed for direct comparison of CTG versus

410 

> CalceinAM based drug response  quantified within the tumour population, which was highly

411 

> concordant (r = 0.88; Suppl. Fig. 7D). Together, this data supports the feasibility and

412 

> specificity of this microscopy-based readout for the use of short-term cultured patient

413 

> samples to determine the tumour fraction over time and, if sufficient material is available, to

414 

> test tumour-specific drug response.

451 Discussion 452 

> To  advance  drug  testing  in  neuroblastoma,  a difficult-to-treat  paediatric  cancer,  we

453 

> developed a microscopy-based viability readout that enables assessment of drug responses

454 

> in the presence of the tumour microenvironment (TME), in both neuroblastoma-immune co-

455 

> cultures and short-term cultured patient samples. This is increasingly relevant as the TME

456 

> influences drug response and because immunomodulatory therapies are emerging for

457 

> neuroblastoma. 10–12  The  readout  strongly  correlated  with  conventional  viability

458 

> measurements  while  providing  additional  value  through  cell  type-specific  viability

459 

> quantification. 7,8

460 

461 

> The microscopy-readout for organoid-PBMC co-cultures enables direct comparison of drug

462 

> effects on tumour cells and PBMCs within the same well, in contrast to approaches that

463 

> assess PBMC toxicity in separate monoculture assays independently from tumour cell

464 

> viability. 8,31,32  Compounds effective against tumour cells either showed comparable PBMC

465 

> toxicity or largely spared PBMC viability. The latter may preserve anti-tumour immune

466 

> activity  in  vivo  and  thereby  support  combination  strategies  with  immunotherapies.

467 

> .CC-BY-NC 4.0 International license available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted June 25, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.24.734168 doi: bioRxiv preprint Microtubule-targeting agents were especially effective against tumour cells and had little

468 

> effect on PBMC viability. These agents disrupt microtubule dynamics, leading to mitotic

469 

> arrest  and  apoptosis,  processes  to  which  highly  proliferative  cells  are  particularly

470 

> vulnerable. 33,34  Both organoid lines proliferated substantially more than PBMCs  in vitro ,

471 

> which may partly explain their dependency on microtubule dynamics and the resulting

472 

> cytotoxic selectivity. Although PBMC viability represents only a limited measure of non-

473 

> tumour toxicity, this high-throughput workflow supports identification of tumour-selective

474 

> compounds to be further validated in future studies.

475 

> Several  compounds  showed  increased  efficacy  in  co-culture  compared  to

476 

> monoculture, including the apoptosis protein antagonist AZD5582 and microtubule targeting

477 

> agent vinblastine. Previous studies have indicated immunomodulatory effects for AZD5582,

478 

> including  enhanced  tumour  infiltration  by  CD8+  T-cells,  reduced  regulatory  T-cell

479 

> abundance, dendritic cell activation and improved T-cell priming. 35–37  Similarly, vinblastine

480 

> has been shown to stimulate anti-tumour immune responses through activation of dendritic

481 

> cells, although dendritic cells are present at low frequencies in PBMCs. 38  These findings

482 

> illustrate the value of the co-culture screen in scalable detection of compounds whose

483 

> efficacy  is  enhanced  by  the  presence  of  immune  cells.  Prioritized  candidates  can

484 

> subsequently be characterized using immune profiling approaches, including evaluation of

485 

> their effects on different immune cell subsets, cytokine production and activation and

486 

> exhaustion markers. 39

487 

> The co-culture readout used allogeneic PBMCs from healthy adult donors to allow

488 

> standardization, reproducibility and scalability, but these may not fully recapitulate the

489 

> immune composition and dysfunction observed in neuroblastoma patients. 12,40  Future

490 

> studies  could  incorporate  autologous  patient-derived  immune  cells,  although  limited

491 

> material  from  minimally  invasive  paediatric  procedures  remains  a challenge.  PBMCs

492 

> represent only part of the TME and incorporating additional cell types such as stromal cells

493 

> would increase physiological relevance. The workflow is also suited for studying emerging

494 

> immune-mediated  therapies,  such  as  CAR-T  cells,  which  are  increasingly  relevant  in

495 

> neuroblastoma and could be incorporated into co-culture screens. 12,41  This co-culture

496 

> workflow provides a scalable assay for tumour-immune drug screening, with potential to

497 

> inform both immunotherapy development and tumour-selective compound prioritization in

498 

> neuroblastoma.

500 

> We extended the microscopy-based viability readout to short-term cultured patient material

501 

> to quantify tumour fraction and tumour-specific drug response in mixed cell populations.

502 

> This addresses an important limitation of current  ex vivo  drug screening approaches, which

503 

> rely on whole-well viability measurements and cannot distinguish tumour from non-

504 

> malignant cells. 42,19

505 

> Combining neuroblastoma-associated markers (L1CAM, NCAM and B7H3) into a

506 

> cocktail enabled distinction of tumour cells from surrounding cells, compensating for intra-

507 

> and interpatient variability in surface marker expression. Although these markers are widely

508 

> used neuroblastoma markers, NCAM can also be expressed by immune cells (particularly NK

509 

> cells) and B7H3  can be expressed by myeloid and endothelial  cells  in  the  tumour-

510 

> microenvironment. 26–29,43–45  Using a threshold-based separation limits false tumour-positive

511 

> signals, as neuroblastoma cells have higher surface expression than immune cells. This

512 

> microscopy-based viability readout for patient samples can be readily adapted to other

513 

> tumour types by substituting tumour-specific markers.

514 

> Limited viable tumour material remains a challenge for  ex vivo  short-term patient

515 

> screens. 8,19,22,42  Therefore, we reduced the cell density per well. Neuroblastoma patient

516 

> samples retained non-malignant cells during short-term culture, showing variable tumour

517 

> fractions (22-66%), but tumour cells showed little to no proliferation during the assay, which

518 

> likely  contributed  to  the  limited  compound  responses  observed.  Longer  pre-culture

519 

> durations  may  increase  viable  tumour  cell  numbers  and  proliferation  rates  but

520 

> simultaneously change TME representation, as illustrated in the MNTI sample. After one

521 

> month of culture, the MNTI sample consisted almost entirely of tumour cells with an

522 

> increased proliferation rate, providing more viable tumour material for screening that

523 

> enabled  testing  of a  larger  compound panel.  The balance between  preserving  TME

524 

> components and obtaining sufficient tumour material requires optimization based on the

525 

> research question and available material.

526 

> Current precision oncology initiatives such as INFORM and ZERO rely on whole-well

527 

> viability measurements for  ex vivo  drug testing. 19,20  For cultures containing both tumour and

528 

> non-malignant cells, tumour-specific viability assessment provides additional  value by

529 

> distinguishing tumour-specific drug responses from effects on surrounding non-malignant

530 

> cells. The predictive value of  ex vivo  drug screening for treatment response in patients
