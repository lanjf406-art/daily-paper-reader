Abstract 18 

Background: Perturb-seq captures transcriptional responses to thousands of 19 

genetic and chemical perturbations, but does not directly resolve the cis-regulatory 20 

elements or transcription factor motifs underlying those responses. Existing 21 

approaches rely on indirect post hoc analyses or external epigenomic annotations, 22 

making it difficult to connect gene-level responses to specific regulatory element 23 

24 

Results: We present GenPerturb, a framework that leverages pretrained sequence-25 

to-expression models to link perturbation-induced expression changes to candidate 26 

cis-regulatory elements. By contrasting perturbation and control states, GenPerturb 27 

prioritizes regulatory regions and transcription factor motifs associated with each 28 

perturbation. The model recapitulates perturbation-dependent gene expression 29 

patterns and enables sequence-level interpretation without requiring matched 30 

chromatin data. Across multiple perturbation types, GenPerturb identifies biologically 31 

meaningful regulatory programs, including lineage-specific and signaling-associated 32 

motif activities, even when corresponding transcription factor expression changes 33 

are limited. 34 

35 

Conclusions: GenPerturb converts gene-level expression responses from Perturb-36 

seq into perturbation-specific, sequence-grounded cis-regulatory hypotheses. By 37 

prioritizing candidate regulatory elements and transcription factor motifs responsive 38 

to each perturbation without requiring matched chromatin data, GenPerturb enables 39 

mechanistic interpretation of transcriptional regulation and guides downstream 40 

experimental validation. 41

Background 47 

Large-scale perturbation profiling approaches such as Perturb-seq have 48 

enabled the measurement of transcriptional responses to hundreds to thousands of 49 

genetic perturbations at single-cell resolution [1–2]. However, Perturb-seq captures 50 

only the gene expression changes following perturbation and does not provide direct 51 

access to the underlying transcriptional regulatory states, such as which transcription 52 

factors are activated or which cis-regulatory elements are involved. In other words, 53 

modeling transcriptional regulatory states based solely on gene expression data is 54 

inherently limited, and additional information, including chromatin states and cis-55 

regulatory context, is fundamentally required. 56 

To address this limitation, a variety of approaches based on existing genome 57 

annotations and prior knowledge networks—such as motif enrichment analysis, 58 

enhancer–gene linking, and regulon inference—have been widely used [3–8]. While 59 

these methods provide effective strategies for indirectly inferring regulatory states, 60 

they often rely on annotations derived from specific cell types or steady-state 61 

conditions. As a result, they may not fully capture context-dependent regulatory 62 

states in newly generated datasets, particularly under perturbation conditions. Given 63 

these limitations, an ideal approach would be to simultaneously measure gene 64 

expression and chromatin states within the same cell, thereby directly linking 65 

transcriptional responses to their regulatory basis. As an experimental solution to this 66 

problem, Multiome Perturb-seq enables the simultaneous measurement of gene 67 

expression and chromatin accessibility in the same cell following perturbation [9–12]. 68 

This approach allows transcriptional responses to be directly associated with 69 

chromatin changes at regulatory regions. However, the addition of epigenomic 70 

measurements introduces constraints in terms of experimental cost and throughput, 71

to a broader perturbation space, including existing datasets, without requiring 75

between gene expression and the underlying sequence information. 77 

A promising computational framework for this purpose is sequence-to-78 

expression modeling, which directly predicts gene expression and chromatin states 79 

from DNA sequences. Models such as Enformer, Borzoi, and AlphaGenome 80 

leverage long-range sequence context spanning hundreds of kilobases and are 81 

capable of capturing regulatory sequence features associated with molecular 82 

phenotypes [13–15]. Through attribution analysis, these models can identify 83 

sequence elements that contribute to their predictions [16-17]. However, these 84 

models are primarily trained on steady-state data, and their attribution outputs reflect 85 

sequence features important for gene regulation under non-perturbed conditions. 86 

Consequently, identifying regulatory elements that are newly engaged or repressed 87 

in response to perturbations requires a framework that explicitly extracts 88 

perturbation-dependent sequence contributions by comparing with control 89 

conditions. 90 

To address this challenge, we propose GenPerturb, an analytical framework 91 

for deriving sequence-grounded regulatory hypotheses from observed Perturb-seq 92 

responses (Fig. 1). GenPerturb uses pretrained sequence-to-expression models as 93 

regulatory sequence representations and adapts them to a target RNA-only Perturb-94 

seq dataset by fitting observed expression outputs for the control condition and each 95 

perturbation condition. For each gene, control and perturbation outputs are fitted 96

sequence-grounded regulatory hypotheses without requiring matched ATAC-seq, 106 

ChIP-seq, or multiome data from the target experiment (see Supplementary Table 1 107

Results 127 

GenPerturb anchors control and perturbation responses to shared sequence 128 

coordinates for differential attribution 129 

GenPerturb was designed to model perturbation-resolved expression 130 

responses within a shared genomic sequence framework (Fig. 1). For each gene, 131 

pretrained sequence representations are fitted to control and perturbation-resolved 132 

expression outputs, so that all condition-specific outputs can be compared on the 133 

same genomic input (Fig. 1a; Fig. S1). After fitting, GenPerturb compares output-to-134 

input attribution profiles between perturbation and control outputs on the same 135 

sequence coordinates. This control-subtracted differential attribution provides the 136 

basis for ranking perturbation-associated candidate regions and motif-level 137 

sequence patterns (Fig. 1b). 138 

We further use in silico motif mutation as a model-internal sensitivity 139 

analysis, testing whether ranked motif instances alter the fitted perturbation-control 140 

difference (Fig. 1c). For each gene-perturbation pair, GenPerturb thus produces 141 

ranked candidate enhancer-like regions and motif instances that can be used for 142 

downstream regulatory interpretation and integration with existing enhancer or motif 143 

annotations. 144

complementary validation layers throughout the remainder of the study: (i) retention 146

Differential attribution prioritizes perturbation-associated cis-regulatory 230 

elements 231 

Having established that GenPerturb preserves perturbation-dependent 232 

expression structure, we next examined whether differential attribution can highlight 233 

cis-regulatory elements associated with perturbation responses. This step is 234 

designed to go beyond post hoc motif enrichment of differentially expressed genes 235 

by asking which sequence elements and motif instances at the corresponding gene 236 

loci are candidates for contributing to each fitted perturbation-associated response. 237 

To define an external benchmark for enhancer-oriented validation, we surveyed 238 

publicly available perturbation multiome resources. Although several candidate 239 

datasets contained paired RNA and chromatin measurements, most could not be 240 

used for quantitative perturbation-resolved enhancer evaluation because 241 

perturbation identities, sufficient perturbation-associated expression changes, or 242 

corresponding perturbation-associated ATAC changes were not available at the 243 

required scale (Supplementary Table 3). We therefore used the Martin et al. 244 

erythroid Perturb-multiome dataset as the primary external benchmark, as it provides 245 

perturbation-resolved chromatin accessibility together with enhancer-gene reference 246

Discussion 443 

In this study, we introduced GenPerturb, a model-based interpretation 444 

framework that converts gene-level perturbation responses into element-level, locus-445 

anchored cis-regulatory hypotheses using pretrained sequence representations. By 446

comparing attribution profiles between control and perturbation states, GenPerturb 448 

prioritizes candidate regulatory regions, motif instances, and perturbation-associated 449 

regulatory programs from Perturb-seq data (Fig. 1). The main contribution is the 450 

translation of gene-level perturbation responses into locus-resolved, sequence-451 

grounded regulatory hypotheses. 452 

This framework addresses a central limitation of standard Perturb-seq 453 

analysis [1–2]. While Perturb-seq captures downstream transcriptional responses, it 454 

does not directly resolve the underlying cis-regulatory elements or sequence motifs. 455 

Conventional approaches typically rely on post hoc analyses, such as differential 456 

expression, pathway enrichment, and motif enrichment [3–8]. In contrast, 457 

GenPerturb directly links perturbation responses to regulatory sequence features 458 

within a shared sequence-based framework, enabling interpretation at the level of 459 

candidate regulatory regions and motif instances. Rather than asking which motifs 460 

are enriched in responsive gene sets, GenPerturb identifies motif instances at 461 

specific loci associated with perturbation responses. 462 

Our results support three main conclusions. First, pretrained sequence 463 

representations can be transferred to Perturb-seq datasets and retain meaningful 464 

perturbation-dependent expression structure across CRISPR activation, interference, 465 

knockout, and compound perturbation datasets. Comparisons with Enformer, Borzoi, 466 

AlphaGenome, and a Simple CNN baseline highlight the importance of pretrained 467 

regulatory features [13–15]. Notably, alignment to the target assay and cell state 468 

requires dataset-specific fitting, indicating that pretrained outputs alone are 469 

insufficient. Thus, GenPerturb uses foundation sequence models as transferable 470 

regulatory representations for interpreting perturbation responses, rather than as 471

can recover biologically meaningful signals. Unlike annotation-based approaches 482

perturbation dataset and identifies sequence features whose contribution changes 484

bridging sequence modeling, enhancer annotation, and regulon inference. This 505 

approach complements multiomic regulatory inference methods and differs from 506 

observational sequence-to-expression models by explicitly contrasting fitted control 507 

and perturbation outputs [40–43]. It is particularly useful for retrospective analysis of 508 

RNA-only Perturb-seq datasets lacking matched chromatin data. 509

observed perturbation conditions within a specific cell type and is not designed to 511 

predict responses to unseen perturbations or cell types, but rather to interpret 512 

responses within a defined experimental context [44]. Second, attribution reflects 513 

model-derived interpretation rather than direct measurement of enhancer activity, 514 

transcription factor binding, chromatin accessibility, or protein-level regulatory 515 

activity; prioritized regions and motif instances should therefore be regarded as 516 

hypotheses requiring experimental validation [45]. This is particularly relevant in 517 

compound perturbation settings, where motif-level signals can nominate candidate 518 

regulatory programs but do not by themselves establish targets or mechanisms of 519 

action. Third, motif interpretation is constrained by motif degeneracy and 520 

transcription factor family ambiguity, limiting unique TF assignment. Fourth, 521

may reduce interpretability. 531 

In summary, GenPerturb provides a practical framework for extracting 532 

sequence-grounded regulatory hypotheses from RNA-only perturbation data by 533 

integrating pretrained sequence representations, perturbation-resolved modeling, 534 

and differential attribution. 535

Methods 707 

Data preprocessing for perturbation data 708 

We assembled twelve perturbation datasets from five published studies covering 709 

CRISPR activation, CRISPR interference, CRISPR knockout, and small-molecule 710 

perturbations: Norman et al. K562 CRISPRa [20], Replogle et al. K562 genome-wide 711 

and essential-gene CRISPRi screens and an RPE1 essential-gene CRISPRi screen 712 

[21], Martin et al. erythroid-lineage Perturb-multiome [12], Jiang et al. compound 713 

perturbations in primary CD4 T, CD8 T, B, and myeloid cells [22], and Srivatsan et 714 

al. sci-Plex compound perturbations in A549, K562, and MCF7 [23]. Processed 715 

single-cell objects for Norman, Replogle, and Srivatsan were obtained from 716 

scPerturb [46] and the sc-pert repository (https://github.com/theislab/sc-pert). The 717 

Jiang et al. datasets were downloaded from the original study repository 718 

(GSE217460, https://data.caltech.edu/records/2cjss-wgh69). The Martin et al. 719 

erythroid Perturb-multiome data were downloaded from GEO (GSE274113). 720 

For CRISPR datasets, unperturbed cells (those that failed gRNA-induced expression 721 

change of the target gene) were excluded using Mixscape [47] as implemented in 722 

pertpy (v0.10.0) [48]. Highly variable genes were defined within each gRNA cell 723 

group using `scanpy.pp.highly_variable_genes` with `min_disp = 0.2` (Scanpy 724 

v1.11.2) [49], and the union of HVGs across gRNA groups was retained. 725 

For all datasets, Pseudobulk expression matrices were generated using adpbulk 726 

(v0.1.4) with `method = "sum"`, summing UMI counts per perturbation condition. 727 

Perturbation conditions represented by 100 cells or fewer after Mixscape filtering 728 

were removed to ensure stable condition-level expression estimates. Pseudobulk 729 

counts were CPM-normalized and log2(x + 1)-transformed, and only genes with 730 

CPM > 2 in at least one perturbation condition were retained. 731

Martin et al. erythroid Perturb-multiome dataset was processed through the same 737 

Mixscape, HVG, pseudobulking, CPM normalization, log2 transform, and gene-filter 738 

steps. For the matched ATAC-seq modality, cells passing the RNA-seq–based 739 

filtering and perturbation-label assignment steps were retained, and ATAC profiles 740 

were pseudobulked using the corresponding perturbation-condition labels. 741 

Control conditions were defined according to each original study: non-targeting 742 

gRNA for Norman, Replogle, and Martin et al.; the drug-free control condition for 743 

Jiang et al.; and vehicle (DMSO) for Srivatsan et al. 744

Reference genome and training/validation/test split 746 

The human reference genome was GRCh38.p14, and gene models were obtained 747 

from the GENCODE v46 basic annotation [50]. Only protein-coding mRNAs and 748 

lncRNAs were retained. For each gene, one representative transcription start site 749 

(TSS) was selected by prioritizing transcripts annotated as Ensembl canonical; when 750

Model architecture 789 

For all backbones the prediction head was a multi-output linear layer mapping the 790 

per-gene embedding to a vector with one element per perturbation condition, 791 

followed by a ReLU activation. The output dimensionality equals the number of 792 

perturbation conditions in the dataset, comprising every perturbation together with 793 

the non-targeting control, and output values correspond to per-gene log2(CPM + 1) 794 

expression for each condition. Importantly, perturbation identity is not provided as an 795 

input feature; perturbation-dependent responses are represented entirely as 796 

differences among the condition-specific output channels anchored to the same 797 

gene locus and sequence coordinate. The same head architecture was used across 798 

AlphaGenome, Enformer, Borzoi, and the Simple CNN baseline, differing only in 799 

input dimensionality. 800
