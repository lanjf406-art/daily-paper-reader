Summary 14 

Modeling cellular responses to genetic perturbations is a significant challenge 15 

in computational biology. Measuring all gene perturbations and their combina-16 

tions across cell types and conditions is experimentally challenging, highlighting 17 

the need for predictive models that generalize across data types to support this 18 

task. Here we present MORPH, a MOdular framework for predicting Responses 19 

to Perturbational cHanges. MORPH combines a discrepancy-based variational 20 

autoencoder with an attention mechanism to predict cellular responses to unseen 21 

perturbations. It supports both single-cell transcriptomics and imaging outputs 22 

and can generalize to unseen perturbations, combinations of perturbations, and 23 

perturbations in new cellular contexts. The attention-based framework enables 24 

inference of gene interactions and regulatory networks, while the learned gene 25 

embeddings can guide the design of informative perturbations, as demonstrated 26 

in two applications. Overall, MORPH is a flexible tool for optimizing perturbation 27 

experiments, enabling efficient exploration of the perturbation space to advance 28 

understanding of cellular programs for fundamental research and therapeutic 29 

applications. 30

Single-cell sequencing and imaging technologies have revolutionized our understanding 32 

of cellular heterogeneity, enabling detailed characterization of various cell states and 33 

functions at scale [1]. Coupled with CRISPR-based perturbation platforms and multi-34 

plexed barcoding strategies, these technologies now allow systematic, high-dimensional 35 

mapping of the heterogeneous cellular responses to individual genetic perturba-36 

tions. Several methods combine genetic perturbation with single-cell RNA sequencing 37 

(scRNA-seq) as a readout for the gene expression changes [2–7], while optical pooled 38 

screens capture cellular phenotypes through imaging data [8–10]. Understanding these 39 

perturbation-induced effects not only provides insights into the structure of gene reg-40 

ulatory networks but also has significant implications for biomedical research, such as 41 

identifying potential therapeutic targets that could drive cells to a desired state [11–13] 42 

and optimizing combination therapies [14–16]. 43 

While the availability of single-cell perturbation data is growing, experimentally 44 

testing all individual gene perturbations – and their combinations – across all pos-45 

sible cell types, states, and diseases contexts remains unfeasible. Therefore, there is 46 

a need for computational methods that leverage the existing data to predict pertur-47 

bation effects across different conditions and data types [17, 18]. Such models would 48 

be crucial for efficiently exploring the vast perturbational space and prioritizing the 49 

most informative experiments to perform. The urgency of this need is reflected in 50 

two recent community efforts: the Cell Perturbation Prediction Challenge launched 51 

in 2023 with a first competition focused on predicting gene perturbation effects on 52 

T-cell state distributions [19]; and the Virtual Cell Challenge, launched in 2025 to 53 

benchmark cross-context generalization at scale [20]. 54 

Current methods for this task [21, 22] perform well in approximating the state 55 

of perturbed cells on average but struggle to capture the full distribution of cellular 56 

responses, and often rely on randomly pairing control and perturbed cells to address 57 

the inherent unpaired nature of high-throughput sequencing data—a fundamental 58 

challenge arising from the destructive nature of these assays. Additionally, many exist-59 

ing methods assume that the effects of gene perturbations are additive [23, 24], limiting 60 

their ability to capture complex, non-additive interactions. More recently, methods 61 

developed concurrently with this work [25], have demonstrated that out-of-distribution 62 

prediction and transfer to unseen cell lines are achievable for transcriptomic data. How-63 

ever, the ability to predict the perturbation outcomes measured in modalities other 64 

than transcriptomics remains largely unexplored, as does the question of model inter-65 

pretability: the extent to which learned representations reflect biologically meaningful 66 

gene relationships rather than opaque mappings. 67 

To address these challenges, we developed MORPH, a MOdular framework for pre-68 

dicting Responses to Perturbational cHanges. Its modular design enables MORPH to 69 

adapt seamlessly to various data modalities, such as transcriptomic or imaging data. 70 

Given single-cell data on control cells and prior knowledge of gene perturbations, 71 

MORPH leverages a conditional variational autoencoder with an attention mechanism 72 

to predict the distribution of cellular responses to unseen perturbations either in tran-73 

scriptomic or imaging modality. We demonstrate that MORPH generalizes to unseen 74 

single and combinatorial perturbations, transfers to new cell lines using only control 75

MORPH enables the prediction of cellular responses to genetic 95 

perturbations at single-cell level 96 

MORPH is designed to predict the effect of a genetic perturbation on an individual 97 

cell. We represent a cell as a vector X· encoding any high-dimensional readout, such 98 

as gene expression profiles from RNA sequencing or morphological features extracted 99 

from imaging. We encode prior knowledge about a gene g of interest (such as pathway 100 

associations, gene interactions, etc.) and its perturbation effect as an embedding vector 101 

vg . This embedding allows MORPH to relate unseen perturbations to previously seen 102 

ones, based on the intuition that perturbations with similar biological properties tend 103 

to have similar effects. Crucially, these embeddings are derived from external data 104 

sources and do not use the target Perturb-seq data, ensuring no leakage of single-cell 105 

perturbation–response information from held-out perturbations. Since these priors are 106 

defined at the level of individual genes and do not encode multi-gene perturbations, 107 

MORPH models combinatorial perturbations by composing individual latent gene 108 

representations within its architecture (Figure 1a). 109 

Formally, given a control cell X∅, an embedding vg for gene g, MORPH learns 110 

a function f that maps the pair ( X∅, vg ) to the predicted perturbed cell ˆXg , i.e., 111 

f (X∅, vg ) = ˆXg . For each gene perturbation in the dataset, MORPH compares the 112 

predicted perturbed distribution of ˆXg to the actual perturbed distribution of Xg ,113 

enabling it to learn the mapping f from unpaired data. This is achieved using max-114 

imum mean discrepancy (MMD) between the predicted cell distribution ˆXg and the 115 

actual cell distribution of Xg (Methods). If combinatorial perturbation data is avail-116 

able (e.g. on genes g1 and g2), this is generalized by comparing f (X∅, vg1 , vg2 ) = 117 

ˆXg1,g 2 to the actual perturbed distribution of Xg1,g 2 .118

To realize the mapping f , MORPH adopts a variational autoencoder (VAE)-based 119 

architecture. A control cell X∅ and gene perturbation embedding vector vg are first 120 

embedded into latent spaces using two separate encoders. To enhance interpretability 121 

and reliability, these latent representations are passed through an attention mecha-122 

nism that guides the model to focus on the most relevant aspects of the input data, 123 

before being decoded into the predicted perturbed cell ˆXg . All encoders and decoders 124 

are implemented as multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) (Figure 1a, Methods). The model 125 

is trained using a variational autoencoder loss on the control cells X∅ and MMD 126 

between the predicted control/perturbed cell distributions and the actual control/per-127 

turbed cell distributions (Methods). Using MMD also for the control cells promotes 128 

attention score changes that more faithfully reflect the magnitude of perturbation 129 

effects (Supplemental Fig. 1). 130 

The design of our attention mechanism was motivated by a previous study [2] 131 

that had represented the effects of genetic perturbations using a bipartite regulatory 132 

network, where perturbations influence the expression of regulated genes. Clustering 133 

perturbations based on shared regulated genes reveals perturbation modules — groups 134 

of perturbations that produce similar cellular effects. Similarly, clustering genes based 135 

on their co-regulation by perturbations yields gene programs — sets of genes that 136 

respond in a coordinated way. These perturbation modules and gene programs reflect 137 

the underlying structure of gene regulation. MORPH integrates a cross-attention 138 

mechanism to capture the regulation from perturbations to gene programs (Meth-139 

ods). It constructs queries by concatenating the latent vectors obtained by encoding 140 

the control cell and the perturbation embedding. These queries attend to a learned 141 

gene-program matrix that serves as keys and values, producing attention scores that 142 

modulate the influence of each gene program on the final prediction through the 143 

network’s non-linear feed-forward layers (Figure 1a). To biologically interpret this non-144 

linear process, we utilized an end-to-end probing procedure that maps these programs 145 

directly to gene expression (Methods). The gene-program matrix is shared across all 146 

cells of the same type and remains fixed at inference time, under the assumption that 147 

gene programs are relatively stable within a given cell type, while their activation 148 

depends on the specific perturbation and the initial state of a cell. 149 

These design choices enable MORPH to address fundamental questions about 150 

genetic perturbations. We demonstrate that MORPH can predict cellular responses to 151 

unseen perturbations in both transcriptomic and imaging data, highlighting its ver-152 

satility in handling various data modalities. Moreover, it can transfer perturbation 153 

effects across cell types, extrapolate genetic interaction types, optimize the design 154 

of genetic perturbations, and facilitate the investigation of genetic regulatory net-155 

works (Figure 1b). In the following sections, we evaluate each of these capabilities and 156 

illustrate how they can be combined for practical applications. 157

We tested the model on two types of data splits (Figure 2b-c and Supplemental 168 

Figure 2). The first uses a standard 5-fold cross-validation, where the dataset was 169 

divided into five equal sets of perturbations. In each iteration, one set of perturbations 170 

served as the test set, and the remaining four were used for training. This process 171 

was repeated for all five folds, and the performance metrics were averaged to evaluate 172 

the model. The second, termed the “outlier distribution split”, specifically included 173 

perturbations producing phenotypes that were most distinct from the control distri-174 

bution in the test split. This was achieved by performing Leiden clustering on the 175 

pseudo-bulk profiles of each perturbation and identifying the clusters whose centers 176 

were farthest from the cluster that contained control, measured by Euclidean distance 177 

between averaged bulk expressions. This split was designed to assess the ability of 178 

the model to generalize to unseen perturbations that induce significantly distinct pro-179 

files, providing a stricter stress test for generalization beyond standard cross-validation 180 

(Supplemental Figure 2). 181 

To assess performance, we used three primary metrics (Figure 2b-c, Methods). 182 

Root mean squared error (RMSE) was used to quantify the mean difference between 183 

predictions and observations; Pearson correlation (delta) was used to measure the 184 

agreement between predicted and observed mean changes relative to the mean of 185 

perturbed training cells [27]; Spearman correlation was used as a complementary rank-186 

based metric. In Supplemental Figures 3–6, we also reported the retrieval rank, defined 187 

as the fraction of other perturbations whose correlation with a predicted effect does not 188 

exceed its self-correlation (Methods), yielding scores between 0 and 1. In addition to 189 

these mean-based metrics, we calculated the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) to 190 

evaluate distributional differences between predicted and true effects in gene expression 191 

observed in individual perturbed cells. Lower MMD values indicate closer alignment 192 

between the two cell distributions. This metric offers more fine-grained information 193 

than metrics that compare only mean gene expression. We calculated these metrics 194 

using the top 50 differentially expressed (DE) genes for each perturbation for a more 195 

focused evaluation (Figure 2 and Supplemental Figures 4-5) as well as using all genes 196 

(Supplemental Figure 6). 197

Finally, we found that MORPH remains robust to statistical perturbations of 271 

the input data. We retrained MORPH on K562-EG under gene masking (randomly 272 

zero-masking 10% or 90% of genes in control cells) and library-size rescaling (total 273 

counts of 20,000 or 100,000). Across all settings, predictive performance remained 274 

strong (Supplemental Table 2). Retrieval rank was evaluated on both the top 50 DE 275 

genes and the full gene set, as this metric is directly comparable across perturbation 276 

settings that alter expression scale. To further assess architectural robustness, we eval-277 

uated MORPH across a range of latent dimensions and decoder depths. Performance 278 

remained stable across configurations, with no single model size consistently outper-279 

forming others (Supplemental Figure 9). Together, these results indicate that MORPH 280 

is robust to relevant variations in both input preprocessing and model capacity. 281 

In summary, we demonstrated that MORPH outperforms prior methods in 282 

predicting cellular responses to unseen single-gene perturbations, achieving robust 283 

performance across all evaluations and particularly excelling at capturing single-cell 284 

response distributions (MMD). DepMap embeddings consistently provided the most 285 

accurate predictions, particularly in challenging scenarios that require generalizing to 286

To evaluate MORPH’s ability to transfer perturbation effects across contexts, we 296 

trained it on RPE1-EG perturbation data and assessed its transferability to K562-EG 297 

(both datasets were obtained from Replogle et al. [7]). The model was first trained on 298 

all RPE1 perturbations, then fine-tuned using only K562 control cells by minimizing 299 

reconstruction loss (Methods). Fine-tuning on the control cells helps the model adapt 300 

to the new cell line’s basal state. For comparison, we included two baseline models: (1) 301 

the control distribution, which predicts the control cells in K562 without perturbation 302 

effects, and (2) the mean shift model, which applies the same perturbation-induced 303 

pseudobulk changes from RPE1 controls to K562 controls to predict perturbed cells in 304 

K562. Our results demonstrate that MORPH trained on RPE1 and fine-tuned on the 305 

control cells from K562 substantially outperforms both baseline models (Figure 3a). 306 

In fact, this model achieves performance comparable to MORPH trained directly on a 307 

subset of random 100 perturbations from K562. Furthermore, MORPH pretrained on 308 

RPE1 and then fine-tuned on 100 perturbations from K562 improves the performance 309 

to a level similar to MORPH trained on 1,500 perturbations from K562, suggesting 310 

that this transfer learning scheme enables MORPH to reach similar performance using 311 

only 7% (100/1,500) of the data. 312 

By default, all model parameters were updated during this fine-tuning process. To 313 

identify which components drive successful transfer, we conducted an ablation study 314 

by fine-tuning specific modules independently. We found that fine-tuning only the 315 

decoder achieved performance comparable to updating the full model, whereas fine-316 

tuning only the encoder or the gene-program matrix was insufficient (Supplemental 317 

Figure 10). This indicates that adapting the mapping from latent representations to 318 

gene expression in the target cell line (i.e., the decoder) is critical for effective transfer. 319 

We hypothesized that perturbations transferring well across cell lines are those 320 

involving pathways or gene sets that are more similar between the cell lines. To test 321 

this hypothesis, we defined a cell line disagreement metric by calculating the Euclidean 322 

distance between the mean shift vectors for each perturbation in K562 and RPE1. We 323 

then compared the prediction performance of the model fine-tuned only on control cells 324 

with this cell line disagreement metric. We observed a positive correlation between 325 

cell line disagreement and prediction loss (Pearson correlation = 0.52, p-value < 0.05), 326 

indicating that perturbations affecting pathways with dissimilar effects between the 327 

two cell lines are harder to predict (Figure 3b). 328 

By analyzing the gene sets with higher cell line disagreement, we identified path-329 

ways such as exosome and mRNA turnover, erythroid differentiation, FACT complex, 330 

40S ribosomal subunit, cytoplasmic processes, and the mediator complex (Figure 3b). 331 

Among these, erythroid differentiation appears to be particularly cell-line-specific. 332 

K562 cells are derived from chronic myeloid leukemia [30] and retain erythroid differ-333 

entiation potential [31, 32]. In contrast, RPE1 cells are non-cancerous immortalized 334 

retinal pigment epithelial cells that lack these erythroid features, likely contributing 335 

to the higher divergence in pathways related to erythroid differentiation between the 336 

two cell lines. 337

MORPH generalizes to predict the effect of unseen 350 

combinatorial gene perturbations 351 

With ongoing technological advances, it is plausible that experimental data for single-352 

gene perturbations across a broad range of genes and cell lines will become increasingly 353 

accessible over time. However, generating experimental data for a comprehensive set of 354 

combinatorial perturbations will remain a significant challenge; for example, even con-355 

sidering perturbations involving only up to 5 genes out of around 20,000 in the human 356 

genome in one cellular context yields over 2 .97 × 10 19 combinations. Computational 357 

methods that can efficiently generate potential responses to combinatorial perturba-358 

tions could offer a scalable alternative that could reduce the burden of exhaustive 359 

experimentation. 360 

To evaluate MORPH’s ability to predict combinatorial perturbations, we investi-361 

gated multi-gene perturbations from Norman et al. [5], which includes 234 single-gene 362 

and double-gene perturbations across 110,000 cells in the K562 cell line. We assessed 363 

performance under varying levels of difficulty: In the simplest case, both genes in a 364 

double perturbation had been individually perturbed and observed during training 365 

(referred to as 0/2 unseen). More challenging settings included cases where only one 366 

(1/2 unseen) or neither (2/2 unseen) of the single-gene perturbations had been seen 367 

during training. 368 

As baselines, we compared MORPH to the control distribution, the perturbed 369 

mean, GEARS, and a family of simple additive models for double-gene perturbations. 370 

SALT [23] predicts double-gene perturbations by summing the single-gene shifts from 371 

the control mean and adding the result back to the control state, assuming additive 372 

effects, and thus requires both constituent single-gene perturbations to be observed 373 

during training. To handle settings where one or both single-gene perturbations are 374 

unseen, we used SALT+, our extension of SALT, which applies the same additive rule 375 

but falls back to the perturbed-mean baseline whenever a required single-gene pro-376 

file is unavailable. Finally, we evaluated a linear-additive model, which also assumes 377 

additivity, but uses the previously described linear regression model (trained to map 378 

DepMap gene embeddings to single-gene expression profiles) to supply missing single-379 

gene effects, enabling predictions when one or both single-gene perturbations are 380 

unseen. 381

As highlighted by Norman et al. [5], gene interaction (GI) types vary, and the effects 391 

of double-gene perturbations are not always simple additive combinations of single-392 

gene effects. To assess how GI types influence prediction performance, we conducted 393 

a 5-fold cross-validation experiment. For each fold, MORPH was trained on all single-394 

gene perturbations and 4/5 of the double-gene perturbations, with the remaining 395 

1/5 of the double-gene perturbations reserved for testing. After gathering predictions 396 

across all folds, we reported the performance stratified by GI type. In this setting, all 397 

single-gene perturbations are observed during training, which implies that the addi-398 

tive baselines (SALT, SALT+, and the linear-additive model) become equivalent; we 399 

therefore reported SALT only. We found that the GI types, potentiation and redun-400 

dancy, posed the greatest challenges for prediction across all methods (Figure 3e). 401 

Notably, current SOTA methods showed a substantial drop in performance for these 402 

GI types, while MORPH remained more robust. For instance, in redundant interac-403 

tions, MORPH improved prediction performance by approximately 40% compared to 404 

the best SOTA methods. 405 

Finally, we tested whether MORPH’s predictions could accurately classify gene 406 

pairs into their correct GI types. By comparing gene interaction scores derived from 407 

model predictions to those based on observed data (Methods and Supplemental 408 

Note 2), we found that MORPH achieved the highest Pearson correlation between 409 

predicted and true scores (Figure 3f). Additionally, when using the predictions to clas-410 

sify gene pairs into GI types with a threshold derived from the given labels in [5], 411 

MORPH achieved the highest area under the receiver operating characteristic curve 412 

(AUC-ROC) (Figure 3f). These results highlight the utility of our approach in not 413 

only predicting perturbation effects but also understanding and classifying the nature 414 

of complex genetic interactions. 415

MORPH’s attention-based framework allows the inference of 488 

gene regulatory programs 489 

To enhance the interpretability and reliability of MORPH, we aimed to extract the 490 

mechanisms that it learned through training. Specifically, we sought to determine 491 

whether analyzing the weights of the different components learned by MORPH from 492 

the Perturb-seq data (Figure 1a) could reveal the underlying gene regulatory network. 493 

As in prior work [2], we modeled gene regulation via a bipartite network, where pertur-494 

bations act as parent nodes and regulated genes as child nodes. Clustering the parent 495 

nodes gives rise to “perturbation modules” such that perturbations within the same 496 

module induce similar effects. Clustering the child nodes gives rise to “gene programs,” 497 

where genes within the same program exhibit similar responses to shared perturba-498 

tions. The directed edges between modules (parent nodes) and programs (children 499 

nodes) represent regulatory effects, providing insights into how perturbations influence 500 

gene expression patterns. 501 

Notably, MORPH is based on a formal causal framework: The proposed regula-502 

tory bipartite network model can be formalized as a causal structural equation model, 503 

and we proved identifiability of the causal regulatory effects in this model (Supple-504 

mental Note 4). In addition, we demonstrated on simulated data, where the bipartite 505 

network structure is known, how MORPH’s attention-based framework can recover 506 

it; more precisely, we simulated control and perturbed gene expression data based on 507 

a given bipartite network, trained MORPH on the simulated data, and analyzed the 508 

learned weights (Figure 5a; Methods). To identify perturbation modules, we clustered 509 

perturbations in the latent space generated by the perturbation encoder (Figure 1a, 510 

bottom panel of the encoder). This analysis showed that perturbations within different 511

To further demonstrate MORPH’s capacity for biological discovery, we applied it to a 645 

challenging biomedical problem, which was the core of our recent Cancer Immunother-646 

apy Machine Learning Challenge ([46], manuscript in preparation): identifying new 647 

genetic targets to prevent T-cell exhaustion. Cytotoxic CD8+ T cells play a key 648 

role in removing abnormal/cancerous cells. However, chronic exposure to weakly 649 

immunogenic tumor-associated self-antigens along with other immunosuppressive sig-650 

nals progressively differentiates naive CD8+ T cells into a spectrum of exhausted T cell 651 

states. Transiently increasing the proportion of progenitor-like cells has been shown 652 

to improve outcomes under immune checkpoint blockade [47]. Our goal was to predict 653 

gene perturbation targets that could shift T cell states to increase the progenitor-like 654 

cell ratio. To this end, we synergistically employed three of MORPH’s capabilities: 655 

transfer perturbation effects across cell lines, perform perturbation prediction, and 656 

perform experiment design to identify optimal perturbations (Figure 7a). 657 

First, we pretrained MORPH using as input the K562-GW dataset from [7], and 658 

a T-cell gene prior that we assembled by extracting data from prior literature. This 659 

included data on perturbations in T cells from proliferation [48–50], production [51– 660 

53], and bulk RNA-seq screens [54], but no Perturb-seq data (Methods). Next, we fine-661 

tuned MORPH to predict perturbation effects using the in-house generated single-cell 662 

Perturb-seq dataset on CD8 T cells generated for our Challenge. This dataset measures 663 

the transcriptional effect of 73 gene knockouts on T cells in vivo after injection into 664 

a mouse harboring melanoma for 14 days. By annotating each single cell as one of 665 

five T cell states: “Progenitor”, “Effector”, “Cycling”, “Terminal exhausted”, and 666 

“Other” (Figure 7b), we summarized the Perturb-seq data for each of the 73 perturbed 667 

genes by a five-dimensional proportion vector of perturbed cells in each state. We 668 

then utilized the fine-tuned MORPH model to predict unseen perturbation effects. 669 

For all 15,006 genes with expression measured in the 73-gene-knockout Perturb-seq 670 

data but not perturbed among the 73 genes, we generated predicted perturbed cells 671 

under the corresponding perturbations. Each predicted perturbed cell was mapped to 672 

one of the five T cell states using an MLP classifier trained on the 73-gene-knockout 673 

data, yielding a predicted cell state proportion for each untested gene. Finally, we 674 

designed targets with potentially improved objectives by ranking the untested genes. 675 

The objective function was designed to capture the increased proportion of progenitor 676 

cells. Specifically, the objective function for a given perturbation was the fraction of 677 

progenitor cells it produces, provided that the proportion of cycling cells was predicted 678 

to exceed 5%; otherwise, the objective was set to zero. The constraint on cycling cells 679 

ensured that the perturbed cells retained differentiation capabilities. 680

# 3 Discussion 699 

In this study, we presented MORPH, a deep-learning framework designed to predict 700 

the single-cell distribution of transcriptional or phenotypic outcomes resulting from 701 

unseen genetic perturbations. By combining prior biological knowledge on genetic per-702 

turbations with transcriptomic or phenotypic information from control cells, MORPH 703 

effectively extrapolates perturbation effects beyond observed data in both sequencing 704 

and imaging modalities. Concurrently and independently, TxPert [25] advanced the 705 

state of the art for transcriptomic perturbation prediction by leveraging multiple bio-706 

logical knowledge graphs, demonstrating that cross-context transfer is achievable for 707 

sequencing-based data. Because this work was developed in parallel with ours, a direct 708 

benchmarking comparison was not included; MORPH’s contributions are best under-709 

stood as complementary: where TxPert focuses on transcriptomic prediction accuracy, 710 

MORPH uniquely addresses cross-modality prediction—extending to imaging pheno-711 

types—and the interpretability of learned gene relationships, enabling inference of the 712 

underlying gene regulatory structure from perturbation responses. Through simula-713 

tion and empirical validation, we found that MORPH learns perturbation-response 714 

patterns by attending to groups of genes that are regulated similarly in response to 715 

a perturbation. This capacity enables MORPH to extrapolate perturbation responses 716 

to unseen single or combinatorial perturbations, leveraging similarities with perturba-717 

tions seen in training or captured in prior biological knowledge. Furthermore, MORPH 718 

can transfer the learned perturbation-response patterns to unseen cell lines using only 719 

control cell data in the new context. We demonstrated that the gene relationships cap-720 

tured by MORPH’s attention framework are biologically meaningful and offer insights 721 

into the cell line-specific underlying gene regulatory networks—a feature not available 722 

in previous methods for predicting perturbation effects. 723 

MORPH’s modular design allows for easy integration of different prior knowledge 724 

sources, making the framework highly adaptable and extensible. Among all sources 725 

of prior knowledge, we identified DepMap [12] as the most informative database on 726
