Abstract 13 

Recent advances in spatial multi -omics technologies provide unprecedented 14 

opportunities to interpret molecular features in tissue microenvironments , but 15 

integrative analysis across heterogeneous datasets remains challenging.  Here we 16 

present SpatialFuser, a deep learning framework for integrative analysis of unpaired 17 

spatial multi -omics data across epigenomics, transcriptomics, proteomics, and 18 

metabolomics. SpatialFuser consists of three coordinated modules: MCGATE, a Multi -19 

head Collaborative Graph Attention auToEncoder  that learns multi -scale spatial 20 

representations to decipher fine -grained spatial heterogeneity beyond predefined spatial 21 

neighbourhoods; an optional geometric pre -matching module that provides coarse 22 

initialization under tissue geometry mismatch; and an iterative matching –fusion 23 

module that couples geometry -constrained optimal transport matching with contrastive -24 

learning -guided modality fusion for cross -slice alignment and integration. Systematic 25 

benchmarks demonstrate superior performance and reliability compared with existing 26 

state -of -the -art methods in spatial domain identification, cross -slice alignment, and 27 

multi -omics integration. Applications to real datasets  illustrate  that SpatialFuser 28 

resolves precise spatial molecular patterns, reveals developmental dynamics, and 29 

recovers complementary signals across modalities. Cross -resolution integration of 30 

weakly correlated modalities by our method further uncovers previously obscured 31 

biological variation.  The  generalizability and versatility  of o ur framework enabl e32 

customized analytical scenarios and potential extension for emerging omics. 33

Introduction 47 

Spatial technologies  enable  researchers to measure  expression levels  and  reveal cellular 48 

heterogeneity across multiple molecular layers  such as  transcriptomics, epigenomics, 49 

proteomics , and metabolomics  at specific spatial locations, and have been widely 50 

applied in studies of tissue organization and molecular dynamics [1]. These 51 

technologies can be roughly categorized into imaging -based  approaches  includ ing 52 

osmFISH [2], MERFISH [3], CODEX [4], MIBI [5], CosMx SMI [6], and MALDI -53 

MSI [7], and sequencing -based  techniques  such as  10 X Visium [8], BaristaSeq [9],54 

Stereo -seq [10 ], MAGIC -seq [11 ], Stereo -CITE -seq [12 ], Spatial ATAC -seq [13 ], and 55 

Spatial ATAC –RNA -seq [14 ]. Recent advances also  cover  AI -powered techniques such 56 

as PLATO [15 ], which leverage computational approaches to overcome current 57 

limitations in biotechnology , such as low sequencing depth . With  different data 58 

modalities convey ing  unique biological insight s from diverse complementary 59 

perspective s, we now have an  unprecedented  opportunity to achieve a comprehensive 60 

spatial landscape of cellular profile, thereby enhancing our understanding of key 61 

processes such as cell function [16 ], tissue development [17 ], and disease 62 

progression [18 ].63

from typically spatial transcriptomics data  with  high -dimension and high -spars ity 65 

through spatial domain detection.  SpaGCN [19 ] employs a graph convolutional network 66

To achieve a  deeper  understanding of complex biological systems, integrative 79 

analysis of  cross -modality  spatial data has become increasingly important.  However, 80 

heterogeneous datasets often differ in tissue geometry, spatial resolution and cellular 81 

composition, while batch effects and modality -specific biases further introduce 82 

discrepancies in feature structures, expression distributions and biological semantics, 83 

posing challenges for effective data integration and slice alignment.  Existing 84 

computational tools attempt to address th ese  issue s but remain  limited. For example, 85 

SIMO [22 ] employs probabilistic alignment to map single -cell multi -omics data onto 86 

homologous spatial transcriptomics slices for detecting spatial cellular topological 87 

patterns, but it requires paired datasets and is restricted to within -slice analysis. 88 

STAligner [23 ] employs a graph attention autoencoder and triplet adversarial learning 89 

to align spatial transcriptomic slices, but it does not extend to multi -omics integration. 90 

SLAT [24 ] uses graph convolutional networks and adversarial learning to reconstruct 91 

3D tissues while correcting batch effects across slices, yet it fails to integrate modalities 92 

with weakly correlated features, such as transcriptomics and proteomics. 93

In the presence of substantial  feature  semantic discrepancies across modalities, 94 

explicitly modeling feature dependencies becomes challenging.  MISO [25 ] addresses 95 

this by learning modality -specific embeddings and their interactions to identify 96 

biologically relevant spatial domains, but it cannot perform cross -slice integration. 97 

Similarly , SpatialGlue [26 ] and  MultiGATE [27 ] apply dual -attention graph models to 98 

jointly analy se spatial multi -omics from the same tissue, yet they heavily rely on shared 99 

spatial coordinate systems or hard -coded biological priors to link weakly correlated 100 

features.  These integrative methods are effective for unimodal or same -slice multi -101 

omics data but lack generalizability in unpaired multi -omics scenarios,  where datasets 102 

do not share matched spatial coordinates and often differ in modality, resolution, 103 

geometry, or cellular composition.  Methodologically, many integrative tools assume 104 

consistent feature structures across  datasets , requiring dimensionality matching through 105 

preprocessing. This can lead to information loss when the intrinsic complexity of raw 106 

modalities differs . Some also assume similar cell compositions across slices and adopt 107 

rigid integration strategies, increasing the risk of over -alignment. Therefore, new 108 

computational tools are  urgently  needed for spatially aware cross -omics integration that 109 

can  offer better biological interpretability and generalizability  through  more flexible 110 

and robust analysis across diverse and emerging spatial omics modalities. 111 

Here, we introduce SpatialFuser, a unified deep learning framework for integrative 112 

analysis of unpaired spatial multi -omics data, enabling accurate spatial interpretation, 113 

effective cross -slice alignment and robust cross -modality integration.  Rather than 114 

relying solely on fixed spatial neighbourhood structures, SpatialFuser learns multi -scale 115 

cellular associations through a collaborative graph attention mechanism. This design 116 

preserves local spatial continuity while incorporating weighted long -range links 117 

between molecularly similar but spatially distant spots, thereby complementing local 118 

neighbourhood aggregation for more informative spatial representation learning.  For 119 

cross -slice analysis, SpatialFuser optionally incorporates geometric pre -matching for 120 

coarse initialization under geometric mismatch.  Rather than treating alignment and 121

The o verview architecture  of SpatialFuser 150 

SpatialFuser is a deep -learning framework designed for both fine -grained single -slice 151 

analysis and cross -sample integrative analysis across spatial epigenomic, 152 

transcriptomic, proteomic, and metabolomic data  (Fig. 1a),  supporting tasks including 153 

spatial interpretation, cross -slice alignment, and cross -modality integration.  It takes 154 

molecular features and spatial coordinates as input, and first represents each spatial 155 

omics slice as graph -structured data  by constructing a spatial adjacency network from 156 

the coordinates.  To accommodate multi -omics datasets with different feature 157 

dimensions and molecular semantics, SpatialFuser employs a Multi -head Collaborative 158 

Graph ATtention autoEncoder (MCGATE) to learn  slice -specific embeddings through 159 

a reconstruction task, rather than enforcing a shared encoder across heterogeneous 160 

inputs  (Fig. 1b  Step 1 ). 161 

In MCGATE  (Fig. 1c),  the predefined spatial adjacency graph serves as the local 162 

attention aggregation backbone, while a latent feature similarity graph introduces 163 

controlled long -range attention links between molecularly similar but spatially distant 164 

spots. This collaborative attention design enables MCGATE to model spatial context at 165 

multiple scales and learn more informative representations of tissue organization. As a 166 

general embedding model for spatial omics data, MCGATE remains modular and 167 

scalable, supporting its application across diverse modalities and experimental 168 

platforms .169 

When substantial geometric mismatch exists across tissue slices, coordinate pre -170 

matching can be critical for providing a coarse geometric initialization for  downstream 171 

spatial omics alignment and integration [29 ]. To accommodate diverse spatial 172 

distributions, SpatialFuser models spatial omics data as 2D point clouds and provides 173 

an optional coordinate registration module based on Iterative Closest Point (ICP) and 174 

Normal Distributions Transform (NDT)  algorithms  (Fig. 1b Step 2) .175

Alignment and integration are not strictly independent tasks [28 ]. SpatialFuser 176 

therefore couples them  through an iteratively trained dual -layer architecture consisting 177 

of a n optimal transport -based matching layer and a contrastive -learning -based fusion 178 

layer.  (Fig. 1b  Step 3 ). In the matching layer, cross -slice alignment is formulated as a 179 

graph matching task and solved through Sinkhorn -based optimal transport. Spatial 180 

geometry constrains the candidate matching space, providing a structural prior when 181 

cross -slice representations are not yet fully aligned during early training. Within this 182 

constrained space, the Sinkhorn algorithm generates soft cross -slice matching scores, 183 

from which high -confidence matched pairs are selected for downstream  integration . To 184 

avoid forcible alignment under cell -type compositional or resolution mismatch, a 185 

dustbin channel mechanism [30 ] is incorporated  to filter out  ambiguous or low -186 

confidence assignments.  In the fusion layer, independent MCGATE embeddings are 187 

first projected into a shared latent space. High -confidence matched pairs from the 188 

matching layer are used as anchor -positive pairs for contrastive learning, while anchor -189 

negative pairs are sampled from non -neighbouring regions within the same slice. This 190 

contrastive objective aligns matched cross -slice pairs while separating within -slice 191 

negatives, reducing batch effects and modality biases without collapsing biological 192 

variation. The updated shared latent space is subsequently used to refine matching in 193 

the next iteration, allowing alignment and modality fusion to improve jointly. Finally, 194 

SpatialFuser applies quality control to achieve whole -slice spot mapping  (Fig. 1b  Step 195 

4), providing a reliable reference for downstream integrative analyses. 196 

In brief, SpatialFuser comprises three main modules: MCGATE, optional 197 

coordinate pre -matching, and an iterative matching –fusion module consisting of an 198 

optimal transport -based matching layer and a contrastive fusion layer.  In this 199 

framework, MCGATE provides a general embedding engine for single -slice spatial 200 

interpretation across diverse platforms and modalities. Building on these slice -specific 201 

representations, the matching –fusion module further couples cross -slice alignment with 202

Accurate  spatial  inference across  diverse  experimental  platforms and  modalities 222 

Accurate single -slice spatial interpretation is a prerequisite for reliable multi -slice and 223 

multi -omics integrative analysis. We therefore first examined the single -slice 224 

representation capacity of SpatialFuser across diverse platforms and modalities.  To 225 

evaluate the performance of SpatialFuser against existing  representation learning 226 

algorithms, we conducted  a benchmark  on spatial domain detection task  using the 227 

classic 10 X Visium [8] human dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) dataset [32 ],228 

which has well -defined morphological boundaries and reliable manual annotations  (Fig. 229 

2a  & Supplementary Fig. 1) . The original labels were used as ground truth, and five 230 

evaluation metrics were employed: Adjusted Rand Index (ARI), Adjusted Mutual 231 

Information (AMI), Homogeneity, Completeness, and V -Measure.  We first compared 232 

SpatialFuser with other state -of -the -art spatial domain detection methods based on the 233 

Mclust [33 ] clustering strategy, including SpaGCN [19 ], STAGATE [20 ], SEDR [21 ],234 

and STAligner [23 ] (Fig. 2b) . On average across the 12 DLPFC slices, SpatialFuser 235 

achieved the highest accuracy (mean ARI = 0.625; max = 0.809; min = 0.533), followed 236 

by STAligner (mean ARI = 0.556; max = 0.675; min = 0.469). Notably, although 237

Spatiotemporal  alignment for  revealing  tissue  heterogeneity and  developmental 312 

dynamics 313 

Integrative and comparative analysis of spatial omics datasets is critical for unravel ling 314 

spatial complexity and temporal change s in tissue systems [39 ]. To assess the 315 

performance of SpatialFuser in pairwise slice alignment and integration, we first 316 

applied it to two adjacent slices from the BaristaSeq mouse visual cortex dataset [9],317 

comprising 1525 and 2042 spots, respectively  (Fig. 3a) . We evaluated spot -to -spot 318 

alignment accuracy across the entire tissue section  (see Methods) , comparing 319 

SpatialFuser with three state -of -the -art methods reported in previous benchmarks [28 ],320 

including  SPACEL [40 ], SLAT [24 ], and STAligner [23 ] (Fig. 3b) . Owing to its use of 321 

ground -truth labels during training  (see Methods) , SPACEL achieved the highest 322 

alignment accuracy (0.9 87 , 1519 matches) , followed closely by SpatialFuser (0.972, 323 

1516 matches) and STAligner (0.949, 1508 matches), while SL AT showed noticeably 324 

lower performance (0.8 57, 1525 matches ). 325 

An effective integration method should not only achieve accurate alignment but 326 

also preserve underlying biological patterns in the latent space. To this end, we further 327 

evaluated the spatial fidelity of the integrated embeddings. UMAP visualization 328 

showed that all four methods effectively removed batch effects  (Fig. 3c) . With original 329

Cross -resolution integration of weakly correlated  spatial  modalities  advances 481 

tissue -level information 482 

Compared to the one -to -one correspondence between the genome and transcriptome, 483 

the linkage between the proteome or metabolome and the transcriptome or epigenome 484 

is generally  less direct [53 ], as protein abundance and metabolite levels are influenced 485 

by multiple regulatory processes beyond gene expression, such as translation, post -486 

translational modifications, and cellular environment [54 , 55 ]. To explore cross -487 

modality integration under such weakly correlated conditions, we applied SpatialFuser ,488 

with NDT -based coordinate pre -matching,  to integrate and align transcriptomic 489 

(MAGIC -seq [11 ]), proteomic (PLATO [15 ]), and metabolomic (MALDI -MSI [7]) data 490 

under varying resolutions  and  mismatched tissue geometries  obtained from consecutive 491 

mouse cerebellum tissue slices. Result shows SpatialFuser effectively captured shared 492 

spatial molecular patterns across different omics layers and successfully corrected 493 

modality biases while preserving true biological variation, as evidenced by the spatial 494 

domains identified through Louvain clustering of the latent embeddings, which aligned 495 

well with the original anatomical annotations  (Fig. 5a) . Most spots across the tissue 496 

were well aligned, with high alignment similarity scores  (Supplementary Fig. 1 3) and 497 

matching accuracy (Supplementary Fig.  14 , Transcriptome –Proteome: 0.84, 498 

Transcriptome –Metabolome: 0.75, Proteome –Metabolome: 0.86). It is noteworthy that 499 

manual annotation is affected by the granularity and distribution discrepancies across 500

Discussion 554 

Spatial technologies are expanding into multi -omics, enabling researchers to measure 555 

transcriptomics, epigenomics, proteomics, metabolomics, and other molecular layers at 556 

defined tissue locations. Underlying this is an unprecedented opportunity to integrate 557 

and interpret molecular  features  from diverse complementary perspectives for a deeper 558 

understanding of biological mechanisms within  tissue  microenvironment [59 -61 ]. In 559 

this study, we  propose  SpatialFuser, a deep learning -based  framework designed for 560 

detailed molecular profiling within individual tissue sections and cross -modality, multi -561 

sample integrative analysis of spatial multi -omics data. Through benchmarking on real 562 

spatial multi -omics datasets generated by diverse technologies, we show that 563 

SpatialFuser outperforms existing state -of -the -art methods in spatial domain detection, 564 

consecutive slice alignment, and spatial multi -omics data integration. Notably, we 565 

further demonstrate SpatialFuser’s  unique capability for cross -resolution integration of 566 

weakly correlated modalities, which has not been previously established.  By combining 567 

flexible single -slice representation learning with coupled matching –fusion optimisation 568 

for cross -slice analysis, SpatialFuser provides a coherent solution for accurate spatial 569 

interpretation, robust cross -slice alignment, and effective cross -modality integration of 570 

unpaired spatial multi -omics data.  To accommodate diverse input modalities, 571

SpatialFuser introduces MCGATE to learn unified representations from multi -platform 572 

spatial omics data. Unlike methods such as STAGATE and SEDR that primarily focus 573 

on local information propagation, MCGATE employs a multi -head collaborative 574 

attention mechanism that further captures long -range feature  correspondences  in the 575 

embedding space, enabling more accurate identification of spatially coherent patterns. 576 

The effectiveness of this design is supported by ablation and interpretability analyses, 577 

and beyond performance gains, the architecture improves model transparency while 578 

providing new perspectives for interpreting spatial molecular organization. 579 

SpatialFuser introduces methodological advances and broader utility in cross -slice 580 

alignment and multi -modality integration compared with previous approaches.  Unlike 581 

alignment methods such as STAligner and SPACEL, SpatialFuser learns slice -specific 582 

embeddings that preserve native feature structures and avoid information loss from 583 

forced dimensionality matching, while applying a more relaxed alignment strategy to 584 

model cross -slice correspondences, thereby better supporting scenarios involving 585 

different technologies.  Beyond alignment, integration  tools  such as MISO, SpatialGlue, 586 

and MultiGATE  are designed for co -profiled data measured on the same tissue section 587 

and often assume similar cellular composition or strong biological priors. Rather than 588 

relying on one -to -one molecular correspondences across modalities, SpatialFuser 589 

captures correlated spatial molecular patterns in the embedding space to correct batch 590 

effects and modality biases. It learns latent correspondences directly from data and is 591 

built to handle unpaired sections across different samples, resolutions, technologies, 592 

and developmental stages, extending multi -omics analysis to a broader range of real -593 

world scenarios. 594 

A current limitation of SpatialFuser lies in  the  memory footprint  of  multi -head 595 

collaborative attention mechanism.  Although multi -head attention improves 596 

representation learning by aggregating feature relationships across multiple subspaces, 597 

the  lack of memory -efficient parallel operations for high -dimensional sparse matrices 598 

in current deep learning frameworks leads to substantial GPU memory demands for 599

Fusion layer 813 

In the fusion layer, a shared multilayer perceptron (MLP) is connected to two 814 

independent MCGATE modules to project the low -dimensional embeddings of spatial 815 

omics data from different platforms  or  modalities into a unified latent space.  To reduce 816 

the risk of learning batch -specific ordering patterns, the embeddings are concatenated 817 

and randomly permuted along the spot dimension before being passed into the MLP. 818 

The fusion layer employs contrastive learning for spatial multi -omics data 819 

integration. Specifically, high -confidence cross -slice  correspondences  derived from the 820 

matching layer  are used  as anchor -positive pairs to guide the correction of batch effects 821 

or modality bias.  Negative samples are randomly drawn from non -neighbouring regions 822 

within the same tissue slice to improve cell -type discriminability while reducing the 823 

probability of sampling false negatives.  Because the number of spatial spots is typically 824 

much larger than the local neighbourhood size,  this strategy provides an efficient 825 

approximation for negative sampling. 826

Whole -slice  Alignment 888 

We propose a whole -slice alignment strategy to support downstream analysis and 889 

comparison with existing alignment methods. By default, the slice with fewer spatial 890 

spots is used as the source slice and is mapped to the slice with more spots.  For each 891 

source  spot  𝑖 , SpatialFuser first identifies a candidate set in the target slice by selecting 892 

target spots within the spatial radius  𝑟 , and  then  compute s the cosine similarity between 893 

the fused embedding of  spot  𝑖  and  those  of  candidate  target  spots.  Inspired by previous 894 

work [24 ], we introduce a probabilistic quality -control  procedure to filter unreliable 895 

assignments . Instead of  randomly pairing spots across both slices, we randomly sample 896 

1,000 spots from the source slice and, for each sampled spot, randomly select a nearby 897 

target  spot within radius  𝑟  to compute  a background similarity score , forming  a stricter 898 

null distribution.  The 95th percentile of this null distribution is used as the quality -899 

control threshold, and the candidate with the highest similarity score above this 900 

threshold is retained as the final alignment result for each source spot. 901

Implementation details of  Spatial Fuser 903 

Spatial  domain  detection  task.  To accommodate datasets with varying complexity, 904 

SpatialFuser adopts different MCGATE configurations. For high -throughput data such 905 

as 10X Visium, Stereo -seq, and MAGIC -seq , a two -layer MCGATE is used, with 906 

hidden dimensions set to 512 and 32 respectively. For low -throughput data such as 907 

CODEX and MALDI -MSI, a single -layer MCGATE with 32 hidden units is employed. 908 

The 32 -dimensional latent space was selected as a compact default representation to 909 

balance input feature diversity and computational efficiency across heterogeneous 910 

spatial omics datasets.  The MCGATE is trained using the Adam optimizer, with the 911 

ELU function selected as the activation. The model is trained for 500 steps by default, 912 

during which the Adaptive Adjacency Expansion Layer is updated every 100 epochs to 913 

refine long -range relationships and their associated attention weights. Both the learning 914 

rate and the long -range attention coefficient  𝛼  are customized for each dataset. 915

Alignment and  integration  task.  The training process is divided into two stages: pre -916 

training and contrastive learning. In the pre -training stage, a fusion layer is connected 917 

to two independent MCGATE encoders and jointly trained with a reconstruction loss 918 

for 500 epochs  by  default, producing high -quality embeddings for downstream 919 

alignment and integration.  For slices with semantically similar molecular profiles, 920 

shared features across slices are used. The matching layer and the fusion layer are then 921 

iteratively optimized to align the embeddings into a shared latent space. Specifically, 922 

the matching layer first identifies anchor -positive pairs based on the pre -trained 923 

embeddings. These identified pairs are then used to guide the fusion layer in integrating 924 

the modalities. By default, triplet pairs are updated every 20 epochs, and this iterative 925 

process continues until 500 steps. The learning rate is dataset -specific  defined .926 

Hyperparameter selection guideline.  Because SpatialFuser handles heterogeneous 927 

spatial omics datasets, a single universal default configuration is not technically 928 

appropriate. All parameter settings used in this study are provided in the tutorials, and 929 

recommended settings according to data characteristics and assay types are summarized 930 

in  Supplementary Table  1.931
