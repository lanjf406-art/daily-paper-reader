# Abstract 

Spatial transcriptomics (ST) technologies enable the study of gene expression within the spatial context of tissues, providing insights into tissue structure, cellular interactions, and disease progression. However, existing dimension reduction methods often overlook spatial information or struggle to distinguish spatial gene patterns from those driven by cell-type differences, limiting biological interpretability by convolving differences in gene expression patterns with differences in cell-type proportions. To address these challenges, we introduce the scalable multi-group nonnegative spatial factorization (smNSF), a computationally-tractable probabilistic framework that integrates spatial coordinates and cell-type labels into a unified matrix factorization model. By using multi-group Gaussian processes (MGGPs) as priors, our model captures complex spatial variation in a cell-type specific way while enforcing nonnegativity to enhance interpretability. We develop a variational inference framework for MGGPs that supports scalable optimization and improves the numerical stability of smNSF. Across seven spatial transcriptomics datasets spanning diverse technologies and tissues, smNSF recovers sparse, interpretable spatial factors and, through its cell-type conditional posteriors, organizes them into cell-type enriched, cell-type specific, and universal spatial programs that are not apparent from marginal factors alone. Given cell-type labels in ST data, smNSF enables cell-type aware spatial decompositions and supports cell-type conditional posteriors for in silico exploration of relationships between spatial patterns and cellular identity. 

# Author summary 

Most current analysis methods for spatial transcriptomics either ignore spatial structure or fail to separate gene-driven spatial patterns from cell-type driven differences. In this work, we develop a method that uses spatial coordinates and cell-type labels together to better uncover patterns of gene expression. Our approach, scalable multi-group nonnegative spatial factorization (smNSF), uses Gaussian processes to model spatial structure, which we extend to capture both spatial structure and cell type within a unified framework called multi-group Gaussian processes. # 1 Introduction 1

Spatially resolved transcriptomics (ST) technologies enable the measurement of gene 2

expression while preserving spatial coordinates of those genes in tissue sections. This 3

spatial information is crucial for understanding how gene activity varies across different 4

tissue regions [1], how cells interact with their neighbors [2], and how multicellular 5

structure and function emerge in health and disease [3]. Such insights have been 6

instrumental in the study of embryonic development [4 –6], tumor progression [7, 8], and 7

immune compartmentalization [9, 10]. 8

Dimension reduction methods such as principal component analysis (PCA), factor 9

analysis (FA) [11], and nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) [12] are commonly used 10 

to analyze high-dimensional single-cell RNA-sequencing (scRNA-seq) data [13–15]. 11 

These methods can also be applied to ST datasets, but they disregard spatial 12 

information. 13 

More recently, spatially aware models such as MEFISTO [16], SpatialPCA [17], and 14 

nonnegative spatial factorization (NSF) [18] use Gaussian processes (GPs) [19] or 15 

related kernel-based priors to capture smooth spatial variation. MEFISTO uses an 16 

explicit GP formulation, while SpatialPCA uses a multivariate normal prior with spatial 17 

kernel-defined covariance, which is functionally similar to a GP. While these models 18 

recover spatial structure, their dense real-valued factors may be difficult to interpret. 19 

NSF improves interpretability by enforcing nonnegativity via exponentiated GP priors, 20 

yielding sparse, parts-based decompositions [18]; mNSF [20] extends NSF to 21 

multi-sample analysis through alignment-free sample-specific spatial correlation 22 

modeling. Other nonnegative spatial methods, including spatial LDA [21], neighborhood 23 

NMF [22], and FISHFactor [23], similarly produce interpretable spatial patterns. 24 

However, none of these methods — nonnegative or otherwise — take cell-type labels as 25 

input, limiting their ability to distinguish spatial patterns driven by gene-expression 26 

changes across space from those driven by cell-type-proportion changes within tissues. 27 

More modern methods such as CELESTA [24] consider spatial context when 28 

inferring cell types. Meanwhile, methods such as cell2location [9], DestVI [25], and 29 

C-SIDE [26] focus on estimating cell-type composition across spatial locations using 30 

reference single-cell data and spatial information. However, these approaches are 31 

designed to recover cell-type labels, not spatially structured gene expression patterns. 32 

A spatial gene expression pattern , as recovered by spatially-aware dimension 33 

reduction methods [16 –18], captures changes in the expression levels smoothly across a 34 

tissue slice. Without explicit modeling and consideration of cell types, it is impossible 35 

to know when spatial changes in gene expression are due to expression changes within a 36 

cell type across the tissue, or changes in cell-type proportions across the tissue, because 37 

cell types generally express different sets of genes [27]. Deconvolving the impact of both 38 

types of changes on gene expression in spatial samples is an important task. 39 

Clarifying the contribution of each source is essential for understanding how spatial 40 

regulation and cellular identity shape tissue architecture. For example, during 41 

embryonic development, positional information precedes cell identity: gradients of 42

## 2.1 Nonnegative spatial factorization 71 

MEFISTO uses Gaussian processes as spatially-aware priors within factor analysis 72 

(FA) [16]. However, because FA is real-valued and not restricted to nonnegative values, 73 

the resulting MEFISTO patterns are often hard to interpret and not sparse [18]. 74 

Nonnegative spatial factorization (NSF) is a method that expands probabilistic 75 

nonnegative matrix factorization (PNMF), a probabilistic version of NMF, by using a 76 

Gaussian process as the prior instead of a univariate Gaussian prior. Restricting weights 77 

and factors to nonnegative values leads to a naturally sparse, parts-based decomposition. 78 

In NSF, this restriction provides interpretable nonnegative factors that capture small 79 

groups of genes that are co-expressed in a spatially smooth way [18]. 80

## 2.2 Multi-group Gaussian processes 89 

Multi-group Gaussian processes (MGGPs) provide a family of positive-definite 90 

covariance kernel functions to perform Gaussian process regression on multi-group 91 

data [29]. The multidimensional inputs, X ∈ RN ×D , also contain group labels, 92 

C = {c1, c 2, . . . , c N }, and outcomes Y ∈ RN : 93 

p(Y |X, C X ; θ) = MGGP( Y |X, C X ; K(( X, C X ), (X, C X ))) ,

where the kernel takes both the input and group label: 94 

K(( x, c j ), (x′, c j′ )) : X × X → R.

Various reductive special cases of MGGPs exist. The simplest case is separate GPs 95 

(SGP), where K(( x, c j ), (x′, c j′ )) = 0, equivalent to training a separate GP per 96 

group [29]. Separate GPs per group do not allow information to be shared across 97 

groups, and SGP struggles when groups are imbalanced in size. In genomics, the SGP 98 

approach is not useful as it means training a separate NSF model per cell type, 99 

resulting in zero shared factors across cell types. The union GP (UGP) is equivalent to 100 

setting K(( x, c j ), (x′, c j′ )) = K(x, x ′), meaning group labels are ignored, which is the 101 

current baseline of the NSF model. Separable MGGPs use the covariance function as 102 

the Kronecker product of two kernels: K(( x, c j ), (x′, c j′ )) = K1(x, x ′)K2(ci, c j′ ). 103 

However, these covariance functions can lead to “ridges” and discontinuities [29, 30]. For 104 

this work, we use the inseparable Mat´ ern-3/2 MGGP kernel constructed in §2.3 (full 105 

derivation in Supplement §3). 106

2.4.1 Stochastic variational multi-group Gaussian processes 131 

Exact inference under the NSF model is intractable: Gaussian process priors scale 132 

cubically with the number of observations, and the Poisson likelihood is non-conjugate, 133 

ruling out closed-form posteriors. We therefore adapt stochastic variational Gaussian 134 

processes (SVGP) [18, 31–34], which introduce a small set of M representative spatial 135 

locations called inducing points Z = {zm}Mm=1 to reduce the computational burden. 136 

Then, SVGP performs variational inference over the function values at those locations. 137 

To extend SVGP to MGGPs, each inducing point also carries a cell-type label cm, 138 

forming inducing groups (Z, C Z ) = {(zm, c m)}Mm=1 that mirror the structure of the 139 

observed group inputs; we sample CZ from the empirical group distribution to ensure 140

exceeds 1 .5× (the threshold used throughout Results); cell-type specific requires the 205 

dominant group’s L1-ratio to stay near 1 while other groups deplete to near zero (i.e., 206 

cross-label depletion, not the single-group ratio alone); universal requires all conditional 207 

L1-ratios close to 1, equivalently near-maximal Shannon entropy across the group axis. 208 

## 2.6 Implementation details 209 

Unlike the original NSF, which requires an NMF warm-start to initialize its factors [18], 210 

smNSF initializes both the nonnegative loading matrix W and the GP variational 211 

parameters ( m, L ) randomly. We find that training reliably converges from random 212 

initialization under MGGP-SVGP and MGGP-LCGP priors (Fig. 2). Recovered factors 213 

are broadly consistent across random seeds; as expected given the convergence to a local 214 

minimum inherent to NMF, factor identity is not strictly seed-invariant, although the 215 

sparsity of the parts-based representation inherent in NMF encourages identifiability up 216 

to label switching. We apply stochastic variational inference with mini-batches of 6,000 217 

cells. For LCGP, we set the neighborhood size K = 50 across all experiments. Spatial 218 

coordinates are standardized to unit scale per axis. All experiments used PyTorch on an 219 

NVIDIA A30 GPU (24 GB). Code is available at 220 

https://github.com/luisdiaz1997/GPzoo (MGGP and LCGP inference), 221 

https://github.com/luisdiaz1997/Probabilistic-NMF (nonnegative and spatial 222 

nonnegative factorizations), and 223 

https://github.com/luisdiaz1997/Spatial-Factorization (full smNSF pipeline). 224

## 2.9 Spatial domain benchmark methodology 294 

To compare smNSF against spatial domain identification methods, we evaluated on the 295 

DLPFC dataset [40] using published benchmarks from SDMBench [41]. smNSF takes 296 

the Maynard et al. cortical layer annotations as its MGGP group labels at training time, 297 

and is a factorization rather than a clustering method. We nonetheless include this 298 

benchmark because SDMBench is the standard comparison axis for spatial 299 

transcriptomics methods on DLPFC. Concretely, we apply K-means ( K = 7, matching 300 

the seven annotated layers) to the learned spatial factors and score the resulting cluster 301 

assignments against the same Maynard et al. layer annotations using the SDMBench 302 

metrics below. “Clustering accuracy” throughout this section refers to that 303 

cluster-vs-layer agreement. 304 

We report the following metrics following the SDMBench protocol: 305 

• Adjusted rand index (ARI) : Clustering accuracy adjusted for chance. 306 

• Normalized mutual information (NMI) : Mutual information between 307 

predicted and true labels, normalized to [0 , 1]. 308 

• Homogeneity (HOM) : Whether clusters contain only members of a single class. 309 

• Completeness (COM) : Whether all members of a class are assigned to the 310 

same cluster. 311 

• CHAOS : Spatial chaos score measuring cluster fragmentation. 312 

• Percentage of abnormal spots (PAS) : Lower percentages indicate better 313 

spatial continuity. 314 

• Average silhouette width (ASW) : Average silhouette width of the factor 315 

embedding. 316 

• Moran’s I : Spatial autocorrelation of factors. 317 

We compared results from smNSF against 14 methods benchmarked in SDMBench, 318 

including SpaGCN, BayesSpace [42], STAGATE [43], SpaceFlow, BASS, and others, 319 

using their published results on identical DLPFC sections. 320

## Overview 343 

We evaluate smNSF on seven spatial transcriptomics datasets spanning MERFISH, 344 

Slide-seq, osmFISH, and 10x Visium technologies: mouse hippocampus (Slide-seqV2, 345 

41,783 spots, 14 groups), human colorectal cancer (MERFISH, 137,693 cells, 13 cell-type 346

cohort), mouse hypothalamic preoptic region (MERFISH, 71,939 cells, 11 cell-type 349 

groups), mouse brain (10x Visium, 2,688 spots, 15 brain region groups), and human 350 

DLPFC (12 Visium sections, ∼4,200 spots each, 7 cortical layer groups). We assess (i) 351 

scalability and computational efficiency of LCGP versus standard SVGP, (ii) factor 352 

interpretability through sparsity and spatial coherence, and (iii) cell-type specificity 353 

through conditional posterior analysis. We benchmark clustering accuracy on DLPFC 354 

against NSF, PCA, NMF, and 14 methods from SDMBench [41]. 355 

## LCGP enables scalable inference with accurate variance 356 

## estimation in smNSF 357 

A key limitation of NSF is the O(M 3) cost of standard SVGP inference, which restricts 358 

inducing points to M ≈ 3,000 under typical GPU memory constraints. For large ST 359 

datasets with >100,000 cells, this severely limits model expressiveness. 360 

We compared LCGP against SVGP on the Slide-seqV2 hippocampus dataset [35] 361 

(∼41,783 spots, 14 groups) to evaluate computational trade-offs (Fig. 2b). Per training 362 

step with L factors and batch size B, SVGP scales as O(LBM 2) while LCGP scales as 363 

O(LBK 3), reflecting the cost of inverting K×K matrices in the local approximation 364 

(full derivation in Supplement §5). Critically, LCGP’s cost depends on K, not M , 365 

allowing M =N without the cubic growth in M that limits standard SVGPs. 366 

For LCGP, we set M = N (inducing points equal to data points) and varied the 367 

neighborhood size K ∈ { 10 , 25 , 50 , 75 }. For SVGP, we used M = 3 ,000 inducing 368 

points—the practical limit under equivalent memory constraints (NVIDIA A30, 24 GB). 369 

At K = 10, LCGP was 4 × faster than SVGP (73 vs. 301 ms/step) with lower peak 370 

memory (5.72 vs. 6.17 GB). At K = 50, per-step time remained faster than SVGP (234 371 

vs. 301 ms) while memory grew to 10.64 GB. At K = 75, both time (397 ms) and 372 

memory (14.77 GB) exceeded SVGP as the full M =N inducing set saturates GPU 373 

capacity. We select K = 50 across all experiments to balance speed and expressiveness. 374 

Beyond computational efficiency, LCGP also produces substantially richer cell-type 375 

conditional posteriors than SVGP (Fig. 2a). Conditioning on individual cell types 376 

reveals distinct modes of spatial organization: some cell types deplete the learned factor 377 

signal (e.g., oligodendrocytes in factors 2 and 4), others isolate it into a sharper spatial 378 

pattern (e.g., dentate pyramidal neurons in factor 4), and others uncover spatial 379 

structure that is only apparent upon conditioning—spatial signals that are diluted or 380 

obscured when all cell types are pooled together, but emerge as distinct patterns within 381 

a single cell-type conditional posterior (e.g., CA1/CA2/CA3 subiculum neurons and 382 

oligodendrocytes in Fig. 2a, where each cell type’s conditional posterior reveals a sharp 383 

tissue program in a factor whose marginal map is near-uniform). Although 384 

ground-truth conditional posteriors are unavailable for these data, the agreement 385 

between SVGP and LCGP—which use fundamentally different variational 386 

approximations—and across random initializations suggests these patterns reflect 387 

genuine biological structure rather than inference artifacts. 388

## Gain from finite group-distance coupling 389 

The previous subsection establishes that MGGP-LCGP produces interpretable cell-type 390 

conditional posteriors. A natural follow-up question is whether the multi-group 391 

component of the MGGP framework is contributing anything beyond what an ensemble 392 

of independent per-cell-type Gaussian processes would deliver. The non-separable 393 

Mat´ ern 3/2 MGGP kernel of §2.2 has a single group-distance parameter a governing 394 

cross-group correlation: at a = 0 the kernel reduces to a standard single-group Mat´ ern 395 

(all cells share one GP regardless of cell type), and at a → ∞ the cross-group covariance 396 

collapses to zero and each cell type is fit by a separate GP, with no shared signal across 397 

groups. The regime 0 < a < ∞ is where the multi-group machinery does useful 398 

work—each cell type’s conditional posterior borrows strength from cells in correlated 399 

cell types without being forced to share a single global mean. 400 

To isolate the contribution of this finite-a coupling we fit two MGGP-LCGP variants 401 

that differ only in the value of a. Everything else—kernel form, K = 50, M = N , 402 

probabilistic neighbor selection, loadings parameterization, training schedule, and the 403 

fixed cell-type distance matrix—is held fixed. The first variant pins a = 1, a moderate 404 

cross-group coupling intermediate between a → 0 (which collapses the prior toward a 405 

single shared GP across all cell types) and a → ∞ (which recovers independent 406 

per-cell-type GPs); the second variant pins a = 10 6, large enough to drive the 407 

off-diagonal MGGP covariance below numerical precision and recover the separate-GPs 408 

limit in practice (Figure 4). 409 

We selected the two universal factors on the finite-a side (factors whose Shannon 410 

entropy of normalized L1 specificity ratios is near uniform across cell types) and 411 

matched them to the a → ∞ side by best Pearson correlation on the per-factor 412 

gene-loading vectors, so that the same biological gene program is being compared across 413 

blocks; exact factor indices are not preserved across fits because nonnegative matrix 414 

factorization is rotation- and permutation-invariant in the nonnegative cone. Cell-type 415 

rows are the four groups (ependymal, oligodendrocytes, astrocytes, neurogenesis) where 416 

the matched a → ∞ factors are most depleted by the L1 specificity ratio. 417 

The marginal posteriors in the top row are broadly similar across the two fits, as 418 

expected for factors with diffuse cell-type usage. The per-cell-type conditionals 419 

illustrate the role of cross-group coupling. Under large a the model is still LCGP—the 420 

probabilistic neighbor-selection scheme continues to weight candidates by kernel 421 

similarity—but the group-distance term drives those weights to favor same-group 422 

neighbors, so each cell type’s conditional posterior is in practice built with cells from its 423 

cell type alone. Cross-group covariance collapses to zero, and the model reduces to a 424 

collection of decoupled per-group GPs that share inducing locations but exchange no 425 

information at inference time. 426 

This decoupling diminishes the conditional posterior of universal factors specifically. 427 

A universal factor carries shared signal across many cell types; without a non-zero 428 

between-group covariance the model has no mechanism for one group’s data to inform 429 

another group’s conditional posterior on that factor. Each per-group GP must then 430 

recover the shared structure from its own observations alone, and where the per-group 431 

sample is small or noisy the conditional reconstruction degrades (Fig. 4). Under finite a, 432 

the same factor is anchored to a shared latent variable through non-zero off-diagonal 433 

group-kernel entries, and the conditional posteriors recover a coherent spatial pattern 434 

across the same groups. 435

## Cell-type labels are enriched across regions; region groups are 464 

## localized 465 

The smNSF result reflects a structural property of the MGGP prior that generalizes 466 

across datasets. When smNSF conditions on cell types , which are scattered throughout 467 

the tissue, signal propagates across the full spatial domain, producing conditional 468 

posteriors that show enrichment of single cell types in specific factors ( L1-ratio > 1.5×). 469 

When it conditions on spatial regions , which are locally confined, the prior collapses 470 

signal to that single location, suppressing enrichment everywhere else. We quantified 471 

this pattern by computing, for every group across all seven datasets, whether at least 472 

one factor reached an L1-ratio above 1 .5× (Table 2). 473 

Across 75 cell-type groups, 61% (46/75) showed enrichment above 1 .5×. Across 28 474 

region groups, 0 out of 28 reached 1 .5×—the strongest region enrichment across any 475 

dataset was 1 .41 × (CA1 subfield, Slide-seqV2 mouse hippocampus). This distinction 476 

holds within individual datasets that contain both group types: in the Slide-seq 477 

hippocampus, 5/10 cell types were enriched above 1 .5× (microglia: 5 .4×, 478 

oligodendrocytes: 3 .2×, astrocytes: 2 .6×) while 0/4 hippocampal subfields were 479 

enriched; in osmFISH, 10/26 cell types were enriched while 0/2 region groups were 480 

enriched. Datasets annotated exclusively with cell types showed consistently high 481 

enrichment rates (colon 69%, MERFISH hypothalamus 82%, liver 87%); datasets 482 

annotated exclusively with regions (10x Visium, DLPFC) showed no enrichment. This 483 

structural distinction—scattered cell types propagate signal, localized region labels 484 

collapse it—explains why smNSF’s cell-type conditional signals are strongest in tissues 485 

with intermingled, heterogeneous cell types and absent in region-annotated data, and 486 

provides a diagnostic for interpreting enrichment patterns in new datasets. 487

## Three specificity classes organize cell-type conditional posteriors 488 

The cell-type-vs-region distinction above establishes that smNSF’s conditional 489 

posteriors carry interpretable cell-type information; the next question is how those 490 

conditional responses are distributed across factors. We organize the per-dataset results 491 

that follow around the three specificity classes introduced in §2.5— cell-type enriched , 492 

cell-type specific , and universal —computed from each factor’s conditional profile across 493 

groups, with cell-type groups specialized to the cell-type conditional case and region or 494 

layer groups specialized to the region- or layer-conditional case. 495 

• Cell-type enriched factors supply most of the biological findings: spatial 496 

programs that are masked by composition effects in the marginal and become 497 

visible only under conditioning on one cell type. We flag a factor as enriched for a 498 

group when its L1-ratio exceeds 1 .5×, the threshold used in the aggregate count 499 

above. 500 

• Cell-type specific factors are present in the marginal and stay present under 501 

their dominant cell type, but deplete toward zero under the others; their 502 

identification therefore depends on cross-label depletion rather than the 503 

single-group L1-ratio alone. 504 

• Universal factors are nearly invariant to label conditioning—all conditional 505 

L1-ratios close to 1, with near-maximal Shannon entropy across the cell-type axis. 506 

These represent tissue-level architecture rather than cell-type specific gene 507 

programs. 508 

The three classes are descriptive end-points of a continuous specificity spectrum, 509 

parameterized by the conditional-to-marginal L1 ratios together with the cross-label 510 

specificity (depletion or entropy) that distinguishes single-label dominance from broad 511 

invariance. The per-dataset walkthroughs that follow name the dominant class for each 512 

factor they discuss, and the curated factor panels in each dataset figure show concrete 513 

examples of all three classes. 514

Group labels and label quality. smNSF requires discrete group labels for 753 

conditional analysis: labels capturing cell types, anatomical regions, or cortical layers 754 

were used across our datasets. When those labels are inferred cell types rather than 755 

ground-truth annotations—most ST datasets lack the latter, so labels are typically 756 

obtained from unsupervised clustering or label transfer from reference scRNA-seq 757 

data [10, 13]—label quality propagates into the conditional posteriors: the MGGP prior 758 

treats the supplied labels as known, so noisy or misassigned labels may smooth or 759 

distort the per-group factor maps. 760 

Equidistant group structure. The choice of group distance dcc ′ in the MGGP 761 

kernel currently treats all distinct group pairs as equidistant. Incorporating cell-type 762 

hierarchies or continuous embeddings could improve modeling of related cell states. 763 

Volumetric data. While we demonstrate extensions to 3D data, systematic 764 

evaluation of smNSF on volumetric tissues remains future work. 765 

Factor capacity. Our analyses used L = 10 factors across all datasets. With richer 766 

factorizations ( L = 50–100), smNSF could in principle reconstruct the full dataset and 767 

support gene-expression imputation under specific cell-type compositions—asking, for 768 

example, how the gene expression of the tissue would appear if only macrophages were 769 

present. More work is needed to scale the MGGP to higher latent dimensions—either by 770 

scaling multi-group LCGP or using a different approximation for the multi-group 771 

GP—while maintaining numerical stability. 772

the fitted MGGP prior rather than a prediction of an experimental perturbation: it 774

on gene expression. Causal interpretation would require an experimental 777 

intervention—e.g., targeted ablation of a cell population or directed differentiation—and 778 

the model’s predictions for such interventions would need separate validation. 779 

smNSF’s conditional posteriors describe how spatial factors shift when conditioning 780 

on a cell type, not how individual genes vary across cell types. This makes factor-level 781 

validation more difficult: the method cannot directly answer questions such as “is gene 782 

X upregulated in macrophages specifically?” The gene programs underlying each factor 783 

are encoded in the loadings W , but linking those programs to a specific cell type and 784 

not to individual factors that cannot be conditioned on cell type would require a 785 

different model. 786 

Our method provides a foundation for several extensions. The MGGP framework 787 

can incorporate multiple grouping variables (e.g., cell type and disease state) through 788 

product kernels, and could be combined with multi-sample extensions of NSF such as 789 

mNSF [20] to enable joint cell-type aware factorization across samples. Integration with 790 

differential expression analysis could identify genes whose spatial patterns differ across 791 

conditions while controlling for cell-type composition. Also, combining smNSF with 792 

velocity or trajectory inference could reveal how spatial gene programs evolve during 793 

development or disease progression. 794 

Taken together, smNSF with LCGP addresses two bottlenecks that have limited the 795 

adoption of GP-based spatial factorization methods: the inability to disentangle 796 

group-driven from gene-expression-driven spatial patterns, and the computational 797 

ceiling that restricted spatial GP priors to a few thousand observations. The consistent 798 

enrichment pattern across 75 cell-type and 28 region groups (103 groups total; Table 2) 799 

provides a practical diagnostic: if group labels are scattered, the MGGP prior enriches 800 

conditionally; if they are spatially confined, it does not—giving us a clear criterion for 801 

when conditional analysis is likely to be informative. More broadly, the group structure 802 

in the MGGP prior is not restricted to cell types: groups can encode treatment 803 

conditions, time points, disease states, donors, or other experimental covariates, 804 

providing a generative framework to ask biological questions directly within a dimension 805 

reduction framework. 806
