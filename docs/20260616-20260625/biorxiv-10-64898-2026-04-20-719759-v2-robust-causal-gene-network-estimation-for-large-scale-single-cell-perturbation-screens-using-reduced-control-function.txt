# SUMMARY 11 

Single-cell CRISPR perturbation screens provide a foundation for causal discovery in gene regu- 12 

latory networks, but existing methods struggle with latent confounding, count-valued expression 13 

data, and high-multiplicity-of-infection (high-MOI) designs. We introduce RICE, a unified frame- 14 

work that addresses all three challenges. At its core is a reformulated control function tailored to 15 

the network-estimation setting, which restores robustness to exclusion-restriction violations that 16 

are pervasive in real CRISPR screens and that cause standard control function approaches to 17 

fail. RICE pairs this estimator with a constrained negative binomial model and a differentiable 18 

acyclicity penalty, accommodating hard and soft interventions and natively supporting high-MOI 19 

designs within a single, GPU-scalable model. Across extensive synthetic benchmarks, RICE 20 

consistently outperforms existing methods and remains stable under strong confounding, exclu- 21 

sion violations, and high-MOI conditions. Applied to CRISPRi screens, RICE achieves stronger 22 

causal-discovery performance on held-out data. RICE further reconstructs the canonical inter- 23 

feron (IFN)-γ signaling pathway in melanoma cells upon immune stimulation, and nominates 24 

stable regulatory candidates that conventional significance tests may overlook due to limited sta- 25 

tistical power. Together, these results establish RICE as a robust and scalable framework for 26 

causal discovery in single-cell perturbation genomics. 27

# INTRODUCTION 30 

Single-cell perturbation screens have become a central tool for studying gene regulation, com- 31 

bining pooled CRISPR perturbations with single-cell transcriptomic readouts to measure how 32 

individual cells respond to targeted genetic intervention 1. Unlike observational single-cell data, 33 

these experiments generate interventional variation, making them a natural foundation for causal 34 

discovery 2–4 . The central goal in this setting is to reconstruct causal gene networks — directed 35 

graphs in which edges encode regulatory influence rather than undirected association — typi- 36 

cally represented as directed acyclic graphs (DAGs). Recovering such structure is essential for 37 

distinguishing direct regulatory effects from those mediated through downstream pathways, and 38 

for understanding how perturbations propagate through regulatory programs 5–7 . 39

A persistent obstacle to this goal is unmeasured confounding. Latent factors such as techni- 40 

cal artifacts, cell-state heterogeneity, and other unobserved variables can influence many genes 41 

simultaneously, inducing dependencies that are statistical but not causal 8. If these spurious de- 42 

pendencies are not explicitly modeled, they can be misinterpreted as regulatory edges, and the 43 

inferred network is corrupted by false positives. This problem is intrinsic to single-cell data, and 44 

any method intended for real perturbation screens must contend with it. 45 

Control function methods, well established in econometrics for estimation under endogene- 46 

ity 9, offer a principled route to addressing latent confounding. They exploit the fact that CRISPR 47 

perturbations, being assigned independently of unmeasured factors, can serve as valid instru- 48 

ments. However, control functions were developed for equation-level estimation — recovering a 49 

single causal relationship — and their direct extension to network estimation does not behave 50 

well. The full control function regresses each gene on the entire perturbation vector, so any 51 

exclusion-restriction violation in a single perturbation contaminates the fitted values for every 52 

downstream gene and propagates into the corresponding edge estimates. Because such viola- 53 

tions are pervasive in real CRISPR screens, the natural tool for the confounding problem fails in 54 

precisely the DAG-estimation setting. 55 

Here we show that a reduced control function resolves this failure. By residualizing each 56 

gene only on its own perturbation indicator, rather than on the full set of perturbations, the re- 57 

duced formulation isolates the directed causal influence among genes while still absorbing latent 58 

confounding. This modification trades a modest amount of statistical power, readily afforded by 59 

the large sample sizes typical of single-cell screens, for a substantial gain in robustness, making 60 

confounding-robust causal discovery tractable at large DAG scale. We develop this idea into 61 

RICE (Robust Identification of Causal Entanglements), an integrated framework for causal gene 62 

network estimation in large-scale single-cell perturbation screens. 63 

RICE naturally accommodates the conditions that modern screens actually present. It mod- 64 

els count-valued transcript data directly through a constrained negative binomial GLM, avoiding 65 

both the linearity assumptions of recent interventional methods such as inspre 10 , IBCD 11 , and 66 

dotears 12 , and the restrictive distributional assumptions of count-based approaches such as 67 

ODS 13 and ZIPBNs 14 . It handles both hard interventions, such as CRISPR knockout, which 68 

removes upstream regulation of the targeted gene, and soft interventions, such as CRISPRi and 69 

CRISPRa, which shift the conditional distribution of the targeted gene while preserving its up- 70 

stream dependence 15–18 (Figure 1B); this distinction destabilizes methods tailored to only one 71 

regime, whereas RICE captures both within a single parameterization. RICE also supports high- 72 

multiplicity-of-infection (high-MOI) designs natively. Low-MOI screens, in which each cell re- 73 

ceives at most one perturbation, scale linearly with library size and are efficient for small panels 74 

but prohibitively expensive at genome scale; high-MOI designs deliver multiple perturbations 75 

per cell and have emerged as a more scalable alternative 6 (Figure 1C). Many computational 76 

pipelines analyze one perturbation at a time, ignoring the combined regulatory effects of ad- 77 

ditional perturbations present in the same cell 19–21 ; RICE instead models the joint effect of co- 78 

occurring perturbations directly. This joint treatment respects the biological structure of high-MOI 79 

experiments and translates into improved performance in these settings. 80

Robustness to unmeasured confounding via reduced control function 196 

A key concern in perturbation-based causal discovery is whether the inferred graph remains reli- 197 

able in the presence of unobserved factors that jointly affect multiple genes. To examine this, we 198 

conducted an additional simulation study in which the number of unmeasured confounders was 199 

increased from 2 to 10 under both soft and hard interventions. As shown in Figure 4A, RICE with 200 

reduced control function (RCF) remained robust as latent confounding became stronger, with 201 

all metrics staying relatively stable across settings. In contrast, competing methods either lost 202 

precision or recovered fewer true signals as the effect of unmeasured confounders increased, 203 

leading to poor performance under strong confounding. 204 

Figure 4A also serves as an ablation study of RCF. When the number of unmeasured con- 205 

founders was small, RICE without RCF showed slightly better performance, since it avoids the 206 

loss of statistical power introduced by residualization. However, its performance deteriorated 207 

rapidly as confounding increased. In particular, removing RCF led to more false positives, indi- 208 

cating that confounding effects were misinterpreted as causal relationships. As a result, although 209 

TPR remained consistently high, the model without RCF showed a marked decline in PPV and 210 

MCC, together with a substantial increase in SHD. These results highlight a tradeoff between 211

Extension to high-MOI screens 214 

To evaluate whether RICE remains effective beyond the conventional low-MOI regime, we con- 215 

ducted an additional simulation study under increasing MOI, in which individual cells may carry 216 

multiple perturbations simultaneously. As described in the model, RICE naturally accommodates 217 

this setting through an additive formulation of perturbation effects without altering the overall es- 218 

timation framework. The competing methods, by contrast, are formulated for single-perturbation 219 

data and provide no native mechanism for multiple co-occurring perturbations, so comparing 220 

them at high-MOI requires adapting the input. We adopted the most direct and transparent adap- 221 

tation available: each cell carrying m perturbations was expanded into m pseudo-observations, 222 

one per perturbation, so that every perturbation–response pair is presented to each method ex- 223 

actly as it would appear in low-MOI data. This construction does not explicitly model perturbation 224 

co-occurrence, but it supplies each method with the full set of single-perturbation signals it was 225 

designed to exploit, rather than discarding high-MOI cells or forcing the methods to fail. 226 

As shown in Figure 4B, RICE remained robust as MOI increased, with performance staying 227 

relatively stable across evaluation metrics over a broad range of settings. By contrast, under 228 

soft interventions, IBCD showed declining performance with increasing MOI, suggesting that 229 

it does not generalize well to high-MOI data. Although the other two competing methods did 230 

not exhibit a pronounced MOI-dependent decline, their performance remained consistently poor 231 

across all settings, indicating limited effectiveness even before entering the high-MOI regime. 232 

Furthermore, performance was continuous from MOI=1 onward, with no jump where cells begin 233 

carrying multiple perturbations, indicating that the baselines were not handicapped, and RICE’s 234 

advantage is genuine rather than an artifact of a weakened comparator. Notably, under hard 235 

interventions, the TPR and MCC of all methods improved marginally as MOI increased. This 236 

reflects the fact that simultaneous perturbation of multiple genes removes their dependence on 237 

upstream regulators, thereby simplifying recovery of the remaining graph. 238 

Taken together, these results highlight the flexibility of RICE across a broad range of MOI 239 

settings. This robustness makes it a practical framework for causal discovery in increasingly 240 

complex single-cell perturbation studies. 241 

Uncovering causal structure in CRISPRi screens of K562 and RPE1 cells 242 

We benchmarked model performance on CRISPRi screens in K562 and RPE1 cells targeting 243 

essential genes with single-gene perturbations 19 , treated as soft interventions in RICE. K562 244 

is a human immortalized myelogenous leukemia cell line, whereas RPE1 is a non-cancerous 245 

hTERT-immortalized retinal pigment epithelial cell line. After preprocessing, we split the data 246 

into two parts: 80% of the cells were used for model training, and the remaining 20% were 247 

reserved for benchmarking. The perturbation proportions in each split were preserved. 248 

RICE identified 2,850 edges in K562 (Supplementary Data S1) and 1,191 edges in RPE1 249 

(Supplementary Data S2). For comparison, inspre, IBCD, and dotears identified 3,252, 4,314, 250 

and 4,817 edges in K562, and 1,813, 1,250, and 2,348 edges in RPE1, respectively. RICE 251 

and IBCD exhibited similar scale-free-like degree distributions (Figures S3 and S4), character- 252 

ized by a concentration near zero and a small number of highly connected genes. This pattern 253 

is consistent with prior reports of power-law behavior in gene regulatory networks 25–27 . RICE 254 

produced the shallowest network, with a maximum depth of 12 layers and most genes located 255

We first examined whether inferred edge weights were associated with total causal effects in 259 

the held-out data. In CRISPRi, gene repression is expected to decrease downstream expression 260 

under positive regulation and increase it under negative regulation, implying a negative correla- 261 

tion between edge weights and total effects. This relationship was strongly observed for RICE, 262 

with R2 = 0 .71 in K562 and R2 = 0 .64 in RPE1 (Figure 5A), compared to at most R2 = 0 .35 for 263 

other methods. Moreover, 95% of RICE-inferred edges had signs opposite to their corresponding 264 

total effects, versus less than 86% for competing methods. 265 

Next, we evaluated inferred edges using both total and direct causal effects (Figure 5B). 266 

Because this analysis was performed on held-out data with a relatively limited sample size (min- 267 

imum of 20 perturbed cells), the resulting p-values were not expected to be extremely small. 268 

Nevertheless, more than 80% of RICE edges were significant ( t-test, p < 0.05 ) for both effect 269 

types in both cell lines, whereas none of the competing methods achieved comparable signifi- 270 

cance. Additionally, in RICE, edges with larger inferred magnitudes were more likely to exhibit 271 

downstream perturbation effects after data normalization (Figures 6A and S5A), providing a nat- 272 

ural basis for prioritizing the identified edges for further investigation. 273 

To assess edge directionality, we examined reversed-edge effects by testing whether pertur- 274 

bation of inferred target genes influenced their corresponding upstream regulators. As shown in 275 

Figures 6B, 6C, S5B and S5C, such reciprocal effects were not observed for RICE, indicating 276 

that the model captures directed causal relationships rather than undirected gene associations. 277 

Collectively, these results suggest that RICE effectively recovers both total causal effects and 278 

non-mediated interactions, enabling more accurate reconstruction of causal pathways. 279 

For each gene, we analyzed whether its inferred upstream regulators exerted causal effects 280 

on its expression; specifically, whether the gene showed expression changes when its identified 281 

parent genes were perturbed. To quantify this, we computed the mean log-odds ratio of the 282 

fraction of upstream genes with significant differential expression (Mann–Whitney U test, p < 283 

0.01 ) (Figure 5C). RICE produced positive values in both datasets (one-sided Wilcoxon signed- 284 

rank test, p < 10 −4) and significantly outperformed all other methods (Mann–Whitney U test, 285 

p < 10 −4). 286

We further characterized the causal network inferred by RICE in K562 cells, identifying 11 293 

modules via the Louvain algorithm 35 . Module sizes ranged from a two-gene doublet to a 118- 294 

gene component (Supplementary Data S1), and several modules captured coherent functional 295 

programs with biologically interpretable internal structure. One representative module (Fig- 296 

ure 6D), comprising 61 genes, contains a coherent ubiquitin–proteasome substructure: it groups 297 

together members of every major proteasome subunit family — PSMA and PSMB (20S core) 298 

together with PSMC and PSMD (19S regulatory particle) — alongside the assembly chaper- 299 

one family PSMG, recovering the structural architecture of the 26S proteasome together with 300

Revealing condition-specific immune regulatory signals in melanoma cells 335 

We studied immune regulatory networks across conditions using a pooled Perturb-CITE-seq 336 

dataset of melanoma cells 44 . In this dataset, cells were co-cultured with autologous tumor- 337 

infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs), treated with interferon (IFN)-γ, or maintained under control con- 338 

ditions, to probe mechanisms of cancer immune evasion. The data consists of 182 genes after 339 

filtration. 340 

RICE was run separately on each condition across 50 bootstrap replicates, and we selected 341 

edges with consistent sign (positive or negative) in at least 80% of replicates for downstream 342 

analysis. Resampling was stratified by perturbation: the number of perturbed cells for each gene, 343 

together with the number of control cells, was preserved in every bootstrap sample, holding the 344 

perturbation design fixed across replicates. This step improves model stability and is motivated 345 

by the sparsity of the data: roughly 40% of genes have mean expression below 0.5, which 346 

destabilizes their negative binomial regression coefficients. As a consequence, an edge may be 347 

selected owing to the low signal-to-noise ratio in these genes rather than a genuine and stable 348 

effect. This limitation of magnitude-based selection becomes pronounced under high sparsity; 349 

because the bootstrap procedure mitigates it, we recommend this approach for similarly sparse 350 

datasets. 351

# DISCUSSION 406 

Across both synthetic and real-world datasets, RICE consistently outperforms existing methods, 407 

and its accuracy remains stable across data distributions and intervention types where current 408 

approaches, typically optimized for a single setting, degrade. Our ablation analysis shows that 409 

the reduced control function is the source of this robustness: removing it leaves true signals intact 410 

but admits a substantial number of false edges under strong confounding, and the resulting loss 411 

of precision is exactly the failure mode that corrupts network reconstruction in real perturbation 412 

data. This establishes the reduced control function not as an incidental design choice but as 413 

the component that makes confounding-robust causal discovery feasible at network scale, at the 414 

cost of a modest and readily affordable reduction in statistical power. 415 

RICE also accommodates high-MOI designs directly, by modeling the combined effects of 416 

multiple perturbations rather than reducing each cell to a single primary perturbation through 417 

the heuristic simplifications common in prior methods. This treatment is increasingly relevant 418 

as high-MOI screens become a practical necessity for genome-scale studies. Supported by 419 

GPU-accelerated optimization, RICE scales to datasets with hundreds of genes and hundreds of 420 

thousands of cells, making it suitable for the size of modern single-cell perturbation experiments. 421 

Several limitations remain. The current framework assumes that perturbation effects com- 422 

bine additively and does not explicitly model higher-order genetic interactions. The acyclicity 423 

constraint, while essential for valid causal interpretation and tractable optimization, precludes the 424 

recovery of feedback loops, which are known to occur in many regulatory systems 59,60 . RICE 425 

therefore recovers the feedforward backbone of the regulatory network, and extending it to ad- 426 

mit cyclic structure is an important direction for future work. In addition, as a point estimator, 427 

RICE selects edges by coefficient magnitude for efficient large-scale optimization; extending it 428 

to settings where edge uncertainty is natively supported is a further direction for methodological 429 

development. 430 

In conclusion, RICE provides a robust and scalable framework for causal network reconstruc- 431 

tion in increasingly complex single-cell perturbation experiments. By making confounding-robust 432 

causal discovery practical at scale, and by recovering networks that both recapitulate known 433 

regulatory biology and nominate new candidate regulators, RICE offers a foundation for moving 434 

from cataloguing perturbation effects toward a causal understanding of gene regulation. 435

Post-training sparsity recovery and hyperparameter selection 807 

Although the ℓ1 penalty in Eq. (4) encourages sparsity during optimization, the estimated weight 808 

matrix is typically not exactly sparse after training. In particular, for computational efficiency, 809 

we employ gradient-based optimizers such as Adam, which do not produce intrinsically sparse 810 

solutions. As a result, many entries remain nonzero but have very small magnitudes, forming a 811 

long tail of weak edge weights. In practice, these near-zero coefficients are difficult to distinguish 812 

from numerical shrinkage artifacts and can obscure the final causal graph if retained directly. We 813 

therefore apply a post-training thresholding step to recover a sparse network from the continuous 814 

edge-weight estimates. 815 

Specifically, after model fitting, we compute the absolute values of all off-diagonal entries in 816 

cW , 817 

akj = | bwkj |, k̸ = j, 

and sort them in decreasing order, 818 

a(1) ≥ a(2) ≥ · · · ≥ a(p(p−1)) .

The resulting ordered sequence typically exhibits an initial regime corresponding to strong causal 819 

signals, followed by a flatter tail dominated by background noise. We identify the transition be- 820 

tween these two regimes by detecting a change point in the sorted weight profile, for example 821 

using methods such as ruptures 62 or kneed 63 , and take the corresponding magnitude as the 822 

threshold. Edges with absolute weight exceeding this threshold are retained, whereas the re- 823 

maining edges are set to zero. An illustration of this procedure is shown in Figure S7A. 824 

This procedure is fully data-driven and avoids the need to tune an edge-selection threshold 825 

using ground-truth graphs or validation labels, which are generally unavailable in real applica- 826 

tions. Compared with imposing a fixed cutoff a priori, change-point-based thresholding adapts 827 

automatically to the scale of the learned weights in each fitted model and produces a sparse 828 

graph that is more readily interpretable from a biological perspective. 829 

As shown in Figure S7B, this post hoc thresholding strategy performs comparably to oracle 830 

optimal thresholding across simulation replicates under both soft and hard interventions, sug- 831 

gesting that it provides an effective practical approximation when the true graph is unknown. 832 

We therefore use the adaptively thresholded graph as the final estimated causal network in all 833 

downstream analyses of real-world data. 834 

The data-driven thresholding strategy further enhances model stability when the ℓ1 penalty 835 

on the weight matrix ( λ1) is small. While a low λ1 may theoretically select more false positives, 836 

these typically manifest as weak signals in the tail of the weight distribution. Consequently, 837 

the change-point detection algorithm tends to identify them as background noise and removes 838 

them automatically. In contrast, an excessively large ℓ1 penalty can over-suppress true causal 839 

signals, making them indistinguishable from noise and leading to their unintended removal during 840 

thresholding. This is evident in the sensitivity analysis in Figure S7C, where higher λ1 values 841 

result in a clear decrease in TPR. 842

Pre- and post-processing of K562 and RPE1 cells 903 

The K562 and RPE1 results were derived from the CRISPRi Perturb-seq functional genomics 904 

maps generated by Replogle et al. 19 . Specifically, we used data from the essential-wide screens 905 

for model illustration and benchmarking. The raw K562 dataset initially contained 310,385 cells 906 

and 8,563 genes, with perturbations targeting 2,057 genes. To obtain a high-quality analytical 907 

dataset, we applied the following filtering criteria: (i) perturbations assigned to fewer than 100 908 

cells were removed; (ii) genes with perturbation-effect z-scores below 5 were excluded; and (iii) 909 

genes with mean expression levels below 0.5 were filtered out. After preprocessing, the final 910 

K562 dataset contained 175,894 cells and 811 genes. The same filtering procedure was applied 911 

to the RPE1 cell line, which initially contained 247,914 cells, 8,749 genes, and 2,393 targeted 912 

perturbations. After filtering, the final RPE1 analytical dataset comprised 86,173 cells and 374 913 

genes. 914 

For both datasets, the library size was calculated as the total UMI count per cell, adjusted 915 

by the scale factor of the corresponding GEM group, and normalized by the total number of 916 

sequenced genes. This library size, along with the mitochondrial read percentage and one-hot- 917 

encoded GEM group identifiers, were included as observed covariates. To further account for 918 

latent technical factors and increase statistical power, we also included the top 50 principal com- 919 

ponents (PCs) derived from the set of excluded genes as additional covariates. To prevent these 920 

PCs from capturing downstream regulatory signals—which could lead to unwanted mediation 921

to their inclusion in the model. For other linear-model-based methods, we applied the Frisch– 923

model inputs, thereby isolating causal effects not explained by observed confounders. 925
